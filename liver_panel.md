# Liver Panel: Comprehensive Reference for a 20-Year-Old Male

This document is a deep reference on the 9 liver markers in a Function Health panel: ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, total bilirubin, albumin, globulin, A/G ratio, and total protein. It is calibrated for a healthy young adult male (age 20). Every quantitative claim is cited inline.

---

## 0. Cross-Cutting Concepts (Read First)

### 0.1 "LFTs" Is a Misnomer

The conventional "liver function tests" panel mostly does NOT measure liver function:

- **ALT, AST, ALP, GGT** are **leakage enzymes** (or membrane-bound enzymes that get cleaved into serum). They go up when hepatocytes or biliary epithelium are damaged or stressed. Their concentration tells you about *injury*, not synthetic capacity (Limdi 2003 Postgrad Med J; Kwo 2017 ACG guideline).
- **Albumin, prothrombin time/INR, and (to a lesser extent) bilirubin** reflect actual hepatic *synthetic and clearance function*. Of the 9 markers in this panel, only **albumin** and **bilirubin** truly assess function. ALT/AST/ALP/GGT assess injury.
- Practical consequence: a person with cirrhosis can have *normal* ALT/AST late in disease because there are few hepatocytes left to leak enzyme — at that stage, low albumin and prolonged INR are the signal (Limdi 2003).

### 0.2 Pattern Recognition: Hepatocellular vs. Cholestatic

The **R ratio** classifies liver-injury patterns: R = (ALT ÷ ULN_ALT) / (ALP ÷ ULN_ALP) (Kwo 2017 ACG; MDCalc R Factor for Liver Injury).

- **R > 5**: hepatocellular pattern (viral hepatitis, NAFLD/MASLD, ischemic, autoimmune, Wilson's, drugs).
- **R < 2**: cholestatic pattern (biliary obstruction, PBC, PSC, drug cholestasis, infiltrative disease).
- **R 2–5**: mixed.

### 0.3 NAFLD → MASLD Nomenclature (2023)

In June 2023, a multisociety Delphi consensus (Rinella, Lazarus, Ratziu et al., *Journal of Hepatology* 2023; AASLD) replaced **NAFLD** with **MASLD** (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease) and **NASH** with **MASH**. Steatotic liver disease (SLD) is the umbrella term. A new category, **MetALD**, captures patients meeting MASLD criteria who also drink moderate-to-heavy alcohol (140 g/week female, 210 g/week male). MASLD requires hepatic steatosis plus ≥1 of 5 cardiometabolic risk factors (BMI/waist, glucose/T2D, BP, triglycerides, HDL) — it is no longer a diagnosis of exclusion (AASLD 2023; Rinella 2023 *Hepatology* multisociety Delphi).

### 0.4 Why MASLD Is the Default Differential in a 20-Year-Old

- US adult NAFLD/MASLD prevalence ranges from ~18.5% (NHANES, hepatic steatosis index) to ~38% (NHANES with CAP imaging; Song 2024 *Liver International*; Frontiers in Nutrition 2024).
- Prevalence reaches ~65% in overweight, ~90% in morbidly obese, and ~70% in T2D (multiple 2024 reviews; Hepatology 2024; ADA Consensus Diabetes Care 2025).
- 99% of NAFLD patients meet MASLD criteria — the natural history is essentially identical (PubMed 37678723 / 2023).
- Globally MASLD is now the most common chronic liver disease, affecting >1/3 of adults (Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol 2024).

### 0.5 FIB-4: Fibrosis Screening Score

**FIB-4 = (age × AST) / (platelets × √ALT)** (MDCalc; Hepatitis C Online UW; Fatty Liver Foundation).

Interpretation in NAFLD/MASLD (AASLD 2023):
- **<1.3**: low risk of advanced fibrosis (negative predictive value ~90%).
- **1.3–2.67**: indeterminate; consider elastography (FibroScan, MR elastography) or repeat.
- **>2.67**: advanced fibrosis likely; >3.25 has 97% specificity for advanced fibrosis.
- **Caveats**: FIB-4 is **not validated <35 years of age** and overestimates fibrosis >65 (use 2.0 cutoff in elderly). For a 20-year-old, FIB-4 is unreliable for ruling out fibrosis — use clinical context, imaging, and serial labs instead (UW Hepatitis C Online; AASLD 2023).

### 0.6 Source Provenance Note

Where I cite "Function Health optimal range," that is the range Function Health uses on their reports as a proprietary tighter target than lab reference ranges. Function Health does not publish a single peer-reviewed source for these — they're synthesized from literature targets and lab-vendor norms. Treat them as informed targets, not consensus guidelines. The harder-to-argue ranges I cite are **ACG 2017 (Kwo)**, **AASLD 2023**, **EASL 2024**, and primary epidemiology (Prati 2002; Ruttmann 2005; Schwertner 1994; etc.).

## 1. ALANINE AMINOTRANSFERASE (ALT)

### 1.1 What It Is

ALT (also called SGPT — serum glutamate-pyruvate transaminase) is a **cytosolic** enzyme that catalyzes reversible transamination: alanine + α-ketoglutarate ↔ pyruvate + glutamate. The reaction requires **pyridoxal 5'-phosphate (vitamin B6)** as cofactor (StatPearls; Wikipedia ALT; Sookoian & Pirola 2014 *Worldj Gastroenterol*).

- **Location**: Highest concentration in the liver (cytosol of hepatocytes); much lower in kidney, heart, and skeletal muscle (StatPearls).
- **Liver specificity**: ALT is the **most liver-specific** of the routine enzymes — when ALT is elevated, the liver is by far the most likely source. AST is found in roughly equal or higher concentrations in heart, skeletal muscle, kidney, brain, and red blood cells (Limdi 2003 Postgrad Med J; eClinpath).
- **Assay**: Continuous spectrophotometric, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)-coupled, IFCC-recommended at 37°C, with or without pyridoxal phosphate supplementation. Without P5P, results can be artifactually low in B6-deficient samples (IFCC method; Schumann 2002).
- **Half-life in serum**: ~36–47 hours (eClinpath; De Ritis review PMC3866949).

### 1.2 Reference Ranges and Optimal Range

| Source | Male reference / "healthy" range |
|---|---|
| Conventional lab ULN (older) | 40–55 U/L |
| Prati 2002 *Ann Intern Med* (donors with no risk factors) | ULN 30 U/L male, 19 U/L female |
| ACG 2017 Kwo guideline (definitive recommendation) | "True healthy" ULN **29–33 U/L male**, 19–25 U/L female |
| AASLD 2023 Practice Guidance | Endorses lowered ULN |
| Function Health "optimal" (typical) | ~10–25 U/L |

**Direction of "better"**: Probably **U-shaped** — moderately low (15–25 U/L in a non-frail young man with intact muscle mass) is best; very low (<10 U/L) raises suspicion for B6 deficiency, sarcopenia, or severe hepatic atrophy (Vespasiani-Gentilucci 2018 *J Gerontol*; Liu 2014 *Crit Rev Clin Lab Sci*).

### 1.3 Causes of HIGH ALT — Magnitude Tiers

(Kwo 2017 ACG; Hepatitis VA provider course; AAFP 2017 review; AASLD core series)

**Borderline (<2× ULN, ~30–60 U/L male):**
- **MASLD/NAFLD** (overwhelmingly the most common cause in young US adults — the *default* explanation).
- Recent vigorous exercise (especially eccentric, resistance training) — releases AST and to a lesser extent ALT.
- Medications: statins, NSAIDs, antibiotics, supplements (green tea extract, kava, anabolic steroids).
- Mild chronic viral hepatitis (HCV, HBV — often asymptomatic).
- Iron overload / hemochromatosis (HFE C282Y homozygous; ~1 in 200 Northern European descent).

**Mild (2–5× ULN, ~60–150 U/L):**
- More overt MASLD/MASH.
- Alcohol use (though typically AST > ALT here — see DeRitis below).
- Chronic viral hepatitis flares.
- Drug-induced liver injury (DILI): supplements (ashwagandha, turmeric/curcumin in supplement form, anabolic-androgenic steroids), acetaminophen at moderate doses, statins.
- Autoimmune hepatitis (often higher).
- Hemochromatosis, Wilson's, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.
- Celiac disease (10–20% have transaminitis).

**Moderate (5–15× ULN, ~150–500 U/L):**
- Acute viral hepatitis (HAV, HBV, HCV, HEV, EBV, CMV).
- Autoimmune hepatitis flare.
- Drug-induced (acetaminophen overdose evolving, idiosyncratic DILI).
- Acute biliary obstruction (paradoxical large ALT bump in first 24–48h before settling into cholestatic pattern).

**Severe (>15× ULN, >500 U/L) and Massive (>1000 U/L; "shock liver" range):**
- **Acetaminophen toxicity** (classic — often >3000 U/L).
- **Ischemic hepatitis** ("shock liver") — hypoperfusion, cardiac arrest, sepsis, severe heart failure.
- Acute viral hepatitis, severe.
- Toxin (Amanita phalloides mushroom).
- Acute autoimmune hepatitis.
- Acute Budd-Chiari, hepatic artery thrombosis (Kwo 2017; Hepatitis VA course).

### 1.4 Causes of LOW ALT (<10 U/L)

- **Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) deficiency** — pyridoxal 5'-phosphate is the obligate ALT cofactor; deficiency artifactually lowers measured ALT activity. Estimated 5–25% of men in Germany/UK/Netherlands have suboptimal B6 intake (Ueland 2024 *Nutrients* narrative review).
- **Sarcopenia, frailty, aging**. Vespasiani-Gentilucci 2018 *J Gerontol Series A*: in elderly, baseline ALT <19 U/L predicts 6-year frailty (RR 1.9), disability (RR 2.1), and reduced survival.
- **End-stage liver disease** — decompensated cirrhosis, fulminant failure (no hepatocytes left to leak).
- **Severe protein-calorie malnutrition** (overlapping with B6 and sarcopenia mechanisms).

For a 20-year-old man with normal muscle mass and diet, isolated very low ALT is uncommon and probably benign; consider B6 status.

### 1.5 AST/ALT (DeRitis) Ratio

(De Ritis 1957; Botros & Sikaris 2013 *Clin Biochem Rev* PMC3866949; Wikipedia AST/ALT ratio; Williams & Hoofnagle 1988 *Gastroenterology*)

- **Normal ratio**: ~0.8–1.0. AST has a shorter serum half-life (16–18h cytosolic, ~10 days mitochondrial) than ALT (~36h), so in chronic hepatocellular injury AST clearance keeps it slightly below ALT.
- **Ratio <1 (ALT > AST)**: classic for **NAFLD/MASLD** without fibrosis, **chronic viral hepatitis** (HBV, HCV) without cirrhosis. NAFLD typically yields 0.5–0.8.
- **Ratio >1 with high enzymes**: progressing fibrosis, cirrhosis (regardless of underlying cause).
- **Ratio ≥2**: classic for **alcoholic liver disease** (true in ~70% of ALD; ~92% of ALD have ratio >1). Mechanism: alcohol depletes pyridoxal-5'-phosphate → preferentially lowers ALT activity, plus mitochondrial AST release.
- **Ratio >>1 with normal/low ALT and high CK**: **muscle source** for AST (rhabdomyolysis, recent intense exercise, statin myopathy, inflammatory myopathies). Always check CK and ALT together when AST is the only abnormal liver enzyme.

### 1.6 Confounders (ALT specifically)

- **Vigorous exercise** in the 24–72h before draw (especially eccentric — running downhill, heavy resistance) — can mildly elevate ALT and significantly elevate AST.
- **BMI**: ALT rises with BMI even within "normal" weight (Prati 2002 found ALT independently associated with BMI and lipid/glucose dysregulation).
- **Diurnal**: ALT can vary up to ~45% within a day; lowest in early morning, highest afternoon (Cordoba 1998 *Hepatology*).
- **Hemolysis** of the sample: minor effect (slight increase).
- **Medications**: statins, acetaminophen, NSAIDs, antiepileptics, isoniazid, anabolic steroids, oral contraceptives.
- **Supplements**: ashwagandha, green tea extract, kava, garcinia cambogia, anabolic supplements, high-dose niacin, turmeric supplements (DILI — increasingly reported in young men; Navarro 2017 *Hepatology*).

### 1.7 Specific Conditions ALT Flags in a Young Adult Male

1. **MASLD/NAFLD** (≈80% of mild persistent transaminitis in young US adults; AASLD 2023).
2. **Drug/supplement-induced liver injury** (DILI).
3. **Chronic viral hepatitis B or C** — order HBsAg, anti-HCV.
4. **Hereditary hemochromatosis** — order ferritin, transferrin saturation.
5. **Wilson's disease** (presents <40, often <20) — order ceruloplasmin, 24-hr urine copper, slit-lamp.
6. **Autoimmune hepatitis** — ANA, ASMA, anti-LKM, IgG.
7. **Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency** — A1AT level + phenotype.
8. **Celiac disease** — tTG IgA + total IgA.
9. **Anabolic steroid use** (specifically common in 20-year-old males; orals like methyltestosterone, anadrol cause cholestatic-mixed pattern).

## 2. ASPARTATE AMINOTRANSFERASE (AST)

### 2.1 What It Is

AST (formerly SGOT) catalyzes: aspartate + α-ketoglutarate ↔ oxaloacetate + glutamate. Like ALT, it requires pyridoxal-5'-phosphate. **Two isoenzymes**:
- **Cytosolic (cAST, GOT1)** — predominantly from RBCs and heart; half-life 16–18 h.
- **Mitochondrial (mAST, GOT2)** — predominantly from liver; half-life ~10 days. Release reflects more severe injury (eClinpath; ScienceDirect AST isoenzyme 2).

### 2.2 Tissue Distribution (Critical — This Is the Confusion Point)

In **decreasing concentration**: heart > liver > skeletal muscle > kidney > pancreas > spleen > lung > erythrocytes (eClinpath; Limdi 2003 Postgrad Med J). **AST is NOT liver-specific**. A high AST has at least three plausible non-liver sources:

1. **Skeletal muscle** — exercise (especially resistance training, eccentric loading), rhabdomyolysis, statin myopathy, inflammatory myopathies (polymyositis, dermatomyositis).
2. **Heart** — historically used pre-troponin for MI (post-MI, AST peaks 18–36h); still elevates with myocarditis, large MIs.
3. **Hemolysis** — both in vivo and sample-handling hemolysis (RBCs contain AST).

### 2.3 Disambiguating AST Elevation

- **Always pair with CK (creatine kinase)**: CK >5× ULN with AST > ALT and normal/low GGT → **muscle source**.
  - Statin myopathy: ~7–29% have muscle symptoms; CK elevation in fewer (Stroes 2015 *Eur Heart J* EAS Consensus).
  - Rhabdomyolysis: CK >10× ULN (often >10,000); AST 5–20× ULN; ALT 2–5× ULN; LDH high; myoglobinuria; risk of AKI (PMC7081005).
  - Recent heavy exercise: CK can hit 1,000–5,000 transiently in untrained or eccentric bouts; AST 2–4× ULN; ALT mildly up.
- **Always check for hemolysis** on the sample report.

### 2.4 Reference Ranges and Optimal

| Source | Male range |
|---|---|
| Standard lab | 10–40 U/L |
| Updated healthy (97.5th percentile, healthy subpopulation, 2024 multicenter validation) | ~49 U/L male ULN |
| Function Health "optimal" (typical) | 10–25 U/L |

(Updated reference limits validated against long-term liver-related outcomes: PMC12628042 / 2024)

### 2.5 Causes of HIGH AST

By tier (when liver is the source):
- **Mild (<5× ULN)**: MASLD, alcohol, viral hepatitis, drugs, exercise-induced (often pure muscle).
- **Moderate (5–15×)**: acute viral, autoimmune, drug, severe alcohol-induced (alcoholic hepatitis classically AST 100–400 with AST/ALT ≥2 and rarely >500).
- **Severe (>15×)**: acetaminophen, ischemic hepatitis, severe acute viral, mushroom toxin.

Non-liver:
- Rhabdomyolysis (CK >10× ULN).
- Recent strenuous exercise (transient).
- Cardiac injury (rarely the main driver — troponin is now the test).
- Hemolysis (in vivo: hemolytic anemia; ex vivo: drawing/handling).

### 2.6 Causes of LOW AST

Same categories as low ALT: B6 deficiency, end-stage liver disease, sarcopenia. Less clinically actionable than low ALT because AST has multi-tissue baseline.

### 2.7 Direction of "Better"

Probably **monotonic** in the lower range (lower is better, down to ~15 U/L) but **U-shaped** with very low values flagging B6 deficiency. There is no evidence that pushing AST below 15 confers benefit.

### 2.8 Confounders

- Recent exercise (24–72h pre-draw) — single biggest preventable confounder.
- Hemolyzed sample.
- Macro-AST: rare immunoglobulin-bound AST that doesn't clear normally; presents as persistent isolated AST elevation with no other abnormality (Litin 1987 *Mayo Clin Proc*).
- Diurnal variation.

## 3. ALKALINE PHOSPHATASE (ALP)

### 3.1 What It Is

ALP is a family of **zinc metalloenzymes** that hydrolyze phosphate monoesters at alkaline pH. Located on the **outer cell membrane** (GPI-anchored), particularly the **canalicular membrane** of hepatocytes and the surface of **osteoblasts** (StatPearls Serum Alkaline Phosphatase; Sharma 2014 *World J Gastroenterol*; Vimalraj 2020 *Gene*).

### 3.2 Tissue Sources (CRITICAL — ALP is multi-tissue)

In serum, ALP comes from:
1. **Liver** (canalicular membrane of hepatocytes; biliary epithelium) — about 50% in adults.
2. **Bone** (osteoblasts) — about 50% in adults; **higher proportion in adolescents/young adults during growth**.
3. **Intestine** — small contribution (up to 20%); higher post-meal in blood-group O/B secretors.
4. **Placenta** — only in 2nd/3rd trimester (not relevant for males).

The **liver/bone/kidney isoform** is encoded by the **TNSALP gene (ALPL)** — it's actually one protein with different glycosylation depending on the tissue. Intestinal and placental are coded by separate genes (ALPI, ALPP, ALPP2; Sharma 2014; Wikipedia ALPL).

### 3.3 The Adolescent / Young Adult Caveat (Important Here)

- During puberty and growth, **bone ALP can be 2–3× the adult range**: pediatric ranges can hit **150–500 IU/L** during growth spurts (vs. adult ULN ~117–147), with peaks around ages 12–15 in boys (Cleveland Clinic; Mayo Pediatric Catalog; e-APEM 2013).
- Boys typically still have elevated bone ALP through age ~17–18, sometimes lingering to **age 20–22** as growth plates fully close (NHANES skeletal maturity data).
- A 20-year-old male whose growth plates have just closed may still sit at the upper end of the "adult" range — that's **not pathological**.

### 3.4 Reference Ranges and Optimal

| Source | Range |
|---|---|
| Adult lab range | 44–147 U/L (varies by lab, e.g., 40–129) |
| Updated healthy male (2024 multicenter) ULN | 108 U/L |
| Function Health "optimal" (typical) | 40–100 U/L |
| Adolescents/young adults during/late growth | up to 500 U/L can be physiologic (peaks age 12–15 boys) |

Sources: Cleveland Clinic; NHANES; PMC12628042 2024; Mayo pediatric reference catalog.

### 3.5 Causes of HIGH ALP

**Mild (1–2× ULN):**
- **Late puberty / growth plate closure** (still relevant at 20).
- MASLD/NAFLD with mild canalicular membrane disturbance.
- Mild biliary disease, drugs (anticonvulsants, OCPs, antibiotics).
- Healing fracture.
- Vitamin D deficiency (osteomalacia, secondary hyperparathyroidism).
- Pregnancy (placental — not for males).

**Moderate (2–5× ULN):**
- Cholestatic liver disease (early PBC, PSC).
- Drug-induced cholestasis.
- Infiltrative liver disease (sarcoid, lymphoma, granulomatous disease).
- Significant biliary obstruction.
- **Paget's disease of bone** (typically older patients but can present <40) — often markedly elevated.
- **Primary hyperparathyroidism** (with high bone turnover).

**Severe (>5× ULN):**
- Major biliary obstruction (stone, stricture, tumor).
- PBC/PSC advanced.
- Bone metastases (older population), osteosarcoma, severe Paget's.

### 3.6 The Critical Differentiation: Liver vs Bone Source

**The key question is GGT** (Kwo 2017 ACG; Cleveland Clinic; CCJM 2000):

| ALP | GGT | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| ↑ | ↑ | **Liver/biliary source** (cholestasis, NAFLD, drugs, obstruction) |
| ↑ | normal | **Bone source** (growth, Paget's, vitamin D deficiency, healing fracture, primary HPT) |
| ↑ | normal | Less commonly: pregnancy/placenta (NA for males), intestinal |

**Direct fractionation** by electrophoresis or immunoassay can split bone ALP (BALP) from liver ALP isoforms. BALP is also separately measured in osteoporosis monitoring (Vimalraj 2020).

### 3.7 Causes of LOW ALP (<40 U/L; clinically meaningful threshold ~30)

Often overlooked, but several real conditions cause low ALP:

- **Hypophosphatasia (HPP)** — autosomal-dominant or -recessive ALPL loss-of-function. Adult presentation may be subtle (recurrent stress fractures, dental loss, chondrocalcinosis); diagnostic clue is persistently low ALP with elevated pyridoxal 5'-phosphate (PLP) and phosphoethanolamine (PMC8555173; Soft Bones; Whyte 2016 GeneReviews). Prevalence of *persistent* low ALP suggesting HPP is ~1 in 15,000–100,000 depending on definition.
- **Zinc deficiency** — ALP is zinc-dependent; severe Zn deficiency can lower ALP (functional cofactor depletion).
- **Magnesium deficiency** — similar mechanism.
- **Hypothyroidism** — reduced bone turnover.
- **Wilson's disease** — copper-induced hemolysis with low ALP relative to bilirubin (ALP/total-bilirubin ratio <4 has 100% sensitivity for fulminant Wilson's; Korman 2008 *Hepatology*).
- **Severe protein-calorie malnutrition** (anorexia nervosa, chronic illness).
- **Vitamin C deficiency** (rare).
- **Pernicious anemia / B12 deficiency** (occasional).
- **Oral contraceptives / estrogens** (mild lowering).
- **Celiac disease, IBD with malabsorption**.

### 3.8 Direction of "Better"

**U-shaped**. Very low ALP raises suspicion for the conditions above; very high ALP indicates pathology. A reasonable "optimal" target band for a healthy 20-year-old male is roughly **50–90 U/L** (above the threshold of concern for hypophosphatasia/zinc, well below the threshold suggesting cholestasis or Paget's).

### 3.9 Confounders

- Age (young adults still in late-bone-growth phase).
- Postprandial intestinal ALP rise (especially in O and B blood group secretors).
- Pregnancy (NA).
- Recent fracture or orthopedic surgery.
- Drugs (anticonvulsants, OCPs, methotrexate).

## 4. GAMMA-GLUTAMYL TRANSFERASE (GGT)

### 4.1 What It Is

GGT is a transmembrane enzyme that catalyzes the transfer of γ-glutamyl groups from glutathione (GSH) to other amino acids, initiating extracellular GSH catabolism so cells can recycle cysteine for new GSH synthesis. It is on the **luminal surface** of secretory epithelia, with highest expression in **bile duct (biliary) epithelium**, **renal proximal tubules**, **pancreas**, and **periportal hepatocytes** (Whitfield 2001 *Crit Rev Clin Lab Sci*; Corti 2010 *FEBS J*; Kunutsor 2016 *Liver Int*).

Functionally, GGT is a node in **redox homeostasis and oxidative stress response** — its activity is **upregulated when cells are under oxidative stress** (this is why GGT goes up so easily and reflects more than "biliary disease").

### 4.2 Reference Ranges and Optimal

| Source | Male reference |
|---|---|
| Standard lab | 8–61 U/L (typical) or 6–50 |
| Updated healthy (97.5th percentile, 2024) | ~48 U/L male |
| Function Health "optimal" (typical) | <30 U/L |
| Practical "great" target (Kunutsor 2016 review) | <20 U/L |

GGT is the **single most sensitive** liver-enzyme marker — but the most non-specific. Almost anything that stresses the hepatobiliary or oxidative-defense systems pushes GGT.

### 4.3 Causes of HIGH GGT

**Mild (<2× ULN):**
- **Alcohol** — GGT is the most sensitive enzyme to alcohol; even moderate drinking can elevate it. A small fraction (~10–15%) of regular drinkers don't show elevated GGT, so a normal GGT does NOT exclude heavy drinking.
- **NAFLD/MASLD** — consistently elevated in fatty liver (Kunutsor 2016).
- **Drugs**: phenytoin, carbamazepine, barbiturates, statins, oral contraceptives, NSAIDs, warfarin, isoniazid.
- **Smoking** — independent association.
- **Obesity, metabolic syndrome, T2D** — independent of NAFLD.
- **Supplements** that are hepatotoxic.

**Moderate–severe (>2× ULN):**
- Biliary obstruction (cholestasis) — GGT and ALP co-elevated.
- Drug-induced cholestasis.
- Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC), primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC).
- Heart failure, MI.
- COPD.

### 4.4 GGT and Mortality

GGT is **independently associated with all-cause and cardiovascular mortality** beyond traditional risk factors:

- **Ruttmann 2005** *Circulation* (n=163,944 Austrians, 17-year follow-up): adjusted HR per log-GGT increase 1.66 (men) and 1.64 (women) for CVD mortality. Effect was **stronger in younger participants**.
- **Lee 2007** Framingham Offspring (PMID 18753951; published in *Arterioscler Thromb Vasc Biol*): 1-SD log-GGT elevation associated with 13% higher CVD risk after adjusting for established factors.
- **UK Biobank prospective** (Kunutsor et al. 2022 *eClinicalMedicine*): higher GGT associated with all-cause, CV, and liver-related mortality in dose-response fashion.
- Mechanism hypotheses: GGT marks oxidative stress, accelerates LDL oxidation in atheromas, reflects hepatic insulin resistance/visceral adiposity (Whitfield 2001; Kunutsor 2016).

This is why GGT is increasingly viewed as more than "an alcohol marker" — it's a **systemic metabolic and oxidative stress biomarker**.

### 4.5 Causes of LOW GGT

- **Hypothyroidism** — modest lowering (Beckett 1985 *JAMA Intern Med*).
- Genuinely healthy, non-drinking, lean people often run GGT 8–15.

Low GGT is generally **not concerning** in the absence of hypothyroid features.

### 4.6 Direction of "Better"

**Mostly monotonic** in the population-relevant range — lower is better, with diminishing returns below ~10–15. There is no evidence of harm from very low GGT in a euthyroid individual.

### 4.7 Confounders

- **Alcohol** in the prior 24h–2 weeks.
- BMI, smoking, glucose intolerance, lipid abnormalities.
- Multiple drugs (particularly enzyme inducers).
- Within-day variability is small relative to other enzymes.

### 4.8 Specific Conditions GGT Flags

1. Alcohol use (most sensitive marker).
2. NAFLD/MASLD.
3. Cholestatic liver disease (with high ALP).
4. Drug-induced liver injury.
5. Cardiovascular and metabolic risk independent of liver disease.
6. Confirms hepatic source of high ALP.

## 5. TOTAL BILIRUBIN

### 5.1 What It Is

Bilirubin is the breakdown product of **heme**: senescent RBCs → heme → biliverdin (via heme oxygenase) → bilirubin (via biliverdin reductase) → conjugated in liver by **UGT1A1 (UDP-glucuronosyltransferase 1A1)** → excreted in bile (Wikipedia bilirubin metabolism; Vitek 2025 *Biochemia Medica* review).

**Two forms** measured:
- **Indirect (unconjugated)**: water-insoluble, albumin-bound, NOT filtered by kidney. Elevated in hemolysis or impaired conjugation.
- **Direct (conjugated)**: water-soluble, can be filtered by kidney (causes dark urine when high). Elevated in hepatocellular injury or biliary obstruction.

**Total** = direct + indirect.

### 5.2 Reference Ranges

| Marker | Adult range |
|---|---|
| Total bilirubin | 0.2–1.2 mg/dL (3–17 µmol/L) |
| Direct (conjugated) | 0.0–0.3 mg/dL |
| Indirect (unconjugated) | 0.2–0.8 mg/dL (calculated as total – direct) |
| Visible jaundice threshold | ≥2.0–3.0 mg/dL |
| Function Health "optimal" total | typically 0.5–1.0 mg/dL band |

Source: Cleveland Clinic; Medscape Bilirubin reference; multiple labs.

### 5.3 Causes of HIGH UNCONJUGATED (Indirect-Predominant) Bilirubin

- **Gilbert syndrome** — by far the most common cause in young adult males. UGT1A1*28 promoter polymorphism (TA7 instead of TA6) → ~30% reduced UGT activity. **Prevalence ~3–10% of general population**, with 12.4% of Caucasian men carrying the syndrome (vs. 4.8% Caucasian women; pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8617539). Bilirubin typically 1.2–3.0 mg/dL, occasionally up to 5.0, **fluctuates with fasting, illness, stress, dehydration, exercise**. Higher in men due to androgen inhibition of UGT and higher RBC turnover. **Benign, no treatment needed** — actually associated with health benefits (see 5.7).
- **Crigler-Najjar syndrome** — extremely rare (~1 per million); Type I has near-zero UGT activity, severe neonatal jaundice; Type II has reduced activity, milder.
- **Hemolysis** — hemolytic anemia (autoimmune, sickle cell, G6PD deficiency, hereditary spherocytosis), large hematomas, transfusion reactions. **Always check reticulocyte count, haptoglobin (low in hemolysis), LDH (high), and peripheral smear**.
- **Ineffective erythropoiesis** — thalassemia, megaloblastic anemia.
- **Neonatal jaundice** (NA for adults).

### 5.4 Causes of HIGH CONJUGATED (Direct-Predominant) Bilirubin

- **Hepatocellular injury** — hepatitis (viral, autoimmune, drug, alcoholic, ischemic), cirrhosis.
- **Cholestasis / biliary obstruction** — choledocholithiasis, pancreatic head cancer, cholangiocarcinoma, PBC, PSC.
- **Drug-induced cholestasis** — anabolic steroids, oral contraceptives, antibiotics (augmentin), TPN.
- **Sepsis** (cholestasis of sepsis).
- **Dubin-Johnson syndrome** — autosomal-recessive ABCC2/MRP2 mutation, defect in canalicular excretion. Benign; bilirubin 2–5 mg/dL.
- **Rotor syndrome** — SLCO1B1/SLCO1B3 mutation, defect in hepatocyte reuptake. Benign.

### 5.5 Approach to Isolated Elevated Total Bilirubin

In a young man with **isolated total bilirubin 1.2–2.5 mg/dL** and otherwise normal liver enzymes:
1. **Fractionate** — direct vs. indirect. If indirect predominates (>80%): Gilbert is overwhelmingly likely.
2. **Rule out hemolysis**: CBC (anemia? high MCV?), reticulocyte count, haptoglobin, LDH, peripheral smear.
3. If hemolysis is excluded and pattern is indirect-predominant → Gilbert syndrome diagnosis is clinical; UGT1A1 genotyping is available but not required.
4. If direct fraction is >20–30%, evaluate for hepatobiliary disease: imaging (RUQ ultrasound), repeat enzymes, hepatitis serologies.

### 5.6 Function Health Reporting

Function Health typically reports total bilirubin only (not fractionated). For a young man with persistently elevated total in the 1.2–2.5 mg/dL range and normal everything else, the next step is to order direct/indirect fractionation; this is usually consistent with Gilbert.

### 5.7 Bilirubin Paradox: Mild Elevation Is Beneficial

This is a counterintuitive but well-replicated finding: **mildly elevated bilirubin (Gilbert range) is associated with LOWER cardiovascular and all-cause mortality**, likely because bilirubin is a potent endogenous antioxidant.

- **Stocker 1987** *Science* (Stocker R, Yamamoto Y, McDonagh AF, Glazer AN, Ames BN. *Science* 235:1043–1046): seminal paper showing bilirubin scavenges peroxyl radicals at micromolar concentrations and outperforms α-tocopherol at physiologic O₂ tensions. Reframed bilirubin from "toxic waste" to "physiological antioxidant."
- **Schwertner 1994** *Clin Chem* 40:18–23: 877 men, inverse relationship between total bilirubin and angiographic CAD; 50% decrease in bilirubin = 47% increase in CAD odds.
- **Vitek 2002** and **Vitek 2014** *Atherosclerosis* / *J Clin Lipidol*: Gilbert carriers have 1/3 the rate of CV disease; mechanism includes lower oxidized LDL, lower CRP, better endothelial function.
- **Inoguchi 2007** *JAMA*: diabetic patients with Gilbert syndrome had ~3× lower prevalence of vascular complications vs. normobilirubinemic diabetics.
- **Horsfall 2011** *Eur J Public Health*: Gilbert protects from all-cause mortality, ischemic heart disease, COPD.
- **Vitek 2019** *Front Pharmacol*: meta-review of cardio-, metabolic-, oncologic-, and neuro-protective effects.

### 5.8 Causes of LOW Total Bilirubin

- Increased RBC clearance not happening (rare).
- Iron deficiency anemia (mild lowering).
- Drugs that induce UGT (phenobarbital, rifampin).
- Some studies show that low bilirubin (<0.4 mg/dL) is associated with **higher** CV and cancer risk (Horsfall 2011; Choi 2018 *Atherosclerosis*) — i.e., the relationship may be **inverse**, not U-shaped.

### 5.9 Direction of "Better"

For total bilirubin, the data point to a **monotonic-or-inverse relationship for cardiovascular outcomes within the normal range** (i.e., higher within the normal-to-mildly-elevated range may be cardioprotective up to ~2.0–2.5 mg/dL). Above ~3 mg/dL, you're looking at either pathology or significant Gilbert during illness/fasting. **For a 20-year-old man, a total bilirubin of 0.7–1.5 mg/dL with pure indirect predominance is essentially perfect** — cardio-protective Gilbert range.

### 5.10 Confounders

- **Fasting** raises Gilbert bilirubin substantially (50% prolonged fast can double it).
- **Illness, surgery, intense exercise, dehydration, sleep deprivation** all elevate bilirubin in Gilbert.
- **Hemolyzed sample** (in vitro) artifactually elevates.
- **Medications**: rifampin, indinavir, atazanavir competitively inhibit UGT1A1 → look like Gilbert.

## 6. ALBUMIN

### 6.1 What It Is

Albumin is the most abundant plasma protein (~60% of total plasma protein), synthesized exclusively by **hepatocytes** at ~10–15 g/day, with a serum half-life of ~17–21 days (StatPearls Physiology Albumin; Levitt 2016 *Int J Gen Med*).

Functions:
- Maintains plasma **oncotic pressure** (~75% of it).
- Carrier for free fatty acids, bilirubin, hormones, drugs (warfarin, NSAIDs), trace metals.
- Buffer.
- **Negative acute-phase reactant** — synthesis decreases in inflammation as the liver pivots to making CRP, fibrinogen, etc.

### 6.2 Reference Ranges

| Source | Range |
|---|---|
| Standard adult lab | 3.5–5.0 g/dL (35–50 g/L) |
| Function Health "optimal" young adult | 4.5–5.0 g/dL |

Source: Mayo, Cleveland Clinic, Medscape Albumin.

### 6.3 Causes of LOW Albumin (Hypoalbuminemia)

- **Chronic liver disease** — cirrhosis (decreased synthesis). Late marker; hepatocellular reserve is large.
- **Acute/chronic inflammation** — negative acute-phase reactant. Even minor systemic inflammation can drop albumin 0.3–0.5 g/dL.
- **Malnutrition** — but slow-changing (long half-life means it takes weeks).
- **Nephrotic syndrome** — urinary protein loss >3.5 g/day.
- **Protein-losing enteropathy** (Crohn's, celiac, lymphangiectasia).
- **Severe burns** — capillary leak + skin loss.
- **Heart failure** (volume expansion + chronic inflammation).
- **Pregnancy** — hemodilution (NA).
- **Capillary leak syndromes / sepsis** — redistribution into interstitium.
- **Thyrotoxicosis** — increased catabolism.

### 6.4 Causes of HIGH Albumin

- **Dehydration** (most common — hemoconcentration).
- High-protein diet doesn't really raise albumin much in healthy state.
- No real "pathologic high" intrinsic to albumin metabolism.

### 6.5 Albumin and Mortality

Albumin is one of the strongest **non-specific** mortality predictors known:
- **Goldwasser & Feldman 1997** *J Clin Epidemiol*: 24–56% increase in odds of death per 2.5 g/L (0.25 g/dL) decrement in albumin, across many cohorts and disease states.
- **Phillips 1989** *BMJ Lancet*: Whitehall study; serum albumin inversely related to all-cause mortality in healthy middle-aged men.
- **Don & Kaysen 2004** *Semin Dial*: marker of inflammation more than nutrition in chronic disease.

### 6.6 Direction of "Better"

**Monotonic up to ~5.0 g/dL**, then plateaus. For a 20-year-old, 4.5–5.0 g/dL is the desirable band; >5.0 likely reflects mild dehydration; <4.0 in a young, otherwise healthy male warrants investigation (occult inflammation, GI loss, kidney disease).

### 6.7 Confounders

- **Hydration status** at draw — intravascular volume affects measured concentration.
- **Posture/tourniquet time** — prolonged stasis can elevate albumin and total protein measurements (hemoconcentration).
- **Pregnancy, OCPs** (mild lowering).
- **Recent illness within ~3 weeks** (negative acute phase).

## 7. GLOBULIN

### 7.1 What It Is

"Globulin" on a basic metabolic panel is a calculated value: **Total Protein − Albumin = Globulin**. It is a heterogeneous mix of:
- **α1-globulins**: α1-antitrypsin, α1-acid glycoprotein, AFP.
- **α2-globulins**: haptoglobin, ceruloplasmin, α2-macroglobulin.
- **β-globulins**: transferrin, complement (C3, C4), β2-microglobulin, LDL.
- **γ-globulins**: immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM, IgD, IgE) — the largest variable component.

The α-globulins are mostly **positive acute-phase reactants** (rise with inflammation). The γ-globulins (immunoglobulins) are produced by plasma cells/B cells.

### 7.2 Reference Ranges

| Range | Value |
|---|---|
| Typical adult globulin | 2.0–3.5 g/dL |
| Function Health "optimal" | typically 2.0–3.0 g/dL |

### 7.3 Causes of HIGH Globulin

**Polyclonal hypergammaglobulinemia** (broad immune activation; SPEP shows broad-based gamma elevation):
- **Chronic infection**: HIV, HBV, HCV, TB, parasitic infections, chronic osteomyelitis.
- **Autoimmune disease**: SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, Sjögren's, autoimmune hepatitis.
- **Chronic liver disease** (cirrhosis especially) — paradoxically high globulin from chronic immune activation, low albumin from hepatic dysfunction.
- **Sarcoidosis, granulomatous disease**.

**Monoclonal gammopathy** (single plasma-cell clone; SPEP shows narrow "M-spike"):
- **MGUS (monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance)**: M-spike <3 g/dL, marrow plasma cells <10%, no end-organ damage. Prevalence ~3% over age 50, much rarer at 20.
- **Multiple myeloma**: M-spike often >3 g/dL, marrow plasma cells >10%, CRAB criteria (Calcium, Renal, Anemia, Bone lesions). Median age 69 — extremely rare at 20.
- **Waldenström macroglobulinemia** (IgM monoclonal).
- **Smoldering myeloma**.

**When to order SPEP**: total globulin >4.0 g/dL, especially with low albumin or low A/G ratio, in any age. In a 20-year-old, very high globulin with no inflammatory signs is more likely chronic inflammation, autoimmune, or chronic infection than myeloma — but SPEP rules it out (Kyle 2003 *NEJM*; AAFP 2005 SPEP guide).

### 7.4 Causes of LOW Globulin

- **Hereditary agammaglobulinemia** (X-linked — Bruton's; would have presented in childhood).
- **Common variable immunodeficiency (CVID)** — typically presents 20s–40s with recurrent infections; **clinically relevant for this patient demographic**.
- **Severe protein-losing enteropathy / nephrotic syndrome** (loses smaller proteins preferentially).
- **Acute hemorrhage / fluid resuscitation** — dilutional.
- **Severe malnutrition**.

### 7.5 Direction of "Better"

**U-shaped**. Very low → immune deficiency. Very high → chronic inflammation or hematologic malignancy. Optimal ~2.2–2.8 g/dL in a healthy young man.

### 7.6 Confounders

- Hydration (same as albumin).
- Recent infection / vaccination (transient rise).
- Sample handling (fibrinogen contributes if plasma rather than serum is measured — would be reported as fibrinogen separately on serum tests).

## 8. ALBUMIN/GLOBULIN (A/G) RATIO

### 8.1 What It Is

Calculated as Albumin ÷ Globulin. It compresses two pieces of information into one number, useful as a **screening signal** — if A/G is abnormal, dig into the components.

### 8.2 Reference Ranges

| Range | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 1.0–2.5 (typical reference) | Normal |
| 1.1–2.0 (more conservative) | Healthy band |
| <1.0 | **Abnormal — investigate** |
| >2.5 | Rare; usually low globulin (immunodeficiency) |

Source: MedlinePlus; Cleveland Clinic; multiple labs.

### 8.3 Causes of LOW A/G Ratio (<1.0)

Low A/G has two mechanistic flavors:

1. **Low albumin** (denominator effect):
   - Cirrhosis, chronic liver disease.
   - Nephrotic syndrome.
   - Severe inflammation.
   - Malnutrition.
2. **High globulin** (numerator effect):
   - Multiple myeloma / MGUS / Waldenström → **order SPEP**.
   - Chronic infection (HIV, hepatitis, TB).
   - Autoimmune disease.
   - Chronic liver disease (does both at once: low alb + high globulin).

In a young adult male, low A/G often reflects systemic inflammation more than cancer; in older adults, the calculus shifts toward myeloma workup.

### 8.4 Causes of HIGH A/G Ratio (>2.5)

- **Hypogammaglobulinemia / immunodeficiency** (CVID, X-linked agamma).
- Less commonly: hyperthyroidism, leukemia of certain types.
- Lab artifact (very low globulin due to sample handling or rare hemodilution).

### 8.5 Direction of "Better"

**U-shaped, narrow**. Optimal ~1.5–2.0. The ratio itself is a derived metric — interpret components.

### 8.6 Confounders

Same as albumin and globulin individually (hydration, posture, inflammation, recent illness).

## 9. TOTAL PROTEIN

### 9.1 What It Is

Total protein = **albumin + globulin** measured directly (biuret method). Reported as g/dL.

### 9.2 Reference Ranges

| Source | Range |
|---|---|
| Standard adult | 6.0–8.3 g/dL (60–83 g/L) |
| Function Health "optimal" | typically 6.5–7.5 g/dL |

### 9.3 Causes of HIGH Total Protein

- **Dehydration** (most common — hemoconcentration; both albumin and globulin elevated proportionally).
- **Multiple myeloma / Waldenström / chronic gammopathy** (driven by globulin).
- **Chronic inflammation / chronic infection** (driven by globulin).
- **Hyperalbuminemia from severe dehydration**.

If total protein >8.3 with normal albumin → globulin is high → consider SPEP.

### 9.4 Causes of LOW Total Protein

- **Liver disease** (decreased albumin synthesis).
- **Kidney disease** (nephrotic syndrome — protein loss).
- **Malnutrition / malabsorption** (celiac, Crohn's, bariatric surgery, severe alcohol use disorder).
- **Hemorrhage** (acute blood loss).
- **Severe burns**.
- **Heart failure** (dilutional).

### 9.5 Direction of "Better"

**U-shaped, plateau**. Optimal probably 6.8–7.5 g/dL for a healthy young man. Very high (>8.3) → globulin investigation; very low (<6.0) → albumin and clinical context.

### 9.6 Confounders

- Hydration / tourniquet time (most important — prolonged stasis hemoconcentrates).
- Recent meal (small effect).
- Pregnancy (NA).
- Posture (supine ~0.3 g/dL lower than upright).

## 10. Putting It Together: Decision Tree for a 20-Year-Old Male

### 10.1 If Liver Enzymes Are Mildly Abnormal

**Step 1 — Confirm and pattern**:
- Repeat in 2–4 weeks (avoid exercise 72h pre-draw, avoid alcohol 1 week pre-draw, fast 8h, well-hydrated).
- Calculate R ratio (hepatocellular vs cholestatic vs mixed).
- Calculate AST/ALT ratio (DeRitis).
- Calculate FIB-4 (with caveat that it's not validated <35).

**Step 2 — Common in young US males, in order of pretest probability**:
1. **MASLD/NAFLD** — by far the most likely cause of mild persistent ALT elevation. Workup: BMI, waist, fasting glucose/insulin, lipids, RUQ ultrasound, FibroScan if access.
2. **Alcohol** — even 14 drinks/week can elevate GGT and AST. Honest history; consider PEth (phosphatidylethanol) test if uncertain.
3. **Drug/supplement** — *full review* including OTC, supplements (especially ashwagandha, turmeric, green tea extract, kava, anabolic steroids/SARMs, pre-workouts).
4. **Viral hepatitis** — HBsAg, anti-HBs, anti-HBc total, anti-HCV (with reflex HCV RNA).
5. **Hereditary hemochromatosis** — ferritin, transferrin saturation; if both high, HFE genotype.
6. **Wilson's disease** — peak presentation 5–35 years; ceruloplasmin, 24h urine copper, slit-lamp Kayser-Fleischer ring.
7. **Autoimmune hepatitis** — ANA, anti-SMA, anti-LKM, IgG.
8. **Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency** — A1AT level + phenotype.
9. **Celiac disease** — tTG IgA + total IgA.
10. **Thyroid disease** — TSH (mild ALT/AST elevation can come from both hypo and hyper).

(AASLD 2023 guidance; Kwo 2017 ACG; Newsome 2018 BSG guideline.)

### 10.2 If Total Bilirubin Is Mildly Elevated (1.2–2.5) With Otherwise Normal Panel

- Fractionate (direct vs indirect).
- If indirect-predominant (>80%): rule out hemolysis (CBC, retic, haptoglobin, LDH, smear). If hemolysis ruled out: **Gilbert syndrome** — reassure; this is benign and likely cardio-protective.

### 10.3 If Albumin and Globulin Are Both Abnormal

- Low albumin + high globulin + low A/G → workup for chronic liver disease, autoimmune, chronic infection, OR (much less likely at 20) hematologic malignancy. SPEP and IgG/IgA/IgM if warranted.
- High globulin alone: SPEP if >4.0 g/dL or persistent; chronic infection/autoimmune workup.
- Low globulin: consider CVID workup if recurrent infections (immunoglobulin levels, vaccine response).
