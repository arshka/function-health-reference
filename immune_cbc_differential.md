# Immune / CBC Differential Markers — Comprehensive Reference

A working reference for interpreting the immune cell components of a Function Health blood draw. Targeted at a healthy 20-year-old male; clinical literature largely defaults to older / sicker populations, so age-specific caveats are flagged where relevant.

> All numeric reference ranges use **×10⁹/L** (equivalent to **×10³/µL** or **K/µL**). 1.0 ×10⁹/L = 1,000 cells/µL.
> "Optimal" ranges (functional medicine) are tighter than "normal" (lab) ranges — both are reported below where they diverge.

---

## 0. Background: What the CBC Differential Actually Measures

The CBC with differential reports total **white blood cell (WBC)** count plus the five normal leukocyte populations:

| Cell | Lineage | Primary role |
|---|---|---|
| Neutrophils | Myeloid (granulocyte) | First-line antibacterial / antifungal innate defense |
| Lymphocytes | Lymphoid (T, B, NK) | Adaptive immunity (T/B) + innate cytotoxicity (NK) |
| Monocytes | Myeloid | Phagocytosis, antigen presentation; precursors of tissue macrophages and DCs |
| Eosinophils | Myeloid (granulocyte) | Antiparasitic (helminth) defense, type-2 / allergic inflammation |
| Basophils | Myeloid (granulocyte) | Histamine release, type-1 hypersensitivity, helminth defense |

### Automated vs. manual differential

Modern hematology analyzers count ~10,000–32,000 cells per sample using flow cytometry-based principles, which is **statistically far more accurate for normal populations than a manual smear** (which typically classifies only 100 cells). However, automated counters cannot reliably distinguish atypical lymphocytes (e.g., from EBV mono), blasts, immature granulocytes, or schistocytes. When the analyzer detects abnormality flags (suspicious cell populations, count discrepancies, NRBCs, platelet clumps), the lab "reflexes" to a manual peripheral smear by a hematologist or trained tech. So a clean automated CBC + diff is robust; a flagged one needs human review. (CAP Q-Probes; *Arch Pathol Lab Med*, 2006)

### Spurious / pseudo-leukocytosis to know about

- **Platelet clumping** (in vitro EDTA-induced) — clumps can be miscounted as WBCs. Resolved with citrate or heparin tube and smear review. (Akwari, *AJCP*, 1982)
- **Cryoglobulinemia** — cold-precipitating immunoglobulins form particles that the counter reads as cells. Counts performed at 37°C resolve it. Worth knowing because it can cause both a false leukocytosis and a false thrombocytosis simultaneously. (Patel, *J Med Case Rep*, 2009)

## 1. White Blood Cell (WBC) Count

### What it is
Total leukocyte concentration in peripheral blood, the sum of all five subsets above. Conventionally reported as ×10⁹/L (or K/µL).

### Reference range
- **Conventional clinical range**: ~4.0–11.0 ×10⁹/L (some labs use 4.5–11.0, Function/many functional labs use 3.4–10.8). (StatPearls 2024; Medscape)
- **Functional / optimal range** cited by Optimal DX, Mito Health, and similar: ~**5.0–7.5 ×10⁹/L** (some go as low as 3.8–6.0). Functional ranges are based on the observation that CV/metabolic risk starts climbing well before 11.0.

### What HIGH (leukocytosis) suggests
- **Bacterial infection** — typically with neutrophilia
- **Acute physical/emotional stress** — demargination of neutrophils into circulation; transient
- **Glucocorticoids** (endogenous Cushing's, exogenous prednisone) — neutrophilia + lymphopenia + eosinopenia
- **Smoking** — *sustained* +20–25% increase, fully reversible with cessation. Multiple studies including Lavi et al., *Am J Med*, 2020 and the Copenhagen General Population Study (Higuchi et al.) found mean WBC ~20–25% higher in current smokers, with the largest absolute increment in granulocytes.
- **Pregnancy, post-exercise, post-vaccine** (transient)
- **Myeloid malignancy** — CML (often >50, with basophilia/eosinophilia), AML
- **Tissue injury / MI / burns**

### What LOW (leukopenia) suggests
- **Viral infection** (mostly hits lymphocytes/neutrophils via marrow suppression)
- **Sepsis** (paradoxical, late) — poor prognostic sign
- **Drug-induced** marrow suppression (chemo, clozapine, antithyroid drugs)
- **Aplastic anemia, MDS, marrow infiltration**
- **B12 / folate deficiency**
- **Autoimmune** (SLE)
- **Benign ethnic neutropenia** (driving low total WBC) — see neutrophil section

### Direction of "better" — **U-shaped**
Both ends are bad. Multiple cohorts (Baltimore Longitudinal Study of Aging, *JACC* 2007; Guangzhou Biobank; Women's Health Initiative — Margolis et al. 2017) show **U-shaped associations between total WBC and all-cause and cardiovascular mortality**. Madjid & Fatemi's 2013 *Texas Heart Institute Journal* review ("Components of the Complete Blood Count as Risk Predictors for Coronary Heart Disease") established WBC as an independent CHD risk predictor at the high end. Karakas et al. (Eur Heart J 2009) similarly tied higher WBC to incident CV events. Even within the "normal" range, a WBC of 9–10 carries higher CV risk than 5–7.

For a healthy 20-year-old male: aim for the **mid-normal zone, ~4.5–7.0 ×10⁹/L**. Drift toward the upper end without an obvious cause (illness, vaccine, exercise) merits attention especially if smoking-correlated.

### Confounders specifically relevant for a young man
- **Time of day**: WBC and neutrophils peak late afternoon / early evening (4–8 PM); morning draws give the lowest values. Diurnal amplitude is ~0.8 ×10⁹/L for WBC. (Sennels et al., *Scand J Clin Lab Invest*, 2011; Feriel et al., *Int J Lab Hematol*, 2021)
- **Exercise**: Acute exercise produces leukocytosis within minutes (catecholamine-mediated demargination), peaking immediately post-exercise, with biphasic kinetics — neutrophils rise further over the next 1–3 hours while lymphocytes drop below baseline. Avoid hard exercise within 24h of draw if you want a clean baseline.
- **Recent illness or vaccination** (within 1–2 weeks)
- **Sleep deprivation** — modestly elevates WBC and shifts subset distribution (Born et al.)
- **Smoking / vaping** — see above; effect is large and sustained

## 2. Neutrophils (absolute and percentage)

### What they are
The most abundant circulating leukocyte (~40–70% of WBC). Polymorphonuclear granulocytes of myeloid lineage; lifespan ~6–8 hours in blood, up to ~7 days in tissue. (Pillay et al., *Blood*, 2010 challenged this with a longer lifespan estimate, but the canonical short half-life remains the operational baseline.)

### Physiological function
- **First responders** to bacterial / fungal infection — chemotaxis, phagocytosis, oxidative burst (NADPH oxidase → ROS)
- **Granule-based killing**: primary (myeloperoxidase, defensins, elastase) and secondary granules
- **NETosis**: release of neutrophil extracellular traps — decondensed chromatin coated with antimicrobial proteins (elastase, MPO, histones, cathepsin G) that trap and kill extracellular pathogens. NETs are central to defense against large pathogens (fungi, parasites) but also implicated in thrombosis (e.g., COVID-19, immune-thrombosis) and autoimmunity (SLE, ANCA vasculitis). (Brinkmann et al., *Science* 2004; Papayannopoulos, *Nat Rev Immunol*, 2018)
- **Vital NETosis** allows a subset of neutrophils to release NETs without dying and continue phagocytosing.

### Reference range
- **Absolute**: 1.5–7.7 ×10⁹/L (some labs 1.8–7.0)
- **Percentage**: 40–70%
- **Severe neutropenia / agranulocytosis**: ANC < 0.5 ×10⁹/L (high infection risk)
- **Functional optimal** for a young adult: ~2.0–5.5 ×10⁹/L; percentage ~50–60%.

### What HIGH (neutrophilia) suggests
- **Bacterial infection** (most common)
- **Acute stress / exercise / catecholamine surge** — demargination
- **Glucocorticoids** — both demargination and reduced egress to tissues
- **MI, burns, surgery, trauma**
- **Smoking** (sustained — neutrophils account for the largest absolute share of smoking-induced leukocytosis)
- **Myeloid malignancy** (CML, AML, MPN) — usually with left shift, basophilia
- **Pregnancy**
- **Solid tumors** (paraneoplastic G-CSF production)

### What LOW (neutropenia) suggests
- **Drug-induced** (most common in young adults via prescription drugs):
  - Chemotherapy (predictable, dose-dependent)
  - Clozapine (~1% incidence of clinically significant neutropenia, regulated REMS monitoring in US)
  - Antithyroid drugs (methimazole, carbimazole, PTU — ~0.2–0.5% incidence)
  - Sulfasalazine, TMP-SMX, ticlopidine, metamizole
  - Idiosyncratic drug-induced neutropenia: 2.4–15.4 cases per million
- **Benign ethnic neutropenia (BEN) / Duffy-null associated neutrophil count (DANC)** — see special section below
- **Viral infections** (transient — flu, EBV, CMV, HIV)
- **Autoimmune neutropenia** (primary or secondary to SLE/RA)
- **B12 / folate deficiency** (with macrocytosis, hypersegmented neutrophils on smear)
- **Marrow failure** (aplastic anemia, MDS, leukemia)
- **Severe sepsis** (consumptive)
- **Congenital** (cyclic neutropenia, severe congenital neutropenia/Kostmann)

### Special: Benign ethnic neutropenia / Duffy-null neutrophil count
This is a **must-recognize** entity. Caused by the rs2814778 polymorphism in *ACKR1* (the Duffy antigen receptor for chemokines, DARC). Duffy-null individuals lack DARC on RBCs, which alters chemokine trafficking and produces **constitutionally lower ANC** (often 1.0–1.5 ×10⁹/L or even lower) without any increased infection risk.

- **Prevalence of Duffy-null phenotype**: ~80% in Black African/Caribbean UK populations, ~65% in African Americans, 88–100% across continental Africa. Very rare in Europeans.
- Of Duffy-null individuals, ~25–50% manifest BEN (chronic ANC < 1.5).
- Authors increasingly advocate replacing the term "benign ethnic neutropenia" with "Duffy-null associated neutrophil count" because the underlying biology is genotype-driven, not "ethnic" per se. (Merz et al., *Blood Advances*, 2023; Reich et al., *PLOS Genet*, 2009; Atallah-Yunes et al., *Blood Rev*, 2019)
- Practical implication: A neutrophil count of 1.0–1.5 in someone of African descent is almost always benign and should not trigger a workup or alter drug dosing (e.g., chemotherapy thresholds need adjustment).

### Diurnal / situational confounders
- Neutrophils peak late afternoon (4–8 PM); morning ANC may be ~25–30% lower than afternoon. People with BEN show even larger diurnal swings — an AM measurement may flag as low while PM is normal-range. (Lakshman, *Exp Hematol*, 2018)
- Demargination from acute exercise/stress: rapid +50–100% within minutes
- Glucocorticoid effect: +1–2× baseline within hours of dose

## 3. Lymphocytes (absolute and percentage)

### What they are
~20–40% of WBC. Three major subpopulations:
- **T cells (~70% of lymphocytes)**: thymus-matured, TCR-bearing. CD4+ helper (Th1/Th2/Th17/Tfh/Treg) and CD8+ cytotoxic.
- **B cells (~10–15%)**: marrow-matured, antibody-producing.
- **NK cells (~5–15%)**: innate cytotoxic, no TCR/BCR — kill MHC-I-low cells (virally infected, malignant).

The standard CBC differential reports total lymphocytes only. To resolve subsets you need flow cytometry (CD4 count, CD8 count, B cell, NK panel) — not part of routine CBC.

### Physiological function
- CD4+ Th cells coordinate adaptive immunity; CD8+ kill virus-infected cells; B cells produce antibodies; NK cells provide rapid innate response to viruses and tumors. Memory T and B cells underpin durable immunity post-infection or vaccination.

### Reference range
- **Absolute**: 1.0–4.8 ×10⁹/L (StatPearls; some labs 1.0–4.0)
- **Percentage**: 20–40%
- **Optimal** for healthy young adult: ~1.5–3.5 ×10⁹/L

### What HIGH (lymphocytosis) suggests
- **Viral infection** — by far the most common cause, especially in young adults:
  - **EBV / infectious mononucleosis** — classic in college-age population. Lymphocyte count often 4–5× normal; ≥50% lymphocytes total + ≥10% atypical lymphocytes ("Downey cells") on smear has 75% sensitivity, 92% specificity. CD8+ T cells dominate. Resolves over weeks. (Dunmire et al., *Curr Top Microbiol Immunol*, 2015)
  - CMV, acute HIV, COVID-19 (variably), other viruses
- **Pertussis** — distinctive: lymphocytosis from a B. pertussis toxin that blocks lymphocyte egress from blood
- **CLL / SLL** — almost never in 20-year-olds; median age ~70
- **Acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL)** — incidence peaks in childhood; secondary peak in elderly; uncommon at 20 but possible (with cytopenias, blasts on smear)
- **Stress / exercise (transient demargination)** — see below
- **Smoking** — modest sustained lymphocytosis
- **Splenectomy** (post-surgical baseline)

### What LOW (lymphopenia) suggests
- **Acute viral infection** (paradoxically, many viruses cause early lymphopenia — including COVID-19, where lymphopenia at admission is a strong severity predictor)
- **Glucocorticoids** (endogenous or exogenous) — apoptosis of immature B cells + redistribution out of circulation into lymphoid tissue
- **HIV** — preferentially CD4+ depletion; total lymph count drops over years
- **Sepsis** (poor prognostic sign)
- **Malnutrition, alcohol use disorder**
- **Chronic kidney disease, ESRD**
- **Post-radiation, chemo**
- **Idiopathic CD4 lymphopenia** (rare)
- **Chronic stress** (cortisol-mediated)

### Direction of "better"
Within the normal range, lymphopenia (<1.0) is consistently associated with worse outcomes (infection, mortality). Hyperlymphocytosis without an obvious viral cause warrants attention. For a young adult, a value of 1.8–3.0 ×10⁹/L is reassuring.

### Special: Stress-induced lymphocyte redistribution ("demargination")
A robust, well-characterized phenomenon. Acute stress (psychological or physical), exercise, or epinephrine/cortisol infusion causes:

1. **Within minutes** (catecholamine-driven, β2-AR mediated): lymphocytes — particularly cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, γδ T cells, and NK cells — demarginate from vessel walls into circulation. Counts can rise 50–100% within 5–30 min. (Dimitrov, Lange & Born, *J Immunol*, 2010; Schedlowski et al.)
2. **Within hours** (glucocorticoid-driven): lymphocytes redistribute *out* of circulation into spleen, marrow, and lymphoid tissues — producing transient lymphopenia. This is the textbook "stress leukogram" pattern: neutrophilia + lymphopenia + eosinopenia.
3. Both effects normalize within hours after the stressor ends.

Practical implication: A blood draw immediately after a stressful event, hard exercise, or an argument can show pseudo-lymphocytosis or pseudo-lymphopenia depending on timing. Standardize draws to a calm, fasted, morning state for serial comparisons. (Dhabhar, *Brain Behav Immun*, 2012)

### Confounders
- Same diurnal pattern as other WBCs — somewhat smaller amplitude than neutrophils
- Acute / chronic stress (above)
- Recent vaccination (mild transient lymphopenia within 24h, then mild lymphocytosis as memory expands)
- Smoking — *increases* counts (different from glucocorticoid pattern)
- Recent infection — count distortion can persist for weeks

## 4. Monocytes (absolute and percentage)

### What they are
~2–10% of WBC; the largest leukocyte by size. Bone marrow myeloid lineage. Three subsets defined by CD14/CD16:
- **Classical (CD14++ CD16-)**: ~85% — phagocytosis, inflammation
- **Intermediate (CD14++ CD16+)**: ~5% — antigen presentation
- **Non-classical (CD14+ CD16++)**: ~10% — patrolling, vascular surveillance

Monocytes circulate ~1–3 days, then traffic to tissue and differentiate into **macrophages** or **dendritic cells**. They are precursors to many tissue macrophages (Kupffer cells, alveolar macrophages, etc., though several tissue macrophage populations are now known to be embryonic-derived and self-renewing rather than monocyte-derived).

### Physiological function
- **Phagocytosis** of microbes, debris, apoptotic cells
- **Antigen presentation** via MHC class II (and cross-presentation on MHC class I) to CD4+ and CD8+ T cells — bridging innate and adaptive immunity
- **Cytokine production** (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6) — central to inflammatory cascades
- **Tissue macrophage / dendritic cell precursor**

### Reference range
- **Absolute**: 0.1–1.0 ×10⁹/L
- **Percentage**: 2–10% (some labs 4–11%)
- **Optimal** for young adult: ~0.2–0.6 ×10⁹/L

### What HIGH (monocytosis) suggests
Defined as absolute monocyte count >0.5–1.0 ×10⁹/L (varies by lab).
- **Chronic infection** — TB, subacute bacterial endocarditis, syphilis, brucellosis, fungal
- **Autoimmune / inflammatory** — RA, SLE, sarcoidosis, IBD
- **Recovery phase from neutropenia / chemotherapy** (monocytes recover before neutrophils)
- **CMML (chronic myelomonocytic leukemia)** — diagnostic threshold: persistent (≥3 months) absolute monocytosis ≥0.5 ×10⁹/L AND ≥10% of WBC, with ≥94% classical (CD14+CD16-) monocytes on flow. Very uncommon in 20-year-olds (median age >70). (Patnaik & Tefferi, *Am J Hematol*, 2024)
- **Other myeloid malignancies** — AML M4/M5, MPN
- **Hodgkin lymphoma** (paraneoplastic)
- **Smoking**
- **Glucocorticoid withdrawal / Addison's**
- **Splenectomy**

### What LOW (monocytopenia) suggests
- **Glucocorticoids** (acute) — though monocyte response to steroids is more variable than neutrophil
- **Aplastic anemia** — pancytopenia
- **Hairy cell leukemia** — pathognomonic finding: **near-absolute monocytopenia** (often functionally zero monocytes) is highly suggestive. Rare in 20-year-olds (median age 55).
- **MonoMAC syndrome / GATA2 deficiency** — rare autosomal dominant immunodeficiency with profound monocytopenia + B/NK lymphopenia + susceptibility to mycobacterial, fungal, and HPV-related disease, plus risk for MDS/AML. Median onset 20s–30s — *this is the rare entity that's most relevant for young adults.* If you see persistent profound monocytopenia (<0.1) without an obvious cause, GATA2 testing is reasonable. (Hsu et al., *Blood*, 2011; Spinner et al., *Blood*, 2014)
- **Severe sepsis**
- **Chemotherapy / radiation**

### Direction of "better"
Likely U-shaped, but less well characterized than total WBC. Both very low (immunodeficiency or marrow failure) and elevated (chronic inflammation) are flags. The Women's Health Initiative analysis found U-shaped associations between monocyte count and all-cause mortality. For a young adult, ~0.3–0.5 is reassuring.

### Confounders
- Diurnal: less pronounced than neutrophils
- Smoking: increases counts
- Recent infection / vaccine
- Strenuous exercise: small acute rise
- Iatrogenic: glucocorticoids, G-CSF (rises monocytes too)

## 5. Eosinophils (absolute and percentage)

### What they are
1–4% of WBC. Bimodal granulocyte (bilobed nucleus, pink granules). Lifespan ~8–18 hours in blood, days–weeks in tissue. Tissue-resident in GI tract, thymus, mammary tissue.

### Physiological function
- **Antiparasitic defense** — primary biologic role, especially against helminths (worms)
- **Type-2 / allergic inflammation** — central to asthma, eosinophilic esophagitis, atopic dermatitis
- **Tissue homeostasis and remodeling** (more recent appreciation)

Eosinophils contain four major cationic granule proteins:
- **Major basic protein (MBP)** — directly damages helminth membranes, binds and disrupts schistosomula, *Brugia* microfilariae, *T. spiralis* larvae
- **Eosinophil cationic protein (ECP)** — toxic to helminths, hemoflagellates, RNA viruses, bacteria
- **Eosinophil peroxidase (EPO/EPX)** — generates reactive halide species
- **Eosinophil-derived neurotoxin (EDN)** — antiviral

Activated eosinophils release granule contents via **piecemeal degranulation**, **compound exocytosis** (especially against parasites — granules fuse before release), or **eosinophil ETosis** (analog of NETosis). (Acharya & Ackerman, *J Biol Chem*, 2014)

### Reference range
- **Absolute**: 0.0–0.5 ×10⁹/L (some labs 0.0–0.4)
- **Percentage**: 1–4% (some 0–6%)
- **Optimal** for young adult: 0.05–0.3 ×10⁹/L

### Eosinophilia tiers (workup-driven)
| AEC (×10⁹/L) | Category | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| 0.5–1.5 | **Mild eosinophilia** | Atopy, allergy, asthma, eczema, common drug reactions, parasites (especially with travel hx) |
| 1.5+ | **Hypereosinophilia** | Triggers a structured workup (see below) |
| 1.5+ with end-organ damage | **Hypereosinophilic syndrome (HES)** | Diagnosis requires sustained AEC ≥1.5 + organ damage attributable to eosinophils |
| ≥1.0 with vasculitis features | **EGPA** (eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis, formerly Churg-Strauss) | Threshold ≥1.0 ×10⁹/L per current criteria; asthma + eosinophilia + vasculitis triad |

### What HIGH (eosinophilia) suggests
**Mnemonic: NAACP → Neoplasia, Allergy/Asthma, Adrenal insufficiency, Connective tissue/Collagen vascular, Parasites.**

In a healthy 20-year-old male, the overwhelmingly likely causes are:
1. **Atopy** — allergic rhinitis, asthma, atopic dermatitis (eczema). Mild persistent eosinophilia of 0.4–0.8 is typical. Atopic dermatitis specifically often produces AEC >0.5.
2. **Drug reaction** — SSRIs, NSAIDs, antibiotics (β-lactams, sulfa, vancomycin), anticonvulsants, allopurinol. Most are benign drug-induced eosinophilia. **DRESS syndrome** is the dangerous variant — see below.
3. **Parasitic infection** — especially with travel history. Helminths (strongyloides, schistosomiasis, hookworm, ascariasis, filariasis) cause robust eosinophilia. Protozoal infections (giardia, malaria) typically do *not*.
4. **Eosinophilic GI disease** — eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE) is increasingly recognized in young men with dysphagia and food impaction; often presents with normal *blood* eosinophils despite tissue eosinophilia.

Less likely but on the differential:
- **HES** (idiopathic, lymphoid-variant L-HES, myeloid-variant M-HES with PDGFRA/PDGFRB/FGFR1/JAK2 fusions)
- **EGPA / vasculitis**
- **Hematologic malignancy** — Hodgkin lymphoma, ALL with hypereosinophilia, MPNs
- **Adrenal insufficiency (Addison's)** — cortisol normally suppresses eosinophils; insufficient cortisol → eosinophilia. Classic teaching.
- **Hyperinfection syndromes** — *Strongyloides* especially (don't immunosuppress without ruling out)

### What LOW (eosinopenia) suggests
- **Acute stress / illness / sepsis** — endogenous cortisol surge suppresses eosinophil release and induces apoptosis
- **Cushing's syndrome / exogenous glucocorticoids**
- **Acute bacterial infection**
- The mechanism: cortisol downregulates IL-5 (the master eosinophil cytokine), induces apoptosis, and sequesters eosinophils into tissues. (Bass et al.; Hassani et al., *Endocrinol Metab*, 2017)

Eosinopenia is rarely a primary concern; it usually points to stress/cortisol elevation rather than an intrinsic problem.

### Direction of "better"
Mid-low normal (0.05–0.25) is generally optimal. Persistent values above 0.5 in a young man without a known atopic explanation deserve attention; >1.5 mandates workup.

### Special: DRESS syndrome (drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms)
A severe cutaneous adverse reaction (SCAR), distinct from common benign drug-induced eosinophilia. Worth recognizing because of high mortality (~10%) and prolonged latency.
- **Latency**: 2–8 weeks after starting offending drug (long compared to typical hypersensitivity reactions)
- **Classic tetrad**: fever + morbilliform rash + lymphadenopathy + eosinophilia + multiorgan involvement (most often hepatitis; also nephritis, myocarditis, pneumonitis)
- **Common triggers**: anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine, phenobarbital), allopurinol, sulfa drugs, vancomycin, dapsone, minocycline, abacavir
- **Lab features**: AEC often 1.5–10+ ×10⁹/L, atypical lymphocytes, transaminitis
- **Management**: stop drug, systemic steroids if severe organ involvement; *do not rechallenge*

If a young man has started a new medication 2–8 weeks before a draw and shows new eosinophilia + rash + lymphadenopathy or hepatitis, DRESS is on the differential. (Cardones, *J Allergy Clin Immunol Pract*, 2025; Husain et al., 2013)

### Confounders
- Diurnal: eosinophils show inverse circadian rhythm — *highest at night/early morning*, lowest in late afternoon. Opposite phase to neutrophils.
- Acute stress / cortisol — depresses count
- Allergy season (pollen exposure)
- Recent helminth treatment can transiently spike AEC
- Glucocorticoid use — suppresses

## 6. Basophils (absolute and percentage)

### What they are
The rarest circulating leukocyte (<1% of WBC). Granulocyte lineage; circulate ~1–2 days. Not the same cell as tissue mast cells (similar function but different lineage and location), though both express FcεRI and release histamine.

### Physiological function
- **Type-1 (immediate) hypersensitivity** — bind IgE via high-affinity FcεRI; on antigen cross-linking, rapidly degranulate releasing **histamine**, **leukotrienes (LTC4)**, prostaglandins, and **IL-4 / IL-13** (driving Th2 polarization)
- **Antiparasitic defense** — particularly tick and helminth
- **Initiating and amplifying allergic inflammation** alongside mast cells

### Reference range
- **Absolute**: 0.0–0.2 ×10⁹/L (often 0.0–0.1)
- **Percentage**: 0–1%
- **Optimal**: 0.0–0.05; commonly reads as zero or near-zero on automated counters

### What HIGH (basophilia) suggests
Absolute >0.1 ×10⁹/L (some labs >0.2) is mild basophilia.
- **Myeloproliferative neoplasms** — most strongly associated. **CML** classically presents with basophilia (often >2% and rising; basophil count >20% defines blast/accelerated phase). PV, ET, PMF can also show basophilia. **A persistent absolute basophilia >1.0 ×10⁹/L is a strong signal for MPN and warrants hematology referral.** (CML is rare at age 20 but median age is ~65, with cases occurring in young adults.)
- **Hypothyroidism** — mild basophilia documented; mechanism poorly understood but thyroid hormone normally suppresses basophil production
- **Allergic / inflammatory** — chronic urticaria, food allergy, atopic conditions
- **Inflammatory bowel disease**
- **Infections** — chickenpox, smallpox, TB (uncommon)
- **Drug hypersensitivity**

### What LOW (basopenia) suggests
Hard to detect because baseline is so low. Reported in:
- **Acute infection / inflammation**
- **Acute stress / glucocorticoids**
- **Hyperthyroidism** (mirror of hypothyroid effect)
- **Anaphylaxis or active allergic reaction** (basophils degranulate and disappear from circulation)

Generally not clinically actionable in isolation.

### Direction of "better"
Below the upper limit is fine. The clinically relevant question for a young man is whether a result is *truly* elevated (>0.1 ×10⁹/L sustained) or just at/above an arbitrary cutoff. Sustained basophilia is a signal worth investigating.

### Confounders
- Automated basophil counts have known accuracy limitations at the low end — manual smear differential confirms.
- Diurnal: similar phase to eosinophils (higher overnight, lower in afternoon)
- Smoking — modest basophilia
- Allergy season

## 7. Neutrophil-to-Lymphocyte Ratio (NLR) — derived

### What it is
NLR = absolute neutrophil count ÷ absolute lymphocyte count. Not directly reported by most standard CBC panels (Function Health does report it), but trivially computed from the differential. It captures the balance of innate (neutrophil-driven, often inflammatory) vs. adaptive (lymphocyte-driven, regulatory) immunity in a single number.

### Reference and "optimal" ranges
- **Healthy reference**: ~0.78–3.53 in a population-based study of healthy adults (Forget et al., *BMC Res Notes*, 2017)
- **Practical interpretation**:
  - **1–2**: low/normal — well-regulated immune state
  - **2–3**: borderline / subclinical inflammation
  - **>3**: elevated — inflammatory state, signal worth investigating
  - **>5**: high — strong inflammation; in cancer/CV literature, prognostic threshold
- For a healthy 20-year-old male, **NLR <2 is optimal**.

### Why it matters — prognostic literature
NLR has accumulated remarkable evidence as a cheap, ubiquitous prognostic marker across diverse conditions:

**Cardiovascular disease**:
- Systematic review/meta-analysis of 38 observational studies, 76,002 participants (Bhat et al., *Expert Rev Cardiovasc Ther*, 2013, and updated meta-analyses): high NLR associated with CAD (OR 1.62), ACS (1.64), stroke (2.36), composite CV events (3.86)
- Prediabetic/diabetic population-based cohort: high NLR → all-cause mortality HR 1.82, CV mortality HR 2.07 (Inflammopharmacology, 2024)
- STEMI meta-analysis (35 studies, 28,756 patients, 2024): high NLR → in-hospital mortality RR 3.52, long-term CV mortality RR 6.67
- Heart failure meta-analysis (36 articles, 18,231 patients, 2023): NLR as continuous variable HR 1.12 per unit increase

**Cancer**:
- Meta-analysis of 100 studies covering >40,000 patients in solid tumors: high pretreatment NLR → worse OS (HR 1.81) (Templeton et al., *J Natl Cancer Inst*, 2014)
- Pretreatment NLR predictive of immunotherapy response in advanced cancer (*Sci Rep*, 2025)
- Common cutoffs: NLR >3.0 (range 2.5–5.0) across solid tumors
- Specific cancers: ovarian HR 1.34; small cell lung cancer HR 1.39; metastatic solid tumors median OS 9.3 mo for NLR ≤4 vs 4.1 mo for NLR >4

**COVID-19**:
- Meta-analysis: NLR at admission AUC 0.81 for severity, 0.86 for mortality
- Each 1-unit increase in NLR: OR 6.2 for severity, OR 12.6 for all-cause mortality (Simadibrata et al.)

**Depression / psychiatry**:
- 2025 meta-analysis (41 studies, >205,000 participants): depressed individuals had significantly elevated NLR (SMD 0.35) vs controls
- Higher NLR correlated with depression severity and suicide risk
- NLR may predict SSRI nonresponse (*Sci Rep*, 2024)

### Direction of "better"
**Lower (closer to 1)** is robustly associated with better outcomes across cardiometabolic, oncologic, infectious, and psychiatric domains. For a young adult: target <2.

### Caveats
- NLR is not specific — many things move it. Acute infection (especially bacterial), hard exercise, recent surgery, trauma, and exogenous glucocorticoids all raise it transiently.
- A single elevated NLR is much less informative than a trend.
- Standardized cutoff values for clinical decision-making remain an open question (no single agreed cutoff across diseases).
- NLR is dominated by neutrophil count when neutrophils are very high; a "high" NLR in a person with leukemoid reaction tells you mostly that they have a left shift.

## 9. Specific implications for a healthy 20-year-old male

### Most likely benign reasons for borderline-abnormal values
1. **Recent exercise / poor pre-draw conditions** → check timing
2. **Subclinical viral illness** in the prior 2 weeks
3. **Mild atopy** (allergic rhinitis, eczema) for mild eosinophilia
4. **Vaping / smoking** for sustained mild leukocytosis with neutrophilia
5. **Acute stress / poor sleep** before the draw

### Patterns that are less common but worth recognizing in this age group
- **EBV mononucleosis**: lymphocytosis with atypical cells, often with sore throat / fatigue — very common at college age (peak incidence 15–24)
- **Acute HIV seroconversion**: lymphopenia, sometimes followed by mono-like lymphocytosis. Test if any risk and unexplained CBC changes.
- **Hodgkin lymphoma**: bimodal age curve with a peak in 20s–30s; can present with eosinophilia, monocytosis, lymphopenia, B symptoms (night sweats, weight loss)
- **ALL**: can occur in young adults with cytopenias and blasts on smear
- **Idiopathic hypereosinophilia / HES**: M:F ~9:1, often presents in 20s–50s with sustained AEC >1.5 + organ damage
- **GATA2 / MonoMAC**: rare but median onset is 20s–30s — profound monocytopenia + B/NK lymphopenia
- **Drug-induced**: any new medication 2–8 weeks before the draw — consider DRESS if eosinophilia + rash; agranulocytosis if antithyroid drugs / clozapine

### What to ignore
- A WBC of 4.0–8.0 with normal differential and no symptoms is essentially never pathologic.
- An NLR of 1–2 is great.
- A basophil count reported as 0.0 or 0.01 is not "low" in any meaningful sense.
- Slightly off percentages with normal absolute counts are usually not actionable — **always interpret from absolute counts**, not percentages.

### What to flag
- **Sustained AEC >0.5** without obvious atopy
- **Sustained absolute basophilia >0.1**
- **NLR persistently >3** without acute cause
- **Lymphocytosis >5.0** that isn't an obvious viral mono
- **Monocytopenia <0.1** sustained
- **Neutropenia <1.0** in a non-Black-ancestry individual without a clear drug/illness cause
- **Any sustained leukocytosis >12** without explanation
