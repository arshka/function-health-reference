# Omega Fatty Acid Biomarkers — Comprehensive Reference

**Subject context:** healthy 20-year-old male interpreting a Function Health blood panel that includes Omega-3 Total, EPA, DHA, DPA, Omega-3 Index, Omega-6 Total, Arachidonic Acid (AA), Linoleic Acid (LA), Omega-6:Omega-3 ratio, and AA:EPA ratio.

---

## 0. Methodology Primer (read this first)

Omega fatty acid panels are NOT all the same test. The matrix and the lipid fraction matter.

- **Plasma / serum total fatty acids** — short-term snapshot; reflects recent dietary intake (hours to days). What you ate yesterday will move it.
- **Plasma phospholipids (PL)** — moderate-term (days to ~3 weeks). The most common research matrix (e.g., Mozaffarian 2013, Wu 2014).
- **Plasma cholesteryl esters (CE)** — short-to-moderate term; LA is unusually concentrated here (>50% of fatty acids in CE can be LA).
- **Red blood cell (RBC) membrane / erythrocyte phospholipids** — long-term, ~120 days (the lifespan of a red cell). Most stable, dietarily robust marker.
- **Whole blood / dried blood spot (DBS)** — what OmegaQuant uses; correlates well with RBC values but the absolute numbers differ slightly from pure RBC. The "Omega-3 Index" trademark refers specifically to the Harris–von Schacky RBC-equivalent measurement.

**The instrument is gas chromatography (GC-FID) or GC-MS** after fatty acids are converted to fatty acid methyl esters (FAMEs); LC-MS is sometimes used. Each fatty acid is reported as a **percentage of total identified fatty acids** (not absolute concentration), so the values must sum within ~95–100%. This is critical: if your LA goes up, your relative AA can fall even if absolute AA is unchanged. ([JAST methodology paper](https://jast-journal.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s40543-021-00287-1)).

**Function Health's omega panel uses a Quest "OmegaCheck"-style assay** (whole blood, GC-FID, FAMEs). The fatty acids are reported as % of total. The "Omega-3 Index" reported by Function is derived from this whole-blood measurement and is calibrated to the Harris–von Schacky RBC-based scale. Reference ranges below cite both Function/OmegaCheck-typical and the underlying scientific literature.

**Why this matters for interpretation:** if a marker is elevated as a *percent of total*, it can mean (a) that marker is genuinely high in absolute terms, or (b) other classes (e.g., saturates, monounsaturates) are low. With omega panels this is rarely a problem in practice because PUFAs are tightly regulated, but it's worth knowing.

## 1. Omega-3 Index (the single most important marker on this panel)

### What it is

Omega-3 Index = (EPA + DHA) expressed as a percentage of total fatty acids in the **red blood cell membrane** (or whole-blood-equivalent calibrated to RBC). Coined by William S. Harris and Clemens von Schacky in their 2004 *Preventive Medicine* paper "The Omega-3 Index: a new risk factor for death from coronary heart disease?" ([Harris & von Schacky 2004, Prev Med 39:212-220, PMID 15208005](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15208005/)).

### Why RBC, not plasma

Harris validated RBC EPA+DHA against actual cardiac tissue EPA+DHA in heart-transplant patients — they correlated tightly. RBC turnover is ~120 days, so the Index reflects long-term intake and genetic conversion capacity, not yesterday's salmon. Plasma EPA/DHA is far more variable and confounded by recent meals. ([OmegaQuant: Discovering the Omega-3 Index](https://omegaquant.com/discovering-the-omega-3-index/)).

### Mechanism

EPA and DHA in membranes:
- Modulate **membrane fluidity** and lipid raft composition.
- Compete with arachidonic acid for COX/LOX enzymes, shifting eicosanoid output from pro-inflammatory 2-/4-series (PGE2, TXA2, LTB4) toward less-inflammatory 3-/5-series (PGE3, TXA3, LTB5).
- Serve as substrates for **specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs)** — E-series resolvins from EPA, D-series resolvins, protectins, and maresins from DHA — which actively terminate inflammation rather than merely suppress it ([Serhan et al., reviewed in MDPI Molecules 2022](https://www.mdpi.com/1420-3049/27/5/1677)).
- Activate **PPAR-α** (driving fatty-acid oxidation and triglyceride clearance) and **suppress SREBP-1c** maturation (lowering hepatic lipogenesis). DHA in particular degrades nuclear SREBP-1c via 26S proteasome and Erk pathways. ([Botolin et al., reviewed in Endocrine Reviews 2018](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/39/5/760/5055100)).
- Stabilize cardiac myocyte ion channels — proposed mechanism for reduced sudden cardiac death.

### Reference ranges

| Category | Omega-3 Index | CV risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Optimal / desirable** | **≥ 8% (8–12% is the target)** | Lowest |
| **Intermediate** | 4–8% | Moderate |
| **High risk** | < 4% | Highest |

Source: [Harris & von Schacky 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15208005/); confirmed in [Harris 2007 Pharmacol Res](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17324586/) and [2018 Framingham Heart Study analysis (J Clin Lipidol)](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874(18)30061-8/fulltext).

### Population averages

- **United States average: ~4–5%** (high risk zone).
- **Japan: ~9–11%** (only Japan, South Korea, and Alaskan/coastal Indigenous populations consistently sit above 8% by population mean) ([Stark et al. — RBC fatty acid patterns from 7 countries, 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0952327822000308); [NutraIngredients 2024 update](https://www.nutraingredients.com/Article/2024/07/10/Japan-South-Korea-top-omega-3-index-while-China-Australia-lower-than-average/)).
- One striking outlier: a cohort of young Palestinian adults averaged **2.56%** — among the lowest ever recorded.
- Most US healthy young adults without supplementation sit between 3.5% and 5.5%.

### Clinical evidence — what high vs low predicts

- **Mortality:** In Mozaffarian et al. 2013 *Annals of Internal Medicine* (Cardiovascular Health Study, n=2,692, ≥65y), highest vs lowest quintile of plasma phospholipid total long-chain n-3s was associated with 27% lower all-cause mortality; participants in the top quintile lived ~2.2 years longer. DHA had the strongest individual association with reduced CHD death (~40% lower risk). ([Mozaffarian 2013, Ann Intern Med 158:515-525, PMID 23546563](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23546563/)).
- **Sudden cardiac death:** Maki et al. 2017 J Clin Lipidol meta-analysis of 14 RCTs found supplemental EPA+DHA ≥250 mg/day reduced SCD by ~35% (OR 0.87, 95% CI 0.76–0.99). ([Maki 2017, J Clin Lipidol 11:1152-1160, PMID 28818347](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28818347/)).
- **All-cause mortality (pooled biomarker analysis):** Harris et al. 2021 in *Nature Communications* pooled 17 prospective cohorts (n=42,466). Higher RBC EPA+DHA associated with 13% lower all-cause, 15% lower CV, and 11% lower cancer mortality, comparing 90th vs 10th percentile of n-3 status. The omega-3 index added prognostic information beyond standard risk factors. ([Harris 2021, Nat Commun 12:2329](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-021-22370-2)).
- The Harris 2018 review ("Omega-3 Index as a New Risk Factor") argues the Index meets all Bradford Hill criteria — strength, consistency, dose-response, biological plausibility, temporality — and should be treated as a CV risk factor on par with cholesterol or smoking ([Harris 2007/2018, PMC1899522](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1899522/)).

### What HIGH means

> 8% — cardioprotective. No upper-limit toxicity demonstrated up to ~12%. Possible, modest signal of increased atrial fibrillation at very high pharmacological doses (≥4 g/d EPA+DHA in REDUCE-IT and STRENGTH; see §11). Bleeding risk at extreme intakes is real but clinically meaningful only in combination with anticoagulants.

### What LOW means

< 4% — increased risk of fatal CHD, sudden cardiac death, and likely all-cause mortality. Reflects low fish intake, no/insufficient supplementation, and/or unfavorable FADS genotype (see Confounders below).

### Direction of "better": HIGHER, up to ~10–12%.

### Optimal target for a healthy 20-year-old male: **≥ 8%** (pragmatically aim for 8–10%; no benefit demonstrated from pushing past 12%).

### Confounders

- **FADS1/FADS2 genotype** — These genes encode Δ5- and Δ6-desaturases that elongate ALA into EPA and DHA. The FADS haplotype "D" (more efficient converter) yields ~24% higher DHA and ~43% higher AA than haplotype "A" homozygotes, independent of diet ([Mathias et al., reviewed in Genes Nutr 2024](https://genesandnutrition.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12263-024-00747-4)). Approximately 80% of African ancestry individuals carry the "D" derived allele vs ~45% of European ancestry. The minor allele of FADS1 rs174537 reproducibly predicts lower blood EPA/DHA.
- **Age:** RBC omega-3s increase modestly with age (~+0.04% per year in observational data).
- **Sex:** women generally have slightly higher DHA than men due to estrogen up-regulating ALA→DHA conversion. Hormonal contraceptives lower EPA.
- **Smoking:** lowers omega-3 index ~0.5–1 percentage point.
- **BMI / obesity:** lower omega-3 index, partly through dilution and partly inflammation-driven turnover.
- **Statins:** small literature on whether statins blunt n-3 incorporation; effect is modest if real.
- **Recent supplementation timing:** supplements take 3–4 months for RBC values to fully equilibrate. A loading-dose blip in plasma normalizes by then.
- **Fish-oil source:** triglyceride form, ethyl ester form, phospholipid (krill) form differ in bioavailability; phospholipid > free fatty acid > triglyceride > ethyl ester per dose, all else equal.

Sources: [Lipids in Health and Disease 2012 — smoking case-control](https://lipidworld.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/1476-511X-11-43); [OCL 2021 — FADS2 PPAR review](https://www.ocl-journal.org/articles/ocl/full_html/2021/01/ocl210018/ocl210018.html).

## 2. EPA (Eicosapentaenoic Acid, 20:5n-3)

### What it is

A 20-carbon, 5-double-bond omega-3. Primary marine source: cold-water oily fish (salmon, sardines, anchovies, mackerel), fish oil, algal oils, and to a smaller extent endogenous synthesis from ALA. Reported as % of total fatty acids in the assay matrix.

### Mechanism

- **Eicosanoid precursor (3-series):** PGE3, TXA3, LTB5 — all weaker pro-inflammatory and pro-thrombotic agents than the 2-/4-series from AA. Anti-platelet and vasodilatory.
- **E-series resolvins (RvE1, RvE2, RvE3):** active terminators of inflammation acting via GPR32, ChemR23 receptors at picogram levels.
- **Triglyceride lowering:** at pharmacological doses (≥2 g/d), via PPAR-α activation, reduced hepatic VLDL output, and N-acyl-taurine generation that inhibits intestinal lipid absorption ([Grevengoed 2021, Nature](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7954591/)).
- **Anti-arrhythmic:** stabilizes cardiomyocyte ion channels.

### Reference ranges (RBC % of total fatty acids)

- Western/US average: ~0.5–0.8%
- Healthy without supplementation: ~0.5–1.5%
- With routine fish intake: ~1.5–2.5%
- With supplementation: ~3–4% (achievable)
- Japanese population mean: ~2.8–3.5%
- **Optimal absolute target for 20M:** typically ~2.5–4% (most labs flag ≥1.5% as the bottom of "intermediate" and ≥2.5% as desirable).

In **plasma phospholipid** terms, EPA typically runs 0.5–2.5% in Western populations vs ~3–5% in Japan.

### What HIGH means

Generally favorable. Indicates fish/fish-oil intake, good FADS conversion, or both. No demonstrated harm from EPA-driven cardiac membrane enrichment up to therapeutic doses (4 g/d icosapent ethyl).

### What LOW means

Low fish/fish-oil intake; ALA-only diet (poor conversion); FADS minor-allele genotype; chronic high omega-6 intake (substrate competition).

### Direction of "better": HIGHER (within physiologic range).

### Cardiovascular evidence — REDUCE-IT vs STRENGTH

This is the most discussed pair of CV trials in nutrition science.

**REDUCE-IT (Bhatt et al. 2019, NEJM 380:11-22):** 8,179 statin-treated patients with high triglycerides (≥150 mg/dL), randomized to 4 g/day **icosapent ethyl (pure EPA ethyl ester)** vs **mineral-oil placebo**. Result: 25% reduction in primary composite of CV death/MI/stroke/revascularization/unstable angina (HR 0.75, 95% CI 0.68–0.83, p<0.001), NNT=21. Atrial fibrillation was modestly increased (5.3% vs 3.9%). ([Bhatt 2019, NEJM](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1812792)).

**STRENGTH (Nicholls et al. 2020, JAMA 324:2268-2280):** 13,078 patients, randomized to 4 g/day **omega-3 carboxylic acid (EPA+DHA mix)** vs **corn-oil placebo**. Result: NULL. Primary composite 12.0% vs 12.2% (HR 0.99, p=0.84). AF risk again increased. ([Nicholls 2020, JAMA](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778119)).

**Why the divergent results?** Two competing explanations, neither fully resolved:

1. **EPA is the active ingredient.** STRENGTH delivered a mixed EPA/DHA preparation; REDUCE-IT delivered pure EPA. Plasma EPA in REDUCE-IT roughly tripled; in STRENGTH it merely doubled. Some pharmacology supports EPA-specific effects on membrane stability and cholesterol crystal formation that DHA does not share.
2. **The placebo mattered.** Mineral oil in REDUCE-IT raised LDL by ~10%, apoB by ~8%, hsCRP by ~32% in the placebo arm — making icosapent ethyl look better by comparison. Corn oil in STRENGTH was essentially neutral on these biomarkers. FDA estimated only ~3% of REDUCE-IT's net benefit was attributable to mineral-oil mischief, but the controversy is unresolved. ([Olshansky 2020, Cardiovasc Drugs Ther](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7537802/); [tctmd.com coverage](https://www.tctmd.com/news/new-biomarker-data-again-draw-eyes-mineral-oil-reduce-it)).

**For a 20-year-old without hypertriglyceridemia, REDUCE-IT does not directly apply** (the trial enrolled patients with established CV disease/diabetes). But it does confirm that pharmacological EPA elevation is bioactive on hard CV endpoints in a high-risk population.

### Confounders

- ALA→EPA conversion is the only endogenous source if no fish/algae are eaten — typically 5–10% in young women, **<5% (often <1%) in men** ([Brenna 2009, Curr Opin Clin Nutr Metab Care](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19053988/)).
- Aspirin and other COX inhibitors don't change EPA levels but change downstream eicosanoid output.
- High LA/AA intake competes for desaturase enzymes.

## 3. DHA (Docosahexaenoic Acid, 22:6n-3)

### What it is

A 22-carbon, 6-double-bond omega-3. Primary source: marine animals and microalgae. The most structural omega-3 in human tissue.

### Mechanism

- **Brain and retina structural lipid:** ~10–20% of total brain lipid is DHA; >90% of n-3 in brain is DHA. Concentrated in synaptic membranes, photoreceptor outer segments (rod/cone discs), and the rhodopsin signaling complex. Critical for neuronal membrane fluidity, signal transduction, neurogenesis, myelination, and synaptic plasticity. ([Lauritzen, reviewed in Nutrients 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4728620/)).
- **Sperm acrosome:** DHA is the most abundant fatty acid in sperm membranes; required for acrosome formation and motility. Low DHA → infertility/poor motility.
- **D-series resolvins, protectins (PD1/NPD1), and maresins** are DHA-derived SPMs.
- **Stronger blood-pressure-lowering effect than EPA** at equimolar doses (probably via membrane fluidity / endothelial NO release).
- **Suppresses SREBP-1c** more potently than EPA — explains some triglyceride and lipogenic gene effects.

### Reference ranges (RBC % of total fatty acids)

- Western/US average: ~3–4%
- Healthy young male without supplementation: ~3–5%
- With routine fish intake: ~5–7%
- Japanese population mean: ~6–8%
- **Optimal target for 20M:** ≥ 5%, ideally 5–7%.

In plasma phospholipids, DHA typically runs 2.5–4% in Western populations.

EPA + DHA combined as a target: ≥ 8% of total fatty acids in RBC = the Omega-3 Index target. Individual ranges that produce that index: EPA ~2.5–4%, DHA ~5–7%.

### What HIGH means

Favorable. DHA up to ~8% RBC has no demonstrated harm.

### What LOW means

- Inadequate marine intake; vegetarian/vegan without algal supplement; FADS minor-allele genotype.
- Linked to: cognitive decline in elderly, possible higher risk of dementia ([Schaefer 2006 Framingham Heart Study, PMID 17101822](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17101822/)), poorer visual development in offspring (relevant for women of reproductive age), reduced sperm motility in men, dry-eye disease.

### Direction of "better": HIGHER (within physiologic range).

### Confounders

- **Endogenous synthesis:** ALA→DHA conversion is **0–4% in men, often undetectable**; women average ~9% conversion thanks to estrogen up-regulation of FADS2 ([Brenna 2002, Brenna 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19053988/)). For a 20-year-old male, dietary DHA (or EPA, which can retro-convert to DHA at ~10–12%) is essentially obligate.
- High dietary LA inhibits ALA→DHA conversion (substrate competition for Δ6-desaturase).
- Pregnancy/lactation depletes DHA.
- Aging modestly increases conversion efficiency.

## 4. DPA (Docosapentaenoic Acid n-3, 22:5n-3)

### What it is

The intermediate between EPA and DHA in the elongation pathway. Distinct from DPA n-6 (the omega-6 isomer with the same name — different molecule). On Function Health panels "DPA" without further qualification is DPA n-3.

### Mechanism

- Substrate for SPMs (n-3 DPA-derived resolvins like RvD1n-3 DPA and protectins).
- Inhibits platelet aggregation independently of EPA/DHA.
- May inhibit smooth-muscle Rho-kinase pathways — relevant for vascular tone.
- A "reservoir": in tissues low in EPA and DHA, DPA can be retro-converted to EPA.

### Reference ranges (RBC % of total fatty acids)

- Typical Western: ~2–3%
- Optimal: not formally established; observational data favor higher levels. Most healthy adults sit in ~2–3.5%.
- DPA is **not** part of the official Omega-3 Index. Harris and colleagues explicitly excluded it because RBC DPA is less responsive to dietary EPA+DHA and they wanted a marker that tracked supplementation cleanly. ([Harris 2018, Prostaglandins Leukot Essent Fatty Acids: "Why DPA is not included in the Omega-3 Index"](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30103927/)).

### What HIGH means

Favorable in epidemiologic data:
- Inversely associated with triglycerides and CRP ([Skulas-Ray 2015, Nutrients 7:6390-6404](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4555130/)).
- Inversely associated with non-fatal MI and total mortality (esp. stroke deaths) in Mozaffarian 2013.
- Some evidence of independent benefit beyond EPA/DHA.

### What LOW means

Low overall n-3 status (DPA is largely intermediate to EPA→DHA), low fish intake.

### Direction of "better": HIGHER, but the marker is given less weight clinically than EPA+DHA.

### Confounders

- DPA is found at higher levels in seal/whale fat and in some red meats/poultry — less predictable from "fish intake" alone.
- Genetic FADS variants affect DPA proportionally.

## 5. Omega-3 Total

### What it is

Sum of all omega-3s detected: ALA (18:3n-3), EPA (20:5n-3), DPA n-3 (22:5n-3), DHA (22:6n-3), and trace others (stearidonic acid, ETA). Reported as % of total FA.

### Mechanism / interpretation

This number is dominated by whichever omega-3 you actually consume. Two patterns produce different "Omega-3 Total" but very different physiology:

- **Marine-pattern:** mostly EPA + DHA. Drives the Omega-3 Index up. Cardio-protective on the strongest evidence.
- **Plant-pattern:** mostly ALA from flax, chia, walnuts. Drives "Total" up but does **not** raise EPA/DHA much in men because conversion is poor (Brenna 2009). Some independent CV benefit at high ALA intake (probably 10–15% reduction in CHD per 1g/d ALA increase per pooled cohort data) but smaller and less consistent than marine n-3.

So Omega-3 Total in isolation is **less informative than the EPA+DHA breakdown** (or the Omega-3 Index). Always look at component fatty acids.

### Reference ranges (RBC, % of total FA)

- Western/US average: ~5–7%
- Optimal: ~10–12%
- DGA recommendation: ≥250 mg/d EPA+DHA (NIH ODS, AHA suggests 1–2 servings of oily fish/week for primary prevention).

### Direction of "better": HIGHER, but pay attention to composition.

### Confounders

If "Omega-3 Total" is high but Omega-3 Index is mediocre, you're consuming a lot of ALA without converting much. Adding direct EPA/DHA (fish or algal oil) is the fix.

## 6. Linoleic Acid (LA, 18:2n-6) — the contested marker

### What it is

The predominant dietary PUFA in the Western diet. ~6–10% of energy intake in the US, mostly from soybean, corn, sunflower, safflower, cottonseed, and grapeseed oils. The essential omega-6 fatty acid (humans cannot synthesize it). Precursor (via Δ6-desaturase / FADS2 → GLA → DGLA → AA via Δ5/FADS1) of arachidonic acid, but conversion to AA is heavily regulated and accounts for only a few percent of consumed LA.

### Reference ranges

- **RBC membrane:** Western average ~11–13% of total FA; remarkably stable across countries; **decreases ~2 percentage points with age** (Harris 2013 observation in 160,000 patients). ([Stark 2022, 7-country RBC study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0952327822000308)).
- **Plasma phospholipids:** ~20–25%.
- **Plasma cholesteryl esters:** can exceed 50% (CE selectively concentrates LA).
- **Plasma total fatty acids / serum total:** typically 25–32% (this is likely the source of the "25–32%" figure cited in many functional medicine references — it applies to plasma total or CE, not RBC PL).

So the answer to "what's optimal for LA?" is **assay-dependent**:
- If your panel reports RBC: optimal ~11–13%.
- If your panel reports plasma total FA or % of plasma: optimal ~25–32%.

Function Health's OmegaCheck (whole-blood) sits closer to the RBC scale for LA, generally 22–28% — confirm against the report's reference range printed alongside your value.

### Mechanism (and the controversy)

- **Substrate for AA biosynthesis** via the Δ6/Δ5 desaturase pathway. In humans, only ~0.2–1% of consumed LA is converted to AA — so "high LA intake → high AA tissue levels" is **not a tight relationship**. Tissue AA is more strongly determined by direct dietary AA intake (eggs, poultry skin, organ meats) and by FADS genotype than by LA intake.
- **OXLAMs (oxidized linoleic acid metabolites)** — 4-HNE, 9- and 13-HODE, others. Generated via lipid peroxidation, especially in oxidized LDL and in cooking oils heated repeatedly. The "seed-oil bad" framing (DiNicolantonio, Hibbeln, Mark Hyman) argues that LA-derived OXLAMs drive atherogenesis, mitochondrial dysfunction, and cardiolipin remodeling. The mechanisms are real in vitro; the question is whether typical dietary LA intake produces clinically meaningful OXLAM exposure in healthy people.
- **Cardiolipin remodeling:** mitochondrial cardiolipin is preferentially LA-rich; the "seed-oil bad" view argues excess LA distorts the cardiolipin profile and impairs ATP synthesis. Mainstream cardiometabolic literature does not find this clinically significant.

### Direction of "better" — this is where evidence and ideology diverge

**Mainstream / large epidemiology view (well-supported):**

- **Marklund 2019, *Circulation* 139:2422-2436** — individual-level pooled analysis of 30 prospective cohort studies, 13 countries. Higher circulating/tissue LA biomarkers were associated with **lower** total CVD (HR 0.93), CV mortality (HR 0.78 — a 22% reduction), and ischemic stroke (HR 0.88), comparing top vs bottom interquintile. ([Marklund 2019](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.118.038908)).
- **Wu 2014, *Circulation*** Cardiovascular Health Study — high circulating LA was inversely associated with total and CHD mortality in older adults. ([Wu 2014, PMID 25124495](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25124495/)).
- **Li 2020, AJCN systematic review** of dietary LA intake and mortality — higher LA intake associated with ~13% lower all-cause mortality. ([Li 2020, AJCN](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7326588/)).
- **Mendelian randomization (Zhao 2019, BMC Med):** Genetically predicted LA was not significantly associated with ischemic heart disease but was favorably associated with diabetes risk and lipid markers. Did not support a *causal* harm. ([Zhao 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30866921/)).

**Functional / "seed oil bad" view (smaller evidence base):**

- DiNicolantonio & O'Keefe 2018 *Open Heart* — "Omega-6 vegetable oils as a driver of coronary heart disease: the oxidized linoleic acid hypothesis." Argues that historical re-analyses (Sydney Diet Heart Study, Minnesota Coronary Experiment) showed harm when saturated fat was replaced with LA, despite LDL falling. ([DiNicolantonio 2018](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6196963/)).
- Hibbeln and colleagues' historical analyses tracking rising US LA consumption against chronic disease incidence.
- Marshall 2023 *Nutrients* narrative review (DiNicolantonio author) argues against current LA intake levels.

**Honest assessment:** the population-scale biomarker data (Marklund 2019 across 30 cohorts) is the strongest evidence class on this panel, and it points clearly to LA being **neutral-to-protective** for CVD at population intakes. The "seed oil bad" mechanistic case is real but most directly applies to (a) repeatedly-heated oils where OXLAMs are pre-formed in the food, and (b) people with very high dietary LA combined with low antioxidant intake. For a 20-year-old eating a varied diet, the data does not support deliberately suppressing LA below the physiological essential range.

That said, the *form* matters: extracted/refined seed oils used for deep frying are categorically different from intact whole-food sources of LA (walnuts, sunflower seeds, tahini). The mainstream advice is "replace saturated fat with PUFA-rich oils"; the reasonable middle-ground advice is "choose whole-food sources, minimize industrially fried foods, prioritize olive oil for daily cooking, keep total LA in normal physiological range — don't load on it, don't suppress it."

### What HIGH means

- Generally favorable in CV/mortality outcome data.
- Mendelian randomization suggests modest cardiometabolic benefit (lipids, diabetes), no signal of harm.
- Outliers: very high intake from refined/heated oils may increase OXLAM exposure (limited evidence).

### What LOW means

- True deficiency is rare but real (skin lesions, growth failure historically — fewer than 2–3% of energy from LA).
- In adults: low LA (RBC <8%) is unusual without a contrived experimental diet and is a marker of overall fat malabsorption or extreme low-fat diet.

### Direction of "better"

The largest evidence base supports physiologically normal levels (RBC 11–13%, or panel-specific reference range). **Neither very high nor very low is clearly optimal**; the data support staying in the middle.

### Confounders

- Recent diet — LA in plasma changes within days; in RBC over weeks.
- FADS variants influence LA→AA conversion, so two people with the same LA intake can have different LA tissue levels.
- Body fat — adipose stores LA, slow to deplete.

## 7. Arachidonic Acid (AA, 20:4n-6)

### What it is

A 20-carbon, 4-double-bond omega-6. Found directly in animal foods (eggs, poultry skin, organ meats, fatty meats — typical Western dietary intake ~100–250 mg/d) and synthesized endogenously from LA via the FADS2 (Δ6) and FADS1 (Δ5) pathway.

### Mechanism — both essential and inflammatory

- **Membrane component:** ~20% of brain fatty acids are AA (similar to DHA). Critical in neuronal, skeletal-muscle, and immune-cell membranes.
- **Eicosanoid precursor (2-/4-series):** PGE2, PGD2, PGI2, TXA2, LTB4, LTC4. These are pro-inflammatory, vasoconstrictive (TXA2), and pro-thrombotic but are also genuinely necessary for normal immune function, gastric mucosal protection (PGE2), platelet hemostasis, and labor (PGE/F).
- **Lipoxin substrate:** AA also yields anti-inflammatory lipoxins (LXA4, LXB4) when COX-2 is acetylated by aspirin — a key reason aspirin shifts AA metabolism toward resolution.
- AA is essential — too low has demonstrable issues (skin barrier, immune function, neural function).

### Reference ranges (RBC % of total FA)

- Western/US average: **~14–17% of total FA in RBC** (remarkably stable across decades and countries; the consistency of this number is notable).
- Japan/Korea: ~12–14% (lower, partly due to higher EPA/DHA crowding the membrane).
- Plasma phospholipid: ~10–12%.
- **Optimal target for 20M:** within 14–17% RBC (i.e., the typical healthy range — there is no demonstrated benefit to suppressing AA, and clear harm from very low values).

### What HIGH means

- High AA intake (eggs, organ meats, poultry skin) AND/OR efficient FADS conversion AND/OR low EPA "competing" with AA.
- Mendelian randomization on **plasma phospholipid AA and CVD** shows no clear causal harm and some signals of benefit ([Zhao et al. 2020 on AA and CVD](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S235239642030565X)). Marklund 2019 suggested possibly favorable, not harmful.
- High AA is not by itself "bad"; it depends on the AA:EPA balance and on what eicosanoid set the body is making.

### What LOW means

- Low AA intake (strict vegan without EPA/DHA-yielding feedback) AND/OR impaired FADS conversion.
- Possible problems: impaired skin barrier, growth in infants/children, immune signaling, neural function. Pregnancy/lactation requires AA for fetal brain development.
- Vegetarians typically run RBC AA only modestly lower than omnivores (often within ~10% relative difference) because endogenous synthesis usually compensates.

### Direction of "better"

**Stay within the typical reference range.** Both extremes (<10% and >18% RBC) are suboptimal. Modulate the *AA:EPA ratio* (next section) rather than AA alone.

### Confounders

- Direct AA intake (eggs, meat) is the strongest determinant.
- FADS genotype (haplotype D = ~43% higher AA at given LA intake).
- Estrogen and growth hormone up-regulate Δ6-desaturase.
- Insulin up-regulates AA synthesis.
- Aspirin / NSAIDs do not change AA levels but redirect downstream eicosanoids toward lipoxins/resolution.

## 8. Omega-6 Total

### What it is

Sum of all omega-6s detected: LA, GLA, DGLA, AA, DPA n-6 (if reported). In Western RBC, ~85–90% of omega-6 is LA + AA; the rest is DGLA (~1–2%), GLA (trace), and DPA n-6 (~0.5–1%).

### Mechanism

LA dominates volumetrically; AA dominates bioactively. GLA→DGLA→PGE1 produces an *anti*-inflammatory eicosanoid (PGE1 — the "good" omega-6 prostaglandin), counterbalancing the AA-derived 2-series. So "omega-6 total" is heterogeneous in its biology.

### Reference ranges (RBC % of total FA)

- Western: ~30–38%
- Optimal: ~28–35% (somewhat lower is often achievable in higher-fish diets where omega-3 displaces some omega-6).

### What HIGH means

Mostly LA. May include high AA. Look at component breakdown to interpret.

### What LOW means

Low PUFA intake overall, very low-fat diet, or a heavily monounsaturated/saturated diet.

### Direction of "better"

Modulate *components* (especially keep AA:EPA in range), don't fixate on Omega-6 Total.

### Confounders

Same as components — diet, genotype, sex, age.

## 9. Omega-6 / Omega-3 Ratio

### What it is

Ratio of total omega-6 to total omega-3 fatty acids (sometimes computed as LA/(EPA+DHA), sometimes as total/total — the lab's footnote matters).

### History and the "ancestral 1:1" framing

- **Simopoulos 2002, Biomed Pharmacother 56:365-379** and a series of follow-ups argued that human evolutionary diet had a ~1:1 omega-6:omega-3 ratio, while the modern Western diet runs **15–20:1**. Excess n-6 was hypothesized to promote inflammation, autoimmunity, and CV disease. ([Simopoulos 2002, PMID 12442909](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12442909/); [Simopoulos 2008, Exp Biol Med](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3181/0711-mr-311); 2016 review).
- This framing has been hugely influential and underlies much "anti-seed-oil" advocacy.

### The contested counter-view

- **Harris and colleagues** (2018 review) argue the ratio is mathematically uninformative because the same ratio can come from dramatically different absolute levels (1% n-6 / 0.2% n-3 vs 30% n-6 / 6% n-3 are both 5:1 but physiologically different). They argue the **absolute Omega-3 Index** is what predicts CV risk — not the ratio.
- The 2025 NMR-derived ratio analysis ([Frontiers in Nutrition 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1693151/full)) argues a refined NMR-measured ratio still adds CV prediction, but other authors argue the value is mostly in the n-3 component.

### Reference ranges

- Modern Western: ~10–20:1
- Mediterranean: ~4–8:1
- Japan: ~2–4:1
- "Ancestral" estimate: ~1:1
- Common targets cited: 4:1 or lower as a pragmatic goal.

### What HIGH means

Either high n-6 (typical Western diet) and/or low n-3 (the more common driver). The actionable response is almost always **raise n-3**, not aggressively cut n-6.

### What LOW means

High n-3 status (good) or unusually low n-6 (rare, possibly indicating overall PUFA deficiency).

### Direction of "better"

Lower is generally better, **but**: raise this lever by raising EPA+DHA, not by suppressing LA. The Omega-3 Index is a more direct and validated CV predictor.

### Confounders

- Same dietary and genetic factors that affect components.
- LA is volumetrically large, so the ratio is dominated by LA changes — but LA changes do not predict CV outcomes (Marklund 2019).

### Honest read

Don't over-interpret this number in isolation. It's a useful *summary* but the underlying components matter more. For a 20-year-old, treat it as a sanity check: if it's >10, your n-3 is almost certainly too low. If it's 4–5, your n-3 status is probably reasonable.

## 10. Arachidonic Acid / EPA Ratio (AA:EPA)

### What it is

Ratio of arachidonic acid to eicosapentaenoic acid in plasma or RBC. This is the biologically meaningful "omega-6-vs-omega-3" ratio because AA and EPA are the **direct competing substrates** at the COX/LOX enzymes that make eicosanoids. Popularized by Barry Sears as a marker of "silent inflammation"; used clinically by OmegaQuant and many functional-medicine labs.

### Mechanism

When AA dominates: COX/LOX make pro-inflammatory PGE2, TXA2, LTB4 (2-/4-series). When EPA is plentiful: it competes for the same enzymes and yields PGE3, TXA3, LTB5 (3-/5-series) — much weaker pro-inflammatory effects, plus E-resolvins for active resolution. The ratio captures this competitive substrate balance.

### Reference ranges

| Category | AA:EPA Ratio |
|---|---|
| **Optimal (Sears / OmegaQuant)** | **1.5 – 3** (target for cardiometabolic protection) |
| Acceptable | 3 – 6 |
| Suboptimal | 6 – 10 |
| Western typical | 10 – 25 |

Source: [OmegaQuant FAQ on AA/EPA](https://omegaquant.com/frequent-question/what-does-my-aa-epa-level-mean/); [Patani et al. 2019, Postgrad Med — AA:EPA review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31063407/).

Note: this ratio is **assay-specific.** Whole-blood ratios differ from RBC and from plasma. Western average in plasma can sit at 15–25; in RBC closer to 8–12. Check the lab's printed reference range.

### What HIGH means

Pro-inflammatory eicosanoid bias. Associated in observational data with:
- Higher coronary artery disease risk
- Higher ACS / MI risk
- Higher stroke risk
- Higher chronic heart failure incidence
- Higher PAD risk
([Patani 2019 review](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00325481.2019.1607414)).

### What LOW means

EPA-dominant balance. Cardioprotective on epidemiologic data.

### Direction of "better": LOWER, target 1.5–3.

### How to lower

Adding 2–4 g/d EPA+DHA can drop AA:EPA from 15+ down to 3–5 within 3–4 months. Reducing direct dietary AA (organ meats, very high egg-yolk intake) modestly lowers the numerator, but raising EPA is the bigger lever.

### Confounders

- Recent fish meal can transiently lower the ratio.
- FADS genotype affects both numerator (AA synthesis from LA) and denominator (EPA from ALA).
- Aspirin doesn't change the ratio but biases AA toward lipoxin output.

## 11. Cross-Cutting Evidence: How Hard Is the CV Evidence?

This is contested terrain. Honest summary:

### Trials that support omega-3 CV benefit

- **GISSI-Prevenzione 1999** — 11,324 post-MI patients, 1 g/d EPA+DHA, 20% reduction in mortality. Foundational but old, before modern statin-based care.
- **JELIS 2007 (Yokoyama, Lancet)** — 18,645 Japanese, EPA 1.8 g/d on top of statin, 19% reduction in major coronary events.
- **REDUCE-IT 2019 (Bhatt, NEJM)** — 25% reduction in CV events with 4 g/d icosapent ethyl in high-risk statin-treated patients with high TG. Caveat: mineral-oil placebo. ([Bhatt 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1812792)).

### Trials that show no CV benefit

- **STRENGTH 2020 (Nicholls, JAMA)** — 4 g/d mixed EPA+DHA, no benefit. ([Nicholls 2020](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778119)).
- **VITAL 2018-19 (Manson, NEJM)** — 25,871 healthy adults, 1 g/d EPA+DHA, no significant CV or cancer benefit overall. Subgroup signals: low fish-eaters and African Americans had reduced MI. ([Manson 2019, NEJM 380:23-32](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1811403)).
- **ASCEND, OMEMI, FORWARD** — null. FORWARD (1 g/d for AF recurrence) was negative. ([Macchia 2013, FORWARD](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23265344/)).

### Meta-analyses

- **Cochrane (Abdelhamid 2020)** — 86 trials, n=162,796; little-to-no effect of supplemental long-chain n-3 on CV mortality, all-cause mortality, or events overall. Modest TG reduction. ALA had similarly minimal CV benefit. ([Abdelhamid 2020 Cochrane](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD003177.pub5/full)).
- **Maki 2017 J Clin Lipidol** — 35% reduction in sudden cardiac death with ≥250 mg/d, but smaller effect for non-fatal events.
- **Harris 2021 Nat Commun** — pooled biomarker analysis, 17 cohorts, n=42,466: high blood n-3 → 13% lower all-cause mortality.

### Atrial fibrillation signal

Multiple trials (REDUCE-IT, STRENGTH, OMEMI) show **increased AF** at high doses (3–4 g/d). Meta-analysis (Gencer 2021, *Circulation*): ~25% relative increase in AF, dose-dependent. ([Gencer 2021](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.121.055654)). At dietary or low-supplement doses (≤1 g/d), no AF signal. For a healthy 20-year-old, this is essentially not relevant unless taking pharmacological doses.

### Bottom line

- **Biomarker-based observational evidence is robust:** higher RBC EPA+DHA → lower mortality, dose-dependent.
- **RCT evidence is mixed:** dose, formulation (EPA-only vs mixed), placebo (mineral oil vs corn oil), and baseline omega-3 status all matter.
- The most defensible interpretation: **low omega-3 status is harmful; correcting deficiency yields meaningful benefit; supplementing on top of already-adequate status yields diminishing returns**. The Omega-3 Index target of 8% likely captures most of the achievable benefit.

## 12. Practical Synthesis for a Healthy 20-Year-Old Male

| Marker | "Good" target (typical RBC/whole-blood scale) | What to do if low | What to do if high |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Omega-3 Index** | **≥ 8%** | Eat fatty fish 2–3×/week, or 2–3 g/d EPA+DHA fish/algal oil | Consider reducing if pharmacologic dose; otherwise nothing |
| **EPA** | 2.5–4% | Direct EPA supplementation; oily fish | Generally fine |
| **DHA** | 5–7% | Algal DHA or fish; critical for brain, sperm, vision | Generally fine |
| **DPA n-3** | 2–3% | Comes along with EPA/DHA | n/a |
| **Omega-3 Total** | ≥ 10% | Above + walnuts/flax for ALA | n/a |
| **LA** | within reference (RBC ~11–13%) | Likely fine — LA deficiency is rare | Don't actively suppress; population data favor normal-range LA. Avoid heated/repeatedly-fried oils |
| **AA** | RBC ~14–17% | Direct dietary AA (eggs, meat) plus FADS-supportive nutrients (zinc, B6, mag) | Ensure adequate EPA to maintain AA:EPA in range; AA itself is essential |
| **Omega-6 Total** | ~30–35% RBC | Probably OK — interpret components | Pull from refined-oil-heavy diet toward more whole-food fats |
| **Omega-6:Omega-3 Ratio** | ≤ 4:1 | Raise n-3 (don't aggressively cut LA) | Raise n-3 |
| **AA:EPA Ratio** | **1.5–3** | Add EPA-rich fish/oil — high-leverage intervention | n/a, lower is better within range |

### What a 20-year-old should actually do based on this panel

1. **Single most actionable item: get the Omega-3 Index to ≥8%.** Three forkfuls of salmon/sardines per week, or a 2 g/d EPA+DHA fish-oil supplement, will get most healthy 20-year-olds there in ~3–4 months. Algal oil (DHA 600 mg + EPA 300 mg) works equivalently for vegetarians/vegans.

2. **Don't fixate on LA/seed oils.** The largest prospective cohort evidence (Marklund 2019, 30 cohorts) supports LA being **neutral-to-protective** for CV risk. The "seed oil bad" framing is mechanistically interesting and applies to repeatedly-heated/oxidized oils, but does not change the lifestyle prescription for a healthy young person eating mostly whole foods.

3. **Don't suppress AA actively.** AA is essential. The lever is the *ratio* with EPA, fixed by raising EPA.

4. **The ratio (n-6:n-3) is dominated by your n-3 status.** Whatever your LA is, fix it from the n-3 side first.

5. **Check the FADS genotype (rs174537 or haplotype A/D)** if 23andMe/genome data are available — it materially shifts what "achievable" omega-3 status is on a given diet.

6. **Re-test at 4 months** if intervening — RBC equilibration takes that long.
