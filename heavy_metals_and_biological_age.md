# Heavy Metals and Biological Age — Reference Guide

Markers covered: **Lead (BLL)**, **Mercury (whole blood total Hg)**, **Biological Age (Levine PhenoAge)**.

Audience: 20-year-old male using Function Health. Quantitative claims are cited inline with primary sources where possible (NHANES, CDC, EPA, peer-reviewed literature). The biological age section is deliberately skeptical — at age 20 the noise floor of a single PhenoAge measurement is large.

---

## 1. LEAD (Blood Lead Level — BLL)

### 1.1 What it measures

Whole-blood lead, typically reported in µg/dL (US convention) or µg/L (multiply µg/dL by 10). The value reflects the **circulating** lead pool, which is in dynamic equilibrium with two storage compartments:

- **Soft tissue** (kidney, liver, brain) — short half-life (~30 days).
- **Bone** (cortical and trabecular) — long half-life. The tibia (cortical bone) half-life is estimated at ~25–50 years; ATSDR and bone-Pb reviews commonly cite ~25–30 years for cortical bone. ~90% of total body lead burden lives in bone in chronically-exposed adults [(NCBI Bookshelf — Lead in Bone: Implications for Toxicology During Pregnancy and Lactation)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1519355/).

This matters because BLL can rise *years after* exposure ends, when bone mineralization shifts (pregnancy, lactation, menopause, hyperthyroidism, prolonged immobilization, weight loss, osteoporosis) [(Silbergeld 1988, Environmental Research)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3168967/).

### 1.2 Assay

Function Health and most clinical labs use **ICP-MS** (inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry) on whole blood, with detection limits typically ~0.1–0.3 µg/dL. Older labs use graphite furnace atomic absorption spectrometry (GF-AAS) with somewhat higher limits of detection. Capillary fingerstick samples are screening only — confirmatory venous draws are required for any elevated capillary BLL [(CDC: Recommended Actions Based on BLL)](https://www.cdc.gov/lead-prevention/hcp/clinical-guidance/index.html).

### 1.3 Mechanism of toxicity

Lead is a **divalent cation that mimics calcium and zinc** at protein binding sites. The biologically destructive consequences flow from this:

- **Heme synthesis inhibition.** Pb²⁺ inhibits **δ-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase (ALAD)** by displacing zinc at its triple-cysteine binding site. ALAD inhibition is the most sensitive biochemical marker of lead exposure and blocks heme biosynthesis, causing buildup of the precursor δ-ALA and decreased heme output (microcytic anemia at higher exposures) [(Tufts: ALAD Inhibition)](https://sites.tufts.edu/leadpoisoning/pathways/inhibition-of-%CE%B4-aminolevulinic-acid-dehydrogenase-alad/) [(Sakai 2005, Annals of Clin & Lab Sci review)](https://www.annclinlabsci.org/content/10/5/402.full.pdf).
- **Neurotoxicity.** δ-ALA accumulation suppresses GABAergic neurotransmission. Pb²⁺ is also a non-competitive, voltage-independent **NMDA receptor antagonist**, blocking long-term potentiation — the cellular substrate of learning and memory [(Sanders et al. 2009, "Neurotoxic Effects and Biomarkers of Lead Exposure")](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2858639/).
- **Nephrotoxicity.** Proximal tubular damage; chronic low-level Pb is a well-established CKD risk factor independent of hypertension [(Muntner et al. 2003, "Blood lead and chronic kidney disease in the general US population: NHANES III")](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12631086/).
- **Hypertension and cardiovascular disease.** Lead competes with Ca²⁺ in vascular smooth muscle, generates reactive oxygen species, inactivates nitric oxide, and impairs endothelial function. The Lanphear NHANES-III analysis quantifies this at the population level (see §1.5).
- **Reproductive toxicity.** Reduced sperm count and motility, decreased libido, ED, and male infertility documented in occupationally exposed cohorts [(StatPearls: Lead Toxicity)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK541097/).
- **Saturnine gout.** Lead inhibits renal urate excretion → hyperuricemia and clinical gout. Historically tied to lead-sweetened wine ("sugar of lead") [(Sun & Dwyer 1989, "Saturnine gout")](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2661030/).
- **Peripheral neuropathy.** Predominantly motor, extensor-muscle distribution — classical "wrist drop" / "foot drop" in chronic high-level exposure [(MedLink Neurology — Lead neuropathy)](https://www.medlink.com/articles/lead-neuropathy).

### 1.4 Sources of high values (in rough order of relevance to a 20-year-old US male)

- **Old housing (pre-1978) lead paint dust and lead pipes / leaded plumbing solder** — still the largest US exposure pathway by population.
- **Drinking water from leaded service lines or fixtures.** Soldered copper plumbing pre-1986 used Pb-Sn solder; brass fixtures pre-2014 could legally contain up to 8% lead.
- **Occupational/hobby exposure:** battery manufacturing, lead smelting, radiator repair, soldering electronics (especially leaded solder), pottery / stained glass with lead glazing, ammunition reloading, **indoor and outdoor shooting ranges**. A 2025 review of 36 studies across 15 countries found firing-range workers commonly exceed 10 µg/dL and frequently >40 µg/dL — orders of magnitude above the general population mean of 1–2 µg/dL [(JKMS 2025, occupational lead in indoor shooting ranges)](https://jkms.org/DOIx.php?id=10.3346/jkms.2025.40.e116).
- **Retained lead bullet/shrapnel** — chronic systemic Pb release; historically reported with retained fragments in joints or near vasculature.
- **Imported spices and foods.** The 2023–2024 cinnamon-applesauce outbreak (WanaBana) involved cinnamon at **2,270–5,110 ppm Pb** sourced from Ecuador, with ≥566 case reports across 44 states and DC. The FDA's working hypothesis is **intentional adulteration with lead chromate** for weight and color [(CDC MMWR April 2025)](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/74/wr/mm7414a2.htm) [(NPR/FDA reporting)](https://www.npr.org/2024/10/24/nx-s1-5119336/cinnamon-lead-fda-recall-what-we-know). Subsequent 2024 FDA alerts flagged additional ground cinnamon brands at 2.03–20 ppm.
- **Ayurvedic / traditional Asian / Hispanic folk medicines** ("rasa shastra"). ~21% of US/India-sourced Ayurvedic pills sampled contain lead, mercury, or arsenic; case reports include BLLs of 102–123 µg/dL with herbal capsule lead content as high as 40,657 ppm [(Frontiers in Pediatrics 2025 case report)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pediatrics/articles/10.3389/fped.2025.1692561/full) [(Endocrinology Diabetes Metabolism Case Reports 2024)](https://edm.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/edm/2024/2/EDM23-0066.xml).
- **Lead-glazed ceramic ware** (especially imported Mexican / South Asian pottery used for cooking acidic foods).
- **Imported cosmetics** (kohl, surma, sindoor).
- **Mobilization from bone** in stress states (above) — without new exposure.

### 1.5 Direction of better — lower is strictly better, with no known safe threshold

The seminal modern reference is **Lanphear et al. 2018, Lancet Public Health** — a prospective NHANES-III cohort (n=14,289 adults, median 19.3y follow-up, 4,422 deaths). Key findings:

- Going from BLL of 1.0 → 6.7 µg/dL (10th–90th percentile) was associated with HR 1.70 (95% CI 1.30–2.22) for cardiovascular mortality and HR 2.08 (95% CI 1.52–2.85) for ischemic heart disease mortality.
- **The same gradient persisted in the subset with BLL <5 µg/dL** — i.e. there is no apparent threshold below which lead is safe.
- Population-attributable fractions: 18% of all-cause mortality, 28.7% of CV mortality, 37.4% of IHD mortality — corresponding to ~412,000 deaths/year (all cause) and ~256,000 from CVD in the US [(Lanphear et al. 2018, Lancet Public Health)](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpub/article/PIIS2468-2667(18)30025-2/fulltext) [(PubMed 29544878)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29544878/).

Older guidelines (e.g. CDC's 1991 "level of concern" of 10 µg/dL in children) have been progressively lowered as evidence accumulated that cognitive and CV harm extends below previous thresholds. The CDC explicitly states the BLRV is a **statistical reference** (97.5th percentile of US children 1–5y), **not a clinical safety threshold** [(CDC 2021 BLRV update, MMWR)](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/70/wr/mm7043a4.htm).

### 1.6 Reference ranges and realistic optimal

| Reference | Threshold | Year | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| LabCorp / Quest "normal" (adult) | <5 µg/dL | current | A statistical not biological threshold; outdated relative to mortality data |
| CDC blood lead reference value (children 1–5y) | **3.5 µg/dL** | updated **Oct 2021** (from 5.0) | 97.5th percentile NHANES 2015–2018; flag for case management | [(CDC 2021 BLRV update)](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/70/wr/mm7043a4.htm) |
| OSHA medical removal protection (general industry) | 60 µg/dL | 1978 standard, unchanged | Considered grossly outdated by occupational medicine community |
| OSHA action level (workplace air) | 30 µg/m³ | 1978 | Unchanged |
| ACOEM 2023 position statement | **30 µg/dL** triggers medical removal | 2023 | Evidence-based update; not yet adopted federally [(ACOEM 2023 position)](https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/lead/bll-reference/index.html) |
| California Cal/OSHA (2024) | 30 µg/dL → MRP; phasing to 20 µg/dL average | Feb 2024 | Most stringent US standard [(Cal/OSHA 2024 update)](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/CCDPHP/DEODC/OHB/Pages/March2024.aspx) |
| **Realistic biological optimum** | **as low as detection allows** | — | No safe threshold |

**Where you should be in the population distribution (NHANES):**

- US adults ≥20y, NHANES 2017–2018 geometric mean BLL: **0.855 µg/dL** (males 0.860, females 0.664) [(EPA Report on the Environment, NHANES BLL)](https://cfpub.epa.gov/roe/indicator_pdf.cfm?i=63).
- Population mean has dropped from ~16 µg/dL in NHANES II (1976–80, pre-leaded-gas phaseout) to ~0.8 µg/dL today — one of the great public-health wins.
- For a healthy 20yo male with no occupational exposure, residing in modern housing, drinking municipal water, eating a varied non-imported-spice-heavy diet, **expect BLL near or below detection limit (<0.5 µg/dL is realistic)**. Anything above ~1.5 µg/dL deserves a source investigation; >3.5 puts you above the children's reference value and is concerning regardless of age.

### 1.7 Conditions associated with elevated lead

Hypertension, CKD (especially in conjunction with hypertension or diabetes), saturnine gout, peripheral neuropathy (motor predominant), microcytic anemia (basophilic stippling on smear at higher levels), cognitive impairment, ED and male infertility, and increased CV mortality [(StatPearls: Lead Toxicity)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK541097/) [(Sanders 2009 review)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2858639/).

### 1.8 Confounders affecting interpretation

- **Recent vs chronic exposure.** A spike from contaminated cinnamon last week and 30 years of low-grade dust exposure look identical on a single BLL. Hair analysis is unreliable for lead. Bone Pb measurement (KXRF tibia) is research-only.
- **Bone-pool mobilization.** A young man's BLL is essentially all from current/recent intake; bone Pb mobilization becomes clinically relevant in pregnancy, lactation, menopause, hyperthyroidism, prolonged bedrest, sudden weight loss [(NCBI: Lead in bone implications)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1519355/).
- **Dietary calcium and iron.** Both compete with Pb absorption in the gut; deficiency increases GI uptake.
- **ALAD genetic polymorphism (ALAD1-1 vs ALAD1-2/2-2).** Affects Pb binding kinetics and may modulate retention; effect size is modest in epidemiology [(Kelada 2001 meta-analysis)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1797830/).
- **Hematocrit.** Pb partitions strongly into RBCs (~99%). Markedly anemic patients have lower whole-blood Pb at the same body burden — generally not relevant in the FH context but worth noting for severe anemia.
- **Sample contamination.** Trace metals demand strict pre-analytic discipline (royal-blue-top tube, no rubber stoppers). Repeat draws if a result is unexpectedly elevated and the source is not obvious.

## 2. MERCURY (Whole Blood Total Hg)

### 2.1 What it measures

Total mercury in whole blood, units µg/L (or ng/mL — equivalent). Whole-blood total Hg is dominated in most adults by **methylmercury (MeHg)** from fish/shellfish; the other species are inorganic Hg²⁺ (Hg(II), industrial/dental) and elemental Hg⁰ (vapor exposure) [(Caldwell et al. 2014, NHANES 2011–2012, total vs methyl Hg)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25173092/).

NHANES 2011–2012 first separately measured both species; in the US adult population MeHg accounted for ~70–75% of total Hg on average (overall geometric means: 0.70 µg/L total vs 0.50 µg/L methyl) [(Caldwell 2014)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5584810/).

### 2.2 Assay and matrices

- **Whole blood total Hg** (ICP-MS): captures recent exposure (~30–60 day window). MeHg blood half-life 45–80 days, commonly cited as ~50 days in adults [(Wikipedia: Methylmercury, citing JECFA/EPA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylmercury) [(Stony Brook Gelfond Fund summary)](https://www.stonybrook.edu/commcms/gelfond/mercury_and_fish/what_you_should_know.php).
- **Hair total Hg**: integrates over ~3 months per centimeter of growth. Hair:blood Hg ratio ~250:1 at steady state. Used in the major epidemiology cohorts (Faroes, Seychelles, Salonen Finnish men).
- **Urine Hg**: reflects **inorganic and elemental** exposure (industrial, dental amalgam, gold mining), not MeHg.
- **Speciated whole blood (MeHg + inorganic Hg)**: only some labs offer; useful when source is unclear.

### 2.3 Mechanism of toxicity

Mercury is a **soft electrophile** with extreme affinity for thiol (-SH) and selenol (-SeH) groups. Mechanisms differ slightly across species but converge on protein dysfunction and oxidative stress:

- MeHg complexes with cysteine and glutathione (GSH); the MeHg-cysteine complex is structurally similar to methionine and is transported across the blood-brain barrier and placenta by the L-type amino acid transporter [(Farina et al. 2011, "Mechanisms of methylmercury-induced neurotoxicity")](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3183295/).
- Binds and inhibits selenoproteins including **glutathione peroxidase** and **thioredoxin reductase**, depleting antioxidant defense → oxidative stress → cell death [(Farina & Aschner 2019, "Glutathione antioxidant system and methylmercury")](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6635095/).
- Developing brain is the most sensitive target — **prenatal/early-life exposure** is the dominant public-health concern. Adult acute neurotoxicity (paresthesias, ataxia, visual field constriction, dysarthria) appears at hair Hg roughly >50 µg/g (blood ~200 µg/L), well above modern dietary exposure ranges [(WHO/JECFA, NCBI Mercury chapter)](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/toxprofiles/tp46-c1.pdf).
- **Cardiovascular**: activates phospholipase A2, promotes lipid peroxidation, blunts the cardioprotective effects of fish-derived omega-3s. The Salonen cohort (Eastern Finnish men) found highest-tertile hair Hg (>2.03 µg/g) had HR 1.60 for acute coronary events vs lowest two tertiles [(Salonen et al. 2005, ATVB)](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/01.atv.0000150040.20950.61).
- Inorganic Hg²⁺ is a renal tubular toxin; elemental Hg⁰ vapor is the form that crosses BBB pre-oxidation and causes classical "erethism" syndrome.

### 2.4 Sources of high values

- **Large predatory fish.** In rough order of MeHg concentration (FDA monitoring data, mean ppm in muscle):
  - **Swordfish ~0.99 ppm**, tilefish (Gulf of Mexico) ~1.45 ppm, **king mackerel ~0.73**, **shark ~0.98**, **bigeye tuna ~0.69 (up to 1.8)**, **marlin ~0.49**, orange roughy ~0.57.
  - Yellowfin and albacore tuna ~0.35 ppm.
  - "Light" canned tuna (skipjack) ~0.13–0.14 ppm — much lower [(FDA Mercury Levels in Commercial Fish 1990–2012)](https://www.fda.gov/food/environmental-contaminants-food/mercury-levels-commercial-fish-and-shellfish-1990-2012) [(Healthline summary)](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/mercury-in-tuna).
- **Frequency matters more than any single meal.** Women eating ≥9 fish meals in 30 days had blood Hg seven-fold higher than non-consumers [(Mahaffey et al. 2009 NHANES analysis)](https://saludparavieques.com/images/pdfs/SALUD/Mahaffey%20-%20Mercury%20exposure%20-%20medical%20and%20public%20health%20issues.pdf).
- **Asian dietary patterns:** NHANES 2011–2012 — Asian Americans had MeHg geometric mean **1.58 µg/L** vs 0.50 µg/L overall — driven by higher fish intake [(Caldwell 2014)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25173092/).
- **Occupational**: chloralkali plants, gold mining (especially artisanal/small-scale), thermometer/manometer manufacture (largely historical), dental staff with old amalgam handling.
- **Dental amalgam fillings.** Each surface contributes ~1–5 µg/L urinary Hg; an individual with ≥7 amalgam surfaces has 30–50% higher urinary Hg than someone with no amalgams. Effect on blood Hg is small in steady state but **transient amalgam removal can cause acute Hg vapor exposure** spikes [(Dunn et al. 2008 — Dental amalgam and urinary Hg in children)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2022658/).
- **Skin-lightening creams** (often imported, illegal in US but obtainable) — high inorganic Hg.
- **Broken CFL bulbs / mercury thermometers** — small acute vapor exposure, rarely meaningful in blood unless prolonged unventilated exposure.
- **Ayurvedic medicines** (rasa shastra) — overlap with lead contamination noted above.
- **Thimerosal** (ethylmercury preservative) — historical concern in vaccines, mostly removed from US childhood schedules. Pharmacokinetically distinct from MeHg with shorter half-life.

### 2.5 Direction of better — lower is preferable, with caveats

Lower whole-blood Hg is biologically better, but it is **not** the only consideration:

- Fish provide long-chain omega-3 fatty acids (EPA, DHA), high-quality protein, vitamin D, selenium, and iodine. **Selenium in particular antagonizes mercury toxicity** by binding Hg with even higher affinity than thiols, sequestering it from selenoenzymes [(Spiller 2017, Clin Tox — Selenium and mercury)](https://henryspiller.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/Spiller-clin-tox-2017-Rethinking-mercury-the-role-of-selenium-in-the-pathophysiology-of-mercury-toxicity.pdf).
- Net cardiovascular effect of fish consumption in most cohorts is favorable despite Hg content. The Salonen cohort is unusual — those men ate large amounts of pike and other freshwater predators with high Hg and likely lower omega-3 than ocean fatty fish.
- The **rational optimization for adults** is: avoid the top-tier predators (shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish, bigeye tuna), limit middle-tier (yellowfin, albacore) to a few servings/week, and load up on low-Hg high-omega-3 species (salmon, sardines, anchovies, mackerel except king, herring, trout) [(EPA/FDA "Advice About Eating Fish")](https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2024-12/blood-methylmercury-fish-consumption-report.pdf).

### 2.6 Reference ranges and realistic optimal

| Reference | Threshold | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| LabCorp (whole blood Hg) | <10 µg/L "normal," elevated above | Operational threshold; not biologically protective |
| Quest (whole blood Hg) | <10 µg/L commonly cited | Same |
| **EPA Reference Dose (MeHg)** | **0.1 µg/kg/day** (maternal); **5.8 µg/L** in cord blood | Derived from Faroe Islands cohort BMDL of 58 µg/L → 10× UF [(Rice et al. 2003, Risk Analysis — EPA RfD derivation)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12635727/) |
| ATSDR Minimal Risk Level (chronic oral MeHg) | 0.3 µg/kg/day | Higher than EPA; protective level |
| WHO/JECFA PTWI | 1.6 µg/kg/week MeHg | Equivalent to ~0.23 µg/kg/day |
| Hair Hg threshold for adverse fetal neurodev | ~1 µg/g (corresponds to ~5.8 µg/L blood) | Faroes-derived BMDL with 10× UF |
| **Realistic biological optimum (adult male)** | **<1 µg/L; ideally <2 µg/L** | Around or below NHANES median |

**Where you should be in the population distribution (NHANES):**

- US adults ≥20y, NHANES 2017–2018: **total blood Hg geometric mean 0.730 µg/L** (95% CI 0.620–0.840); MeHg geometric mean ~0.50 µg/L [(EPA Report on the Environment, Blood Mercury)](https://cfpub.epa.gov/roe/indicator_pdf.cfm?i=64).
- 90th percentile typically 2.5–3.5 µg/L; 95th around 4–5 µg/L in recent cycles.
- 5–10 µg/L: middle of "fish-heavy diet" range; not toxic, but worth attention if you are also planning conception (partners) or have neurological symptoms.
- 10+ µg/L: reduce predator-fish intake; expect ~50-day half-life for clearance.
- 50+ µg/L: clinical toxicology consult warranted.
- Note Asian Americans run ~2× higher at baseline; an Asian-American 20yo with blood Hg of 1.5 µg/L is at population median for that subgroup.

### 2.7 Conditions associated with elevated mercury

- **Methylmercury poisoning** (Minamata disease — paresthesias, cerebellar ataxia, dysarthria, constricted visual fields, hearing loss). Adult-acquired clinical syndrome is rare in the modern US.
- **Erethism mercurialis** (inorganic/elemental Hg) — fine tremor, irritability, insomnia, social withdrawal, gingivitis.
- **Acrodynia** ("pink disease") — historical infant exposure to teething powders.
- **Peripheral neuropathy**, chronic fatigue at sustained higher levels.
- **Possible CV mortality signal** in highly-exposed populations (Salonen).
- **Idiosyncratic / autoimmune effects** (membranous nephropathy from inorganic exposure) — uncommon.

### 2.8 Confounders affecting interpretation

- **Recent fish consumption.** A single tuna steak 3 days before a draw can roughly double background blood Hg in a normally low-fish eater. Standardize: avoid predator fish for ~7 days before draw if you want a "trough" reading.
- **Total Hg vs MeHg.** Most adults in NHANES have ~70% MeHg / 30% inorganic. If your total is high but MeHg fraction is low, suspect dental amalgam or occupational/environmental inorganic exposure.
- **Selenium status.** High dietary Se (Brazil nuts, ocean fish, organ meats) appears to mitigate Hg toxicity at the tissue level even if blood Hg is elevated [(Spiller 2017)](https://henryspiller.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/Spiller-clin-tox-2017-Rethinking-mercury-the-role-of-selenium-in-the-pathophysiology-of-mercury-toxicity.pdf).
- **Hair color treatment / chelation therapy** can affect hair Hg; not relevant for blood.
- **Body composition.** MeHg distributes preferentially to muscle and brain; lean mass differences modestly shift kinetics.

## 3. BIOLOGICAL AGE — Function Health uses Levine PhenoAge

### 3.1 What it is

Function Health's "Biological Age" is the **Levine Phenotypic Age (PhenoAge)** — a mortality-calibrated composite of chronological age plus 9 clinical lab markers, originally published in **Levine et al. 2018, *Aging* (Albany NY)** [(PMC5940111)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5940111/) and validated in **Liu et al. 2018, *PLOS Medicine*** on independent NHANES IV data [(Liu et al. 2018)](https://journals.plos.org/plosmedicine/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pmed.1002718). Function Health's public biomarker page describes the concept but does not publicly disclose the algorithm; the underlying lab inputs they collect map exactly to Levine's 9-marker model, and other independent reviewers confirm the algorithm in use [(Function Health — Biological Age)](https://www.functionhealth.com/biomarker-categories/biological-age).

**Critically, Function Health's "Biological Age" is NOT:**
- DNA methylation-based (Horvath, Hannum, GrimAge, DunedinPACE — all require methylation arrays from blood/saliva/buccal cells, not standard chemistry panels).
- A telomere length measurement.
- A proprietary deep-learning aging clock.

It is a **clinical chemistry composite** trained against mortality data, which makes it cheap and reproducible but also relatively coarse and noisy. Methylation clocks (especially **GrimAge** and **DunedinPACE**) are more validated for mortality and pace-of-aging respectively, with test-retest reliabilities ~0.93 (GrimAge) and ~0.73 (DunedinPACE) and stronger effect sizes against incident disease [(Belsky et al. 2022, eLife — DunedinPACE)](https://elifesciences.org/articles/73420).

### 3.2 The 9 markers (memorize these — this is what actually drives the number)

| # | Marker | Interpretation in PhenoAge |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Albumin | Higher = younger (nutritional/hepatic) |
| 2 | Creatinine | Higher = older (nominally; complicated by muscle mass — see §3.7) |
| 3 | Glucose | Higher = older (metabolic) |
| 4 | C-reactive protein (log-transformed) | Higher = older (inflammation) |
| 5 | Lymphocyte % | Higher = younger (immune) |
| 6 | MCV (mean corpuscular volume) | Higher = older (hematologic stress) |
| 7 | RDW (red cell distribution width) | Higher = older (hematologic) |
| 8 | Alkaline phosphatase | Higher = older (hepatobiliary/bone — but elevated in adolescents/young adults from bone growth) |
| 9 | White blood cell count | Higher = older (inflammation/immune) |

Plus chronological age.

### 3.3 The actual formula

Step 1 — compute mortality score *xb*:

```
xb = -19.907
     - 0.0336 × albumin (g/L)
     + 0.0095 × creatinine (µmol/L)
     + 0.1953 × glucose (mmol/L)
     + 0.0954 × ln(CRP, mg/L)            ← note: log-transformed
     - 0.0120 × lymphocyte percent (%)
     + 0.0268 × MCV (fL)
     + 0.3306 × RDW (%)
     + 0.00188 × alkaline phosphatase (U/L)
     + 0.0554 × WBC count (10⁹/L)
     + 0.0804 × chronological age (years)
```

Step 2 — Gompertz mortality conversion to PhenoAge:

```
PhenoAge = 141.50225 + ln( -0.00553 × ln(1 - M) ) / 0.09165

where M = 1 - exp( -1.51714 × exp(xb) × (e^(0.0076927 × 120) - 1) / 0.0076927 )
```

(Equivalently: PhenoAge solves for the age at which the modeled 10-year mortality equals the predicted 10-year mortality from the input markers under a Gompertz hazard.) Several public calculators implement this verbatim [(Levine 2018 supplementary material)](https://www.aging-us.com/article/101414/supplementary/SD1/0/aging-v10i4-101414-supplementary-material-SD1.pdf) [(Steele PhenoAge calculator)](https://andrewsteele.co.uk/biological-age/).

**PhenoAge Acceleration** = PhenoAge − chronological age. Negative is "younger than chronological," positive is "accelerated aging."

### 3.4 What the number means biologically

Levine's interpretation, validated in Liu 2018: each 1-year increase in PhenoAge above chronological age is associated with roughly **9% increased all-cause mortality** in adults, after adjustment for chronological age [(Liu et al. 2018, PLOS Med)](https://journals.plos.org/plosmedicine/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pmed.1002718).

The **mechanism** of any positive PhenoAge acceleration is mechanically traceable to which of the 9 markers is contributing — this is the test's real value. A 20-year-old with PhenoAge 28 didn't "age faster" in a mystical sense; one or more of {CRP, glucose, WBC, RDW, ALP, lymphocyte %, MCV, albumin, creatinine} is in a range typical of older adults. The component-level decomposition tells you whether the issue is inflammation, glucose dysregulation, immune activation, or hematologic stress.

### 3.5 Direction of better

Lower PhenoAge (younger) is generally better at the population level. **At the individual level the relationship breaks down for young people** (see §3.7).

### 3.6 Reference ranges and what to expect at age 20

- Levine 2018 trained on NHANES III (1988–94) adults aged 20–84.
- The Liu 2018 validation in NHANES IV showed valid mortality prediction across age strata down to age 20 — but the mortality signal is dominated by older participants because deaths in 20-year-olds during a 12-year follow-up are rare.
- Independent reanalyses on >20,000 NHANES participants ages 12–79 found PhenoAge has a **regression slope of ~1.21 against chronological age** (i.e. it grows ~21% faster than chronological age), with the youngest age decile showing **mean PhenoAge acceleration of −5.8 years** purely as a calibration artifact [(see "Quantifying Biological Age" reanalysis by M. Lustgarten)](https://michaellustgarten.com/2019/09/09/quantifying-biological-age/).
- **Expected PhenoAge for a healthy 20-year-old male: ~14–17.** A PhenoAge of 17 in a 20-year-old (delta −3) is typical, not exceptional. A PhenoAge of 22 (delta +2) at age 20 is a yellow flag worth tracing to the offending marker.
- **A negative PhenoAge delta in a young person is largely artifactual** and does not credibly translate to mortality benefit at the level Levine demonstrated for older adults.

### 3.7 Limitations — be skeptical, especially at age 20

**1. Training population skew.** NHANES III enrolled in 1988–1994. The mortality outcomes used for calibration occurred mostly in older adults. The model is most accurate where most deaths occurred — middle-aged and older. At age 20–30 the calibration is weak and the formula's behavior is partly determined by extrapolation [(Levine 2018)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5940111/).

**2. Measurement noise dominates the signal at young ages.** Single-day variability in:
- **CRP**: huge — a minor cold, recent vaccine, intense workout 24h prior, poor sleep, or even fasting status can move hsCRP by 2–10×. Since CRP is **log-transformed** in the formula, the absolute variation is somewhat damped, but a CRP of 3 vs 0.3 still moves PhenoAge by ~1 year.
- **WBC**: ±15–25% diurnal and inter-day variation, more during/after exercise, infection, stress.
- **Lymphocyte %**: also moves with stress, infection, time of day.
- **Glucose**: fasting status, prior night's carbs, sleep, illness.

A single PhenoAge measurement at age 20 can plausibly swing ±3–4 years just from these effects. **Repeat and trend** rather than treating any single number as truth.

**3. Creatinine encodes muscle mass.** PhenoAge penalizes higher creatinine. A young muscular male with creatinine 1.2 mg/dL gets a PhenoAge bump that has nothing to do with kidney function. This is a meaningful confound for athletic 20-year-old males.

**4. Alkaline phosphatase in young people.** ALP is elevated during bone growth. Adolescents and even early-20s males can have ALP in the 90–130 range that is purely physiological (active bone remodeling), not pathologic. The formula reads this as "older," incorrectly.

**5. Only captures dimensions in the input markers.** Will not detect: lipid abnormalities, hypertension, hormone imbalances, vitamin deficiencies, heavy metal load, mitochondrial dysfunction, sleep architecture, fitness (VO₂max), grip strength, body composition, telomere length, methylation patterns, autoimmunity. PhenoAge says nothing about whether you have premature atherosclerosis from familial hypercholesterolemia.

**6. Population-mean reference, not age-specific.** Because PhenoAge translates 9 biomarker values to "the chronological age at which this combination of markers gives equal mortality risk," it implicitly compares you to the entire adult population. A 20-year-old with markers normal for 20-year-olds will register as "younger than chronological" simply because 20-year-old markers are healthier than the 30/40/50 population average.

**7. Not diagnostic.** It is an epidemiological summary statistic, not a clinical decision tool. The FDA does not regulate it as a diagnostic device.

**8. Better aging clocks exist but require methylation.** GrimAge (PMID 30669119, *Lu et al. 2019*) integrates methylation-predicted plasma protein levels and smoking pack-years, with stronger mortality effect sizes than PhenoAge. **DunedinPACE** measures the **rate** of aging (years of biological aging per year of chronological time), with effect sizes comparable to or larger than GrimAge and superior incremental predictive validity for morbidity and mortality after adjustment for other clocks [(Belsky 2022, eLife)](https://elifesciences.org/articles/73420). Test-retest reliabilities: GrimAge r=0.93, DunedinPACE r=0.73, while clinical-chemistry-derived PhenoAge has lower reliability because the inputs are more variable day-to-day. None of these are what Function Health gives you.

### 3.8 Practical guidance for the 20-year-old user

- **Don't over-interpret a single PhenoAge result.** Especially at age 20, a delta of ±3 years is mostly noise.
- **Track the 9 component markers, not the composite.** That is where the signal lives. Track over multiple draws (Function Health is twice yearly).
- **Standardize draw conditions.** Same lab, same time of day, fasting, no acute illness in prior 2 weeks, no intense workout in prior 48h, no recent vaccine. Reduces noise meaningfully on CRP / WBC / glucose.
- **A *positive* delta (e.g. PhenoAge 24 at chronological age 20) is the result worth investigating.** Identify which marker(s) are abnormal for a young adult — usually CRP (chronic inflammation, undiagnosed infection, dental disease, autoimmunity, obesity), glucose (insulin resistance), WBC (subclinical infection, smoking), or ALP (still-active bone growth — usually benign at age 20). RDW and MCV abnormalities point to anemia, B12/folate deficiency, or other hematologic issues.
- **A negative delta is reassuring but not actionable.** Do not assume PhenoAge of 16 at age 20 means you "will live longer" — the algorithm wasn't calibrated to make individual-level predictions, and the data on lifestyle interventions reducing PhenoAge in young adults is sparse.

### 3.9 Specific conditions associated with elevated PhenoAge in young adults

Not a diagnostic test, but accelerated PhenoAge in a 20-year-old should prompt screening for:
- Chronic inflammation (autoimmunity, IBD, periodontitis, untreated H. pylori, occult infection)
- Metabolic syndrome / insulin resistance / NAFLD
- Anemia (iron-deficiency, hemolytic, B12/folate deficiency)
- Smoking, vaping, heavy alcohol use
- Renal dysfunction (rare at 20 but possible)
- Untreated obstructive sleep apnea
- Eating disorder / undernutrition (low albumin, low lymphocytes)
- Recent acute illness that hasn't fully resolved
