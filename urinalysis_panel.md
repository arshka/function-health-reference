# Urinalysis Panel — Comprehensive Reference

> Audience: 20-year-old male, Function Health blood test interpretation. The Function Health basic membership includes a complete urinalysis (macroscopic, dipstick chemistry, and microscopic sediment). Microalbumin (urine albumin) is covered separately under the **Kidney panel** and is only briefly cross-referenced here.

This document covers ~19 urinalysis markers in three sections:

1. **Macroscopic** — color, appearance
2. **Dipstick chemistry** — specific gravity, pH, glucose, ketones, bilirubin, occult blood, protein, nitrite, leukocyte esterase
3. **Microscopic** — RBC, WBC, bacteria, squamous epithelial cells, hyaline casts, calcium oxalate crystals, yeast, amorphous sediment

For each marker: what it is and indicates, methodology, normal vs abnormal, common false positives/negatives, reference ranges, conditions flagged, confounders. The cross-cutting context section at the end pulls together collection technique, vitamin C interference, AUA 2020 microhematuria guideline (Barocas et al.), and IDSA 2019 asymptomatic bacteriuria guideline (Nicolle et al.).

---

## How a Urinalysis is Performed

A complete urinalysis has three phases performed on a single specimen, ideally a clean-catch midstream first-morning void:

1. **Macroscopic / physical**: visual inspection for color and clarity (appearance/turbidity).
2. **Dipstick / dry chemistry**: a multi-pad reagent strip dipped briefly in urine. Each pad contains chemistry that produces a colorimetric or photometric readout for a specific analyte (specific gravity, pH, glucose, ketones, bilirubin, urobilinogen, blood, protein, nitrite, leukocyte esterase). Readings are taken at manufacturer-specified intervals; modern labs use automated reflectance photometers rather than visual reading. Per *StatPearls Urinalysis* and Krishnan et al.'s 2019 review of dry-chemistry assays, dipstick chemistry is fast, cheap, and sensitive but has predictable interferences (highly buffered urine, ascorbic acid, hemoglobin, drugs, dilution).
3. **Microscopic**: a centrifuged or flow-cytometry-based examination of the sediment for cells, casts, crystals, bacteria, yeast, and amorphous material.

**Specimen handling matters enormously.** Urine should be examined within ~1 hour at room temperature, or refrigerated at 4°C and examined within ~4–24 hours. Storage delay causes: pH rises (urea-splitting bacterial overgrowth), bacteria multiply, casts dissolve in alkaline urine, RBCs and WBCs lyse, glucose is consumed by bacteria, ketones evaporate, bilirubin is photo-oxidized, and crystals (especially amorphous urates and amorphous phosphates and calcium oxalate) form *de novo* — this is one of the most common pitfalls in interpretation.

# Section 1 — Macroscopic

## 1. Color, Urine

**What it is.** The color of urine reflects (a) urochrome concentration (the main endogenous pigment, derived from heme catabolism), (b) hydration state, and (c) pigments from foods, drugs, or pathology. Per Foot and Fraser's *BMJ* urinalysis review and the *Harvard Health* color guide, urine color spans pale yellow → straw → amber → dark amber as concentration rises, with a wide differential for off-spectrum colors.

**Methodology.** Visual inspection against a white background. Newer labs may report machine-derived color codes from the urinalysis instrument.

**Normal.** Pale yellow to dark amber; no specific reference range — interpreted in clinical context.

**Spectrum and what abnormal colors indicate:**

- **Pale / colorless (yellow → almost clear).** Overhydration, recent diuretic, diabetes insipidus, primary polydipsia. With a low specific gravity (<1.005) and large urine volume, suspect DI. With a fixed SG ~1.010 across samples, suspect isosthenuria from CKD.
- **Dark amber / "tea-colored".** Concentrated urine from dehydration; can also signal bilirubinuria or myoglobinuria (see brown/cola).
- **Red / pink.** Differential is broad and clinically critical:
  - **True hematuria** — UTI, stones, glomerulonephritis (IgA nephropathy classic in young men post-URI), bladder/kidney malignancy (rare under 35), trauma, exercise, polycystic kidney disease, sickle cell trait, thin basement membrane disease.
  - **Beeturia** — beet ingestion. Per *StatPearls Beeturia* (Sandhu and Kerry), pink-red urine occurs in ~10–14% of the population after beet ingestion, more frequent with iron deficiency or malabsorption. Benign, dipstick-blood negative.
  - **Drugs** — rifampin (also orange), phenytoin, phenazopyridine (orange-red), senna, ibuprofen (rare), deferasirox.
  - **Porphyria** — porphyria cutanea tarda urine is red-brown in daylight, may darken on light exposure ("port-wine urine"). Confirm with quantitative urine porphyrins.
  - **Myoglobinuria / hemoglobinuria** — see brown/cola.
  - **Food dyes** — beets, blackberries, rhubarb, artificial dyes.
- **Brown / cola / black.** Severe dehydration, conjugated bilirubin (bilirubinuria — frothy and beer-colored), myoglobinuria (rhabdomyolysis), hemoglobinuria (intravascular hemolysis), alkaptonuria (urine darkens on standing — homogentisic acid), melanuria (advanced melanoma), drugs (metronidazole, nitrofurantoin, methyldopa, levodopa, methocarbamol).
- **Orange.** Rifampin, phenazopyridine (Pyridium/Azo — vivid orange that masks dipstick chemistry; see "Confounders" below), high-dose B-complex vitamins (especially riboflavin), warfarin, dehydration with high urochrome, sulfasalazine, carrots/carotene in extreme intake.
- **Green / blue.** Per Tan et al. (*PMC* 2024 case review of blue-green urine) and Foot/Fraser:
  - **Pseudomonas aeruginosa UTI** — pyocyanin pigment; usually with pyuria and symptoms.
  - **Methylene blue** — found in some urinary tract analgesic combos (Uribel, Urogesic Blue).
  - **Propofol** — extra-hepatic metabolite, benign, <1% incidence.
  - **Amitriptyline** — blue-green tinge in some patients.
  - **Indigo carmine, indomethacin, cimetidine, promethazine, triamterene.**
  - **Biliverdin** — rare biliary stasis state.
  - **Familial benign hypercalcemia ("blue diaper syndrome")** — pediatric.
- **Milky-white.** Chyluria (lymphatic-urinary fistula, classically filariasis), pyuria with dense pus, lipiduria from nephrotic syndrome, propofol metabolites, severe phosphate or urate crystalluria.

**Confounders / artifacts.**
- Dehydration alone produces dark amber that can be confused with pathology. Re-test after rehydration.
- Many drugs cause benign discoloration that resolves within 24–48 h after stopping; phenazopyridine in particular requires explicit documentation, because its orange pigment can falsely alter dipstick color readings for blood, leukocyte esterase, nitrite, glucose, ketones, protein, and bilirubin (per JAMA-Csako and *Dr. Oracle*).
- Photo-oxidation: bilirubin in urine left on a counter degrades to biliverdin (green) within hours.
- Specimen left to stand becomes more amber as urea-splitting bacteria multiply.

**Young-male notes.** A sudden episode of red/cola urine in a young man, particularly 24–72 h after pharyngitis or URI, is the classic presentation of **IgA nephropathy** (Berger disease) — the most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide. This contrasts with post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis, which appears 1–3 weeks after strep pharyngitis, not concurrently.

**Sources:** *Harvard Health* Red/Brown/Green Urine; *StatPearls Beeturia* (Sandhu & Kerry, 2023); Tan et al. *PMC* 2024 ("Diagnostic Approach to Abnormal Urine Colors"); Foot & Fraser *BMJ* urinalysis review; *Medscape* Urinalysis Reference Range.

## 2. Appearance, Urine

**What it is.** Clarity of the urine — graded clear / slightly cloudy / cloudy / turbid. Reflects suspended particulate matter: cells, crystals, casts, bacteria, mucus, lipids, sperm, or contamination.

**Methodology.** Visual inspection; some automated analyzers report nephelometric turbidity values.

**Normal.** Clear (transparent). Note: slightly cloudy first-morning urine can be entirely normal due to mucus and a few epithelial cells.

**Causes of cloudy / turbid urine:**

- **Phosphate precipitation in alkaline urine.** Per *AAFP urinalysis review* (Simerville et al. 2005): "cloudy urine often is a result of precipitated phosphate crystals in alkaline urine." Common after a vegetarian/vegetable-heavy meal, alkaline urine of any cause, and in refrigerated samples. **Benign.**
- **Pyuria** — UTI, urethritis, prostatitis (heavy WBCs make urine milky).
- **Crystalluria** — amorphous urates (acidic urine), amorphous phosphates (alkaline urine), uric acid, calcium oxalate.
- **Hematuria** — heavy bleeding can opacify.
- **Bacteriuria.**
- **Lipiduria / chyluria** — milky white, classic in nephrotic syndrome (Maltese cross fatty casts on polarized light) or filariasis.
- **Mucus** — generally benign in small amounts; large amounts can come from cervical secretions, urethral inflammation, or cystitis.
- **Contamination** — vaginal discharge, feces, talc, semen.

**Confounders.**
- Refrigeration almost always increases turbidity by precipitating amorphous urates or phosphates and calcium oxalate. Re-warming to room temperature can re-dissolve them.
- Sample left to stand at room temperature accumulates bacteria and pH drift, causing artifactual turbidity.

**Cloudy urine in healthy young people is most often benign phosphate or urate precipitation, especially after refrigeration or a high-vegetable meal.** A repeat clean-catch at room temperature usually clears the picture.

**Sources:** *AAFP* Simerville et al. 2005; *Cleveland Clinic* Cloudy Urine; *Medscape* Urinalysis.

# Section 2 — Dipstick Chemistry

## 3. Specific Gravity

**What it is.** The ratio of urine density to water density, a proxy for urine concentration (and indirectly, the kidney's tubular concentrating ability). Correlates with urine osmolality but less precisely — solutes such as glucose, protein, and contrast media disproportionately increase SG without proportionally affecting osmolality.

**Methodology.**
- **Refractometry** (most accurate, lab gold standard).
- **Dipstick** — uses a polyelectrolyte that releases protons in proportion to ionic strength of urine, with a pH indicator that shifts color. *Limitations:* dipstick SG is insensitive to non-ionic solutes (glucose, urea), so it underestimates SG when these are present. Reads in 0.005 increments only.
- Urinometer is largely obsolete.

**Reference range.** 1.005–1.030 is typical; well-hydrated young adults usually 1.010–1.020. Range can extend 1.001–1.035 with extreme hydration / dehydration.

**Interpretation:**

- **Low (<1.010)** — dilute urine.
  - Overhydration / large fluid intake.
  - Diuretics.
  - Diabetes insipidus (central or nephrogenic) — classically SG <1.005 with high urine output (>3 L/day) and rising serum sodium when water-restricted.
  - Primary (psychogenic) polydipsia — SG <1.010 in a euvolemic patient with normal/low serum sodium.
  - CKD with loss of concentrating ability.
- **High (>1.025)** — concentrated.
  - Dehydration.
  - SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate ADH).
  - Decreased renal perfusion (heart failure, hypovolemia).
  - **Glucosuria** — each 1 g/dL of glucose adds ~0.004 to refractometer SG (medical algorithms / Clinical Methods correction). 1% glucose → 0.004 increment.
  - Heavy proteinuria (~0.003 per 1 g/dL).
  - Radiographic contrast media — IV contrast can drive SG above 1.040, well above what kidney concentration alone can produce. SG >1.035 with recent contrast should be regarded as artifact and the sample considered uninterpretable for tubular function (Pranger 1980, *Effects of radiographic contrast media on urinalysis*).
- **Fixed at 1.010 (isosthenuria).** Per *Medscape* and *IRIS Kidney*: SG fixed near 1.010 across multiple samples regardless of hydration indicates the kidney can neither dilute nor concentrate the glomerular ultrafiltrate — characteristic of **advanced CKD with tubulointerstitial damage**, typically once ~2/3 of nephron function has been lost. Pair with serum creatinine, eGFR, and urine osmolality to confirm.

**Confounders.**
- Glucose, protein, contrast media, mannitol, dextran — all raise SG artifactually relative to true osmolality.
- Highly alkaline urine (pH >7) can give falsely low dipstick SG by ~0.005.
- Refrigerated samples may need warming before refractometry.

**Young-male context.** A fit, well-hydrated young adult should typically run 1.010–1.025. A first-morning void around 1.020–1.025 is normal and concentrated; values >1.030 most often reflect dehydration. Persistently <1.005 with polyuria warrants investigation for diabetes insipidus.

**Sources:** *Medscape* Urine Specific Gravity (Khurana 2023); IRIS Kidney; *Clinical Methods* (NCBI Bookshelf NBK302); Pranger 1980.

## 4. pH, Urine

**What it is.** A measurement of urine acid-base balance. The kidney is one of the body's main long-term acid excretion routes (the other being CO2 via the lungs).

**Methodology.** Dipstick pad uses a double indicator system (methyl red + bromothymol blue) that yields colors from orange (acidic) through green to blue (alkaline). Reads in 0.5–1.0 unit increments, range 4.5–9.0. Lab pH meter is more precise.

**Reference range.** 4.5–8.0; typical young-adult range 5.5–6.5 (slightly acidic).

**Acidic urine (pH <5.5):**
- High-protein / meat diet (sulfur amino acids).
- Ketoacidosis — DKA, alcoholic ketoacidosis, prolonged fasting/keto diet, starvation.
- Respiratory acidosis (chronic lung disease, retention of CO2).
- Chronic diarrhea (loss of bicarbonate).
- Diabetes mellitus (uncontrolled).
- Drugs — ammonium chloride, methenamine, mandelic acid.
- Uric acid stones favor pH <5.5.

**Alkaline urine (pH >7):**
- Vegetarian / high-vegetable, citrus, dairy diet.
- UTI with **urea-splitting organisms** — *Proteus mirabilis* (100% urease+), *Klebsiella*, *Pseudomonas*, *Ureaplasma urealyticum*. Persistently pH >7 with recurrent infection should raise suspicion for **struvite (magnesium-ammonium-phosphate) stones**, which form only above pH ~7.2 (per *StatPearls Struvite Stones* and Bichler et al. *Kidney Int* 1978).
- **Renal tubular acidosis (RTA)**, especially **type 1 (distal RTA)** — paradoxically alkaline urine (pH >5.5) despite systemic metabolic acidosis, because the distal tubule cannot secrete H+. Type 2 (proximal RTA, Fanconi syndrome) has acidic urine once filtered bicarbonate is exhausted.
- Vomiting (loss of HCl → metabolic alkalosis → urine HCO3 wasting).
- Aged / stale specimen — urea-splitting bacteria multiply over hours, raising pH to 8–9. **Always interpret an alkaline pH cautiously if the sample is more than 1–2 hours old.**

**Confounders / artifacts.**
- Stale specimen (most common false-alkaline cause).
- Heavily buffered/alkaline urine can leach reagent off other dipstick pads, producing false-positive protein readings.
- Ammonium urate crystals form in alkaline urine.

**Young-male context.** Meat-heavy or keto diets push pH into the 5.0–5.5 range and favor uric acid/calcium oxalate stones. Persistent alkaline urine (>7) without diet explanation is unusual in a young man and should prompt evaluation for occult UTI (especially Proteus), distal RTA, or recurrent vomiting.

**Sources:** *StatPearls Struvite Stones* (Das et al. 2024); *Medscape* Urinalysis; *AAFP* Simerville 2005.

## 5. Glucose, Urine

**What it is.** Glucose in urine. Normally absent because the proximal tubule reabsorbs essentially 100% of filtered glucose via SGLT2 (high-capacity) and SGLT1 (high-affinity).

**Methodology.** Dipstick uses **glucose oxidase / peroxidase** chromogen reaction — glucose oxidase converts glucose to gluconic acid + H2O2, which then oxidizes a chromogen via peroxidase. Highly **specific for glucose**, does not detect fructose or galactose.

**Reference range / normal.** Negative.

**Renal threshold.** Plasma glucose ~180 mg/dL — once exceeded, the proximal tubule's reabsorptive capacity is overwhelmed and glucose spills into urine. The threshold is somewhat lower in pregnancy (renal blood flow up, transporter expression unchanged) and in some young, otherwise healthy individuals.

**Causes of glucosuria:**

- **Hyperglycemic glucosuria:**
  - Type 1 / type 2 diabetes mellitus, especially uncontrolled.
  - Stress hyperglycemia (acute illness, steroids, sepsis).
  - Acromegaly, Cushing, pheochromocytoma.
  - Pancreatitis.
- **Normoglycemic ("renal") glucosuria** — glucose in urine despite normal blood glucose:
  - **SGLT2 inhibitors** (canagliflozin, dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) — pharmacologic glucosuria is the mechanism; expect 50–80 g/day of glycosuria with SGLT2i.
  - **Familial renal glucosuria** — *SLC5A2* (SGLT2) loss-of-function mutations; benign isolated glucosuria.
  - **Fanconi syndrome** — generalized proximal tubule dysfunction → glucosuria, aminoaciduria, phosphaturia, bicarbonaturia (proximal RTA), uricosuria. Causes include cystinosis (kids), heavy metals (lead, cadmium), tenofovir, ifosfamide, multiple myeloma (with light-chain proximal toxicity), Wilson disease.
  - **Pregnancy** — physiologic lowering of renal glucose threshold.

**False positives.**
- Strong oxidizing cleaning agents (bleach) on collection containers.
- Some peroxidase contaminants.
- Phenazopyridine (color interference).

**False negatives.**
- **Vitamin C (ascorbic acid).** Per Brigden et al. (*Ann Clin Lab Sci* 2015) and Nagel et al. (multicenter, *Clinical Biochemistry* 2016): with high-dose vitamin C in urine, **42.3% of dipstick glucose tests with serum glucose >180 mg/dL gave false-negative results**. This is one of the largest interferences in dipstick chemistry. Relevant for anyone taking high-dose vitamin C supplements (>500 mg/day).
- High specific gravity / dilute urine.
- Bacterial overgrowth in old samples consumes glucose.
- Ketones at very high concentration.

**Young-male notes.** A single positive urine glucose in a young, asymptomatic male should be confirmed with a fasting plasma glucose, HbA1c, or random glucose. If glucose is positive and blood glucose is normal, consider familial renal glucosuria (benign), Fanconi syndrome workup (ammonia, phosphate, bicarbonate, urate, urine amino acids), or SGLT2 inhibitor use.

**Sources:** *StatPearls Glycosuria* (Khan et al.); Calado et al. *PubMed* 2009 (familial renal glucosuria); Brigden et al. 2015; Nagel et al. 2016.

## 6. Ketones, Urine

**What it is.** Detection of ketone bodies — products of fatty acid oxidation when carbohydrate availability is low. Three ketones circulate in roughly 1:1:0.1 ratios in stable fasting and ~10:1:rare in DKA: **β-hydroxybutyrate (β-OHB) ≫ acetoacetate (AcAc) ≫ acetone**.

**Methodology.** **Sodium nitroprusside reaction** (1908) — reagent reacts with ketone groups to produce a purple color. Critically, this reaction:

- **Detects acetoacetate strongly.**
- **Detects acetone weakly (only in presence of glycine).**
- **Does NOT detect β-hydroxybutyrate at all** — even though β-OHB is the dominant ketone in DKA (10:1 ratio over AcAc).

This is the single most important limitation of urine ketones. Per *Diabetes Care* (Misra & Oliver 2015) and Smith et al. *Acad Emerg Med* 2008: serum or capillary β-OHB measurement is preferred for diagnosing and managing DKA. Negative urine ketones do **not** rule out DKA. Conversely, during DKA recovery, β-OHB is oxidized back to AcAc, so urine ketones can paradoxically rise even as the patient improves clinically — **rising urine ketones during treatment of DKA can falsely suggest worsening**.

**Reference range / normal.** Negative or trace.

**Positive findings indicate:**
- **Diabetic ketoacidosis** (urine + plasma glucose >250, anion gap acidosis).
- **Alcoholic ketoacidosis.**
- **Starvation / prolonged fasting.**
- **Ketogenic / very-low-carb diet** — mild ketonuria is expected and benign on a ketogenic diet (typically small to moderate, not large).
- **Vomiting / hyperemesis** (especially pregnancy).
- **Prolonged intense exercise / endurance events.**
- **Isopropyl alcohol ingestion** (metabolized to acetone).
- **Uncontrolled hyperthyroidism, fever** (hypermetabolic states).
- **Glycogen storage disease.**

**False positives.**
- Highly pigmented urine (phenazopyridine, beets).
- Drugs containing free sulfhydryl groups — captopril, mesna, **N-acetylcysteine** (very common false-positive cause), penicillamine, levodopa metabolites.
- Highly concentrated urine.
- Acidic urine (mild over-read).

**False negatives.**
- **Predominantly β-OHB ketosis** (most DKA, alcoholic, starvation) — the urine test underestimates true ketosis.
- Old / improperly stored sample (acetoacetate volatilizes).
- Dilute urine.

**Clinical pearl: urine glucose POSITIVE + urine ketones POSITIVE = workup for DKA** (especially with symptoms or known diabetes). Urine ketones POSITIVE alone with negative glucose, in a young person on a ketogenic diet or after a long fast, is benign nutritional ketosis.

**Sources:** Misra & Oliver *Diabetes Care* 2022; Smith et al. *Acad Emerg Med* 2008; *Medscape* DKA Workup; *StatPearls Urinalysis*.

## 7. Bilirubin, Urine

**What it is.** Detection of **conjugated (direct) bilirubin** in urine. Unconjugated bilirubin is bound to albumin and not filtered. Once conjugated by the liver, bilirubin becomes water-soluble and a small fraction can spill into urine if conjugated bilirubin rises in plasma.

**Methodology.** Diazo coupling reaction (Ehrlich-type) — bilirubin couples with a diazonium salt under acidic conditions to form a brown to red-violet azo dye on the dipstick pad.

**Reference range / normal.** Negative. Bilirubinuria is **always abnormal** in adults.

**Positive findings indicate (always conjugated hyperbilirubinemia):**
- **Hepatocellular disease** — viral hepatitis (A, B, C, E), alcoholic hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury (acetaminophen, isoniazid, methotrexate, anabolic steroids).
- **Cholestasis / biliary obstruction** — choledocholithiasis, primary biliary cholangitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, pancreatic head mass, parasitic infection (liver flukes).
- **Cirrhosis.**
- **Dubin-Johnson syndrome** (benign hereditary defect in conjugated bilirubin excretion).

Per *StatPearls Bilirubinuria* (Sticova & Jirsa), urine bilirubin can appear **before clinical jaundice is detectable** (jaundice requires plasma total bilirubin >2–3 mg/dL but urine dipstick can detect at lower thresholds). It is therefore an early sign of hepatobiliary pathology.

**Pattern with urobilinogen (sometimes co-reported):**
- Bilirubin (+) and urobilinogen normal or low → **biliary obstruction** (bile not reaching gut, no urobilinogen formed).
- Bilirubin (+) and urobilinogen high → **hepatocellular disease** (impaired liver recycling of urobilinogen).
- Bilirubin (–) and urobilinogen high → **hemolysis** or early hepatocellular dysfunction.

**False positives.**
- Phenazopyridine, indican, chlorpromazine — discoloration may cause false reading.
- Highly pigmented urine.

**False negatives.**
- **Vitamin C** (ascorbic acid) — significant false-negative cause; per Nagel 2016, ascorbic acid is among the worst offenders for bilirubin dipstick.
- **Light exposure** — bilirubin photo-oxidizes to biliverdin in minutes to hours; specimens left exposed to light underestimate.
- Long storage (degradation).
- Nitrites at high concentration.

**Sources:** *StatPearls Bilirubinuria*; *MedlinePlus* Urobilinogen; Nagel 2016.

## 8. Occult Blood, Urine

**What it is.** Detection of **heme** — present in intact RBCs, free hemoglobin, and **myoglobin**. Cannot distinguish among these on dipstick alone; correlation with microscopy is essential.

**Methodology.** Dipstick uses the **pseudoperoxidase activity of heme** — heme catalyzes oxidation of a chromogen (tetramethylbenzidine or similar) by an organic peroxide, producing a green-blue color. Detects 1–5 RBCs/µL or 0.015–0.062 mg/dL hemoglobin.

**Reference range / normal.** Negative or trace.

**Interpretation of dipstick + microscopy combination:**

| Dipstick blood | RBCs on microscopy | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Positive | Many RBCs | True hematuria — workup as below |
| Positive | No / few RBCs | **Hemoglobinuria** (intravascular hemolysis) or **myoglobinuria** (rhabdomyolysis) |
| Negative | RBCs present | Recent RBC lysis in alkaline / dilute urine, ascorbic acid interference, nitrite high |
| Positive | Positive only on second sample | Menstrual contamination, march hematuria, recent UTI |

**Causes of true hematuria (RBCs present):**

- **UTI / hemorrhagic cystitis.**
- **Stones** — ureteral or kidney; classic colicky flank pain + gross hematuria.
- **Glomerulonephritis** — IgA nephropathy (Berger disease) is the **classic cause of post-URI gross hematuria in young men**, with episodes 24–72 h after pharyngitis, often with loin pain. Other glomerulonephritides: post-streptococcal (1–3 weeks post-infection), Alport syndrome, lupus nephritis, ANCA vasculitis, anti-GBM. Look for **dysmorphic RBCs / acanthocytes ≥5%** on phase-contrast microscopy and red-cell casts.
- **Thin basement membrane nephropathy (benign familial hematuria)** — persistent dysmorphic microhematuria, normal renal function, family history.
- **Polycystic kidney disease** — episodes of gross hematuria, palpable kidneys, family history.
- **Sickle cell trait or disease** — papillary necrosis, microscopic to gross hematuria.
- **Bladder/kidney malignancy** — uncommon in <35yo without smoking history; per AUA 2020 microhematuria guideline (Barocas et al.), risk-stratified workup is recommended. A young, never-smoker male with low-risk microscopic hematuria can often be evaluated with repeat UA and renal ultrasound rather than full cystoscopy + CT urogram.
- **Trauma** — direct GU trauma, instrumentation.
- **Exercise / "march hematuria"** — present in ~20–25% of runners after long runs (per Urakami et al. 2019). Mechanisms: footstrike hemolysis (releases free hemoglobin), bladder mucosal contusions from posterior wall striking the fixed bladder neck during running, renal ischemia, nutcracker phenomenon. Resolves within 48–72 h. **Always retest hematuria 48–72 h after a hard workout to exclude exercise-induced cause before initiating workup.**
- **Menstruation** — major confounder in women.
- **Anticoagulants** — may unmask underlying lesion; do NOT attribute hematuria to anticoagulation alone, per AUA 2020.
- **Schistosomiasis** — endemic exposure history.

**False positives.**
- **Myoglobinuria** (rhabdomyolysis) — heme from muscle. Classic presentation: positive dipstick blood, **few or no RBCs on microscopy**, dark "tea-colored" urine, with elevated CK and serum myoglobin. Note: per Hatamabadi et al. (*ScienceDirect* 2013) and Bagley et al., this classic dipstick-positive/microscopy-negative pattern is only 41–55% sensitive for rhabdomyolysis; a normal dipstick does not rule it out.
- **Hemoglobinuria** — intravascular hemolysis (transfusion reaction, malaria, G6PD crisis, mechanical heart valve, paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria).
- **Menstrual contamination.**
- **Microbial peroxidases** (UTI with certain organisms).
- **Highly concentrated urine.**
- Hypochlorite contamination of container.

**False negatives.**
- **Ascorbic acid (vitamin C)** — per Nagel 2016, ~10% false-negative rate for blood dipstick in vitamin-C-positive specimens. **High-dose vitamin C (>500 mg/day) is a real-world issue.**
- **High specific gravity / glucose / protein** — slight depression of sensitivity.
- Very acid urine.
- Captopril.
- Old sample with RBC lysis (paradoxically — should still detect free hemoglobin, but if heavily diluted, may miss).
- Nitrite at high concentration.

**Per AUA 2020 guideline (Barocas et al. *J Urol*): microscopic hematuria is defined as ≥3 RBCs/HPF on properly collected specimen. Dipstick positivity alone is NOT sufficient — requires confirmation by microscopy.** Risk stratification:

- **Low risk** (ALL of: <40yo women / <50yo men; never-smoker; 3–10 RBC/HPF; no risk factors): repeat UA in 6 months OR cystoscopy + renal ultrasound.
- **Intermediate risk**: cystoscopy + renal ultrasound.
- **High risk** (>60yo, >30 pack-year smoking, >25 RBC/HPF, gross hematuria history, occupational/chemical exposure, prior GU disease): cystoscopy + CT urography.

**Young-male notes.** A 20yo male with isolated microscopic hematuria after exercise, on no medication, with no urinary symptoms — most likely benign exercise-induced or thin basement membrane disease. Repeat the urinalysis 48–72 h after avoiding exercise. If persistent, AUA low-risk pathway applies; if dysmorphic RBCs / red-cell casts / proteinuria appear, refer to nephrology for IgA nephropathy / glomerulonephritis workup. Gross hematuria 24–72 h post-URI is the IgA nephropathy classic — even if it self-resolves, deserves nephrology evaluation.

**Sources:** AUA/SUFU 2020 Microhematuria Guideline (Barocas et al. *J Urol* 2020 + 2025 update); Urakami et al. *IJU Case Reports* 2019; *AAFP* Rhabdomyolysis (Sauret et al. 2002); Moreno et al. *AJKD* 2022 (glomerular hematuria review).

## 9. Protein, Urine

**What it is.** Detection of urinary protein. Normal kidney filters very little protein and reabsorbs most of what it filters; healthy adult excretes <150 mg/day total protein, <30 mg/day albumin.

**Methodology.** Dipstick uses **"protein error of indicators"** — at constant pH (3), tetrabromophenol blue changes color (yellow → green → blue) as protein binds, even though pH is buffered. **The pad is much more sensitive to albumin** than to globulins or low-molecular-weight proteins. Specifically, dipsticks **miss Bence-Jones proteins** (light chains in multiple myeloma) and tubular proteins (β2-microglobulin) — these require sulfosalicylic acid (SSA) precipitation or immunoassay.

**Reference range / normal.** Negative or trace; quantitatively <150 mg/day total protein, <30 mg/g albumin-creatinine ratio.

**Dipstick semi-quantitative categories:**
- Trace: ~15–30 mg/dL
- 1+: ~30–100 mg/dL
- 2+: ~100–300 mg/dL
- 3+: ~300–1000 mg/dL
- 4+: >1000 mg/dL (nephrotic-range)

Note: dipstick is concentration-dependent — a 1+ in dilute urine (SG 1.005) may represent more protein loss than a 1+ in concentrated urine. Always confirm with **albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR)** or 24-hour urine protein.

**Causes of proteinuria:**

- **Transient (functional) proteinuria** — usually <1 g/day, resolves within weeks:
  - **Vigorous exercise** (resolves 24–48 h).
  - **Fever.**
  - **Acute illness, dehydration.**
  - **Cold exposure.**
  - **Emotional stress.**
  - **Heart failure (acute).**
- **Orthostatic (postural) proteinuria** — see young-male notes below.
- **Glomerular disease (most common pathologic):**
  - Diabetic nephropathy.
  - Hypertensive nephrosclerosis.
  - **Glomerulonephritis** — IgA nephropathy, FSGS, minimal change disease, membranous, lupus, post-infectious, ANCA vasculitis, anti-GBM.
  - **Nephrotic syndrome** (>3.5 g/day) — minimal change (kids), FSGS, membranous (adults), diabetic, amyloid.
- **Tubulointerstitial disease** — pyelonephritis, NSAID nephropathy, lithium, heavy metals, sarcoidosis.
- **Overflow proteinuria** — multiple myeloma (Bence-Jones), hemoglobinuria, myoglobinuria. **Dipstick missed light chains.**
- **Pre-eclampsia** (pregnancy).
- **CKD of any cause.**
- **Reflux nephropathy.**

**False positives.**
- **Highly alkaline urine (pH >8)** — can leach buffer and trip indicator regardless of protein. Most common false-positive.
- **Highly concentrated urine.**
- **Phenazopyridine** color interference.
- **Chlorhexidine, quaternary ammonium compounds** in collection container.
- Gross hematuria (visible blood).
- IV iodinated contrast.

**False negatives.**
- Dilute urine.
- **Light-chain (Bence-Jones) proteinuria** — dipstick essentially blind; requires SSA or urine protein electrophoresis.
- Tubular proteinuria.

**Young-male context: ORTHOSTATIC PROTEINURIA.**

Per *StatPearls Orthostatic Proteinuria* (Guthrie et al.), Wash U Kidney Center (Glassock 2007), and Cleveland Clinic: ~2–5% of young, thin adults — **especially adolescent and young-adult males** — exhibit isolated proteinuria when upright that resolves when supine. Total protein typically <1 g/day, never nephrotic-range. **Long-term follow-up over 20–50 years confirms benign course with normal renal function.**

**Diagnosis:**
1. Detect proteinuria on a random or daytime sample.
2. Have the patient empty bladder before bed (discard).
3. Collect **first-morning void** after at least 8 hours recumbent.
4. If first-morning UA is negative for protein (or ACR <30 mg/g), **diagnosis is orthostatic proteinuria. Benign. No further workup needed beyond annual recheck.**

If first-morning protein is also positive → not orthostatic; pursue full proteinuria workup (ACR, 24-hour urine protein, kidney function panel, possibly nephrology referral).

A 20yo male with 1+ protein on a daytime sample, normal kidney function, no hematuria, no edema, no hypertension — orthostatic proteinuria is far and away the most likely diagnosis and the appropriate next step is a first-morning void.

**Sources:** *StatPearls Orthostatic Proteinuria*; *AAFP* Carroll & Temte 2000; KDIGO 2024 CKD Guideline; Glassock 2007 Wash U Kidney.

## 10. Nitrite, Urine

**What it is.** Detection of nitrite, formed when bacteria reduce dietary nitrate (in urine) to nitrite via bacterial nitrate reductase enzyme.

**Methodology.** **Greiss reaction** — nitrite + sulfanilamide + diazonium → pink-red azo dye on the dipstick pad.

**Reference range / normal.** Negative.

**Positive indicates UTI with a nitrate-reducing organism.** Most enteric Gram-negative rods reduce nitrate:
- *Escherichia coli* (most common UTI cause)
- *Klebsiella pneumoniae*
- *Proteus mirabilis*
- *Enterobacter*
- *Citrobacter*

**Performance:** sensitivity ~45–60%, **specificity ~95%** — a positive result is highly meaningful, but a negative does not rule out UTI.

**False negatives (numerous, important):**
- **Organisms that don't reduce nitrate** — Gram-positives (*Staphylococcus saprophyticus*, *Enterococcus*, *Streptococcus*), *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Candida*. *S. saprophyticus* is the **second most common UTI organism in young, sexually active women** and is typically nitrite-negative.
- **Insufficient bladder dwell time** — bacteria need ~4 hours in the bladder to reduce enough nitrate for detection. Frequent voiding (in irritated bladder of a UTI) can give false negative.
- **Dietary nitrate insufficient** — vegetarian/varied diet usually has plenty; rare issue.
- **Ascorbic acid (vitamin C)** — at concentrations ≥50 mg/dL, can fully invalidate the nitrite test (Brigden 2015). Real-world relevance for high-dose vitamin C users.
- **Very dilute urine.**

**False positives.** Rare; very dark phenazopyridine-colored urine, contamination with old sample (bacterial overgrowth post-collection).

**Combined leukocyte esterase + nitrite for UTI screening:**
- Both positive → high probability UTI (PPV ~85% in symptomatic patients).
- Either positive → reasonable sensitivity (~70–90%) for UTI in symptomatic patients.
- Both negative → strong NPV (~90–95%) for ruling out UTI in low-prevalence settings.

Per the 2025 *Clinical Microbiology & Infection* meta-analysis, combined LE+nitrite has reasonable sensitivity but interpretation should always integrate symptoms.

**Sources:** *AAFP* Simerville 2005; Schmiemann et al. *PMC* 2010; *Clinical Microbiology & Infection* 2025 meta-analysis (older adults).

## 11. Leukocyte Esterase, Urine

**What it is.** Detection of an esterase enzyme released by **neutrophils** (and some macrophages, eosinophils, basophils) — a surrogate marker for pyuria.

**Methodology.** A pyrrole or indoxyl ester is hydrolyzed by leukocyte esterase; the product reacts with a diazonium salt to produce purple color.

**Reference range / normal.** Negative.

**Positive indicates pyuria.** Causes:
- **UTI** — bacterial cystitis, pyelonephritis, urethritis.
- **STI urethritis** — chlamydia, gonorrhea (often nitrite-negative because organisms don't reduce nitrate).
- **Vaginitis / cervicitis** — contamination by inflamed cells (relevant in women).
- **Prostatitis** in men (positive LE in midstream urine, especially with prostate massage).
- **Sterile pyuria** — see below.
- **Glomerulonephritis** — mild WBC elevation.
- **Renal stones** — inflammation around stone.
- **Tuberculosis (genitourinary)** — classic sterile pyuria cause.
- **Fungal UTI.**

**Sterile pyuria** (positive LE, negative routine bacterial culture):
- Recently treated UTI (within 2 weeks).
- Genitourinary TB.
- Chlamydia, gonorrhea, ureaplasma.
- Interstitial cystitis / painful bladder syndrome.
- NSAID-induced interstitial nephritis.
- Renal stones, foreign body.
- Bladder cancer (especially CIS).
- Schistosomiasis.
- Pregnancy.

**Performance.** Sensitivity ~72–96%, specificity ~78–90% for UTI (varies by population).

**False positives:**
- Vaginal/preputial contamination.
- Highly contaminated specimen with squamous cells.
- Trichomonas.
- Strenuous exercise (mild WBC elevation).
- Phenazopyridine.

**False negatives:**
- **High glucose, high specific gravity, high protein** — can suppress LE reading.
- **High ascorbic acid** (vitamin C) — ~8% false-negative rate per Nagel 2016.
- **Antibiotics** — cephalosporins, gentamicin, tetracyclines, nitrofurantoin can falsely depress LE.
- Lymphocyte-predominant inflammation (LE is neutrophil-specific) — chronic inflammation, viral, transplant rejection, lupus nephritis interstitial.
- Boric acid preservative if used in sample container.

**Sources:** *AAFP* Simerville 2005; *NEJM* Sterile Pyuria (Wise & Schlegel 2015); *StatPearls Sterile Pyuria*; Nagel 2016.

# Section 3 — Microscopic Examination

## 12. Red Blood Cells, Urine

**What it is.** Direct visualization of erythrocytes in centrifuged urine sediment.

**Methodology.** Centrifuged sediment examined under high-power field (HPF, 400×). Modern flow cytometry analyzers (Sysmex UF, iQ200) detect RBC count and morphology (dysmorphic vs isomorphic).

**Reference range / normal.** **0–2 RBCs/HPF** (some labs ≤3). **≥3 RBCs/HPF on a properly collected specimen = microscopic hematuria** per AUA 2020 (Barocas et al.).

**Morphology distinction (important):**
- **Isomorphic RBCs** (round, biconcave) → **non-glomerular** source: bladder, urethra, ureter, prostate, renal pelvis (stones, infection, tumor, trauma, exercise).
- **Dysmorphic RBCs**, especially **acanthocytes (G1 cells, ring-shaped with surface blebs)** → **glomerular** source. Per Moreno et al. (*AJKD* 2022) review: ≥5% acanthocytes carries 90–100% specificity for glomerular disease (IgA nephropathy, lupus, ANCA vasculitis, post-infectious GN, Alport, thin basement membrane).

**Workup of microscopic hematuria** (AUA 2020 risk-stratified):
- **Low-risk**: men <50yo, never-smoker, 3–10 RBCs/HPF, no other risk factors → repeat UA at 6 months OR cystoscopy + renal ultrasound.
- **Intermediate**: cystoscopy + renal ultrasound.
- **High-risk**: cystoscopy + CT urography.
- **Anyone with proteinuria, dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, elevated creatinine, hypertension** → **nephrology referral first**, regardless of risk category.

**Confounders:**
- Menstruation (defer test).
- Recent vigorous exercise (defer 48–72 h).
- Recent sexual activity.
- Trauma from instrumentation (Foley, recent biopsy).
- Old specimen — RBCs lyse in alkaline / dilute urine, leading to false-negative microscopy with positive dipstick blood.

**Young-male notes:**
1. **Exercise-induced ("march") hematuria** — repeat off-exercise.
2. **IgA nephropathy** — gross hematuria 24–72 h post-URI, dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts.
3. **Thin basement membrane disease** — persistent isolated dysmorphic microhematuria, family history.
4. **Stones** — colicky pain, isomorphic RBCs.
5. **UTI** — usually with LE+/nitrite+, symptoms.

**Sources:** AUA/SUFU 2020 Microhematuria Guideline (Barocas et al. *J Urol* 2020); Moreno et al. *AJKD* 2022; *StatPearls Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy*.

## 13. White Blood Cells, Urine

**What it is.** Direct count of leukocytes in urine sediment.

**Methodology.** Centrifuged sediment, HPF microscopy or automated flow cytometry.

**Reference range / normal.** **0–5 WBCs/HPF.** **≥10 WBCs/HPF = pyuria** by most labs.

**Causes of pyuria:**
- **Bacterial UTI / pyelonephritis** (with nitrite+/LE+, symptoms).
- **STI urethritis** — chlamydia, gonorrhea, mycoplasma.
- **Prostatitis** (in men) — pyuria with prostatic-fluid expression after massage.
- **Vaginitis** (women, contamination).
- **Sterile pyuria** — see leukocyte esterase section.
- **Renal stones.**
- **Glomerulonephritis** (modest pyuria).
- **Interstitial nephritis** (drugs — NSAIDs, PPIs, antibiotics).
- **Genitourinary tuberculosis.**
- **Bladder/upper tract neoplasm.**

**WBC casts** (not asked but relevant) → **acute pyelonephritis** or **interstitial nephritis** — pinpoints renal origin of pyuria.

**Confounders:**
- Vaginal contamination → high WBCs + high squamous cells = repeat with proper clean-catch.
- Old specimen — WBCs lyse, falsely lowering count.
- Lubricants, antiseptics.

**Sources:** *AAFP* Simerville 2005; *NEJM* Sterile Pyuria.

## 14. Bacteria, Urine

**What it is.** Direct visualization of bacteria in centrifuged urine sediment, often graded few / moderate / many per HPF.

**Methodology.** Centrifuged sediment, HPF; Gram stain optional; automated flow analyzers.

**Reference range / normal.** **None to few/HPF.**

**Interpretation:**
- **Few bacteria, no pyuria, asymptomatic, high squamous cells** → **contamination**. Repeat clean-catch.
- **Many bacteria + pyuria + LE+ + nitrite+ + symptoms (dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain)** → **UTI**. Treat empirically per local antibiogram.
- **Many bacteria + pyuria + asymptomatic** → **asymptomatic bacteriuria (ASB)** — usually NOT treated.

**IDSA 2019 ASB Guideline (Nicolle et al. *Clin Infect Dis* 2019):**

ASB is defined as ≥10⁵ CFU/mL of one organism on culture (or ≥10² CFU/mL with same organism on two consecutive voids in women) **without symptoms attributable to UTI**.

**Do NOT screen or treat ASB in:**
- Healthy non-pregnant women (pre- or postmenopausal).
- Adults with diabetes.
- Older community-dwelling adults (functionally impaired or not).
- Long-term care residents.
- Patients with spinal cord injury.
- Patients with indwelling catheters (with limited exceptions).
- Solid-organ transplant recipients (≥1 month post-transplant).
- Children.

**DO screen and treat ASB in:**
- **Pregnant women** (associated with pyelonephritis and preterm birth).
- **Patients undergoing endoscopic urologic procedures with mucosal trauma.**

**Why no treatment?** Antibiotics for ASB do not reduce symptomatic UTI rates and increase antimicrobial resistance, *Clostridioides difficile* risk, and adverse drug events.

**Confounders:**
- Vaginal/preputial contamination.
- Old specimen — overgrowth in transit (single most common cause of "many bacteria" in healthy people).
- Refrigeration delay.

**Young-male context.** UTI in a young man without risk factors (catheter, instrumentation, anatomic anomaly) is uncommon and warrants further evaluation (especially recurrent). A single positive bacteria count without symptoms or pyuria in a young male is most likely contamination — repeat clean-catch.

**Sources:** Nicolle et al. *Clin Infect Dis* 2019 (IDSA ASB Guideline); *StatPearls Asymptomatic Bacteriuria*.

## 15. Squamous Epithelial Cells

**What it is.** Large, flat, polygonal cells with central nucleus — line the **distal urethra, perineum, vaginal introitus, prepuce**. NOT renal in origin.

**Methodology.** Centrifuged sediment, HPF.

**Reference range / normal.** 0–5/HPF.

**Interpretation:**
- **High counts (>5/HPF) = sample contamination** by skin/genital epithelium.
- **>15–20/HPF "many" → repeat with clean-catch.** Bacteria, WBCs, and LE are all less reliable in this setting; the sample should be re-collected before any UTI treatment decision.

**NOT pathologic per se.** Squamous cells in urine do not indicate kidney disease. They are simply a quality-control signal.

**Other epithelial cell types** (relevant to contrast):
- **Renal tubular epithelial cells** — small, eccentric nucleus; pathologic; suggest acute tubular necrosis, acute interstitial nephritis, glomerulonephritis.
- **Transitional (urothelial) cells** — intermediate size; some normal sloughing; many or atypical → bladder/upper tract pathology.

**Confounders:**
- Female collection generally has more squamous cells than male; threshold for contamination interpretation may differ.
- Uncircumcised males may have higher squamous counts from preputial sloughing.
- Mid-cycle (women) — increased cervical/vaginal cell turnover.

**Sources:** *AAFP* Simerville 2005; *Medscape* Urinalysis; *PMC* DUTY children's urine contamination study.

## 16. Hyaline Casts, Urine

**What it is.** Cylindrical molds of the renal tubule lumen made of **Tamm-Horsfall protein (uromodulin)**, secreted by the thick ascending limb. Hyaline casts are colorless, low-refractile, transparent rods.

**Methodology.** Centrifuged sediment, low-power field (LPF, 100×). Phase-contrast microscopy improves detection because hyaline casts are nearly transparent.

**Reference range / normal.** **0–2/LPF**; up to ~5/LPF can be normal in concentrated urine.

**Interpretation.** Per *eClinpath* and *StatPearls*: hyaline casts are the **least pathologic cast type** and the only casts that can occur in healthy individuals. Numbers increase with:

- **Dehydration / concentrated urine.**
- **Strenuous exercise** (with mild proteinuria).
- **Fever.**
- **Heat stress.**
- **Heart failure** (decreased renal perfusion).
- **Mild proteinuria of any cause** (pregnancy, diabetes early).
- **Diuretic use.**

In contrast, **pathologic casts** (not in the standard Function panel but worth distinguishing from hyaline) include:
- **RBC casts** → glomerulonephritis (always pathologic).
- **WBC casts** → pyelonephritis or interstitial nephritis.
- **Granular casts** → acute tubular necrosis, glomerular disease.
- **Waxy / broad casts** → advanced CKD ("renal failure casts").
- **Fatty casts (Maltese cross under polarized light)** → nephrotic syndrome.
- **Renal tubular epithelial cell casts** → ATN.

**Confounders / artifacts:**
- Dissolve quickly in alkaline / dilute / old urine — false-negative.
- Mucus threads can be misread as casts.
- Refrigeration → casts can precipitate or dissolve depending on pH.

**Young-male context.** Mild hyaline casts after a hard workout in a healthy young man are normal and benign. Any *cellular* casts (RBC, WBC, granular, waxy) warrant further evaluation.

**Sources:** *eClinpath* Casts; *Wikipedia Urinary Cast*; Naderi et al. *PMC* 2022 (hyaline casts and BNP).

## 17. Calcium Oxalate Crystals, Urine

**What it is.** Crystals of calcium oxalate, the most common urinary crystal type. Two forms:
- **Calcium oxalate dihydrate (weddellite)** — bipyramidal "envelope" or "Maltese cross" shape, common in normal urine.
- **Calcium oxalate monohydrate (whewellite)** — dumbbell, ovoid, or "picket fence" needle shapes, more associated with pathology.

**Methodology.** Polarized-light microscopy of centrifuged sediment is helpful (CaOx is birefringent).

**Reference range / normal.** Few crystals are common and **non-pathologic** in normal urine, particularly:
- Acidic urine (pH 5–6.5).
- Concentrated urine.
- After meals high in oxalate (spinach, rhubarb, beets, nuts, chocolate, tea, soy).
- Cold/refrigerated specimens (artifact).

**Pathologic associations:**

- **Recurrent calcium oxalate stones** — ~75–80% of all kidney stones. Risk factors: low fluid intake, high sodium, high animal protein, low calcium intake (paradoxically — increases oxalate absorption), high oxalate diet.
- **Hyperoxaluria:**
  - **Primary hyperoxaluria** types I/II/III (rare genetic, AGXT/GRHPR/HOGA1 mutations) — early-onset stones, nephrocalcinosis, ESRD.
  - **Enteric hyperoxaluria** — fat malabsorption frees oxalate from calcium binding in the gut → increased absorption. Classic in IBD (especially Crohn with ileal disease/resection), short bowel syndrome, post-Roux-en-Y gastric bypass, cystic fibrosis.
  - **Dietary hyperoxaluria** — extreme oxalate intake (tea drinkers, vegan with heavy spinach/chocolate/nut intake, "oxalate dumping").
  - **Vitamin C overdose** — high-dose vitamin C is metabolized to oxalate; doses >1 g/day chronically increase urinary oxalate.
- **Ethylene glycol poisoning (medical emergency).** Ingested ethylene glycol (antifreeze) is metabolized via alcohol dehydrogenase → glycolaldehyde → glycolic acid → glyoxylate → **oxalic acid**, which precipitates as calcium oxalate **monohydrate** (needle-shaped) in renal tubules and other tissues. Presentation: **anion-gap metabolic acidosis + osmolal gap + AKI + crystalluria (often bipyramidal CaOx dihydrate AND needle-shaped CaOx monohydrate)** within 4–24 hours of ingestion. Treat with fomepizole and dialysis. Per Jacobsen et al. and Eder et al. *PubMed* studies, the monohydrate form is the most specific finding.

**Confounders / artifacts:**
- **Refrigeration** — calcium oxalate precipitates *de novo* during cold storage; this is the **most common cause of CaOx crystals** on a routine UA. Re-warm and re-examine if clinically unclear.
- High oxalate meals 4–24 h before sample.
- Old / standing specimen.
- Contamination (oxalate from collection container).

**Young-male context.** Occasional calcium oxalate crystals in a young, healthy male are usually benign and dietary, not pathologic. Recurrent or numerous crystals + flank pain + hematuria → workup for nephrolithiasis (24-hour urine for calcium, oxalate, citrate, uric acid, sodium, volume; serum electrolytes). Sudden onset of confusion, AKI, severe metabolic acidosis with calcium oxalate crystalluria → **rule out ethylene glycol immediately**.

**Sources:** *StatPearls Hyperoxaluria* (Bhasin et al.); *StatPearls Urinary Crystals* (Reid et al. 2024); Jacobsen et al. *AJKD* 2009; Eder et al. *J Anal Toxicol*.

## 18. Yeast, Urine

**What it is.** Visualized fungal cells, typically *Candida* (most often *C. albicans*, *C. glabrata*, *C. tropicalis*). Appear as oval budding cells, sometimes with pseudohyphae.

**Methodology.** Microscopy; can be confused with RBCs (yeast lacks central pallor, may show buds).

**Reference range / normal.** Absent.

**Interpretation:**

- **Contamination** is the most common explanation in healthy outpatients, especially women with vaginal candidiasis. Repeat clean-catch.
- **True candiduria** — significant only in specific contexts:
  - **Indwelling urinary catheter** (most common true cause; biofilm formation).
  - **Diabetes mellitus (especially uncontrolled)** — glucosuria + impaired immune response promote candida growth; per Yashavanth et al. *PMC* 2017, candiduria correlates with glucosuria and HbA1c.
  - **Recent broad-spectrum antibiotic use.**
  - **Immunosuppression** — chemotherapy, transplant, HIV/AIDS, neutropenia, corticosteroids.
  - **Recent urologic instrumentation.**
  - **Recent hospitalization / ICU.**
  - **Pregnancy (lower threshold for treatment).**

**In immunocompromised patients, candiduria can be a sentinel for disseminated candidiasis (the kidney is the primary deposition site in ~80% of candidemia).**

**Symptomatic candida UTI** (rare in healthy young men) — fever, dysuria, pyuria, persistent yeast on repeat clean-catch. Requires treatment (fluconazole if susceptible, otherwise echinocandin or amphotericin).

**Confounders / artifacts:**
- **Vaginal contamination** (most common in women).
- **Preputial contamination** in uncircumcised males.
- Confusion with RBCs (look for buds, lack of biconcave shape).
- Long storage — yeast can multiply.

**Young-male context.** Yeast in the urine of a healthy 20yo male without diabetes, immune compromise, antibiotics, or catheter — almost always contamination from preputial flora. Repeat clean-catch with retraction of foreskin. If persistent, consider screening for diabetes (FPG, HbA1c).

**Sources:** Yashavanth et al. *PMC* 2017; Kauffman et al. *Saudi J Kidney Dis* 2008; Behzadi et al. *Future Microbiol* 2020 (Candida UTI review); Merck Manual Fungal UTIs.

## 19. Amorphous Sediment, Urine

**What it is.** Microscopic crystalline material without distinct geometric form — clumped fine grains. Two main types based on urine pH:

- **Amorphous urates** — sodium/potassium/calcium/magnesium **urate salts**. Form in **acidic urine (pH <5.8)**. Often appear pink, salmon, or "brick-dust" red-orange in concentrated specimens.
- **Amorphous phosphates** — **calcium / magnesium phosphates**. Form in **alkaline urine (pH >6.3)**. Appear white-gray and make urine look milky-cloudy.

**Methodology.** Microscopy of centrifuged sediment; can mimic bacterial cocci (but immobile).

**Reference range / normal.** **No clinical significance — generally a normal finding.**

**Interpretation:**
- Common in concentrated urine.
- Common after refrigeration — per *CellWiki*, ~55% of refrigerated specimens develop amorphous urates overnight.
- Acidic urine, dehydration, high-protein meal → urates.
- Alkaline urine, vegetarian diet, urea-splitting UTI, RTA, stale sample → phosphates.

**Pathologic associations (rare):**
- **Severe hyperuricosuria** — gout, tumor lysis syndrome, high-purine diet — may have urate crystallization in addition to amorphous urates.
- **Persistent amorphous phosphates in alkaline urine with pyuria** → consider chronic Proteus UTI.

**Confounders / artifacts:**
- **Refrigeration is the dominant cause** of amorphous sediment on routine UA. Warming the specimen often clears it.
- Can obscure pathologic findings (casts, bacteria) on microscopy — re-warming and re-examining is recommended.
- Often mistaken for bacteria (clumped granular appearance) — distinguish by lack of motility and reaction to heating.

**Sources:** *CellWiki* Amorphous Precipitation; *StatPearls Urinary Crystals* (Reid et al. 2024); *Levels* Amorphous Sediment Guide; *AAFP* Simerville 2005.

# Cross-Cutting Context

## Specimen Collection — the #1 source of error

- **Clean-catch midstream** is the standard. Retract foreskin (men) / spread labia (women), wipe with antiseptic, void initial 30 mL into the toilet, collect mid-stream, finish in toilet.
- **First-morning void** is preferred for protein, orthostatic proteinuria evaluation, specific gravity (most concentrated), bilirubin.
- **Catheterized specimen** is more reliable in women / non-toilet-trained / suspected contamination but introduces risk of catheter-associated UTI.
- **Suprapubic aspiration** is the gold standard but rarely needed outside infants.
- **Process within 1 hour** at room temperature, **or refrigerate at 4°C for up to 24 hours**. Beyond this window: pH rises, casts dissolve, RBCs and WBCs lyse, glucose is metabolized, ketones evaporate, bilirubin oxidizes, bacteria proliferate, crystals form artifactually.

## Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) interference — highly relevant for high-dose vitamin C supplementation

Per Brigden et al. *Ann Clin Lab Sci* 2015 and Nagel et al. *Clinical Biochemistry* 2016 (multicenter study of >7000 specimens):

| Dipstick parameter | False-negative rate when ascorbic acid in urine |
|---|---|
| **Glucose** | up to 42.3% (when serum glucose >180 mg/dL) |
| **Blood (heme)** | ~10.6% |
| **Bilirubin** | significant (unquantified, large effect) |
| **Nitrite** | invalid at ≥50 mg/dL ascorbic acid |
| **Leukocyte esterase** | ~8.2% |

Vitamin C is detectable in urine of ~18% of routine outpatient samples in modern populations. **If you take >500 mg vitamin C daily, mention this to the lab and consider stopping for 24–48 hours before a UA** to avoid false negatives.

## Drug-induced interference — phenazopyridine

Phenazopyridine (Pyridium / AZO) is a widely-used OTC bladder analgesic that turns urine bright orange. **It interferes with virtually every dipstick parameter** by direct color quenching: blood, LE, nitrite, glucose, ketones, protein, bilirubin. If a UA is needed for diagnostic purposes and the patient is on phenazopyridine, microscopy should be relied upon over dipstick, and the patient should ideally hold the drug 24–48 hours before testing.

## AUA 2020 Microhematuria Guideline (Barocas et al. *J Urol* 2020; 2025 update)

Microhematuria = **≥3 RBCs/HPF** on microscopy of properly collected specimen. Dipstick alone is insufficient.

| Risk | Criteria | Workup |
|---|---|---|
| **Low** | (Women <50 / Men <40) AND never-smoker AND <10 pack-years AND <10 RBCs/HPF AND no other risk factors | Repeat UA in 6 months; if persistent, cystoscopy + renal ultrasound OR shared decision |
| **Intermediate** | Women 50–59 / Men 40–59, OR 10–30 pack-years, OR 11–25 RBCs/HPF | Cystoscopy + renal ultrasound |
| **High** | ≥60yo OR >30 pack-years OR >25 RBCs/HPF OR gross hematuria history OR occupational exposures | Cystoscopy + CT urography (or MR urography) |

**Anyone with proteinuria, dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, elevated creatinine, or hypertension → nephrology referral first**, regardless of risk category.

The 2025 update incorporates urinary biomarkers (Cxbladder) for low/intermediate risk patients.

## IDSA 2019 Asymptomatic Bacteriuria Guideline (Nicolle et al. *Clin Infect Dis* 2019)

- **DO NOT screen or treat ASB** in healthy non-pregnant adults, diabetics, older adults (any setting), spinal cord injury, catheter, transplant >1mo post.
- **DO screen and treat** in **pregnancy** (4–7 days antibiotics) and **before endoscopic urologic procedures with mucosal trauma**.
- Asymptomatic candiduria same — do not treat unless above categories or planned urologic procedure.

## Young-Male Specific Patterns Worth Memorizing

| Finding | Most likely benign cause | Most likely pathologic cause |
|---|---|---|
| Microscopic hematuria | Exercise, contamination, thin basement membrane | IgA nephropathy, stones, UTI |
| Gross hematuria 24–72h post-URI | (None — workup) | **IgA nephropathy** (Berger disease) |
| Proteinuria on daytime sample | **Orthostatic proteinuria** (~3–5% of young men) | Glomerulonephritis, diabetic kidney |
| Glucosuria with normal blood glucose | Familial renal glucosuria | Fanconi syndrome, SGLT2 inhibitor |
| Mild hyaline casts after workout | Exercise / dehydration | (None) |
| CaOx crystals in acidic urine | High-oxalate meal, refrigeration | Recurrent stones, hyperoxaluria, ethylene glycol |
| Ketonuria + normal glucose | Keto diet, fasting, exercise | Starvation ketoacidosis, alcoholic KA |
| Nitrite+ / LE+ / bacteria | (Rarely benign in symptomatic men) | UTI — workup more thoroughly than in women (anatomic abnormality, instrumentation) |
| Yeast on UA | Preputial contamination | Diabetes (worth checking HbA1c), immunocompromise |
| Squamous epithelial >15/HPF | Contamination — repeat | (None) |
| SG fixed at 1.010 across samples | (None) | CKD with isosthenuria |
| SG <1.005 with polyuria | Excess fluid intake | Diabetes insipidus |
