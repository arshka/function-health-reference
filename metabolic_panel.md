# Metabolic Panel — Deep Reference for a 20-Year-Old Male

A comprehensive interpretive guide for **Glucose (fasting), Hemoglobin A1c, Insulin (fasting), Leptin, and Uric Acid**. The report distinguishes ADA / endocrine-society **consensus** ranges from **metabolic-optimization** framings (Attia, Kraft, Crofts, Levels Health) and labels each accordingly. Quantitative claims are cited; provenance is flagged when a finding is consensus vs. fringe but plausible vs. speculative.

---

## 0. How to read this document

Three reference frames appear throughout:

1. **Consensus / regulatory** — ADA Standards of Care (2025/2026), Endocrine Society, NGSP, lab reference intervals (Quest, Labcorp). These define disease, not optimization.
2. **Lean-young-male target** — what's reasonable to want at age 20 with normal BMI, derived from healthy-cohort CGM/biomarker data (Hall 2018, Shah 2019).
3. **Metabolic-optimization framing** — Attia, Kraft, Crofts, Levels — tighter targets aimed at "dysfunction far before disease." Evidence base ranges from solid (CGM data, hyperinsulinemia precedes hyperglycemia) to weaker (specific A1c cutoffs).

Everything in **frame 3** is presented honestly: the directional logic is supported but the exact cutoffs are not regulatory-grade.

## 1. Glucose (Fasting, Serum/Plasma)

### 1.1 What it is and how it's measured

Glucose is a six-carbon monosaccharide, the body's primary circulating fuel. Fasting glucose is measured on serum or plasma collected after ≥8 h fast, typically with **enzymatic methods** in clinical labs:

- **Hexokinase / G6PD method** — glucose + ATP → G6P → 6-phosphogluconate, generating NADPH measured at 340 nm. This is the **reference method**; more specific than glucose oxidase ([NCBI Bookshelf, Hexokinase Method](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK587446/)).
- **Glucose oxidase–peroxidase (GOD-POD)** — glucose oxidized to H₂O₂, then chromogenic readout. Susceptible to interference from uric acid, ascorbate, bilirubin, hemoglobin, glutathione, tetracycline (falsely low) ([NCBI Bookshelf, Peroxidase-Coupled Glucose](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK594277/)).
- **Pre-analytical:** glycolysis in the tube can drop glucose ~5–7% per hour; tubes contain sodium fluoride to inhibit it ([NCBI Bookshelf, Hexokinase Method](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK587446/)).

### 1.2 Physiology

Fasting glucose is the dynamic equilibrium of:
- **Hepatic glucose output** (glycogenolysis + gluconeogenesis), suppressed by insulin
- **Peripheral uptake** (skeletal muscle, adipose, brain), driven by GLUT4/GLUT1
- **Counter-regulatory hormones**: glucagon (primary), cortisol, epinephrine/norepinephrine, growth hormone

In the fasted state, hepatic gluconeogenesis dominates; insulin remains low but non-zero. The brain consumes ~120 g glucose/day at baseline.

### 1.3 Reference and optimal ranges

| Frame | Fasting glucose |
|---|---|
| ADA normal | 70–99 mg/dL (3.9–5.5 mmol/L) ([ADA Standards 2025](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/48/Supplement_1/S27/157566/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes)) |
| ADA prediabetes (IFG) | 100–125 mg/dL ([ADA 2025](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/48/Supplement_1/S27/157566/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes)) |
| ADA diabetes | ≥126 mg/dL on 2 occasions ([ADA 2025](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/48/Supplement_1/S27/157566/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes)) |
| Lean young male target | 70–90 mg/dL — within normal but on the lower half (Levels, [healthy CGM data](https://www.levels.com/blog/what-should-my-glucose-levels-be-ultimate-guide)) |
| Severe hypoglycemia | <55 mg/dL (Whipple's) ([Endotext, NBK279137](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK279137/)) |

**Optimal at 20:** **70–90 mg/dL fasting**. In a healthy, non-diabetic CGM cohort (Shah et al., 2019), mean glucose was 98–99 mg/dL across ages with 96% time in 70–140 mg/dL range ([Shah JCEM 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/10/4356/5479355)). Levels Health cites optimal fasting 72–85 mg/dL ([Levels guide](https://www.levels.com/blog/what-should-my-glucose-levels-be-ultimate-guide)) — provenance: optimization framing, directionally supported by healthy-cohort data.

### 1.4 What HIGH values indicate

- **Type 2 diabetes / prediabetes** — by far the most common cause; insulin resistance with progressive β-cell failure
- **Type 1 diabetes** — autoimmune β-cell destruction; classically presents younger with DKA
- **Acute stress hyperglycemia** — illness, sepsis, trauma, MI, surgery. Cortisol can rise ~10×; epinephrine 50× in shock; both stimulate hepatic gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis and induce peripheral insulin resistance via TNF-α/IL-6 ([Critical Care 2013, Stress hyperglycemia](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3672537/)).
- **Dawn phenomenon** — early-morning rise in glucose from overnight surge of GH and cortisol, sometimes 2 a.m.–8 a.m.
- **Cushing's syndrome** — excess glucocorticoids; ~50% of patients have glucose dysregulation ([Frontiers Endocrinol 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2023.1248985/full))
- **Acromegaly** — excess GH; ~50% glucose dysregulation
- **Pheochromocytoma** — catecholamine excess; ~50% develop hyperglycemia
- **Pancreatitis / pancreatic injury** — β-cell loss
- **Drugs** — glucocorticoids, atypical antipsychotics (esp olanzapine, clozapine), thiazide diuretics, β-blockers (less commonly), tacrolimus/cyclosporine, niacin at high dose, protease inhibitors
- **MODY** — monogenic diabetes (GCK, HNF1A, HNF4A); rare but worth considering in young patients with mild fasting hyperglycemia and a strong family history

### 1.5 What LOW values indicate

Endocrine Society defines diagnostic hypoglycemia by Whipple's triad: symptoms + glucose <55 mg/dL + relief on glucose ([Endotext, NBK279137](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK279137/)).

Differential:
- **Insulinoma** — endogenous hyperinsulinism; gold-standard 72-hour fast (90–95% diagnosed by 48 h) with insulin ≥3 µIU/mL, C-peptide ≥0.6 ng/mL, proinsulin ≥5 pmol/L, β-hydroxybutyrate ≤2.7 mmol/L, negative sulfonylurea screen ([JCEM 2024 Approach to Insulinoma](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/4/1109/7334393))
- **Drug-induced** — insulin/sulfonylureas (most common in clinical practice); also quinolones, quinine, pentamidine, IGF-2-like effects from non-islet tumors ([NCBI Bookshelf NBK573079](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK573079/))
- **Alcohol** — inhibits hepatic gluconeogenesis; depletes glycogen
- **Adrenal insufficiency / Addison's** — cortisol deficiency impairs gluconeogenesis
- **Sepsis / critical illness** — increased glucose utilization, impaired hepatic output ([NCBI Bookshelf NBK573079](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK573079/))
- **Prolonged fasting / malnutrition / anorexia**
- **Post-bariatric (esp. Roux-en-Y) dumping syndrome** — late post-prandial hypoglycemia from exaggerated insulin response
- **Glycogen storage diseases**, hypopituitarism, autoimmune insulin antibodies

### 1.6 Direction of "better" — U-shaped

Both ends are bad. At 20 with no diabetes risk, optimal is the lower end of normal (70–90 mg/dL). Going below ~60 mg/dL fasting is biologically unusual outside of athletic, very lean populations.

### 1.7 Confounders

- **Recent food intake** — even small carbs invalidate "fasting"
- **Acute exercise** — can lower glucose; intense exercise can transiently raise it
- **Acute illness / stress** — even minor URI can elevate
- **Stress / sympathetic activation** — needle-stick anxiety can transiently raise glucose by 5–10 mg/dL
- **Sleep deprivation** — single night of poor sleep raises fasting glucose and reduces insulin sensitivity
- **Time of day** — dawn cortisol surge

### 1.8 CGM data in healthy young people (high signal)

The two key references:

- **Shah et al., JCEM 2019** — 153 healthy non-diabetics, blinded Dexcom G6 for ~10 days. **Mean glucose 98–99 mg/dL** (104 in age >60). **Median time 70–140 mg/dL: 96%.** Median time >140: only 2.1% (~30 min/day). Median time <70: 1.1%. **Within-person CV: 17 ± 3%.** ([Shah JCEM 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/10/4356/5479355))
- **Hall et al., PLOS Biology 2018 (Glucotypes)** — 57 participants, 2–4 weeks CGM. Even **standard-normoglycemic individuals reached prediabetic ranges 15% of time and diabetic ranges 2% of time** in response to specific foods. Identified "glucotypes" (low/moderate/severe variability) as a more granular phenotype than fasting glucose ([Hall PLOS Biol 2018](https://journals.plos.org/plosbiology/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pbio.2005143)).

**Implication for a healthy 20M:** post-prandial peaks <140 mg/dL, time-to-baseline <2 h, peak rise <30 mg/dL above baseline are realistic targets ([Levels guide](https://www.levels.com/blog/what-should-my-glucose-levels-be-ultimate-guide)). Mean post-meal glucose peak in healthy adults is ~99 ± 10.5 mg/dL after a balanced meal but can hit 133 ± 14 mg/dL after refined carbs.

## 2. Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)

### 2.1 What it is and how it's measured

HbA1c is **non-enzymatically glycated hemoglobin** — glucose binds the N-terminal valine of the β-chain via a Schiff base that rearranges to a stable Amadori product. Once formed, it persists for the life of the RBC.

**Measurement methods** (each with distinct interferences):
- **Cation-exchange HPLC** — separates Hb species by charge; most common, most susceptible to **hemoglobinopathy variant interference** ([NGSP Interferences](https://ngsp.org/interf.asp))
- **Boronate-affinity HPLC** — binds cis-diol of glucose; less variant-sensitive
- **Immunoassay (turbidimetric)** — antibody to glycated N-terminus; intermediate variant tolerance
- **Enzymatic** — fructosyl peptide oxidase
- **Capillary electrophoresis** (e.g., Capillarys 3) — variant-tolerant ([Lab Med 2024 6-method comparison](https://academic.oup.com/labmed/article/55/6/708/7683086))

**Variant interference matters**: HbS, HbC, HbE, HbD all have charge-altering β-chain substitutions. Pure cation-exchange HPLC can give wildly inaccurate HbA1c. Several modern platforms (Bio-Rad D-100, Capillarys 3, Mindray BS-600M, Premier Hb9210, Roche c501) show no interference from S/C/D/E ([Lab Med 2024](https://academic.oup.com/labmed/article/55/6/708/7683086)). NGSP maintains the [interference table](https://ngsp.org/interf.asp).

### 2.2 Physiology — the 3-month average, weighted

RBC lifespan ~120 days. Older RBCs accumulate more glycated hemoglobin. HbA1c reflects glycemia over ~12 weeks but is **weighted toward the most recent 4 weeks (~50% of the signal)**, with prior month ~25% and 2–3 months prior ~25% ([Mindray, RBC lifecycle](https://www.mindray.com/en/media-center/blogs/understanding-hba1c-red-blood-cell-life-cycle); [Wikipedia, Glycated hemoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycated_hemoglobin)). This means a recent dietary change can move A1c noticeably within 4–6 weeks.

### 2.3 Reference and optimal ranges

| Frame | HbA1c |
|---|---|
| ADA normal | <5.7% (39 mmol/mol) ([ADA 2025](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/48/Supplement_1/S27/157566/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes)) |
| ADA prediabetes | 5.7–6.4% (39–47 mmol/mol) ([ADA 2025](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/48/Supplement_1/S27/157566/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes)) |
| ADA diabetes | ≥6.5% (≥48 mmol/mol) ([ADA 2025](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/48/Supplement_1/S27/157566/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes)) |
| ADA "high-risk prediabetes" | A1c >6.0% — aggressive intervention ([ADA 2025](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/48/Supplement_1/S27/157566/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes)) |
| Lean young male reasonable target | 4.8–5.4% |
| Attia "optimal" framing | 5.0–5.5%, ideal ~5.1% ([Attia HbA1c piece](https://peterattiamd.com/problems-with-hba1c/); [Bolt Pharmacy summary](https://www.boltpharmacy.co.uk/guide/peter-attia-hba1c-target)) |

**Provenance note:** Attia's targets are personalized-preventive medicine, not regulatory guidance; the directional logic ("hyperinsulinemia precedes A1c rise by years") is well supported, but the specific 5.0–5.5% threshold isn't validated by RCT in healthy young men.

### 2.4 What HIGH values indicate

- **Diabetes / prediabetes** (true hyperglycemia)
- **Iron deficiency anemia** — falsely elevated. Mechanism: prolonged RBC lifespan (older cells exposed to glucose longer) + increased malondialdehyde-driven hemoglobin glycation. One series found mean HbA1c 6.8 ± 1.4% in iron-deficient subjects vs. controls; correction with iron lowers A1c ([Increased Levels of HbA1c and IDA review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6857442/)).
- **B12/folate deficiency** — similar mechanism (older RBCs)
- **Splenectomy** — no RBC removal → older population
- **Chronic kidney disease / uremia** — carbamylated Hb interferes with some assays
- **Hemoglobinopathies** — variant-dependent and assay-dependent (can falsely raise OR lower) ([NGSP Factors](https://ngsp.org/factors.asp))
- **Hypertriglyceridemia, hyperbilirubinemia** — interfere with some immunoassays

### 2.5 What LOW values indicate

- **Hemolysis / shortened RBC lifespan** — falsely lowers; e.g., G6PD deficiency, hereditary spherocytosis, autoimmune hemolytic anemia
- **Recent transfusion / acute blood loss** — dilution + younger RBCs
- **Pregnancy** — increased RBC turnover
- **Hemoglobinopathies** (some)
- **Erythropoietin therapy / iron repletion phase** — younger RBC population
- **Hypersplenism** — increased RBC clearance
- **Liver cirrhosis** — shortened RBC lifespan

### 2.6 Direction of "better" — generally lower, with caveats

Carson et al. (Circ Cardiovasc Qual Outcomes 2010) — NHANES III, 14,099 non-diabetic adults followed up to 12 years. **HbA1c <4.0% was associated with increased all-cause mortality**, but participants with low A1c also had higher mean cell volume, ferritin, and liver enzymes — i.e., the signal is **largely confounded by hemolysis, liver disease, and inflammation**, not a true hazard of low glycation ([Carson 2010 Circ CV Qual Outcomes](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/circoutcomes.110.957936)).

**Reasonable interpretation:** in a metabolically healthy young man, lower A1c (down to ~4.8%) within the normal range is fine and probably better; very low A1c (<4.5%) should prompt screening for hemolysis or liver dysfunction, not be celebrated.

### 2.7 Confounders (clinically critical)

- **Hemolysis** (low) and **iron deficiency** (high) are the two big ones — both common, both move A1c by a meaningful margin (~0.3–1.0%)
- **Hemoglobin variants** — assay-dependent
- **Ethnic differences** — small but real; African Americans average ~0.2% higher than non-Hispanic Whites at the same fasting/post-load glucose ([NIDDK A1c Variants blog](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/professionals/diabetes-discoveries-practice/interpreting-a1c-diabetes-and-hemoglobin-variants))
- **Age** — A1c drifts up with age slightly even without dysglycemia
- **Pregnancy** — falsely low

### 2.8 When to trust A1c vs. CGM/OGTT

Attia's argument ([Problems with HbA1c](https://peterattiamd.com/problems-with-hba1c/)): A1c is a lagging average that misses post-prandial spikes and hyperinsulinemia. In a 20-year-old, **fasting insulin + an OGTT-with-insulin (Kraft-style)** detects dysfunction years before A1c moves. CGM gives the post-prandial picture. A1c remains useful as a cheap, integrated index — but its sensitivity for early dysfunction in lean young people is poor.

## 3. Insulin (Fasting)

### 3.1 What it is and how it's measured

Insulin is a 51-aa peptide hormone (A and B chains linked by disulfides), secreted by pancreatic β-cells in response to glucose, amino acids (esp. leucine, arginine), incretins (GLP-1, GIP), and parasympathetic tone. Half-life ~5–6 minutes; cleared mostly by liver (first-pass extraction ~50%) and kidney.

**Assay methods**:
- **Immunoassay (chemiluminescent or ELISA)** — the standard; cheap, fast. Major issue is **cross-reactivity** with proinsulin, C-peptide-containing intermediates, exogenous insulin analogs, and anti-insulin antibodies, leading to **inter-assay variability >20%** between platforms ([Wiley 2022, JCLA assay comparability](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/jcla.24521)).
- **LC-MS/MS (intact insulin)** — Quest's "Insulin, Intact, LC/MS/MS" uses immunoaffinity enrichment + tandem mass spec; differentiates by molecular weight; immune to most cross-reactivity ([Quest Insulin Intact LC-MS/MS](https://testdirectory.questdiagnostics.com/test/test-guides/TS_Insulin_Intact/insulin-intact-lcmsms)).
- **Reference procedure**: ID-LC-MS/MS traceable to WHO IRP 83/500 ([PubMed 40353876](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40353876/)).

**Practical note for a young patient:** if comparing fasting insulin across draws or across labs, ask whether the same assay was used. Cross-platform differences can mimic biological change.

### 3.2 Physiology and HOMA-IR

Insulin drives glucose uptake (GLUT4 in muscle/adipose), suppresses hepatic glucose output, suppresses lipolysis (very sensitive — adipose IR is the earliest defect in some), and promotes protein synthesis.

**HOMA-IR** (Matthews 1985) — homeostatic model from fasting glucose and fasting insulin:

```
HOMA-IR = (fasting glucose [mg/dL] × fasting insulin [µIU/mL]) / 405
```

(Or, in mmol/L: glucose × insulin / 22.5.)

[Matthews 1985, PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3899825/); [MDCalc HOMA-IR](https://www.mdcalc.com/calc/3120/homa-ir-homeostatic-model-assessment-insulin-resistance).

**HOMA-IR cutoffs by source:**

| Source | Cutoff for IR |
|---|---|
| Matthews 1985 (original) | ~2.7 |
| HOMA2 (updated 1998) | ~1.8 |
| Geloneze 2009 (BRAMS, Brazilian) | HOMA1-IR 2.71; HOMA2-IR 1.8 ([Geloneze 2009 ABEM](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4016563/) cited in EPIRCE) |
| Tang 2015 (Chinese, prospective) | ~2.0–2.5 in Asian; lower in Han Chinese ([Tang 2015 Drug Discov Ther, PubMed 26781921](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26781921/)) |
| EPIRCE (Spain) | 2.6 men / 2.5 women ([EPIRCE PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4016563/)) |
| Korean general adult | 2.2–2.5 ([Korean DMJ](https://www.e-dmj.org/journal/view.php?number=561)) |

**Optimization framing**: HOMA-IR <1.0 is the often-cited target for excellent insulin sensitivity in lean young men. This is reasonable but not regulator-defined.

**Ethnicity matters**: South Asians have ~30–50% higher HOMA-IR than Europeans at any given BMI and develop diabetes at lower BMI thresholds ([Kelly West Lecture 2024, Diabetes Care](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/47/1/7/154008/Diabetes-in-South-Asians-Uncovering-Novel-Risk)). For a South Asian 20M, the same HOMA-IR carries more risk than for a European 20M.

### 3.3 Reference and optimal ranges

| Frame | Fasting insulin |
|---|---|
| Lab "normal" (Quest, Labcorp) | ~2.6–24.9 µIU/mL ([Labcorp test 004333](https://www.labcorp.com/tests/004333/insulin)) |
| Quest flag for elevation | >16 µIU/mL ([Quest Insulin FAQ](https://www.questdiagnostics.com/healthcare-professionals/clinical-education-center/faq/faq170)) |
| Functional medicine optimal | 2–6 µIU/mL ([Veri](https://www.veri.co/learn/optimal-fasting-insulin-level)) |
| Attia / Kraft / Crofts "optimal" | <5 µIU/mL ideal; <8 acceptable ([Superpower review](https://superpower.com/blog/optimal-fasting-insulin)) |
| HOMA-IR optimal | <1.0 |
| HOMA-IR "borderline" | 1.0–1.9 |
| HOMA-IR insulin resistant | ≥2.0–2.7 (population-dependent) |

**Provenance**: lab "normal" is consensus and based on population means including the obese and overweight; the <5 µIU/mL target is **Kraft/Crofts/Attia optimization framing** with directional support but no large-scale RCT validation. The framing is plausible because:

1. Hyperinsulinemia precedes hyperglycemia by years to decades (Kraft data).
2. The lab "normal" upper bound (~25 µIU/mL) corresponds to clearly insulin-resistant physiology.
3. Lean, young, metabolically healthy individuals empirically run 2–6 µIU/mL.

### 3.4 The Kraft / Crofts dynamic insulin testing framework

Joseph Kraft (clinical pathologist, Chicago) ran ~14,000 5-hour OGTTs with paired glucose AND insulin sampling at 0/30/60/120/180 minutes ([Crofts 2016, IUMB analysis of Kraft database, PubMed 27344544](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27344544/); [Crofts JMH](https://journalofmetabolichealth.org/index.php/jmh/article/view/49/180)).

**Five patterns** (I = euinsulinemic, II–V = increasingly hyperinsulinemic). Critical finding: **of 3,428 people with impaired glucose tolerance or diabetes by glucose criteria, 93% also had a hyperinsulinemic Kraft pattern** — and many people with "normal" fasting glucose had Pattern II/III ("diabetes in situ"). Crofts redefined patterns to ensure all tests categorize ([Crofts 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27344544/)).

**Diagnostic criteria simplified (Crofts):**
- Fasting insulin >7–10 µIU/mL or
- 1-h post-load insulin >40–50 µIU/mL or
- Failure of insulin to return toward baseline by 3 h

**Practical**: Kraft testing is rarely available commercially (Meridian Valley Lab in Tukwila, WA offers it as the "Kraft Prediabetes Profile"; [Meridian Valley](https://www.meridianvalleylab.com/services/kraft-prediabetes-profile/)). It's high-signal for early metabolic dysfunction but logistically demanding (5 hours, multiple draws). For a 20-year-old with normal fasting insulin, value is marginal; for one with fasting insulin 8–15 µIU/mL or family history, it's the most informative single test.

**Provenance flag**: Kraft's data are large, peer-reviewed (the Crofts re-analysis is in PubMed-indexed journals), and the directional claim — that hyperinsulinemia precedes hyperglycemia — is mainstream endocrinology. The specific cutoffs and the term "diabetes in situ" are non-mainstream branding.

### 3.5 What HIGH values indicate

- **Insulin resistance** — by far the most common cause in a 20M; correlates with central adiposity, NAFLD, dyslipidemia, hypertension. Even at "normal" BMI, can occur with high visceral fat or in TOFI ("thin outside, fat inside") phenotypes, particularly in South Asians.
- **Obesity / metabolic syndrome / NAFLD**
- **PCOS** (n/a for male)
- **Cushing's, acromegaly** — counter-regulatory hormone excess driving compensatory hyperinsulinemia
- **Insulinoma** — rare; Whipple's triad + 72-h fast diagnostic criteria above
- **Familial hyperinsulinism** — congenital (KATP channel mutations); usually presents in infancy
- **Anti-insulin antibodies** (insulin autoimmune syndrome / Hirata disease) — rare; can give very high apparent insulin
- **Exogenous insulin** — lab won't distinguish unless C-peptide is also measured (low C-peptide + high insulin = exogenous)

### 3.6 What LOW values indicate

- **Type 1 diabetes** — autoimmune β-cell destruction
- **Late-stage type 2** — β-cell exhaustion after years of hyperinsulinemia
- **MODY** (some forms — esp. HNF1A)
- **Severe malnutrition / anorexia**
- **Post-bariatric** (especially malabsorptive)
- **Normal fasting** — fasting insulin 2–4 µIU/mL is healthy in lean young men

### 3.7 Direction of "better" — lower (within reason)

For a metabolically healthy 20M, **lower fasting insulin within the normal range is better**. There is no established risk to fasting insulin of 2–3 µIU/mL in someone who is well-nourished and not in starvation. The concern is biological plausibility, not regulatory approval.

### 3.8 Confounders

- **Must be truly fasted ≥10 h** — even tiny carb intake distorts
- **Pulsatile secretion** — a single fasting draw catches one trough or peak in a ~10–15 min cycle; coefficient of variation across consecutive draws can be 20–30%
- **Exercise within 24–48 h** — generally lowers
- **Sleep deprivation** — raises
- **Acute stress** — counter-regulatory hormones can transiently raise insulin
- **Recent illness** — raises (insulin resistance from cytokines)
- **Coffee / caffeine** — acutely raises insulin and reduces sensitivity
- **Assay platform variability** — see §3.1

### 3.9 Companion markers

- **C-peptide** — co-secreted 1:1 with insulin from proinsulin; longer half-life (~30 min), no first-pass clearance, more stable index of β-cell secretion. Useful when insulin is ambiguous (exogenous insulin use, antibodies). Optimal C-peptide ~1–2 ng/mL fasting.
- **Triglyceride/HDL ratio** — surrogate for insulin resistance. Cutoffs for IR: ~2.8 men, 2.5 women in pooled meta-analysis; population-dependent (3.5 in Mexican-American men; 3.1 in some European cohorts) ([MDPI 2024 TG/HDL review](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-9059/12/7/1493); [J Lipid Res](https://www.jlr.org/article/S0022-2275(20)35305-0/fulltext)). For optimization framing, TG/HDL <1.5 is often cited as optimal in non–African Americans (the marker has poor performance in African American populations — [Endocrine Connections 2021](https://ec.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/ec/10/11/EC-21-0414.xml)).
- **Adiponectin** — inversely correlated with IR; not in a typical Function panel.

## 4. Leptin

### 4.1 What it is and how it's measured

Leptin is a 167-aa peptide (16 kDa) hormone encoded by the *LEP* gene, secreted **by adipocytes** (with smaller amounts from gastric mucosa, placenta) — discovered by Friedman's lab at Rockefeller in 1994 via positional cloning of the *ob* gene in obese mice ([JCI Long Road to Leptin](https://www.jci.org/articles/view/91578)).

**Assay methods**:
- **ELISA (sandwich)** — two monoclonal antibodies; the standard. R&D Systems' Quantikine ELISA detection limit ~7.8 pg/mL ([R&D Systems Leptin DLP00](https://www.rndsystems.com/products/human-leptin-quantikine-elisa-kit_dlp00))
- **Radioimmunoassay (RIA)** — older; polyclonal antibodies detect free + bound; correlations with ELISA r=0.99 ([Sci Direct ELISA paper](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0009898100002035))
- LC-MS/MS exists for research but uncommon clinically

### 4.2 Physiology

Leptin is the body's primary **long-term adiposity signal to the hypothalamus**. Adipose mass and leptin correlate at r ≈ 0.85 ([South Indian leptin study, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5904587/)). Leptin acts on:

- **Arcuate nucleus** of the hypothalamus
- **Activates POMC neurons** → α-MSH → MC4R → appetite suppression, ↑ energy expenditure
- **Inhibits NPY/AgRP neurons** → ↓ orexigenic drive

**Diurnal rhythm**: peak 22:00–03:00, trough 08:00–17:00; ~3.6 pulses/24h, mostly 2–3 hours post-meal. Meal timing entrains the rhythm — shifting meals 6.5 h shifts leptin rhythm by 5–7 h ([JCI 1996, meal timing](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC508375/); [JCEM 1998 enteral nutrition](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/83/6/1893/2865218)). For interpretation, **time-of-day at draw matters** — morning fasting samples are conventionally lowest.

**Sex difference**: women have ~2–3× men's leptin at the same BMI, due to higher subcutaneous fat and estrogen effects on *LEP* expression ([Considine NEJM 1996](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199602013340503)).

### 4.3 Reference and optimal ranges (men)

| Frame | Men, ng/mL |
|---|---|
| Lab "normal" | 0.5–13.8 (varies by lab) |
| Median in healthy men | ~7.2 ng/mL (range 3.3–14.3) ([South Indian study, PMC5904587](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5904587/)) |
| Mean in normal-weight men | 7.5 ± 9.3 ng/mL ([Considine NEJM 1996](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199602013340503)) |
| Mean in obese men | 31.3 ± 24.1 ng/mL ([Considine NEJM 1996](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199602013340503)) |
| Cardiometabolic-risk threshold (men) | ~6.45 ng/mL ([Spanish reference values](https://www.revespcardiol.org/en-leptin-reference-values-cutoffs-for-articulo-S1885585714004605)) |
| Lean young man typical | 1–5 ng/mL |
| Lean man with leptin >10 | suggests early leptin resistance / higher visceral fat than expected for BMI |

### 4.4 What HIGH values indicate

- **Obesity** (most common; near-mechanistic from increased adipose mass)
- **Leptin resistance** — central, not peripheral. In obesity, leptin is high but POMC neurons are unresponsive due to mTOR/SOCS3 overexpression, JAK2-STAT3 signaling impairment, BBB transport defects, and PTP1B/TCPTP overactivity ([Mol Metab review PMC12486228](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12486228/); [Friedman lab 2025 Rockefeller News](https://www.rockefeller.edu/news/37433-researchers-discover-a-cause-of-leptin-resistance-and-how-to-reverse-it/)). The lean person with leptin disproportionate to BMI may be in early leptin resistance.
- **Acute inflammation** — cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) raise leptin; leptin is itself pro-inflammatory
- **Pregnancy** — placenta produces leptin
- **CKD** — reduced renal clearance
- **Rare leptin-producing tumors** — case reports only

### 4.5 What LOW values indicate

- **Low body fat** — endurance athletes, lean models, eating disorders
- **Anorexia nervosa** — leptin can fall below 1 ng/mL; low leptin contributes to hypothalamic amenorrhea, low T3, infertility, cold intolerance
- **Lipodystrophy syndromes** — congenital generalized lipodystrophy (Berardinelli-Seip), familial partial, acquired. Treated with **metreleptin** (FDA-approved 2014 for generalized lipodystrophy) ([FDA approval review, PMC4931926](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4931926/))
- **Congenital leptin deficiency** — extremely rare (handful of families globally); severe early-onset hyperphagia and obesity; **dramatically responsive to recombinant leptin therapy** — the single intervention that produces near-complete remission of hyperphagia ([Rockefeller 30-year quest](https://www.rockefeller.edu/news/37460-a-scientists-30-year-quest-reveals-why-a-fat-sensing-hormone-can-lose-its-effectiveness-leading-to-obesity/))
- **Prolonged fasting / starvation**

### 4.6 Direction of "better" — context-dependent

There is no "lower is better" or "higher is better" for leptin in isolation. The clinically useful framing is:

- Leptin should be **proportionate to body fat**
- **Leptin/adiposity ratio** elevated relative to expected (e.g., a lean 20M with leptin 12 ng/mL) suggests early leptin resistance and warrants attention
- **Very low leptin** in a non-anorexic, normal-weight person is unusual and may indicate lipodystrophy, congenital deficiency (extremely rare), or prolonged caloric restriction
- For a lean 20M at 10–15% body fat, **1–5 ng/mL is typical**

### 4.7 Confounders

- **Sex** — women 2–3× men at same BMI
- **Body fat %** — the dominant variable
- **Diurnal** — peak at night, trough mid-day; standardize draw time
- **Recent meal** — leptin rises 4–6 h post-meal; fasting recommended
- **Acute illness / inflammation** — raises
- **Sleep deprivation** — lowers (one night of 4-h sleep can drop leptin ~18%)
- **Energy balance** — chronic restriction lowers leptin disproportionately to fat loss; chronic surplus raises it
- **Smoking** — lowers
- **Insulin** — directly stimulates leptin secretion ([Stimulation of leptin by insulin, PMC3602982](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3602982/))

### 4.8 Leptin resistance — the key concept

Friedman's central insight (2025 lab work): **mTOR signaling in POMC neurons drives leptin resistance**; rapamycin restored leptin sensitivity in diet-induced obese mice ([Rockefeller News 2025](https://www.rockefeller.edu/news/37433-researchers-discover-a-cause-of-leptin-resistance-and-how-to-reverse-it/)). Mechanistically, leptin resistance is a **"triple defect"**: (1) impaired BBB transport, (2) disrupted JAK2-STAT3 signaling via SOCS3/PTP1B, (3) downstream POMC/MC4R unresponsiveness ([Mol Metab review 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12486228/)).

**Clinical implication for a 20M**: leptin in a typical Function panel is most useful as a **flag**. A lean young man with leptin >10 ng/mL, despite normal BMI, may have higher visceral fat and earlier metabolic dysfunction than weight alone suggests. This is suggestive, not diagnostic.

## 5. Uric Acid

### 5.1 What it is and how it's measured

Uric acid is the **end product of purine metabolism in humans** (and great apes — we lost functional uricase ~15 mya via inactivating mutations in *UOX*). Most other mammals convert urate to allantoin and excrete it cleanly. We don't.

**Measurement**: enzymatic colorimetric assay using **uricase** (yes, we use the enzyme we ourselves lack). Uric acid + O₂ → allantoin + H₂O₂; H₂O₂ then drives a peroxidase chromogenic reaction. Highly accurate and specific.

### 5.2 Physiology

Purine metabolism: nucleic acid breakdown → hypoxanthine → xanthine → **uric acid** (xanthine oxidase). ~⅔ of urate is excreted renally (URAT1 reabsorbs in proximal tubule; ABCG2, OAT4 secrete); ~⅓ via gut.

**Solubility**: uric acid is poorly soluble in plasma. **Saturation point ~6.8 mg/dL at body temperature/pH** ([Hyperuricemia StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK459218/); [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/17808-hyperuricemia-high-uric-acid-level)). At 7.4 pH, ~98% exists as the monosodium urate (MSU) anion. Above the solubility limit, MSU crystals can precipitate in joints (gout), soft tissues (tophi), and renal tubules (uric acid stones).

**Antioxidant role**: Ames et al., PNAS 1981 ([Ames PNAS 1981](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.78.11.6858)) proposed that uric acid is one of the major aqueous antioxidants in human plasma — concentration ~300 µM, considerably higher than ascorbate. Urate scavenges singlet oxygen, peroxyl radicals, and oxo-heme oxidants; protects erythrocytes from peroxidative damage. The hypothesis was that the loss of uricase in great apes was a positive selection event giving us a longevity-associated antioxidant boost. **Provenance**: this is a real PNAS paper widely cited; the **antioxidant function in vitro is well-established**, but the in vivo significance is contested. More recent work (e.g., [Tandf 2025](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13510002.2025.2498105); [Sci Rep 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-86962-0)) suggests uric acid is **paradoxically pro-oxidant intracellularly** despite extracellular antioxidant activity — generating superoxide via NADPH oxidase activation in adipocytes and endothelial cells. Net effect is context-dependent.

### 5.3 Reference and optimal ranges (men)

| Frame | Uric acid, mg/dL |
|---|---|
| Lab "normal" men | 3.5–7.2 ([Medscape Uric Acid](https://emedicine.medscape.com/article/2088516-overview)) |
| MSU saturation limit | 6.8 (above → crystallization risk) |
| Gout treatment target | <6.0 ([Postgrad Med 2016 target review](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00325481.2016.1221732)) |
| Lowest mortality in men (Korean cohort) | 4.0–4.9 ([Sci Rep 2020](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-63134-0)) |
| Lean young male target | 3.5–5.5 |
| ↑ Mortality threshold | ≥7 mg/dL (and <3.0 mg/dL) ([U-shape CV death, Frontiers 2022](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/cardiovascular-medicine/articles/10.3389/fcvm.2022.858889/full)) |

### 5.4 What HIGH values indicate

**Hyperuricemia ≥6.8 mg/dL** (men, varies slightly by lab):

- **Diet-driven** — purines (organ meats, sardines, anchovies, mussels, scallops), **fructose** (esp. from sugar-sweetened beverages/HFCS), **alcohol** (beer > spirits >>> wine; beer doubly hits because guanosine-rich)
- **Gout** — clinical syndrome; ~10–20% of hyperuricemics develop gout
- **Uric acid nephrolithiasis** — accounts for ~10% of all kidney stones; classically in acidic urine ([StatPearls NBK560726](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK560726/))
- **Metabolic syndrome / NAFLD / insulin resistance** — bidirectional association; insulin reduces renal urate excretion via URAT1 stimulation
- **Obesity, hypertension** — strongly associated; hyperuricemia precedes hypertension in many longitudinal cohorts
- **Diuretics** — esp. thiazides, loop diuretics (compete for URAT1)
- **Low-dose aspirin** (<300 mg/d) — reduces excretion
- **Cyclosporine, tacrolimus, pyrazinamide, ethambutol, niacin**
- **Lead poisoning** — saturnine gout
- **CKD** — reduced excretion
- **Lymphoproliferative / myeloproliferative disorders**, polycythemia vera, hemolytic anemia — ↑ purine turnover
- **Tumor lysis syndrome** — massive cell death post-chemo; can spike urate >15 mg/dL with acute kidney injury ([Clin Kidney J 2008](https://academic.oup.com/ckj/article/1/5/307/415539))
- **Lesch-Nyhan syndrome** — X-linked HPRT1 deficiency; severe hyperuricemia, intellectual disability, self-mutilation; rare ([NCBI Bookshelf NBK556079](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK556079/))
- **Psoriasis** — increased epidermal turnover
- **Genetic variants** — SLC2A9, ABCG2 polymorphisms account for substantial heritability of urate

### 5.5 Fructose — uric acid — metabolic syndrome connection

This is the **Johnson hypothesis**, evolved from work in the early 2000s through ongoing reviews ([Johnson group review, Tandf 2024](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10408398.2024.2392150); [PMC10083579 fructose DNL](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10083579/); [Imrpress 2019](https://www.imrpress.com/journal/FBL/24/2/10.2741/4713)).

**Mechanism (in depth)**:

1. Fructose enters the liver via GLUT2; phosphorylated by **fructokinase C (KHK-C)** to fructose-1-phosphate.
2. KHK-C reaction is **not feedback-regulated** (unlike hexokinase) — it consumes ATP rapidly, producing AMP.
3. AMP is degraded via AMP deaminase → IMP → inosine → hypoxanthine → **xanthine → uric acid**.
4. Uric acid then **causes endoplasmic reticulum stress** in hepatocytes, activating SREBP-1c.
5. SREBP-1c upregulates lipogenic enzymes (ACC, FASN, SCD1) → **de novo lipogenesis** → hepatic triglyceride accumulation → NAFLD.
6. Uric acid also inhibits AMPK and stimulates fructokinase further → positive feedback loop.
7. Mitochondrial dysfunction: uric acid generates ROS via NOX4, impairs aconitase, shifts metabolism toward lipogenesis.

**Quantitative**: intake >50 g fructose/day reliably induces hyperuricemia, IR, mitochondrial dysfunction, and de novo lipogenesis ([fructose review 2020 PMC](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0753332220309884); [Tandf 2024](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10408398.2024.2392150)).

**For a 20M**: **uric acid is a useful early flag for fructose excess and incipient metabolic syndrome**, often elevated before glucose, A1c, or insulin show abnormalities. The pathway is well-mechanism-supported in animal and human studies.

**Provenance**: the broad fructose→urate→IR→NAFLD pathway is mainstream; the specific Johnson framing (uric acid as a *driver* not just a marker, and clinical urate-lowering as IR therapy) is partially mainstream — the urate-lowering-for-IR clinical trials have been mixed.

### 5.6 What LOW values indicate

- **SIADH** — dilutional + increased urate clearance
- **Fanconi syndrome** — proximal tubule dysfunction; loses urate, glucose, amino acids, phosphate
- **Severe liver disease** — reduced purine production
- **Wilson's disease** — secondary Fanconi
- **Allopurinol / febuxostat** (xanthine oxidase inhibitors) — therapeutic
- **Probenecid, lesinurad, benzbromarone** (uricosurics) — therapeutic
- **Low-purine, low-fructose diet**
- **Pregnancy** (first/second trimester — increased renal clearance)
- **Hereditary xanthinuria** (XO deficiency) — very rare
- **High-dose salicylates** (>2 g/d — uricosuric; opposite of low-dose)

### 5.7 Direction of "better" — U-shaped

Multiple cohorts show a J/U-shape vs. all-cause and CV mortality:

- Korean nationwide cohort: lowest hazard 4.0–4.9 mg/dL in men; ↑ mortality both ≥7 and <3 mg/dL ([Sci Rep 2020](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-63134-0))
- Coronary artery disease cohort (33,034): U-shape, with reference 5.95–6.8 mg/dL; below 5.0 also showed ↑ HR ([Frontiers Cardiovasc Med 2022](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/cardiovascular-medicine/articles/10.3389/fcvm.2022.858889/full))
- Hypertensive cohort: stroke mortality rose with low UA; cardiac mortality with high UA ([Hypertension stroke/heart 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33534782/))

**Caveat**: most "low UA → mortality" associations are confounded by liver disease, malnutrition, severe illness, cachexia. Whether **healthy** low uric acid is itself harmful remains controversial — consistent with the Ames antioxidant hypothesis but not proven causal.

**Practical for a 20M**: target **3.5–5.5 mg/dL**. Above 6.0–6.5 → reduce fructose (especially SSBs), reduce alcohol, reconsider purine load. Below 3.0 → unusual; consider Fanconi/SIADH/severe liver disease workup if persistent.

### 5.8 Confounders

- **Recent purine-rich meal** — modest acute rise
- **Fructose / SSB intake** in the prior 24–48 h — can transiently raise
- **Alcohol** in the prior 24 h — raises
- **Intense exercise** — transient rise from purine catabolism in muscle (AMP → IMP → hypoxanthine pathway); excretion increases after exercise ([Med Sci Sports Exerc 1988](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3343917/); [Frontiers Sports 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11348809/))
- **Fasting / ketosis** — paradoxically raises serum urate by inhibiting renal excretion (β-hydroxybutyrate competes for URAT1) ([Metabolism 1976](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0026049576900093))
- **Dehydration** — concentrates
- **Diuretic use** (thiazides especially)
- **Aspirin dose**: low (<300 mg) raises; high (>2 g) lowers
- **Recent rapid weight loss / tissue catabolism** — raises

## 6. Integrated interpretation for a 20-year-old male

### 6.1 What "good metabolic health at 20" looks like

| Marker | Lean young M target | Lab "normal" |
|---|---|---|
| Fasting glucose | 70–90 mg/dL | 70–99 |
| HbA1c | 4.8–5.3% | <5.7% |
| Fasting insulin | 2–6 µIU/mL | 2.6–24.9 |
| HOMA-IR | <1.0 | <2.0–2.7 (population-dependent) |
| Leptin | 1–5 ng/mL | 0.5–13.8 |
| Uric acid | 3.5–5.5 mg/dL | 3.5–7.2 |
| TG/HDL ratio (companion) | <1.5 (Caucasian); higher acceptable in some Asian populations | varies |
| C-peptide (companion) | 1–2 ng/mL fasting | 1.1–4.4 |

### 6.2 Patterns and what they mean

| Pattern | Likely interpretation |
|---|---|
| Normal glucose + normal A1c + insulin 8–15 µIU/mL | **Early hyperinsulinemia / Kraft pattern II** — most actionable finding; insulin resistance is brewing despite normal glucose ([Crofts JMH](https://journalofmetabolichealth.org/index.php/jmh/article/view/49/180)) |
| Normal glucose + A1c 5.6–5.8% + normal insulin | Possibly iron deficiency, hemoglobinopathy, splenic dysfunction (consider CBC, ferritin, hemoglobin electrophoresis) |
| Normal glucose + A1c <4.5% | Consider hemolysis (haptoglobin, LDH, retic count), liver disease (LFTs), or recent transfusion |
| Glucose 95–105 + insulin 4 + A1c 5.0% | Probably normal physiology / dawn phenomenon variant |
| Uric acid 6.5–7.5 in a lean 20M | High fructose intake (SSBs, fruit juice), alcohol, possibly genetic (SLC2A9, ABCG2); cardiometabolic flag well before glucose moves |
| Leptin 10–15 ng/mL in lean 20M | Disproportionate to body fat → workup for visceral adiposity, NAFLD; consider DEXA, hepatic ultrasound, liver enzymes |
| Glucose <60 fasting | Repeat to confirm; if persistent, evaluate for insulinoma, adrenal insufficiency, recent alcohol, drug effects |

### 6.3 What's missing from this 5-marker panel

To round out metabolic phenotyping in a young man:

- **C-peptide** — disambiguates insulin
- **Triglycerides + HDL** — dyslipidemia and TG/HDL ratio
- **ALT, AST, GGT** — NAFLD / metabolic-associated steatotic liver disease screening
- **hs-CRP** — low-grade inflammation
- **Ferritin** — A1c confounder and metabolic marker
- **DEXA / waist circumference / VAT** — body composition (BMI is misleading at 20)
- **CGM (2-week burst)** — post-prandial picture
- **OGTT with paired insulin** — Kraft testing if insulin is borderline
