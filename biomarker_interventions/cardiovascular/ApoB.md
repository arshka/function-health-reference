# Apolipoprotein B (ApoB) — Intervention Research

> **Highest-leverage non-pharm stack:** Saturated-fat reduction (≤7% kcal) + viscous fiber (10–15 g/d psyllium or β-glucan) + Portfolio-diet plant-protein swap together drop ApoB ~20% (≈−20 mg/dL from baseline 100). Add a high-intensity statin and ApoB falls 50–60% from untreated baseline; add ezetimibe and PCSK9i and the ceiling reaches −75–80%.

**Cross-link:** Biology and clinical interpretation in `../heart_lipids_basic.md` §"ApoB" and `../heart_lipids_advanced.md`. Existing effect-size summary in `../effect_sizes_cardiovascular.md` lines 33–66. This file is INTERVENTION-only and expands to per-food / per-supplement / per-wearable detail.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT (≥100 participants or pivotal design) |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology / physiology extrapolation |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial, unreplicated |

Population caveat: most evidence is NW European adults aged 40+ with elevated baseline ApoB (~100–130 mg/dL). Floor effects in lean 20-something males with ApoB <80 typically shrink the absolute effects 30–60%; report magnitudes as percentages where possible.

## 1. Foods that LOWER ApoB

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Oats / oat bran (β-glucan) | 3 g/d β-glucan (~70 g dry oats or 40 g oat bran) | ApoB −4–7% (LDL-C −0.20 mmol/L; ApoB tracks ~0.6×) | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Whitehead et al., *Am J Clin Nutr* 2014;100:1413; Ho et al., *Br J Nutr* 2016 PMC5394769 | Plateau above 5 g/d β-glucan; effect attenuates if oats cooked with high-SFA dairy (butter, cream) |
| Barley (β-glucan) | 3–8 g/d β-glucan | ApoB −4–8% | 6–12 wk | **[Meta]** | AbuMweis et al., *Eur J Clin Nutr* 2010;64:1472 (8 RCTs) | Same mechanism as oat β-glucan; can be used interchangeably |
| Psyllium husk | 10–15 g/d (5 g TID with meals) | ApoB −7–10% (LDL-C −13 mg/dL pooled) | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Jovanovski et al., *Am J Clin Nutr* 2018;108:922; Gibb et al., *AJCN* 2015;102:1604 | Bile-acid sequestration; stacks additively with statins and Portfolio diet (different mechanism than sterols) |
| Soy protein (whole soy) | 25 g/d isolated soy protein replacing animal protein | ApoB −5–7% (LDL-C −3–4%) | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Blanco-Rojo et al., *J Nutr* 2020 (43 RCTs); FDA health claim review 2017 | Effect is from displacement of animal protein/SFA, not isoflavones per se; tofu, tempeh, edamame, soy milk all qualify |
| Almonds | 45–85 g/d replacing carbohydrate snacks | ApoB −3–7%; LDL-C −5.79 mg/dL pooled | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Lee-Bravatti et al., *J Am Heart Assoc* 2019;8:e010422 (15 RCTs, n=534) | Substitute, do not add — caloric displacement matters; MUFA + viscous fiber dual mechanism |
| Walnuts | 30–60 g/d | ApoB −5%; LDL-C ~−7 mg/dL | 8 wk | **[Meta]** | Guasch-Ferré et al., *Nutr Metab Cardiovasc Dis* 2024 (113 trials); Banel & Hu, *AJCN* 2009 | ALA + PUFA-rich; modest TG reduction; PREDIMED nut arm reduced events |
| Pistachios | 40–80 g/d | ApoB −5%; LDL-C −9 mg/dL; non-HDL-C −12 mg/dL | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Hernández-Alonso et al., *Nutr Rev* 2019;77:691 | Pistachios additionally lower oxidized-LDL and improve HDL function |
| Hazelnuts | 30–70 g/d | ApoB −4–6%; LDL-C −0.16 mmol/L | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Perna et al., *Nutrients* 2016;8:747 | High MUFA; less data than almonds/walnuts |
| Plant sterols / stanols (in margarine, fortified yogurt, supplement) | 2 g/d (usually 3 servings of fortified spread) | ApoB −7–10% (LDL-C −10%) | 3–4 wk | **[Meta]** | Ras et al., *Eur J Nutr* 2014;53:1557; Musa-Veloso et al., *Lipids* 2011 (41 trials) | Dose-response: 1 g→−6%, 2 g→−10%, 3 g→−12%; plateaus above 3 g; ApoB:LDL-C reduction ratio ~0.8 |
| Chia seeds / flaxseed (ground) | 25–30 g/d ground flaxseed | ApoB −5–10%; LDL-C −10% | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Ursoniu et al., *Pharmacol Res* 2016;103:35 (28 RCTs flaxseed) | Whole flaxseed not absorbed — must be ground; combines viscous fiber + ALA + lignans |
| Beans / legumes (chickpeas, black beans, lentils) | 130 g/d cooked (~½ cup dry, ≥4 servings/wk) | ApoB −4–6%; LDL-C −5–8 mg/dL | 6 wk | **[Meta]** | Ha et al., *CMAJ* 2014;186:E252 (26 RCTs, n=1,037) | Independent of weight loss; resistant starch + soluble fiber mechanism |
| Avocado (whole) | 1 medium fruit/d (~150 g) substituted for SFA | ApoB −4–7%; LDL-C −13 mg/dL in moderate-to-high baseline | 5 wk | **[Meta]** | Mahmassani et al., *AJCN* 2018;107:523 (10 RCTs); Pacheco et al., *J Am Heart Assoc* 2022 | Effect driven by MUFA replacing SFA; no benefit if added to existing SFA-heavy diet |
| Olive oil (extra virgin) | 25–50 mL/d replacing butter / coconut / palm oil | ApoB −5–8%; LDL-C −7–10 mg/dL when replacing SFA | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Schwingshackl et al., *Lipids Health Dis* 2014;13:154; PREDIMED 5-year RCT | EVOO polyphenols reduce oxidized-LDL independent of LDL-C drop (EUROLIVE, *Ann Intern Med* 2006) |
| Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines) | 2 servings/wk (~250 g/wk total) | ApoB ±0–2% (modest); TG −10–20% | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Mozaffarian & Wu, *J Am Coll Cardiol* 2011;58:2047 | EPA/DHA per serving ~1–1.5 g; ApoB effect minimal — value is in TG and remnant cholesterol; DHA can mildly raise LDL-C particle size (not number) |
| Garlic (raw or aged extract) | 600–1200 mg aged garlic extract or 4 g raw/d | ApoB −2–4%; TC −17 mg/dL pooled; LDL-C −9 mg/dL | 8–24 wk | **[Meta]** | Ried et al., *J Nutr* 2016;146:389S; Sun et al., *Phytother Res* 2018 | Modest, heterogeneous; aged extract better tolerated; not statin-strength |
| Green tea (catechins, EGCG) | 3–5 cups/d or 200–400 mg EGCG | ApoB −3–5%; LDL-C −2.6 mg/dL | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Zheng et al., *AJCN* 2011;94:601; Onakpoya et al., *Nutr Metab Cardiovasc Dis* 2014 | Effect modest; mechanism via reduced cholesterol absorption + LDLR upregulation |
| Whole grains (intact, not flour) | ≥3 servings/d (~90 g) | ApoB −3–6%; LDL-C −3–5 mg/dL | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Hollænder et al., *AJCN* 2015;102:556 (24 RCTs) | Effect driven by β-glucan content and displacement of refined grains; intact-kernel forms (oats, barley, brown rice, quinoa, bulgur) > flour-based |
| Apples / pears (pectin) | 2 fresh apples/d or equivalent pectin 6 g/d | ApoB −4%; LDL-C −7 mg/dL | 8 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Koutsos et al., *AJCN* 2020;111:307 (1 RCT, n=40) | Pectin is a soluble fiber; mechanism similar to psyllium |
| Cocoa (high-flavanol dark chocolate) | 50–100 g/d 70%+ dark chocolate; or cocoa flavanols 200–500 mg/d | ApoB −2–3%; LDL-C −5 mg/dL | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Tokede et al., *Eur J Clin Nutr* 2011;65:879 (10 RCTs) | Effect modest; calorie cost real (~500 kcal/100 g); polyphenols additionally reduce oxLDL |
| Whole eggs (substituting for refined breakfast carbs in lean individuals) | 2–3/d | ApoB ±0% (no change in ~⅔ of population — "hypo-responders"); +5–10% in dietary cholesterol "hyper-responders" | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Vincent et al., *J Am Coll Nutr* 2018; Berger et al., *AJCN* 2015 | Egg-cholesterol response highly variable; LXR/SREBP-2 downregulation in hypo-responders. Better to test individually with fasting ApoB before/after 6-wk trial. |

## 2. Foods that RAISE ApoB

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Coconut oil | 30 g/d (substituting MUFA/PUFA) | ApoB +6–10%; LDL-C +10–15 mg/dL vs PUFA control | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Neelakantan et al., *Circulation* 2020;141:803 (16 RCTs); Eyres et al., *Nutr Rev* 2016 | ~85% SFA (lauric, myristic); raises both LDL-C and HDL-C but ApoB effect is unfavorable; popular health-food positioning is unsupported |
| Butter / ghee (saturated dairy fat) | 30–50 g/d replacing oil | ApoB +5–8%; LDL-C +0.16 mmol/L per 5% kcal SFA | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Mensink, WHO 2016 systematic review; Hooper, *Cochrane* 2020 | Per 1% kcal SFA→PUFA swap: LDL-C −2.1 mg/dL, ApoB −1.3 mg/dL; ghee = clarified butter, no nutritional advantage over butter for lipids |
| Palm oil / palm kernel oil | Common in ultra-processed foods | ApoB +5–8%; LDL-C +6–10 mg/dL vs MUFA | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Sun et al., *Lipids Health Dis* 2015;14:147 | ~50% SFA (palmitic 16:0, the most LDL-raising fatty acid); ubiquitous in baked goods, instant noodles, processed snacks |
| Industrial trans fat (partially hydrogenated oil) | 2% kcal iTFA | ApoB +8%; LDL-C +3.4%, HDL-C −6.8%, Lp(a) +25% | 4–8 wk | **[RCT]** | Mensink & Katan, *NEJM* 1990;323:439; Mozaffarian et al., *NEJM* 2006 PMC2830458 | Now banned in US (2018); residual risk via imported foods, restaurant fryers, vanaspati ghee |
| Processed red meat (sausage, bacon, salami, deli meats) | 50–100 g/d | ApoB +5–10%; LDL-C +6–12 mg/dL | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Guasch-Ferré et al., *Circulation* 2019;139:1828 (36 RCTs) | Compared to plant protein: ApoB clearly higher. Compared to fresh red meat: similar lipid effects but processed has worse CV outcomes (cohort) |
| Fatty cuts of beef / lamb / pork | ≥150 g/d | ApoB +3–8%; LDL-C +5–10 mg/dL | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Guasch-Ferré et al., *Circulation* 2019 | Mostly SFA-mediated; lean cuts (sirloin, tenderloin) have smaller effect |
| Cheese (hard, full-fat) | 100 g/d full-fat cheddar | ApoB +3–6%; LDL-C +5–8 mg/dL vs butter-equivalent SFA but **less than butter** | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | de Goede et al., *J Am Heart Assoc* 2015;4:e002787 | Cheese matrix effect: calcium soaps and milk-fat globule membrane reduce cholesterol absorption by ~10–20%, so effect is smaller than predicted from SFA content |
| Refined baked goods (pastries, croissants, cookies) | Daily | ApoB +5–10%; LDL-C +10–20 mg/dL | 4–12 wk | **[Cohort]** | EPIC and NHS cohorts; Mozaffarian et al. *NEJM* 2011 | Combined SFA + trans-fat + refined carb; the worst single food category for ApoB |
| Cream / heavy whipping cream | 50 mL/d | ApoB +3–5%; LDL-C +5–7 mg/dL | 4–8 wk | **[RCT]** | Lefevre et al., *AJCN* 2005;82:957 | High SFA milk fat; smaller effect than butter due to matrix |
| Whole-milk dairy in excess | >500 mL/d full-fat milk | ApoB +2–5%; LDL-C +3–5 mg/dL | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Benatar et al., *PLOS One* 2013;8:e76480 | Modest; low-fat milk neutral; some cohort data show neutral CVD outcomes despite ApoB rise — matrix effect debated |
| MCT oil (medium-chain triglyceride supplements) | 30 g/d | ApoB +5%; LDL-C +6–10 mg/dL | 4–8 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Mumme & Stonehouse, *J Acad Nutr Diet* 2015;115:249 | Marketed as "metabolism boost" in keto/biohacker space; raises ApoB with no compensating benefit |
| Sugar-sweetened beverages (especially HFCS) | ≥600 mL/d | ApoB +5–10%; TG ↑ 30%; LDL-C ↑ slightly via remnant accumulation | 8–10 wk | **[RCT]** | Stanhope et al., *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2011;96:E1596 | Hypercaloric fructose drives de novo lipogenesis and VLDL secretion → more ApoB-containing particles |
| Excess alcohol (binge or chronic >2 drinks/d) | >40 g/d ethanol | ApoB ±0% direct, but VLDL-ApoB ↑ in heavy drinkers; LDL-C may drop while remnants rise | 4–12 wk | **[Cohort]** | Van de Wiel, *Int J Vasc Med* 2012 | LDL-C is misleading in heavy drinkers — measure ApoB or non-HDL-C directly |
| Fried foods (deep-fried in re-used oils) | ≥4 servings/wk | ApoB +5–8%; oxLDL ↑ | 8–12 wk | **[Cohort]** | Cahill et al., *Circulation* 2014;130:18 | Mechanism: oxidized lipids + repeated heating creates aldehydes and trans isomers |

## 3. Supplements

### Lowering / improving

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Psyllium husk powder | 10–15 g/d split into 2–3 doses with water | ApoB −7–10%; LDL-C −13 mg/dL | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Jovanovski et al., *AJCN* 2018; Gibb et al., *AJCN* 2015 | Take ≥2 h apart from medications (binds drug absorption); start low (5 g) for GI tolerability |
| Plant sterols / stanols (Benecol, CardioWise, generic) | 2 g/d, taken with the largest fat-containing meal | ApoB −7–10%; LDL-C −10% | 3–4 wk | **[Meta]** | Ras 2014; Musa-Veloso 2011 | Does not stack with ezetimibe (same mechanism); modest stacking with statins (different mechanisms) |
| Red yeast rice (standardized monacolin K) | 3–10 mg monacolin K (~600–2400 mg RYR) | ApoB −15–25%; LDL-C −15–35 mg/dL (depending on dose) | 8–24 wk | **[Meta]** | Banach et al., *Pharmacol Res* 2022 PMC8802088; Cicero et al., *Pharmacol Res* 2019 | Chemically = lovastatin; monacolin-K content varies wildly across brands; FDA limits OTC to <2 mg in US (most products non-compliant); test for citrinin contamination; same myopathy/hepatotoxicity profile as statins |
| Berberine HCl | 500 mg TID with meals (1500 mg/d) | ApoB ~−10–15% (inferred from LDL-C and TG); LDL-C −15–19 mg/dL; TG −25–33 mg/dL | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Kong et al., *J Clin Lipidol* 2013;7:344 (16 RCTs); Zamani et al., *Pharmacol Res* 2019 | Mechanism: LDLR upregulation + AMPK activation + reduced hepatic VLDL secretion; ~80% of trials in Chinese populations; transportability uncertain; GI side effects common; CYP3A4 inhibitor (drug interactions) |
| Bergamot polyphenol extract (BPF) | 500–1500 mg/d standardized | ApoB −15–20%; LDL-C −15–30%; HDL-C +5–10% | 8–24 wk | **[Meta]** | Lamiquiz-Moneo et al., *Pharmacol Res* 2020;156:104783 (12 trials); Huang et al., *Front Nutr* 2024 | Most trials are Italian (single research group, conflict-of-interest concerns); naringin + brutieridin are claimed HMG-CoA partial inhibitors (mechanism analogy to statins); independent replication thin |
| Soluble fiber blends (acacia, glucomannan, guar) | 5–10 g/d | ApoB −3–6%; LDL-C −5 mg/dL per 5 g/d soluble fiber | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Brown et al., *AJCN* 1999;69:30 (67 trials); Soliman, *Nutrients* 2019;11:1155 | Glucomannan: 3 g/d → LDL-C −10%; viscosity is the active property |
| Marine omega-3 (EPA+DHA, prescription or high-purity OTC) | 2–4 g/d EPA+DHA | ApoB ±0–3% (DHA can mildly raise particle size, ApoB usually unchanged); TG −20–45% | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | AHA Science Advisory, *Circulation* 2019;140:e673; JAHA 2023 dose-response | EPA-only (icosapent ethyl) does not raise LDL-C; DHA can raise LDL-C +3% — choose EPA-dominant products if LDL-C/ApoB is the target |
| Niacin (immediate-release) | 1.5–2 g/d | ApoB −15–25%; LDL-C −15–18%; HDL-C +20% | 8–16 wk | **[Meta]** | Carlson, *J Intern Med* 2005;258:94; AIM-HIGH, HPS2-THRIVE | **No CV outcomes benefit** in AIM-HIGH and HPS2-THRIVE despite ApoB drop; flushing, hepatotoxicity, glucose elevation; not first-line; caveat: niacin moves ApoB but the outcomes data argue against using it as a target-driven therapy |
| Pantethine (active form B5) | 600–900 mg/d | ApoB −10–15%; LDL-C −10%; TG −15% | 12–16 wk | **[Meta]** | Evans et al., *Vasc Health Risk Manag* 2014;10:89 (28 trials, mostly small Italian/Japanese) | Heterogeneous evidence base; mechanism via CoA pathway, ACC inhibition; modern, large-scale RCTs lacking |
| Curcumin (high-bioavailability formulations) | 500–1500 mg/d (Meriva, BCM-95) | ApoB ~−5%; LDL-C −7–8 mg/dL (heterogeneous) | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Simental-Mendía et al., *Pharmacol Res* 2019;141:300 (20 RCTs) | Effect inconsistent; bioavailability the limiting factor; better evidence for inflammation than for ApoB |
| Artichoke leaf extract | 600 mg/d standardized | ApoB ~−4%; TC −17 mg/dL; LDL-C −15 mg/dL | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Sahebkar et al., *Pharmacol Res* 2018;128:24 (9 RCTs) | Modest; mostly European trials; mechanism: HMG-CoA partial inhibition + bile acid increased excretion |
| Spirulina | 1–8 g/d | ApoB −3–5%; LDL-C −10 mg/dL pooled | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Serban et al., *Clin Nutr* 2016;35:842 (7 RCTs) | Heterogeneous; small trials; mechanism unclear |

## 4. Activities & Behaviors

| Activity | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Aerobic exercise (continuous) | ≥150 min/wk moderate (zone 2; 65–75% HRmax) | ApoB −2–5 mg/dL (~−3–5%) | 12–26 wk | **[Meta]** | Holme et al., *J Intern Med* 2007 (Oslo DOSE); Wang et al., *PMC10036419* 2023 aerobic-exercise meta-analysis | Greatest effect when paired with weight loss or caloric deficit; standalone effect is modest |
| Resistance training | 3 sessions/wk, ≥8 exercises, full body | ApoB −2–4%; ApoB:ApoA1 ratio improves more than ApoB alone | 12–24 wk | **[Meta]** | Costa et al., *Sports Med* 2019;49:95 | Effect is independent of aerobic; combined aerobic + resistance is additive (~−5–7%) |
| HIIT (high-intensity interval training) | 3×/wk, 4×4 min @ 85–95% VO₂max + 3 min recovery | ApoB −5–8%; comparable or slightly better than continuous moderate aerobic per minute spent | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Wewege et al., *Obes Rev* 2017;18:635 | Greatest effect in those starting low VO₂max; HIIT does not appear to lower ApoB more than equivalent-volume moderate aerobic in lean trained individuals |
| NEAT / step count increase | +4000 steps/d above sedentary baseline | ApoB −3–5% (when accompanied by weight loss); minimal without weight change | 12–24 wk | **[Cohort]** | Bassett et al., *Med Sci Sports Exerc* 2010;42:S20 | Effect mediated mostly through caloric expenditure / weight; step count alone is weaker than structured exercise per minute |
| Sleep duration optimization (7–9 h vs <6 h chronic) | 7–9 h/night | ApoB indirect effect via insulin resistance / weight; chronic sleep restriction (<6 h) raises ApoB ~3–5% (cohort) | 4–12 wk for sleep changes; lipid changes lag | **[Cohort]** | Kruisbrink et al., *BMJ Open* 2017;7:e018585 | Sleep deprivation increases ghrelin / decreases leptin → weight gain → ApoB rise; correcting sleep helps lipids primarily via weight & insulin sensitivity |
| Sleep regularity (consistent bedtime ±30 min) | Sleep regularity index (SRI) > 75 | ApoB indirectly improved; lipid effect not directly RCT-tested | Chronic | **[Cohort]** | Lunsford-Avery et al., *Sci Rep* 2018;8:14158 | Irregular sleep → metabolic syndrome → ApoB; effect mediated, not direct |
| Stress reduction (MBSR, meditation) | 8-wk program, 30 min/d | ApoB ±0–small reduction; LDL-C −5 mg/dL in some trials | 8–24 wk | **[Meta]** | Robert McComb et al., *J Altern Complement Med* 2004; Levine et al. AHA Scientific Statement 2017 | Effect small and confounded by lifestyle changes that often accompany meditation (better sleep, less alcohol) |
| Sauna (Finnish dry sauna) | 4–7 sessions/wk, 15–20 min @ 80–100°C | ApoB ±0–small reduction; LDL-C −5–8 mg/dL in 1 small RCT | 8–12 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Pizzey et al., *Exp Physiol* 2021;106:1317; Laukkanen Kuopio cohort (CV mortality association) | Cohort data on CV mortality strong (Laukkanen 2015); RCT lipid data sparse; mechanism via repeated heat shock → endothelial function |
| Cold exposure (cold plunge, cryotherapy) | 2–5 min @ 10–15°C, 3×/wk | ApoB ±0%; brief LDL-C drop of unclear durability; brown adipose tissue activation theoretical | Variable | **[Mechanism]** | Søberg et al., *Cell Rep Med* 2021 (small n=8); brown-fat literature | No durable lipid RCT data; physiological effects (norepinephrine, BAT activation) do not translate to consistent ApoB reductions |
| Alcohol intake | <14 g/d men | ApoB ±0%; HDL-C +5 mg/dL at 30 g/d (offset by TG and metabolic harm); >40 g/d raises VLDL-ApoB | Acute and chronic | **[Meta]** | Rimm et al., *NEJM* 1993; MR analyses Holmes et al., *BMJ* 2014 | MR: even moderate alcohol associated with no causal CV benefit; **do not start drinking** for lipids; current drinkers don't gain ApoB benefit from moderate intake |
| Smoking cessation | — | ApoB −5–7% over 12–24 months (mostly via inflammation / oxidation reduction); HDL-C +3.9 mg/dL more reliable | 12–24 mo | **[Meta]** | Forey et al., *Biomark Res* 2013;1:31; Eliasson et al., *Prev Med* 2003 | Indirect effect on ApoB; direct effect modest; but smoking is a strong oxidative stressor making ApoB more atherogenic per mg/dL |
| Caffeine (filtered coffee) | 3–4 cups/d filtered | ApoB ±0%; LDL-C ±0% | — | **[Meta]** | Cai et al., *Eur J Clin Nutr* 2012;66:872 | Filtered coffee: no lipid effect. Unfiltered (French press, espresso, Turkish) — see next row |
| Unfiltered coffee (cafestol/kahweol) | 5+ cups/d unfiltered (French press, Turkish, espresso, boiled Scandinavian) | ApoB +5–10%; LDL-C +6–18 mg/dL (dose-dependent) | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Cai 2012; Jee et al., *AJCN* 2001;73:532 | Cafestol + kahweol diterpenes downregulate hepatic LDLR; paper filter removes them; espresso retains a moderate amount; this is the single most overlooked dietary ApoB elevator in coffee drinkers |
| Time-restricted eating (16:8) | 8-h feeding window | ApoB ±0% direct; small reduction (−3–5%) when accompanied by weight loss | 12–24 wk | **[Meta]** | Cienfuegos et al., *Cell Metab* 2020;32:366; Lin et al., *Ann Intern Med* 2024 | Effect mediated through caloric reduction; without weight loss, no lipid effect |
| Prolonged fasting (24–72 h) | Single bouts 24–72 h | ApoB acute drop −10–20% during fasting; rebounds within days of refeeding | Acute | **[Mechanism]** | Multiple metabolic ward studies | Not durable; useful only as part of repeated cycle that reduces total energy intake |
| Hydration | 2–3 L water/d | ApoB ±0% (no direct effect) | — | **[Mechanism]** | No RCT evidence for hydration affecting lipids | Hydration affects hemoconcentration (small artifactual variation in measured concentrations) but no biological intervention effect |
| Standing / posture (NEAT increase via standing desk) | +2 h/d standing vs sitting | ApoB ±0% direct; small TG reduction in lean | 12 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Healy et al., *AHA Position* 2016 | Standing alone is insufficient stimulus; effect needs to come from added walking/movement |

## 5. Wearable-Trackable Metric Relationships

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention (non-pharm):** Saturated fat reduction from typical Western (~12–15% kcal) to <7% kcal, achieved by replacing butter/cheese/coconut/red meat with EVOO/nuts/avocado/fish. Effect: ApoB −10–15% in 8–12 wk in elevated-baseline subjects, ~−5–7% in lean baseline.
- **Best stack of 3 (additive, ~70% of linear sum):**
  1. SFA reduction to <7% kcal
  2. Viscous fiber 10–15 g/d (psyllium 10 g + oat β-glucan 5 g)
  3. Plant sterols 2 g/d **OR** Portfolio diet pattern (nuts 45 g + soy 25 g)
  - Combined expected delta: ApoB −20–25% from elevated baseline (e.g., 110 → 85); −10–15% from lean baseline (e.g., 80 → 68)
- **Realistic 12-week delta for healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (ApoB ~70 mg/dL):** −5 to −10 mg/dL (−7–14%) with maximum lifestyle intervention
- **Realistic 12-week delta for elevated baseline (ApoB >100 mg/dL):** −15 to −25 mg/dL (−15–25%) with the 3-stack above
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:** ApoB >90 mg/dL persisting >6 months with maximum lifestyle, or ApoB >80 with elevated Lp(a), strong family history, or coronary calcium >0. Statin (rosuvastatin 5–10 mg) adds another −30–40% on top of the lifestyle ceiling.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- The MASALA cohort (Kanaya et al., UCSF) found that South Asian individuals carry a characteristic dyslipidemia phenotype: **lower HDL-C, higher TG, modestly elevated LDL-C, but disproportionately high ApoB** relative to LDL-C. This means the LDL-C → ApoB inference systematically *under-estimates* atherogenic particle load in SA individuals; ApoB or non-HDL-C is the more accurate target.
- **NLA 2021 statement** (Patel et al., *J Clin Lipidol* 2021;15:402) explicitly recommends measuring ApoB (not LDL-C alone) in South Asians given the discordance.
- **START-India** open-label atorvastatin 20 mg in SA subjects produced ~−43% LDL-C, similar to non-SA (~−41%) — statin pharmacodynamics are preserved in SA individuals. No specific ApoB outcome reported.
- **Lean SA males with normal BMI (<23) but elevated ApoB**: Often driven by insulin resistance + visceral adiposity. Carbohydrate reduction + resistance training (to add lean mass and improve insulin sensitivity) may give better ApoB response per unit weight loss than caloric restriction alone.
- **Genetic context:** Higher prevalence of *APOE* ε3/ε4 and ε4/ε4 in some SA sub-populations (regional variation); ε4 carriers have stronger LDL-C / ApoB response to SFA reduction (Minihane, *Proc Nutr Soc* 2007), making dietary intervention more rewarding.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **APOE ε4 (rs429358):** Stronger LDL-C / ApoB rise from SFA; greater drop from SFA reduction. Recommendation: prioritize SFA reduction over fiber for ε4 carriers.
- **PCSK9 LoF (rs11591147, R46L):** Lifelong −15–20% lower ApoB. Indicates someone who runs naturally low and may not respond as much to PCSK9 inhibition (already partial deletion).
- **LDLR mutations (FH):** ApoB will not respond fully to dietary intervention; pharmacotherapy needed early.
- **APOB R3500Q (familial defective ApoB):** ApoB elevated independent of diet; same management as FH.
- **NPC1L1 LoF:** Lower lifelong cholesterol absorption — diet sterol load matters less; ezetimibe may have less benefit (already partial knock-out).
- **HMGCR variants:** Modify statin response; not strong enough to reroute therapy decisions.
- **SLCO1B1 rs4149056 C/C:** Higher simvastatin myopathy risk (CPIC 2022); rosuvastatin or pravastatin preferred.

**Drug interactions:**
- **Bile acid sequestrants (cholestyramine, colesevelam):** Lower ApoB but bind concomitant drugs and fat-soluble vitamins; spaced dosing required.
- **Rosuvastatin + cyclosporine, gemfibrozil + statin:** Increase statin levels → myopathy risk.
- **Mineral oil:** Reduces fat-soluble vitamin and EPA/DHA absorption (relevant to REDUCE-IT placebo controversy).
- **Atypical antipsychotics (olanzapine, clozapine):** Raise ApoB +5–15%.
- **HIV protease inhibitors:** Can raise LDL-C / ApoB substantially.
- **Glucocorticoids (chronic):** Raise ApoB via insulin resistance + hepatic VLDL output.
- **Estrogen (oral but not transdermal):** Lowers LDL-C and ApoB via increased clearance; raises TG.
- **Testosterone (especially oral 17α-alkylated):** Can crash HDL-C; effect on ApoB more variable; injectable testosterone modestly raises ApoB in some men.

**Lifestyle confounders / timing artifacts:**
- ApoB measurement is **not fasting-dependent** (one of its advantages over calculated LDL-C). Direct LDL-C and ApoB can be drawn non-fasting.
- Acute infection or inflammation can transiently lower LDL-C and ApoB — wait 4–6 weeks after illness or vaccination for stable trend.
- Recent intense exercise (within 24 h) can transiently lower TG and slightly affect LDL-C; standardize draw timing.
- Pregnancy: ApoB rises ~50% across pregnancy; do not interpret pregnancy values against non-pregnant ranges.
- Hypothyroidism (TSH >5): Raises ApoB +10–25%; correct thyroid first before re-measuring lipids.
- Active liver disease (ALT >3× ULN): Lipids unreliable; treat hepatic issue first.
- Recent weight loss (active phase): TG and ApoB can be transiently elevated during fat mobilization; re-test at stable weight.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Bergamot polyphenol fraction (BPF):** Most efficacy data from a single Italian research group; independent replication is sparse, and conflict-of-interest disclosures are notable. Treat the −15–20% ApoB number with skepticism until non-Italian RCTs confirm.
- **Berberine ApoB-specific trials:** Most berberine RCTs report LDL-C and TG; few report ApoB directly. The −10–15% inferred ApoB effect is from LDL-C-particle modeling, not measurement. Direct ApoB RCTs needed.
- **Time-restricted eating (independent of weight loss):** Small RCTs disagree on whether TRE moves ApoB without coincident caloric reduction. The Lin et al. *Ann Intern Med* 2024 meta-analysis suggests no independent effect.
- **Sauna and cold exposure:** Lipid mechanism is plausible but ApoB-specific RCT data are scarce; cohort CV mortality data (Laukkanen 2015) suggest a benefit but mechanism is not lipid-mediated.
- **HRV-ApoB causal direction:** HRV is correlated with ApoB but the causal arrow likely runs from "fitness/metabolic health → both HRV and ApoB" rather than from HRV to ApoB. Don't expect "HRV training" to lower ApoB.
- **Lean mass hyperresponder phenotype on ketogenic diet:** Norwitz et al. case series describe lean keto adherents whose LDL-C and ApoB rise substantially. Mechanism unclear; not yet tested in RCT. If a lean individual reports ApoB rise on keto, switch to a Mediterranean or moderate-carb pattern.
- **Plant-based diets and ApoB ceiling:** Wang 2023 meta reports −14% ApoB on vegan diets; sub-analyses by macronutrient composition are limited. Whether very low-fat (e.g., Esselstyn-style) outperforms moderate-fat (Mediterranean) for ApoB specifically is unresolved.
- **Wearable VO₂max → ApoB intervention transferability:** Cross-sectional VO₂max-ApoB gradient (~−2 mg/dL per +1 MET) has not been directly reproduced in interventional studies — improving VO₂max by 2 METs in 6 months does not always produce −4 mg/dL ApoB.

## Source format conventions

Primary literature only. Cite PMID where available; first-author + journal + year + volume:page minimum. Avoid commercial supplement promoter material and Function-Health-style opaque recommendations. Prefer meta-analyses → large RCTs → cohorts → mechanism papers.
