# LDL Cholesterol (LDL-C) — Intervention Research

> **Highest-leverage non-pharm stack:** Portfolio diet (soy + nuts + viscous fiber + sterols) reaches LDL-C −17–28 mg/dL — approximately the effect size of low-dose lovastatin. Beyond that, statin pharmacotherapy is required to achieve LDL-C <70 mg/dL. The lifestyle ceiling is roughly −25 to −35% from baseline; pharmacotherapy adds another −30–60%.

**Cross-link:** Biology, lab interpretation, and target ranges in `../heart_lipids_basic.md` §"LDL-C". Existing effect-size summary in `../effect_sizes_cardiovascular.md` lines 70–116. This file is INTERVENTION-only and expands per-food / per-supplement / per-wearable detail.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT (≥100 participants or pivotal design) |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology / physiology extrapolation |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial, unreplicated |

Population caveat: most LDL-C intervention RCTs enrolled adults aged 50–70 with baseline LDL-C 130–180 mg/dL. Lean 20-something males with baseline LDL-C 80–100 mg/dL typically achieve 30–60% of the cited absolute reductions due to floor effects (less LDLR upregulation potential, lower bile acid pool turnover available for sequestration, etc.).

## 2. Foods that RAISE LDL-C

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Saturated fat from any source | Each +1% kcal SFA | LDL-C +2.1 mg/dL when displacing PUFA; +1.6 mg/dL when displacing MUFA; +0.8 mg/dL when displacing carbohydrate | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Mensink RPM, WHO 2016 systematic review (84 RCTs); Hooper et al., *Cochrane* 2020 | Effect linear; baseline-dependent (larger absolute change at higher baseline) |
| Coconut oil | 30 g/d substituting unsaturated fat | LDL-C +10–15 mg/dL vs PUFA control | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Neelakantan et al., *Circulation* 2020;141:803 (16 RCTs); Eyres et al., *Nutr Rev* 2016 | ~85% SFA; raises HDL-C too but unfavorable LDL-C effect; popular health-food positioning unsupported |
| Butter | 30–50 g/d replacing oil | LDL-C +0.16 mmol/L (+6 mg/dL) per 5% kcal SFA increase | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Mensink WHO 2016 | Per 1% kcal SFA→PUFA: −2.1 mg/dL LDL-C |
| Ghee / clarified butter | 30 g/d | LDL-C +5–8 mg/dL | 4–12 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Sharma et al., *J Diet Suppl* 2009;6:73; small Indian RCTs | Same SFA composition as butter; no nutritional advantage; popular in SA cuisine |
| Palm oil | Common in ultra-processed foods | LDL-C +6–10 mg/dL vs MUFA (canola/olive) | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Sun et al., *Lipids Health Dis* 2015;14:147 | ~50% SFA (palmitic 16:0, the most LDL-raising fatty acid) |
| Industrial trans fat | 2% kcal iTFA | LDL-C +3.4%; HDL-C −6.8%; Lp(a) +25% | 4–8 wk | **[RCT]** | Mensink & Katan, *NEJM* 1990;323:439; Mozaffarian *NEJM* 2006 | Now banned in US; residual via imported foods, restaurant fryers, vanaspati |
| Processed red meat (sausage, bacon, salami, hot dogs) | 50–100 g/d | LDL-C +6–12 mg/dL vs plant protein | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Guasch-Ferré et al., *Circulation* 2019;139:1828 (36 RCTs) | Worse than fresh red meat in cohort outcomes |
| Fatty cuts of beef/lamb/pork | ≥150 g/d | LDL-C +5–10 mg/dL | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Guasch-Ferré 2019 | Mostly SFA-mediated; lean cuts smaller effect |
| Hard cheese (cheddar, parmesan, gouda — full fat) | 100 g/d | LDL-C +5–8 mg/dL (~half the predicted SFA effect due to matrix) | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | de Goede et al., *J Am Heart Assoc* 2015;4:e002787; Soerensen et al., *Eur J Clin Nutr* 2014 | Calcium soaps + milk-fat globule membrane reduce cholesterol absorption ~10–20% (matrix effect) |
| Refined baked goods (croissants, pastries, muffins, doughnuts, cookies) | Daily | LDL-C +10–20 mg/dL | 4–12 wk | **[Cohort]** | EPIC; NHS; Mozaffarian *NEJM* 2011;364:2392 | Combined SFA + refined carb + frequent residual trans fat; the worst single food category |
| Cream / heavy cream | 50 mL/d | LDL-C +5–7 mg/dL | 4–8 wk | **[RCT]** | Lefevre et al., *AJCN* 2005;82:957 | High SFA milk fat |
| MCT oil supplements | 30 g/d | LDL-C +6–10 mg/dL | 4–8 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Mumme & Stonehouse, *J Acad Nutr Diet* 2015;115:249 | Marketed in keto/biohacker space; raises LDL-C |
| Egg yolks (in dietary cholesterol "hyper-responders", ~⅓ of population) | 2–3 whole eggs/d | LDL-C +5–10 mg/dL in hyper-responders; ±0 in hypo-responders | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Vincent et al., *J Am Coll Nutr* 2018;37:99; Berger et al., *AJCN* 2015 | Highly variable; LXR-mediated suppression of endogenous synthesis in ⅔ of population blunts effect; test individually with 6-wk before/after |
| Sugar-sweetened beverages (especially HFCS) | ≥600 mL/d | LDL-C +0.29 mmol/L (~+11 mg/dL) hypercaloric; minimal isocaloric | 8–10 wk | **[RCT]** | Stanhope et al., *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2011;96:E1596 | Effect requires positive energy balance |
| Unfiltered coffee (cafestol/kahweol) | 5+ cups/d unfiltered (French press, Turkish, espresso, Scandinavian boiled) | LDL-C +6–18 mg/dL (dose-dependent) | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Cai et al., *Eur J Clin Nutr* 2012;66:872; Jee et al., *AJCN* 2001;73:532 | Cafestol downregulates hepatic LDLR; paper filter removes diterpenes; espresso retains moderate cafestol; the most overlooked dietary LDL elevator in coffee drinkers |
| Low-carb / ketogenic diet (in lean individuals) | <50 g carb/d | LDL-C **+** 4.8–5.1 mg/dL mean; up to +50–100 mg/dL in "lean mass hyperresponders" | 12–24 wk | **[Meta]** | PLOS One 2020; AJCN 2024 meta; Norwitz et al., *Curr Dev Nutr* 2022 | LMHR phenotype: lean (BMI <22), athletic, high HDL; LDL-C may rise dramatically; ApoB also rises; check ApoB directly |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention (non-pharm):** Portfolio diet pattern (or equivalently aggressive SFA reduction + viscous fiber + sterols stack). Magnitude: LDL-C −15–28 mg/dL. Time: 4–12 wk.
- **Best stack of 3 (additive but with diminishing returns ~70%):**
  1. Saturated fat reduction to <7% kcal (replacing with MUFA/PUFA): LDL-C −10–20 mg/dL
  2. Psyllium 10 g/d + plant sterols 2 g/d: LDL-C −15–20 mg/dL combined (different mechanisms)
  3. Almonds 45 g/d or walnuts 30 g/d in place of snacks: LDL-C −5 mg/dL
  - Combined estimated delta from elevated baseline (LDL-C 130): −25 to −35 mg/dL
- **Realistic 12-week delta for healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (LDL-C ~80 mg/dL):** −5 to −10 mg/dL with maximum lifestyle intervention (floor effects)
- **Realistic 12-week delta for elevated baseline (LDL-C >130 mg/dL):** −20 to −35 mg/dL with the 3-stack
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:** LDL-C >100 mg/dL persisting >6 months on max lifestyle with elevated risk factors (Lp(a) high, family history, ApoB >90, calcium >0); LDL-C >190 mg/dL irrespective of lifestyle (suggests FH); LDL-C >70 mg/dL with established ASCVD. Statin (rosuvastatin 5–10 mg) adds another −40–50%.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- MASALA cohort (Kanaya, UCSF) shows mean SA LDL-C 112 ± 32 mg/dL — within "desirable" by NCEP — yet CAC scores and event rates disproportionately elevated. **LDL-C alone systematically under-predicts ASCVD risk in SA individuals.** Use ApoB or non-HDL-C alongside LDL-C.
- START-India (open-label atorvastatin 20 mg in SA) → ~−43% LDL-C, similar to non-SA (~−41%). Statin pharmacodynamics preserved.
- **Vanaspati ghee (partially hydrogenated)** historically common in Indian sweets and fried foods; still legal in many South Asian markets; raises LDL-C and Lp(a) substantially. Eliminate if present.
- **Ghee (clarified butter)** is pure SFA milk fat; popular cultural food; reducing or replacing with mustard oil or olive oil is high-yield.
- **Coconut oil** widely promoted in India and Sri Lanka as health food; raises LDL-C; replace with sunflower or mustard oil.
- **Lean SA male with normal BMI but elevated LDL-C and ApoB** — often visceral adipose-driven. Diet + resistance training to add lean mass + reduce visceral fat tends to give better lipid response than caloric restriction alone.
- *APOE* ε4 prevalence: variable across SA sub-populations but generally similar to European frequencies; ε4 carriers respond more to SFA reduction.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **APOE ε4 (rs429358):** Stronger LDL-C rise from SFA; greater drop from SFA reduction (Minihane 2007).
- **PCSK9 LoF (rs11591147, R46L):** Lifelong −15–20% lower LDL-C.
- **LDLR mutations (FH):** LDL-C will not respond fully to dietary intervention; pharmacotherapy needed early.
- **APOB R3500Q (familial defective ApoB):** LDL-C elevated independent of diet.
- **NPC1L1 LoF:** Lower lifelong cholesterol absorption; sterol-load matters less; ezetimibe less benefit.
- **HMGCR variants:** Modify statin response.
- **SLCO1B1 rs4149056 C/C:** Higher simvastatin myopathy risk (CPIC 2022); rosuvastatin or pravastatin preferred.
- **APOA5, LPL variants:** Affect TG more than LDL-C, but in remnant-rich phenotypes calculated LDL-C (Friedewald) becomes unreliable.

**Drug interactions:**
- **Bile acid sequestrants:** Bind concomitant drugs and fat-soluble vitamins; spaced dosing.
- **Atypical antipsychotics (olanzapine, clozapine, quetiapine):** Raise LDL-C +5–15 mg/dL.
- **HIV protease inhibitors:** Substantial LDL-C rise.
- **Glucocorticoids (chronic):** Raise LDL-C via insulin resistance.
- **Estrogen (oral but not transdermal):** Lowers LDL-C ~10%; raises TG.
- **Cyclosporine, tacrolimus:** Raise LDL-C; complicate statin use.
- **Diuretics (thiazides):** Small LDL-C rise.
- **β-blockers (non-vasodilating):** Small unfavorable lipid shift.

**Lifestyle confounders / timing artifacts:**
- **Calculated LDL-C (Friedewald) becomes inaccurate at TG >400 mg/dL or in remnant-rich phenotypes.** Direct LDL-C or ApoB preferred when TG elevated or low-carb diet.
- **Recent intense exercise (within 24 h)** can transiently affect LDL-C.
- **Pregnancy:** LDL-C rises ~30–50% across pregnancy.
- **Hypothyroidism (TSH >5):** LDL-C +10–25%; correct thyroid before measuring.
- **Active liver disease (ALT >3× ULN):** Lipids unreliable.
- **Recent weight loss (active phase):** Triglycerides and LDL-C can be transiently elevated during fat mobilization; re-test at stable weight.
- **Acute infection or vaccination:** Transient LDL-C drop; wait 4–6 wk post-illness for stable trend.
- **Posture during draw:** Standing for ≥15 min before draw can elevate measured LDL-C ~5–10% via hemoconcentration.
- **Tourniquet time:** Prolonged (>2 min) tourniquet → hemoconcentration → small LDL-C elevation.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Bergamot polyphenol:** Most data from one Italian group; replication thin.
- **Berberine LDL-C transferability:** ~80% of trials Chinese. NW European / SA RCTs sparse.
- **Lean mass hyperresponder phenotype on keto:** Norwitz et al. case series describe lean keto adherents whose LDL-C and ApoB rise 50–200%. Mechanism unclear (saturated fat overload + LDLR downregulation in lean state); not yet RCT-tested. If a lean individual reports LDL-C rise on keto, switch to Mediterranean/moderate-carb pattern.
- **Egg cholesterol — population-level effect:** Despite the ⅔ "hypo-responder" majority, the ⅓ "hyper-responder" minority is meaningful and not predictable a priori. 6-week before/after with eggs added/removed is the only reliable test.
- **Probiotics:** L. plantarum and L. reuteri NCIMB 30242 show modest LDL-C reductions but evidence is heterogeneous and brand-specific. Not yet validated to a standard that justifies routine recommendation.
- **Sauna and cold exposure:** Lipid mechanism plausible but RCT data scarce; cohort CV mortality data (Laukkanen 2015) suggest a benefit but mechanism is not lipid-mediated.
- **LDL-C interpretation on low-carb diet:** Low-carb diet often raises LDL-C (especially in lean individuals) while reducing TG and ApoB. Friedewald-calculated LDL-C is unreliable; direct LDL-C and especially ApoB are needed. Whether the rise is causally atherogenic in lean individuals with otherwise improved metabolic phenotype is actively debated (no RCT outcomes data).
- **Wearable VO₂max → LDL-C transferability:** Cross-sectional gradient (~−2 mg/dL per +1 MET) is not fully reproduced in interventional trials.
