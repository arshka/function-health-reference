# Triglycerides (TG) — Intervention Research

> **Highest-leverage non-pharm stack:** Carbohydrate restriction (especially refined carbs and fructose) + alcohol elimination + weight loss together can reduce TG by 30–50% in individuals with TG >150 mg/dL within 8–12 weeks. High-dose marine omega-3 (2–4 g/d EPA+DHA) adds another −20–40%. Pharmacotherapy (fibrates, prescription EPA) is usually reserved for TG >500 mg/dL or persistent elevation despite lifestyle.

**Cross-link:** Biology and clinical interpretation in `../heart_lipids_basic.md` §"Triglycerides". Existing effect-size summary in `../effect_sizes_cardiovascular.md` lines 150–177. This file is INTERVENTION-only.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT (≥100 participants or pivotal design) |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology / physiology extrapolation |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial, unreplicated |

Population caveat: TG response is highly baseline-dependent. Lean young adults with TG <100 mg/dL show small absolute changes; insulin-resistant individuals with TG >200 show large absolute changes. Acute (24–48 h) postprandial TG can vary 50–100% — fasting status critical for measurement reliability.

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention:** Alcohol elimination (if any drinking) OR carbohydrate/sugar reduction (if no alcohol). Both can produce TG −20–40% in 4–8 wk.
- **Best stack of 3 (additive ~70% of linear sum):**
  1. Alcohol elimination (if drinking >7 drinks/wk): TG −30–50 mg/dL
  2. Refined carb/sugar reduction (especially fructose-containing beverages): TG −20–30 mg/dL
  3. Marine omega-3 (2–4 g/d EPA+DHA): TG −40–70 mg/dL
  - Combined estimated delta from elevated baseline (TG 200): −80–120 mg/dL (to ~80–120)
- **Realistic 12-week delta for healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (TG <80 mg/dL):** −5 to −15 mg/dL with maximum lifestyle (floor effects)
- **Realistic 12-week delta for elevated baseline (TG >180 mg/dL):** −60 to −100 mg/dL with the 3-stack
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:** TG >500 mg/dL (pancreatitis prevention); TG >200 mg/dL with established ASCVD on statin (icosapent ethyl per REDUCE-IT). Fibrates (fenofibrate 145 mg/d) reduce TG −30–50%; pemafibrate failed CV outcomes (PROMINENT 2022) despite TG drop, complicating the rationale for fibrate use solely on TG basis.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- SA individuals have a **high prevalence of hypertriglyceridemia even at normal BMI**, driven by insulin resistance + visceral adiposity (MASALA cohort; Kanaya et al., *Diabetes Care* 2014).
- Mean TG in MASALA SA cohort: ~125 mg/dL vs ~110 mg/dL in non-SA comparators. ~30% of SA in MASALA had TG >150.
- Mechanism: SA-typical adipose distribution (visceral > subcutaneous) → free fatty acid flux to liver → elevated VLDL → high TG.
- Implication: TG response to carbohydrate restriction in SA is mechanistically expected to be similar or larger than in European populations, but RCT data in SA are sparse. The MASALA cohort sub-analyses confirm associations but no SA-specific intervention RCT has been published.
- **Vanaspati (partially hydrogenated vegetable oil)** historically common in SA cuisine still legal in some markets; raises TG and LDL-C and Lp(a). Eliminate if present.
- **Cultural sweets (mithai)** and refined-flour staples (white rice, naan, parathas) are major TG drivers in SA diets. Substitution with whole grains and reduction of mithai consumption is high-yield.
- *APOC3* loss-of-function variants are rare but protective; *LPL* variants modify TG; *APOA5* variants modify TG response to fasting and carbohydrate. Polygenic TG scores are higher in SA than European average.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **APOC3 LoF (rs138326449, R19X):** Lifelong TG ~40% lower; protective against CHD per MR (TG-Glycemic Working Group, *NEJM* 2014).
- **LPL variants (loss-of-function):** Elevated TG; severe variants → familial chylomicronemia.
- **APOA5 variants (rs662799, S19W):** Elevate TG; modify TG response to high-fat meals.
- **GCKR variants (rs780094):** Modulate hepatic glucokinase; modify TG response to dietary carbohydrate.
- **TM6SF2 rs58542926 T allele:** Lower TG (lower hepatic VLDL secretion) but higher hepatic steatosis.

**Drug interactions:**
- **Estrogen (oral but not transdermal):** TG +30–50%; first-pass hepatic effect.
- **Glucocorticoids (chronic):** TG +20–50%.
- **Atypical antipsychotics (olanzapine, clozapine):** TG +20–80%.
- **Beta-blockers (non-vasodilating):** Small TG rise.
- **Thiazide diuretics:** Small TG rise.
- **Retinoids (isotretinoin, acitretin):** TG +20–80%; common cause of secondary hyper-TG.
- **HIV protease inhibitors:** Substantial TG rise.
- **Anabolic steroids (oral):** TG +20–50%.
- **Tamoxifen:** TG rise in some women; can precipitate pancreatitis at high baseline.
- **Bile acid sequestrants (cholestyramine):** May raise TG paradoxically, especially in TG >200 baseline.
- **Cyclosporine, tacrolimus:** Raise TG.
- **Mineral oil:** Reduces fat-soluble vitamin and EPA/DHA absorption.

**Lifestyle confounders / timing artifacts:**
- **TG measurement is highly fasting-sensitive.** Postprandial TG can be 50–100% higher than fasting. Standard requirement: 8–12 h fast.
- Acute alcohol within 24 h: TG transiently elevated.
- Recent intense exercise (within 24–48 h): TG transiently lowered (LPL upregulation).
- Pregnancy: TG rises 200–400% across pregnancy; not interpretable against non-pregnant ranges.
- Hypothyroidism (TSH >5): TG +10–25%; correct thyroid first.
- Diabetic ketoacidosis or uncontrolled T1DM: TG can be massively elevated.
- Active liver disease (NAFLD, cirrhosis): TG variable.
- Recent weight loss (active phase): TG can transiently elevate during fat mobilization.
- Recent illness, surgery, or vaccination: TG transiently elevated as part of acute-phase response.
- Standing vs. seated draw: small artifactual variation via hemoconcentration.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **The PROMINENT puzzle:** Pemafibrate (selective PPARα, *NEJM* 2022) reduced TG ~26% and remnant cholesterol but **did not reduce CV events** in 10,497 patients with TG 200–499 on statin. This challenges whether TG per se is the causally relevant variable, or whether the particle context (remnants, ApoB-containing TRLs) matters more. MR data on remnant cholesterol support causality; pemafibrate failure may be specific to its mechanism (increased ApoB production from PPARα activation may offset benefit from TG reduction).
- **REDUCE-IT mineral oil controversy:** The placebo in REDUCE-IT (mineral oil) modestly raised LDL-C, hs-CRP, and other biomarkers, raising concerns that the −25% MACE benefit of icosapent ethyl 4 g/d EPA may be partially driven by placebo harm rather than active drug benefit. STRENGTH trial (omega-3 carboxylic acids, EPA+DHA 4 g/d) showed no benefit using corn oil placebo. Net: high-dose EPA may be beneficial in TG >200 on statin, but magnitude uncertain.
- **Berberine TG-specific transferability:** ~80% of trials Chinese; magnitude uncertain in SA / NW European.
- **Bergamot polyphenol:** Most data from one Italian group.
- **Lean mass hyperresponder phenotype on keto:** Lean keto adherents who drop TG dramatically while LDL-C/ApoB rise dramatically. Net atherogenic risk debated; no RCT outcomes data.
- **Postprandial TG vs fasting TG as risk marker:** Cohort data suggest postprandial (non-fasting) TG predicts CV events as well or better than fasting TG (Bansal et al., *JAMA* 2007;298:309). Whether interventions should target postprandial vs fasting TG specifically is unresolved.
- **CGM-guided carb restriction for TG:** Logical but not RCT-tested. Personalized carb restriction based on CGM glucose response (Hall et al. 2018) may be more efficient than population-average advice.
- **Time-restricted eating independent of weight loss:** Lin 2024 meta suggests no independent TG effect without weight loss.
