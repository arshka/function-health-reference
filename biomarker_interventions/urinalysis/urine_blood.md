# Urine Blood (Hematuria / Occult Blood) — Intervention Research

> Urine blood is **negative in healthy adults**. Most actionable interventions are **pre-test behaviors that prevent false-positives** (defer 48–72 h after intense exercise, no menstruation, no recent sex, no instrumentation, hold high-dose vitamin C 24–48 h to avoid false-negatives, retract foreskin during clean catch). For confirmed pathologic hematuria, interventions depend on cause: **stones** → hydration ≥2.5 L/d + dietary modification (low-Na, low-animal-protein, normal Ca, low oxalate) + thiazide if hypercalciuric; **UTI** → targeted antibiotics; **IgA nephropathy** → ACEi/ARB + corticosteroids in selected patients (TESTING trial); **bladder cancer** → urology referral. Per AUA 2020 (Barocas et al.), microhematuria is defined as ≥3 RBCs/HPF on properly collected specimen — dipstick alone is insufficient.

### Causing hematuria as side effect

| Drug | Mechanism | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Anticoagulants** (warfarin, DOACs, heparin) | May unmask underlying lesion; per AUA do not attribute hematuria to anticoagulation alone | Halpern et al. *J Urol* 2016 (PMID 27114367) |
| **Antiplatelets** (aspirin, clopidogrel) | Same as above | AUA 2020 |
| **Cyclophosphamide / ifosfamide** | Hemorrhagic cystitis from acrolein metabolite; prevent with mesna + hydration | Stillwell et al. *Cancer* 1988 |
| **NSAIDs** (chronic) | Papillary necrosis → hematuria; analgesic nephropathy | Henrich et al. *Am J Kidney Dis* 1996 |
| **Quinolones** (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) | Crystalluria — quinolone crystals (rare) | DiPiro et al. |
| **Sulfonamides** | Crystalluria | Same |
| **Indinavir** (HIV PI, mostly historical) | Indinavir crystals → stones | Saltel et al. *AIDS* 2000 |
| **Atazanavir** (HIV PI) | Atazanavir crystals → stones | Hamada et al. *AIDS* 2012 |
| **Ceftriaxone** | Pseudolithiasis (biliary > urinary) | Park et al. *Pediatr Nephrol* 2009 |

| **Acetazolamide** | CaPhos stones via urine alkalinization | Same mechanism |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single pre-test behavior**: avoid intense exercise 48–72 h before urinalysis. Eliminates exercise-induced hematuria — by far the most common false-positive in young athletic males.
- **Highest-leverage single chronic intervention for stone-related hematuria**: hydration ≥2.5 L/d (50% recurrence ↓ over 5 years).
- **Best stack of 3 (CaOx stone-former with recurrent hematuria)**:
  1. ≥2.5 L water/d
  2. Sodium <2 g/d, animal protein <1 g/kg/d, normal calcium 1000 mg/d (NOT low-Ca diet)
  3. Potassium citrate 30 mEq BID (or 4–6 oz lemon juice in 2L water as natural alternative)
  4. Add thiazide if hypercalciuric on 24-h urine
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for healthy 20yM with recurring exercise-induced hematuria: avoid intense exercise 72 h before any UA → 100% resolution; if it persists despite no exercise, AUA workup (low-risk pathway = repeat UA at 6 mo OR cystoscopy + renal U/S).
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for stone-former with recurrent hematuria: hydration + dietary modification + K-citrate → 50% reduction in recurrence + hematuria at 6–12 months.
- **Where pharmacotherapy / urgent referral is needed**:
  - Gross hematuria, especially smokers >35yo → urgent urology + cystoscopy + CT urography (AUA high-risk pathway)
  - Hematuria + proteinuria, dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, ↑Cr, HTN → nephrology referral first
  - Gross hematuria 24–72 h post-URI → IgA nephropathy workup (24-h urine protein, complement, possible biopsy)
  - Recurrent UTI in male <35 → urology referral (anatomic anomaly evaluation)

**AUA 2020 risk-stratified workup (essential reference for engine):**

| Risk | Criteria | Workup |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Men <40, never-smoker, <10 RBCs/HPF, no risk factors | Repeat UA at 6 mo OR shared-decision cystoscopy + renal U/S |
| Intermediate | Men 40–59, OR 10–30 pack-years, OR 11–25 RBCs/HPF | Cystoscopy + renal U/S |
| High | ≥60yo OR >30 pack-years OR >25 RBCs/HPF OR gross hematuria history OR occupational exposure | Cystoscopy + CT urography |

Anyone with proteinuria, dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, ↑creatinine, or hypertension → nephrology referral first regardless of risk category.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- IgA nephropathy is the most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide and especially common in East and South Asians. **Episodic gross hematuria (often coca-cola colored) within 24–72 h of an upper respiratory infection in a young SA male is the classic presentation.** Refer nephrology even if self-resolving.
- Asian populations have higher uric acid stone prevalence — diet and target urine pH (6.5–7.0) shifted vs. Western norm.
- South Asian young males with recurrent gross hematuria + proteinuria + family history → consider Alport syndrome (COL4A3/4/5) or thin basement membrane disease.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**False positives (dipstick blood positive but no actual hematuria):**
- Myoglobinuria (rhabdomyolysis) — heme from muscle, RBC microscopy usually negative; check CK
- Hemoglobinuria (intravascular hemolysis: malaria, G6PD crisis, mechanical valve, PNH, transfusion reaction, snake envenomation)
- Menstrual contamination (women)
- Microbial peroxidases (UTI organisms)
- Highly concentrated urine
- Hypochlorite contamination of container
- Beeturia is RED COLOR but dipstick blood NEGATIVE — beet pigment doesn't react with peroxidase

**False negatives (actual hematuria but dipstick negative):**
- **Vitamin C** (ascorbic acid) — ~10% false-negative rate per Nagel et al. *Clin Biochem* 2016. Hold high-dose vit C 24–48 h before test.
- High specific gravity, glucose, protein in urine
- Very acid urine
- Captopril
- Old sample — RBC lysis but free hemoglobin should still be detected
- Nitrite at high concentration

**Drug confounders:**
- Anticoagulants don't fully explain hematuria — workup required regardless
- Phenazopyridine (Pyridium) — orange dye obscures dipstick reading
- Rifampin — orange urine, may interfere with reading
- Acidic urine from very high-protein diet — modest effect

**Confounding lifestyle factors:**
- Recent ejaculation can cause minor blood
- Recent prostate exam / biopsy
- Recent urinary catheterization
- Recent vigorous exercise (#1 cause of false positive in young men)
- Recent trauma (mountain biking, contact sports)
- Foley catheter

**Genetic modifiers:**
- COL4A3/4/5 mutations → Alport syndrome / thin basement membrane disease (persistent dysmorphic microhematuria)
- ADPKD (PKD1/PKD2) — gross hematuria from cyst rupture
- Sickle cell trait (HbAS, ~8% AA Americans) — papillary necrosis → microhematuria
- AGXT (PH1), GRHPR (PH2), HOGA1 (PH3) — primary hyperoxaluria → recurrent CaOx stones
- Cystinuria (SLC3A1, SLC7A9) — recurrent cystine stones from childhood

**Timing:**
- AUA 2020 requires ≥3 RBCs/HPF on PROPERLY COLLECTED specimen — dipstick alone insufficient
- Some labs require 2 of 3 abnormal UAs before workup
- Asymptomatic dipstick + microscopy positive in young athletes — repeat at 48–72 h after rest before workup

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Optimal frequency of follow-up UA in low-risk patients post-initial negative workup** — guidelines suggest 6 mo then annually but evidence sparse
- **Cranberry vs. D-mannose head-to-head for UTI prevention** — no large RCT
- **Methenamine hippurate as long-term UTI prophylaxis vs. low-dose antibiotic** — ALTAR 2022 supports non-inferiority, real-world adoption increasing
- **Lumasiran cost-effectiveness vs. dietary management for primary hyperoxaluria 1** — long-term data emerging
- **Whether urinary biomarkers (Cxbladder, NMP22, BTA) replace cystoscopy in low/intermediate-risk hematuria** — AUA 2025 update incorporates these for selected use
- **Best initial imaging for renal/ureteral stones in microhematuria** — ultrasound vs. low-dose CT debated
- **TESTING trial: optimal duration of corticosteroid therapy for IgA nephropathy** — 6 vs 12 months, individualized
- **High-dose vitamin C effect on stone risk in women** — Thomas 2013 showed risk in men but not women; mechanism unclear
