# Urine Glucose — Intervention Research

> Urine glucose is **negative in healthy adults**. It turns positive only when (1) plasma glucose exceeds the renal threshold (~180 mg/dL), (2) the patient takes an SGLT2 inhibitor (deliberate, expected glycosuria of 50–80 g/day), or (3) the proximal tubule is damaged (Fanconi syndrome) or genetically defective (familial renal glucosuria). The dominant intervention to make urine glucose negative is glycemic control: HbA1c <7% reliably keeps blood glucose <180 mg/dL throughout the day. SGLT2 inhibitors WILL cause glycosuria — this is the mechanism of action, not a side effect.

**Why glycosuria matters clinically.** A positive urine glucose dipstick in someone NOT on an SGLT2 inhibitor is presumptive evidence of either (a) hyperglycemia >180 mg/dL → diabetes workup, or (b) renal glucose handling defect (rare). The dipstick is qualitative — for diabetes screening, fasting plasma glucose, HbA1c, and OGTT are far more sensitive (urine glucose is positive only when plasma glucose >180, missing prediabetes and most controlled diabetics).

## 3. Supplements & Medications

### Causing glycosuria DELIBERATELY (expected mechanism)

| Drug | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| **Sotagliflozin** (SGLT1+SGLT2) | 200–400 mg/d | Glycosuria + intestinal glucose absorption blockade | First dose | **[RCT]** | SOLOIST/SCORED (Bhatt 2020) |  |

**Important: SGLT2 inhibitors are now first-line for HFrEF, HFpEF, T2DM with CV/renal disease, CKD with proteinuria. The glycosuria they cause is THE THERAPEUTIC MECHANISM and should NOT be flagged as abnormal in patients on these drugs.**

### Lowering glycosuria (i.e., improving glycemic control to keep glucose <180)

| Drug | Mechanism | Magnitude |
|---|---|---|
| Metformin 500–2000 mg/d | ↓ hepatic gluconeogenesis | HbA1c ↓ ~1.0–1.5% |
| GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) | ↑ incretin, ↓ gastric emptying | HbA1c ↓ 1.0–1.8% |

| Pioglitazone | ↑ insulin sensitivity | HbA1c ↓ 1.0% |
| Sulfonylureas (glipizide, glimepiride) | ↑ insulin secretion | HbA1c ↓ 1.0–1.5% (hypoglycemia risk) |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** in T2DM with positive urine glucose: **Metformin** (HbA1c −1–1.5%; usually drops blood glucose below 180 throughout the day, eliminating glycosuria). For patients on SGLT2i, the glycosuria is intentional and not to be reduced.
- **Best stack of 3 (lifestyle, T2DM)**:
  1. 5–10% weight loss (DiRECT-style intervention)
  2. Mediterranean / low-GI diet pattern
  3. 150–300 min/wk moderate aerobic + 2× resistance training
  Combined: HbA1c ↓ 1.0–1.5%; glycosuria typically resolves.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for healthy 20yM with positive urine glucose:
  - First step is workup (fasting plasma glucose, HbA1c, OGTT) to determine cause.
  - If T1DM/T2DM diagnosed → treat accordingly.
  - If familial renal glucosuria → benign, no intervention needed (lifelong glucose +1–4+ in urine; normal blood glucose).
  - If Fanconi syndrome → identify cause (heavy metals, drugs, multiple myeloma, Wilson) and treat.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for established T2DM with HbA1c 8.5%, urine glucose 2+: metformin + GLP-1 → HbA1c ~6.8%; urine glucose typically negative within 2–4 weeks.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary**: any T1DM (insulin); T2DM with HbA1c >7.5% despite lifestyle (metformin first-line per ADA 2024; add SGLT2i or GLP-1 if ASCVD/CKD/HF).

**Crucial recommendation engine rule:** If patient's medication list includes any "-gliflozin" drug (canagliflozin, dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, ertugliflozin, sotagliflozin, bexagliflozin), urine glucose 1+ to 4+ is **expected and therapeutic**, NOT a flag for diabetes workup.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- South Asian men have ~3× T2DM risk at lower BMI. A lean SA young male with positive urine glucose → check HbA1c, fasting glucose, and OGTT (impaired glucose tolerance is more common than impaired fasting glucose in this group).
- Lean MODY (Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young) — autosomal dominant, presents <25, often misdiagnosed as T1 or T2. HNF1A-MODY especially has lower renal threshold for glucose → urine glucose with normal-low blood glucose. Genetic testing if family history.
- Familial renal glucosuria (SLC5A2 mutations) is more common than appreciated; isolated glucosuria with normal HbA1c does not require diabetes treatment.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

- **SGLT2 inhibitors** — expected glycosuria; NOT a sign of poor control or new diabetes
- **Pregnancy** — physiologic lowering of renal glucose threshold; mild glucosuria in 50% of pregnancies, MOST are not gestational diabetes (still confirm with OGTT)
- **Familial renal glucosuria (SLC5A2)** — benign isolated glycosuria; lifelong; ~6 g–80 g/day urinary glucose
- **Fanconi syndrome** (cystinosis, Wilson disease, multiple myeloma with light-chain proximal toxicity, heavy metals, tenofovir, ifosfamide) — glucosuria + aminoaciduria + phosphaturia + bicarbonaturia + uricosuria
- **Drug confounders for dipstick**:
  - Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) — major false-negative; up to 42% false-negative rate in samples with high vitamin C and serum glucose >180 (Brigden et al. *Ann Clin Lab Sci* 2015 PMID 26116618; Nagel et al. *Clin Biochem* 2016)
  - Phenazopyridine — color interference
  - Levodopa — false negative
  - Strong oxidizing cleaners (bleach) on container — false positive
- **Stress hyperglycemia** — sepsis, MI, stroke, surgery, glucocorticoids, severe illness — transient glucosuria
- **Acute pancreatitis, Cushing's, acromegaly, pheochromocytoma** — secondary causes
- **High-fructose loads** — minimal glycosuria (fructose has separate transport; not detected on dipstick which is glucose-oxidase-specific)
- **Galactose, lactose** — not detected on dipstick (glucose-oxidase specific). Galactosemia in infants needs reducing-substance test (Benedict's), not dipstick.
- **Genetic modifiers**:
  - SLC5A2 (SGLT2) mutations — familial renal glucosuria
  - HNF1A — MODY3 with low renal threshold
  - GCK — MODY2 (mild stable hyperglycemia, often urine negative)
  - HNF4A — MODY1
  - GLUT2 mutations (Fanconi-Bickel) — pediatric

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Optimal HbA1c target to reliably eliminate glycosuria** is individual; PP excursions matter as much as HbA1c. Many controlled diabetics with HbA1c 7.5% still show post-prandial glycosuria.
- **Time-restricted eating effects on glycosuria** specifically (vs. HbA1c) not directly measured.
- **Whether brief glycosuria in the absence of diabetes carries any independent risk** (e.g., recurrent UTI risk via glucose substrate) — limited data; clinically usually benign.
- **Cinnamon and HbA1c** — meta-analyses split between modest effect and no effect; mechanism debated.
- **SGLT2i in non-diabetic patients with isolated FRG**: theoretical concern about excessive glycosuria; no clinical data.
