# Urine Microscopy (RBCs, WBCs, Bacteria, Epithelial Cells, Casts, Crystals, Yeast, Amorphous) — Intervention Research

> Microscopy gives sediment-level diagnostic precision: dysmorphic RBCs / RBC casts → glomerular bleeding (IgA nephropathy, lupus, Alport); WBC casts → pyelonephritis or interstitial nephritis; granular/waxy casts → ATN/CKD; fatty casts ("Maltese cross") → nephrotic syndrome; squamous epithelial cells → contamination (re-collect); calcium oxalate crystals → stone risk + dietary oxalate / refrigeration; struvite (triple phosphate) crystals → urea-splitting UTI (Proteus); uric acid crystals → hyperuricosuria + acidic urine; cystine crystals → cystinuria (rare, hereditary); yeast → contamination usually, true candiduria with diabetes/catheter/immunosuppression; amorphous urates/phosphates → benign artifact of refrigeration. The dominant pre-test interventions are **clean-catch midstream technique with foreskin retraction / labial spread, processing within 1 hour at room temp (or refrigerate ≤24 h, then re-warm)**, and **avoidance of intense exercise 48–72 h pre-test** to eliminate exercise-induced hematuria/casts. Disease-specific therapies are detailed below by sediment finding.

**Cross-link:** Reference biology / clinical interpretation in `../../urinalysis_panel.md` §12 (RBCs), §13 (WBCs), §14 (Bacteria), §15 (Squamous Epithelial), §16 (Hyaline Casts), §17 (Calcium Oxalate Crystals), §18 (Yeast), §19 (Amorphous). | **Defer test during menstruation (women)** | RBC contamination | Eliminates menstrual contamination | **[Mechanism]** | AUA 2020 |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

### By sediment finding:

**For CALCIUM OXALATE crystals (most common):**
- Highest-leverage single intervention: **water ≥2.5 L/d** (stones ↓ 50%)
- Stack: hydration + low-Na (<2 g/d) + normal Ca (1000 mg/d) + low animal protein + low-oxalate diet + K-citrate + thiazide if hypercalciuric
- Realistic 12-week delta: stone-former with recurrent CaOx → 50–70% reduction in stones at 1 year

**For URIC ACID crystals:**
- Highest-leverage: K-citrate (target pH 6.5–7.0)
- Stack: K-citrate + low-purine diet + allopurinol if hyperuricemic + hydration

**For STRUVITE crystals (urea-splitting UTI):**
- Highest-leverage: targeted antibiotics + complete stone clearance (PCNL)
- Acidify urine if needed (methenamine, ascorbic acid)

**For CYSTINE crystals (cystinuria, hereditary):**
- Highest-leverage: tiopronin or D-penicillamine + alkalinization (target pH ≥7.5) + ≥3 L water/d

**For RBCs / RBC CASTS / DYSMORPHIC RBCs (glomerular):**
- Highest-leverage: ACEi/ARB + control of underlying disease (steroids for IgAN per TESTING)
- Workup: nephrology referral, urgent if RBC casts present
- Avoid intense exercise 48–72 h pre-test to rule out exertional cause

**For WBCs / WBC CASTS (pyelonephritis or AIN):**
- Highest-leverage: appropriate antibiotic for pyelonephritis (cipro, ceftriaxone)
- For AIN: discontinue offending drug (NSAID, PPI, β-lactam, allopurinol); consider steroids

**For BACTERIA (UTI):**
- Highest-leverage: targeted antibiotic per IDSA + culture
- Asymptomatic bacteriuria: do not treat (except pregnancy, pre-procedure) per IDSA 2019

**For YEAST (candiduria):**
- Highest-leverage: identify cause (catheter, DM, immunosuppression, antibiotics)
- Remove catheter; control glucose; treat with fluconazole only if symptomatic or before urologic procedure

**For SQUAMOUS EPITHELIAL CELLS:**
- Highest-leverage: re-collect with proper clean-catch technique
- Not pathologic — quality-control signal

**For HYALINE CASTS:**
- Highest-leverage: hydration; usually benign in dehydration, exercise, fever, mild proteinuria
- Distinguish from pathologic casts (RBC, WBC, granular, waxy, fatty)

**For GRANULAR / WAXY CASTS:**
- Suggests acute tubular necrosis or chronic kidney disease
- Workup: BMP, urine protein, nephrology

**For FATTY CASTS / OVAL FAT BODIES (Maltese cross under polarized light):**
- Suggests nephrotic syndrome
- Workup: 24-h urine protein, lipid panel, kidney biopsy

**For RENAL TUBULAR EPITHELIAL CELLS:**
- Suggests acute tubular necrosis, AIN, glomerulonephritis
- Workup: BMP, urine eosinophils (if AIN), nephrology

**For AMORPHOUS URATES / PHOSPHATES:**
- No clinical significance (refrigeration artifact most common)
- Re-warm sample if obscuring other findings

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- **IgA nephropathy** is the most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide and especially common in South/East Asians. Episodic gross hematuria 24–72 h post-URI in young SA male + RBC casts + dysmorphic RBCs → urgent nephrology referral. TESTING trial supports steroids for proteinuria >0.75 g/d.
- **Genitourinary TB** is endemic in South Asia. Sterile pyuria + acidic urine + hematuria + weight loss + TB exposure → urine AFB ×3 + urine TB PCR + imaging.
- **Schistosomiasis** (S. haematobium) — bladder cancer + RBC + eosinophiluria; relevant if travel/residence in endemic areas.
- **Urinary stone disease** in SA: often calcium oxalate, sometimes uric acid (with hyperuricemia); cystinuria more common in some Saudi/Middle East populations than NW European.
- **Lean SA males with new diabetes** can present with candiduria as sentinel for hyperglycemia → check HbA1c.
- **Anabolic steroids** + body-building culture in young males (any background) → FSGS-pattern glomerular hematuria + proteinuria; classic young-male population (Herlitz 2010).
- **G6PD deficiency** higher in SA — fava bean / drug-induced hemolysis → pigmenturia + pigment casts.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**False positives (microscopy artifact / contamination):**
- **Refrigeration → amorphous urates/phosphates, CaOx crystals form de novo** (~55% of refrigerated samples per Cell Wiki) — re-warm to interpret
- **Old specimen (>1 h)** → bacterial overgrowth, RBC/WBC lysis, pH drift, casts dissolve (alkaline)
- **Vaginal/preputial contamination** → squamous cells + bacteria + yeast + WBCs all elevated
- **Menstruation** → RBCs
- **Recent sex** → squamous + sperm + occasional RBCs
- **Catheterization** → RBCs from instrumentation, bacteria from biofilm
- **Lubricants, antiseptics** → various artifacts
- **Talc / powder contamination** → crystals
- Mucus threads → can be misread as casts
- **Oxalate from collection container** → false CaOx crystals (rare)

**False negatives:**
- **RBC lysis in alkaline / dilute / old urine** → microscopy negative despite dipstick blood positive
- **Cast dissolution in alkaline urine** → may miss casts in old samples
- **WBC lysis** → false-low pyuria
- **High glucose / SG / protein** → suppresses LE chemistry alongside microscopy

**Drug confounders:**
- Phenazopyridine — color obscures everything
- Antibiotics — sterilizes urine, suppresses bacteria/yeast
- High-dose vitamin C — false-negative dipstick (alters interpretation context)

**Genetic / disease modifiers:**
- COL4A3/4/5 (Alport, thin basement membrane) — persistent dysmorphic microhematuria
- SLC3A1, SLC7A9 (cystinuria) — cystine crystals from childhood
- AGXT, GRHPR, HOGA1 (primary hyperoxaluria 1/2/3) — recurrent CaOx + nephrocalcinosis
- APOL1 G1/G2 (~13% African ancestry) — FSGS, HIVAN risk
- ADPKD (PKD1/2) — gross hematuria, episodic
- Sickle cell trait/disease — papillary necrosis
- HFE C282Y (hemochromatosis) — late liver/renal
- Wilson disease (ATP7B) — Fanconi-like proximal tubule
- Diabetes — yeast, bacteria, papillary necrosis

**Important pattern interpretations (engine rules):**

| Pattern | Suggests |
|---|---|
| Many bacteria + many WBCs + LE+/nitrite+ + symptoms | UTI — treat |
| Many bacteria + few WBCs + many squamous + asymptomatic | Contamination — recollect |
| Many RBCs + RBC casts + proteinuria + dysmorphic RBCs | Glomerulonephritis — nephrology |
| Many RBCs (isomorphic) + flank pain | Stones — imaging |
| Many WBCs + WBC casts + fever + flank pain | Pyelonephritis |
| Many WBCs + sterile culture + drug history | AIN — stop drug |
| Many WBCs + acidic urine + hematuria + endemic exposure | GU TB |
| Renal tubular epithelial cells + granular casts + AKI | ATN |
| Fatty casts + Maltese crosses + heavy proteinuria | Nephrotic syndrome |
| Hyaline casts + dehydration / exercise + no protein | Benign |
| Yeast + glycosuria | Diabetes — check HbA1c |
| Yeast + catheter | CAUTI yeast — usually no treatment unless symptomatic |
| CaOx crystals + acidic urine + dehydration | Diet + stones risk |
| Struvite (coffin-lid) + alkaline + Proteus on culture | Struvite stones — antibiotics + stone clearance |
| Hexagonal cystine crystals | Cystinuria — genetic |
| Needle-shaped CaOx monohydrate + AKI + osmolal gap + acidosis | Ethylene glycol poisoning — EMERGENCY (fomepizole + dialysis) |

**IDSA 2019 ASB Guideline (essential):**
- Do NOT treat asymptomatic bacteriuria in: healthy non-pregnant, diabetics, older adults (any setting), SCI, catheter, transplant >1 mo
- DO treat in: pregnancy (4–7 d antibiotics), pre-endoscopic urologic procedure with mucosal trauma

**AUA 2020 Microhematuria Guideline (essential):**
- Microhematuria = ≥3 RBCs/HPF on properly collected specimen; dipstick alone insufficient
- Risk-stratified: low (men <40, never-smoker, <10 RBCs/HPF), intermediate, high
- Anyone with proteinuria, dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, ↑Cr, HTN → nephrology FIRST regardless of risk

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Cxbladder, NMP22, BTA, FISH urinary biomarkers** — replacing cystoscopy in selected hematuria workup; AUA 2025 update incorporates
- **Automated flow cytometry (Sysmex UF, iQ200) accuracy vs. manual microscopy** — generally good but variable on dysmorphic RBC quantification
- **Phase-contrast microscopy adoption for glomerular hematuria** — improves dysmorphic RBC detection but not universally available
- **D-mannose, cranberry effectiveness** — Cochrane updates mixed; not first-line per IDSA 2024
- **Methenamine non-inferiority** to abx prophylaxis (ALTAR 2022) — adoption nascent in US
- **TESTING trial steroids for IgAN** — duration optimization (6 vs 12 mo) ongoing
- **Lumasiran cost-effectiveness** for primary hyperoxaluria 1
- **Whether candiduria needs treatment in non-immunocompromised, asymptomatic patients with catheter** — IDSA says no; controversy persists
- **Smart-toilet microscopy** — emerging, not validated
- **Genetic testing for non-stone-forming microhematuria** (Alport vs. thin BM disease) — clinical utility increasing but threshold debated
- **Urine eosinophils for AIN diagnosis** — sensitivity/specificity poor (Muriithi et al. *CJASN* 2013)
