# Ferritin — Intervention Research

> Highest-leverage levers: **(1) Treat the source** of loss before stacking interventions — occult GI bleed in any non-menstruating adult with low ferritin trumps every dietary tactic. **(2) Oral iron** at 60 mg elemental on **alternate days** (Stoffel 2017, PMID 29032957) absorbs ~34% more per dose than daily and replets ferritin from <15 to 50–100 µg/L over 12–20 weeks. **(3) Vitamin C co-ingestion** with non-heme iron meals matters most in high-phytate vegetarian diets (single-meal RCT effect 2–3×, real-diet effect modest — Cook 2001, PMID 11124756). **(4) IV ferric carboxymaltose** 1000 mg is the pharmacologic ceiling, achieving repletion in 4–6 weeks. For **elevated ferritin**, the dominant levers are **eliminating the inflammatory/hepatic confound** (alcohol cessation, MASLD treatment, infection resolution) and — only if true overload (TSAT >45%, HFE genotype) — **therapeutic phlebotomy**, which lowers ferritin ~30–50 µg/L per 500 mL session.

**Cross-link:** Reference biology and clinical interpretation lives in `../../nutrients_vitamins_minerals.md` §1 (Ferritin) and `../../effect_sizes_nutrients.md` §Ferritin. This file is INTERVENTION-only — what moves the marker, by how much, in how long, with what evidence.

**Critical note:** Ferritin is an **acute-phase reactant**. Inflammation (CRP >5 mg/L), recent illness, MASLD/NAFLD, alcohol use, and hepatocellular injury can elevate ferritin independent of true iron stores. Always pair ferritin with **TSAT + CRP** before classifying iron status. A ferritin of 250 with TSAT 18% and CRP 12 is most likely *functional iron deficiency in inflammation*, not adequate stores. See §8.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs with consistent direction |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT (≥100 participants or pivotal design) |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational; can't establish causality |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization — genetic proxies estimating causal effect |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology / physiology extrapolation; no direct human RCT for *this specific effect* |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial worth knowing but not yet replicated |
| **[GL]** | Guideline-derived (BSH, AGA, AASLD, EASL, ASH) |

Population caveat: most intervention magnitudes derive from iron-depleted women (menstruating, post-pregnancy) and elderly with chronic disease. **Lean healthy young men with ferritin 60–120 µg/L typically gain only 30–60% of cited magnitudes** because absorption is hepcidin-throttled at higher stores. Conversely, frank-deficient adults (ferritin <15) show *larger* absolute responses than the cited averages.

## 3. Supplements

### Raising ferritin (when low)

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ferrous sulfate, daily** | 60 mg elemental Fe (≈325 mg ferrous sulfate) once daily, with vitamin C, away from meals/calcium/coffee | Ferritin +10–25 µg/L per 8 wk in depleted women; Hgb +1.0–1.4 g/dL over 8 wk | 4–12 wk for Hgb; 3–6 mo for ferritin repletion | **[RCT, Meta]** | Stoffel 2017 *Lancet Haematol* PMID 29032957; Cançado 2024 *Am J Med* meta | GI side effects (nausea, constipation, dark stools, epigastric pain) in ~25%; dose-response flattens above 40–60 mg/d due to hepcidin saturation |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

**For LOW ferritin (<30 µg/L, or <15 frank deficiency):**

- **Highest-leverage single intervention:** IV ferric carboxymaltose 1000 mg achieves repletion in 4–6 weeks and bypasses all absorption barriers. For oral repletion of frank deficiency, **alternate-day ferrous sulfate 60 mg elemental** (or equivalent ferrous bisglycinate 25 mg) reaches ferritin 50–100 µg/L over 12–20 weeks.
- **Best stack of 3 (oral repletion):** (1) Ferrous bisglycinate 25 mg every other morning, fasting + 100 mg vitamin C; (2) Increase heme iron meals 3–4×/wk (lean beef, lamb, oysters/clams 1×/wk); (3) Eliminate tea/coffee/dairy within 2 h of iron supplement and iron-rich meals. Expect ferritin +30–60 µg/L over 12 wk in moderate deficiency.
- **Realistic 12-week delta for healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (ferritin 60–120 µg/L):** Negligible gain from any oral intervention — hepcidin is suppressed and absorption is throttled to ~5–10%. **Avoid supplementing iron above maintenance** unless deficiency confirmed; risk of iatrogenic overload trajectory (especially if HFE C282Y heterozygous).
- **Realistic 12-week delta for someone with elevated baseline (ferritin <15 with TSAT <16%):** Alternate-day ferrous sulfate 60 mg + vitamin C → ferritin 30–60 µg/L by week 12; full repletion (>100) typically by 4–6 mo. IV FCM short-circuits to ferritin 100–200 by week 6.
- **Where pharmacotherapy (IV iron) becomes necessary:** Oral failure (no ferritin response after 4–8 wk of adherent therapy), GI intolerance preventing adherence, ongoing GI/uterine blood loss outpacing oral repletion, malabsorption (celiac, IBD, post-bariatric), CKD with ESA, severe deficiency requiring rapid correction (Hgb <8, pre-op), TMPRSS6 LOF variant suspected.

**For HIGH ferritin (>300 µg/L men, >200 women — workup priority list):**

- **Step 1: Confirm — repeat fasting morning ferritin + TSAT + CRP.** Inflammation/recent illness explains many spurious elevations.
- **Step 2: Identify the driver.** Order CRP, ALT/AST, GGT (alcohol), HbA1c/lipids (DIOS), HFE genotype if TSAT >45%. The clinical question is iron overload vs. confounder.
- **Step 3 — confounder treatment (most cases):** Alcohol cessation (4–8 wk → ferritin re-check; expect 30–50% decline if alcohol-driven); 7–10% weight loss for MASLD/DIOS (6–12 mo → ferritin ↓ 30–50%); treat infection/inflammation.
- **Step 4 — true overload only (HFE+ or TSAT>45% confirmed):** Therapeutic phlebotomy 500 mL weekly until ferritin 50–100 µg/L (typically 1–2 yr induction), then maintenance every 3–4 mo (EASL 2022).
- **What does NOT work for elevated ferritin without true overload:** "Iron-binding" supplements (IP6, lactoferrin), low-iron diets in absence of HFE/overload (impractical and not indicated), curcumin (chelation magnitude tiny relative to body iron stores).

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- **Vegetarian SA diet is the dominant population-level driver of low ferritin in SA men.** Combination of high phytate (legumes, whole wheat roti, brown rice), low heme iron (lacto-vegetarian or vegetarian), often low vitamin C with cooked meals, and chai (tea) post-meal: non-heme iron bioavailability collapses to 2–5% vs 15–35% for heme iron. Practical strategy:
  1. Vitamin C (citrus, tomato, capsicum, lemon) at every iron-rich meal — single-meal effect is real even if real-diet is modest
  2. Separate chai/coffee from meals by ≥1 h (ideally 2 h)
  3. Soak and ferment legumes (idli, dosa batter, dhokla) → phytate ↓ 50–80% (Hotz & Gibson, frontiersin 2024 maize fermentation data; Gibson 2018 *Nutr Rev*)
  4. Sourdough leavening for whole-grain breads (lactobacillus phytase)
  5. Cook in cast iron (Indian "loha kadai") — meaningful iron leach into acidic foods (tomato curries gain ~2–3 mg Fe/serving)

- **TMPRSS6 (rs855791) T allele is more common in SA populations than European** (~50–60% allele frequency in SA vs 30–40% European). T allele encodes loss-of-function matriptase-2 → higher hepcidin → blunted oral iron response. **A SA male with low ferritin who fails to respond to 6–8 wk of adequate oral iron should escalate earlier to alternate-day dosing or IV iron** rather than persisting with daily ferrous sulfate.

- **HFE C282Y is rare in SA (<1% allele frequency vs ~6% NW European), but H63D is moderately prevalent in SA (~10%).** H63D homozygotes / compound heterozygotes can have mildly elevated ferritin/TSAT but rarely clinical overload. Don't assume hemochromatosis from elevated ferritin in SA without HFE testing.

- **Alcohol contribution to ferritin elevation** is sometimes underweighted in SA male assessments because cultural reporting biases under-state intake. Always ask quantitatively (drinks per week) when ferritin is elevated and TSAT normal.

- **Lean SA male phenotype with insulin resistance** (high HbA1c, low HDL, high TG) on a westernized diet can develop DIOS even at BMI 22–25 — metabolic hyperferritinemia in apparently lean individuals is documented (Valenti consensus 2023).

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **HFE rs1800562 (C282Y)** — homozygotes (C282Y/C282Y) absorb excess iron from any source; heterozygotes mildly elevated ferritin/TSAT in some but rarely clinical overload. **Do NOT recommend iron supplementation to a C282Y homozygote** without hematology consult.
- **HFE rs1799945 (H63D)** — compound heterozygotes with C282Y have low penetrance overload risk; H63D/H63D rarely clinically significant.
- **TMPRSS6 rs855791** — T allele → higher hepcidin → reduced oral iron absorption → harder to replete with daily oral iron → **recommend alternate-day dosing earlier or escalate to IV**. Common in SA populations.
- **TFR2** rare variants — hemochromatosis type 3.

**Drug interactions (raise ferritin / iron measures):**
- IV iron → ferritin spikes acutely (test acute-phase elevation); re-check at 4–8 wk, not 48 h
- Hormone replacement / oral contraceptive pills → elevate transferrin/TIBC → can affect TSAT calculation but ferritin minimally affected directly
- Glucocorticoids → can elevate ferritin via mild hepatic stress

**Drug interactions (lower iron absorption / blunt ferritin response):**
- PPIs (≥2 yr) — IDA OR 2.49 (Lam 2017 PMID 27890768); H2 blockers OR 1.58
- Antacids, sucralfate — gastric pH and binding effects
- Tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, levothyroxine, bisphosphonates, mycophenolate — bind iron in gut; separate by ≥2–4 h
- Cholestyramine, colestipol — bind iron
- Curcumin high-dose, polyphenol supplements — chelate Fe
- Calcium and zinc supplements — DMT1 competition

**Lifestyle / physiologic confounders for ferritin (CRITICAL — ferritin is acute-phase reactant):**
- **Inflammation (CRP >5 mg/L)** — ferritin can be 2–5× elevated independent of iron stores. Always pair with TSAT + CRP. Use sTfR or sTfR/log-ferritin ratio when CRP elevated.
- **Recent illness / infection** — ferritin can be elevated for 2–6 wk post-resolution
- **Alcohol** (>2 drinks/d sustained) — hepatic ferritin release; 2–5× spurious elevation
- **MASLD/NAFLD** — DIOS pattern, ferritin 300–1000 with normal/mildly elevated TSAT in 30–35% MASLD
- **Hepatocellular injury (any cause — drug, viral, ischemic)** — ferritin release
- **Endurance training (acute, single session)** — APR-driven transient ferritin ↑ for 24–72 h
- **Endurance training (chronic, sustained)** — opposite direction: ferritin ↓ over weeks
- **Pregnancy** — ferritin falls physiologically; trimester-specific cutoffs apply
- **OCP/HRT** — minimal direct ferritin effect but raises transferrin → affects TSAT denominator
- **Hyperthyroidism** — elevates ferritin
- **Adult-onset Still's disease, hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), macrophage activation syndrome** — ferritin >10,000 µg/L is a diagnostic flag; emergent

**Timing artifacts:**
- Iron supplements within 24 h of draw → false elevation of serum iron + TSAT (ferritin less affected acutely)
- Recent IV iron → acute ferritin spike for weeks
- Diurnal: ferritin has minor diurnal variation (smaller than serum iron)
- Fasting: ferritin not strongly affected by fasting status

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Lactoferrin oral supplementation** — small RCTs in pregnancy IDA show modest benefit but evidence is too limited and inconsistent to recommend routinely. Mechanism plausible (lactoferrin binds Fe and may bypass hepcidin) but not pivotal.
- **Heme iron polypeptide supplements** ("Proferrin") — promoted as superior absorption but RCT data are limited and mixed; no convincing evidence of superiority over ferrous sulfate at equivalent elemental dose.
- **Vitamin C real-world effect on ferritin** — single-meal isotope studies show ×2–3 absorption enhancement; long-term mixed-diet data (Cook 2001) shows much smaller real-world effect (~no detectable change with 51→247 mg/d range). The mechanism is real; the realistic sustained ferritin gain from "eat more vitamin C" alone is small. Larger if combined with iron supplement and/or in high-phytate vegetarian context.
- **Optimal ferritin target for healthy young men (no deficiency)** — debated. Conventional reference range 30–400 µg/L; functional medicine often targets 70–150; sports medicine often targets 50–80 in endurance athletes for performance. **Insufficient RCT evidence** that targeting ferritin 100 vs 60 yields meaningful health gain in non-deficient adults. Avoid supplementing into the upper range — iron overload risk, oxidative stress.
- **Curcumin clinically meaningful chelation** — mechanism real (Jiao 2009 *Blood*) but case reports rare; magnitude in typical supplement doses is debated. Flag as interaction with iron supplements but not as a routine therapy for elevated ferritin.
- **Alternate-day vs daily oral iron in non-anemic ID (low ferritin + normal Hgb)** — Stoffel data are in iron-depleted but not necessarily anemic women; transportability to males, healthy young adults, athletes is presumed but not directly tested in pivotal trial.
- **TMPRSS6-guided iron therapy** — concept well-supported mechanistically; no large clinical trial yet stratifying patients by genotype to guide oral vs IV iron decision.
- **Soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR) and sTfR/log-ferritin index** — superior to ferritin in inflammation; not on most consumer labs (including Function Health typical panels) — important confound when CRP elevated. Recommend ordering sTfR via Quest/Labcorp when ferritin and CRP both elevated.
- **Hepcidin assay** — research-grade; not yet clinically standardized. Would be the ideal marker for iron-supply status independent of inflammation if affordable/available.
