# Arachidonic Acid (AA, 20:4n-6) — Intervention Research

> **Highest-leverage:** AA in RBC/plasma is determined by (a) direct dietary AA from animal foods (eggs, poultry skin, organ meats, fatty meats — typical Western intake ~100–250 mg/d), (b) FADS1/2 desaturase activity converting LA → AA, and (c) competitive crowding by EPA/DHA. The actionable lever is rarely "lower AA" in absolute terms — AA is essential for membrane structure, immune function, gastric mucosal protection, and platelet hemostasis. The clinically meaningful target is the **AA:EPA ratio** (see `AA_EPA_ratio.md`), which is moved by raising EPA, not by suppressing AA. Direct AA reduction (vegan diet, no organ meats) lowers AA modestly (~10–15% relative) but rarely below the optimal range in adults with intact FADS conversion.

**Cross-link:** Biology, reference ranges, and clinical interpretation in `../../nutrients_omega_fatty_acids.md` §7. Mendelian-randomization context (no clear causal CV harm) summarized there. This file is INTERVENTION-only.

Population caveat: most data NW European/US adults. AA is remarkably stable across populations and decades (~14–17% RBC) — one of the most homeostatically-defended PUFAs.

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention:** Raise EPA/DHA (fatty fish or fish oil 2 g/d EPA+DHA) — this lowers AA by ~1–2 pp via membrane displacement AND lowers the AA:EPA ratio dramatically. Most clinically meaningful since AA itself is rarely the problem; the EPA-side is what shifts eicosanoid balance toward resolution.
- **Best stack of 3 (for lowering AA / improving AA:EPA balance):**
  1. Fish oil 2 g EPA+DHA/d (or EPA-enriched 1.5 g EPA/d)
  2. Reduce daily eggs to ≤4/wk; reduce poultry skin and organ meats
  3. Replace some refined LA-rich oils with olive oil (modest LA→AA pathway reduction)
  → AA ↓ ~1.5–2.5 pp in 16 weeks while maintaining AA in physiological range; AA:EPA falls dramatically.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (AA ~15%): −1 to −1.5 pp from 2 g/d fish oil. AA at the lower end of the range is favorable provided EPA is adequate.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for very high baseline (AA >18% RBC, high egg/organ-meat intake): −2 to −3 pp via fish oil + dietary reduction. Re-test at 16 wk.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:** Almost never — AA is essential. Only consider AA-lowering pharmacology in research-protocol contexts. Pharmacologic AA-eicosanoid suppression (NSAIDs, aspirin) modulates downstream signaling without changing the biomarker.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- **FADS2 rs66698963 SA insertion** carriers (~70%) have ~43% higher AA at matched LA intake (Mathias 2014 *Mol Biol Evol*; OCL 2021 review). SA males with this allele often run RBC AA at the high end of the reference range (16–18%) even on moderate-egg, low-organ-meat diets.
- **AA:EPA ratio runs higher** in SA carriers because the same FADS efficiency boosts AA synthesis from LA. Direct EPA supplementation has greater impact on this ratio in SA than in European-ancestry peers.
- **Vegetarian SA males** often have AA at the higher end of the vegan range (RBC ~13–14%) — endogenous synthesis from high-LA Indian cuisine (sunflower, soybean ghee/oil) plus FADS efficiency more than compensates for absent dietary AA.
- The recommendation engine should NOT push SA males to lower AA below ~13% RBC absent pharmacologic context.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **FADS1 rs174537, FADS2 rs174575**: minor allele "A" → less efficient LA→AA conversion → lower tissue AA at matched intake. "D" haplotype → +43% AA at matched intake.
- **FADS2 rs66698963 insertion** (SA "vegetarian allele"): +25–40% conversion → higher AA at matched LA/ALA intake.
- **PLA2G6 / PLA2G4 (phospholipase A2 variants)**: affect AA release from membrane PL into eicosanoid synthesis pool — modify clinical eicosanoid output without changing total RBC AA.

**Drug interactions:**
- **NSAIDs / aspirin**: do NOT lower tissue AA; modulate downstream eicosanoid output.
- **Fibrates (PPAR-α agonists)**: minor AA-lowering effect via lipid metabolism redirection.
- **Statins**: no direct AA effect.
- **Glucocorticoids**: suppress phospholipase A2 → reduce AA release into eicosanoid pool; total AA unchanged.
- **Insulin / metformin / SGLT2 inhibitors**: insulin sensitivity changes can shift FADS expression and AA synthesis modestly.

**Lifestyle confounders / timing:**
- **RBC equilibration:** ~120 days for full AA pool turnover.
- **Recent egg-heavy meal:** plasma AA shifts within hours; RBC unchanged.
- **Acute illness / inflammation:** AA mobilization from membrane to eicosanoid synthesis ↑; RBC AA may transiently fall.
- **Hyperinsulinemia / insulin resistance:** chronically up-regulates FADS2 → higher AA. Insulin-sensitizing interventions may modestly lower AA.

**Assay artifacts:**
- AA % is matrix-dependent (RBC ~14–17%, plasma PL ~10–12%). Compare within same lab.
- Because reported % is relative to total identified FA, raising EPA/DHA *automatically* lowers the relative AA % even with constant absolute AA — this is real biology (membrane competition) but the "as % of total" metric amplifies the appearance of change.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Causal CV effect of AA:** Mendelian randomization (Zhao 2020; FORCE consortium MR) does NOT support causal harm of higher AA on CVD outcomes; some signals of benefit (Marklund 2019 *Circulation*). The "AA = inflammation = bad" framing is mechanistically intuitive but epidemiologically not supported.
- **AA upper bound:** No clear evidence of harm at RBC AA up to ~18%. The "seed-oils → high AA → CVD" pathway is mechanistically tenuous because LA→AA conversion is only ~0.2–1% (Hussein 2005 *J Lipid Res*).
- **AA lower bound:** AA below ~10% is uncommon in adults; clinical sequelae poorly characterized. Pediatric AA deficiency causes growth failure / skin issues; adult equivalent unclear.
- **AA and exercise adaptation:** Animal models suggest AA-derived PGE2 critical for muscle hypertrophy signaling. Whether AA "supplementation" enhances training adaptation in humans is contested (Roberts 2007 small positive trial; subsequent trials mixed).
- **AA in inflammatory disease:** RA, IBD, asthma — all have elevated AA-eicosanoid output, but lowering tissue AA does not consistently reverse disease activity. Pharmacologic eicosanoid blockade (anti-leukotriene, anti-PGE2) is more effective than dietary AA reduction.
- **Pediatric and pregnancy AA requirements:** AA is essential; restriction in pregnancy/lactation may impair fetal brain development. Recommendation engine must NOT push aggressive AA reduction in these populations.
- **Wearable correlates:** none well-established for AA alone; AA:EPA ratio has slightly better wearable correlations (HRV).
