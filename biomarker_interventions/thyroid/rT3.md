# Reverse T3 (rT3, 3,3',5'-Triiodothyronine) — Intervention Research

> rT3 is the inactive D3-generated metabolite of T4. It rises **reliably** in non-thyroidal illness syndrome (NTIS), prolonged fasting, severe caloric/CHO restriction, propranolol use, high-dose glucocorticoids, and amiodarone. It is **not** a Function Health panel default — it's an add-on. The "FT3/rT3 ratio < 20 → low-T3 syndrome → treat with T3" framework is biologically plausible but lacks RCT outcome data in ambulatory healthy adults (Halsall & Oddy 2021). The 2014 ATA hypothyroidism guidelines explicitly state that rT3 is not useful for guiding therapy. The few interventions that *lower* rT3 in healthy people are mostly the inverse of the things that raise it (eat enough food/CHO, taper propranolol, treat the underlying illness).

## 1. Foods that LOWER rT3

The only validated way to lower rT3 in a healthy adult is to **stop doing the things that raise it** (caloric deficit, CHO restriction, prolonged fasting, alcohol). No food has been shown to directly reduce rT3 in a euthyroid adult.

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Refeeding carbohydrates** (after deficit) | 150+ g CHO/day | rT3 normalizes (D3 downregulates) within days–weeks of restoring energy availability; the inverse of fasting/keto rise | 1–2 wk | [RCT] | Spaulding *JCEM* 1976 (T3 ↓ −47% on no-CHO 800 kcal × 2 wk; reverses with refeeding); Boelen *Endocr Rev* 2011 NTIS review | The most reliable food-based intervention for an elevated rT3 is **eat enough food, including carbohydrate.** |
| **Adequate protein + caloric balance** | ≥1.0 g/kg protein, eucaloric | rT3 normalizes if it was driven by energy/protein deficit | Weeks | [Cohort] | Loucks 1994 *J Appl Physiol* (athlete energy availability); Mountjoy IOC RED-S 2018 | Indirect; works through restoring caloric availability. |
| **Selenium-replete foods** (Brazil nuts, sea fish, eggs, organ meats) | 1–2 Brazil nuts/d or equivalent | If selenium-deficient: rT3 may ↓ via restored D1 activity (D1 clears rT3) | 8–12 wk | [Mechanism] | Bianco 2019 *Endocr Rev* | Effect requires baseline Se deficiency. |

(Note: the literature base for "foods that lower rT3" in healthy euthyroid people is essentially null.)

## 2. Foods / Patterns that RAISE rT3

| Food / pattern | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Prolonged fasting** | >24 h | rT3 ↑ 30–60%; T3 ↓ 25–50% | 24–72 h | [RCT] | Vagenakis *JCEM* 1975;41:191; Boelen 2011 NTIS review | Reversible with refeeding. Adaptive energy-conservation. |
| **Very-low-CHO / ketogenic diet** | <50 g CHO/d sustained | rT3: typically modest ↑ in chronic keto; **note Spaulding 1976 found NO rT3 change with no-CHO 800 kcal × 2 wk** despite −47% T3 | Weeks | [RCT/Mixed] | Spaulding 1976 (no rT3 change despite T3 fall — exception to the rule); other keto studies show modest rT3 ↑; Iacovides 2022 PLoS One | Nuanced: T3 falls reliably, rT3 less reliably. |
| **Severe caloric deficit (VLCD)** | <800 kcal/d | rT3 ↑ 30–60% | Days | [RCT] | Vagenakis 1975 | Marker of NTIS-like adaptation. |
| **Alcohol (chronic heavy)** | >3 drinks/d | rT3 ↑ in chronic heavy use; pattern resolves with abstinence | Weeks | [Cohort] | Hegedüs 1988; multiple alcoholism cohorts | Confounded with hepatic dysfunction. |
| **Acute large meal of carbohydrate after fast** | Refeed | rT3 may transiently spike then normalize | Days | [Mechanism] | Boelen 2011 | Marginal; not a clinical concern. |

## 3. Supplements & Drugs

### Lowering rT3

| Supplement / Drug | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Selenium (selenomethionine), if deficient** | 100–200 µg/d | rT3 ↓ if D1 was impaired by Se deficiency (D1 clears rT3); minimal effect in Se-replete | 8–12 wk | [Mechanism/1-Trial] | Bianco 2019 *Endocr Rev* (D1 is selenoenzyme); Olivieri *Clin Sci (Lond)* 1996 (Se in elderly → improved D1 activity) | Effect clearest in documented Se deficiency. No convincing RCT in healthy euthyroid. |
| **Stop offending drug** (propranolol, glucocorticoid, amiodarone) | Cessation/taper per indication | rT3 normalizes within 1–4 wk of D1-inhibitor removal | 1–4 wk | [Mechanism] | Wiersinga *Acta Endocrinol* 1982 (propranolol); standard pharmacology | Often the highest-yield intervention. |
| **Treat underlying NTIS cause** (resolve illness) | — | rT3 normalizes as illness resolves; T3 recovers slower | Weeks | [Cohort] | Boelen *Endocr Rev* 2011 | T3/T4/rT3 recovery is a marker of NTIS resolution, not a treatment target. |

| Supplement / Drug | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Propranolol** [Rx, non-cardioselective β-blocker] | 80–160 mg/d | rT3 ↑ 20–40% via D1 inhibition (T3 ↓ 10–20%; T4 unchanged) | 1–2 wk | [RCT] | Wiersinga 1982 *Acta Endocrinol*; standard pharmacology | Cardioselective β-blockers (atenolol, metoprolol) lack D1 inhibition. |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** for elevated rT3 in an ambulatory healthy adult:
  - **Restore caloric and carbohydrate availability.** Energy deficit + low CHO is by far the most common reversible cause of high rT3 in lean optimizers. Add 200–500 kcal/d (especially CHO) and recheck in 4–8 wk.
  - If the user is on a high-dose β-blocker (especially propranolol), high-dose glucocorticoids, or amiodarone, those drugs are likely driving rT3 — discuss alternatives with prescribing physician.
- **Best stack of 3** (for FT3:rT3 ratio < 20 with no overt thyroid disease):
  1. **Energy / CHO restoration** (highest leverage).
  2. **Selenium 200 µg/d × 12 wk** if Se status unknown (Bianco 2019 mechanism; D1 clears rT3).
  3. **Sleep regularization** to 7–9 h with consistent timing (modest indirect effect).
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for a healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (rT3 ~15 ng/dL, mid-normal): minimal change from any non-pharmacological intervention. rT3 in an ambulatory healthy euthyroid is largely set by D1/D3 expression and not easily moved.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for a lean over-trained 20yM with high rT3 (~30 ng/dL): with energy restoration, rT3 commonly normalizes to mid-normal (15–20) within 4–8 wk.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary**:
  - rT3 elevation in NTIS is **not** a treatment target — multiple RCTs of T3/T4 in ICU NTIS show no outcome benefit (and some showed harm).
  - rT3 elevation due to a specific drug (propranolol, glucocorticoid, amiodarone) → adjust the offending drug per indication, not add T3.
  - Combination LT4+LT3 therapy in **treated** hypothyroid patients with persistent symptoms is the only legitimate context for T3 supplementation — not "to lower rT3" per se, but to address persistent low FT3 (Jonklaas ATA 2014; Hennessey 2019 review).
  - The ATA 2014 hypothyroidism guidelines (Jonklaas et al. *Thyroid* 2014;24:1670) state explicitly: "evidence that the analysis of serum rT3 concentration can aid further modifications to treatment such as T3 therapy is currently lacking, and the use of rT3 in this manner cannot be recommended."
  - Halsall & Oddy *Ann Clin Biochem* 2021 reaffirm: rT3 has **one** validated clinical role — distinguishing NTIS (high rT3) from central hypothyroidism (low/normal rT3) in hospitalized patients.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- South Asian endurance athletes / lean vegetarian optimizers are a high-risk group for "low-T3 athlete" phenotype (low FT3, high rT3) due to combined low body fat + low caloric availability + iron deficiency.
- No SA-specific RCT evidence for rT3-targeted interventions; default to general population evidence.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **DIO1 polymorphisms** (rs2235544, rs11206244): modulate D1 activity → T3:rT3 ratio shifts; population effects subtle.
- **DIO3 expression**: not commonly genotyped; D3 upregulation is the proximate driver of rT3 generation in NTIS.
- **SBP2 (SECISBP2) mutations**: rare; impair selenoprotein synthesis → low FT3 / high rT3 / abnormal T4 (Schoenmakers *J Clin Invest* 2010).

**Drug confounders (key rT3 elevators):**
- **Biotin ≥5 mg/d** → assay artifact (typically falsely elevated on competitive immunoassays). Stop ≥72 h before draw.
- **Propranolol, nadolol** (non-selective β-blockers) → D1 inhibition → rT3 ↑; cardioselective β-blockers do not have this effect.
- **High-dose glucocorticoids** (prednisone ≥20 mg/d) → rT3 ↑.
- **Amiodarone** → rT3 ↑↑ (D1 inhibition + 37% iodine content).
- **Iopanoic acid, ipodate** (older oral contrast media) → rT3 ↑.
- **Phenytoin, carbamazepine** → variable rT3 effects via T4 catabolism induction.
- **Salicylates (high-dose)** → rT3 ↑ acutely.
- **Furosemide (high IV dose)** → displaces T4 from TBG; may transiently affect rT3 measurement.

**Lifestyle / timing artifacts:**
- **Caloric/CHO deficit, fasting, prolonged exercise** → rT3 ↑.
- **Acute illness** → NTIS, rT3 ↑↑.
- **Heavy endurance training + low body fat** → "low-T3 athlete."
- **rT3 has minimal diurnal variation** (more stable than TSH); time-of-day effects are smaller than for TSH.
- **rT3 rises naturally in recovery from acute illness for days–weeks** as the system normalizes.

**Assay limitations:**
- rT3 is measured by immunoassay (Quest, LabCorp) or LC-MS/MS (Mayo). Inter-platform CV is large (15–25%); never compare absolute rT3 values across labs.
- Reference range typically 9–24 ng/dL (Quest, LabCorp); some labs use 10–35.
- The "FT3/rT3 ratio" requires both values from the same draw; ratio > 20 is the popular optimization target but is not validated.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Does treating an elevated rT3 in an asymptomatic ambulatory adult improve any outcome?** No RCT data. Halsall & Oddy 2021 conclude: no.
- **Is the "FT3/rT3 ratio" a reliable diagnostic tool outside NTIS?** Not validated in ambulatory healthy populations.
- **Does selenium meaningfully lower rT3 in healthy euthyroid Se-replete adults?** Probably not (D1 not rate-limited at adequate Se). Evidence dominated by Se-deficient elderly cohorts.
- **Whether the "low-T3 syndrome" of chronic dieting is harmful or adaptive** is debated. In recovery from acute illness it is clearly adaptive; in chronic mild restriction it is unclear. No outcome RCT.
- **Combination LT4+LT3 specifically to "lower rT3"**: not a guideline-supported indication.
- **Does cold exposure / sauna meaningfully alter chronic rT3?** Largely untested.
- **Is rT3 a useful longitudinal biomarker in healthy young men?** Compared to FT3 and TSH, the incremental information added by rT3 is small in non-NTIS contexts and the assay variability is high. ATA does not recommend routine inclusion.
