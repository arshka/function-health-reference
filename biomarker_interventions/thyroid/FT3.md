# Free T3 (Free Triiodothyronine) — Intervention Research

> Free T3 is the **biologically active** thyroid hormone and the most acutely sensitive to peripheral conversion changes. The two largest movers in real life are **caloric/carbohydrate restriction** (drops FT3 20–47% via D1 downregulation; "low-T3 syndrome") and **acute illness/NTIS**. The most useful *positive* intervention is **eating enough food and carbohydrate** — chronic deficit is the dominant cause of unexpectedly low FT3 in healthy lean optimizers. Selenium repletion in deficiency, iron repletion in deficiency, and combination LT4+LT3 therapy are the validated paths to raising FT3 in pathological lows. Most "T3 booster" supplements are unsupported.

## 3. Supplements

### Raising FT3

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Selenium (selenomethionine), if deficient** | 100–200 µg/d × 8–12 wk | FT3 ↑ +5–15% in selenium-deficient subjects (D1/D2 restoration); **no consistent FT3 change in selenium-replete** | 8–12 wk | [Mechanism/1-Trial] | Bianco 2019 *Endocr Rev*; Bonfig 2007 *Eur J Endocrinol*; Negro *JCEM* 2007; Huwiler 2024 meta confirms minimal FT3 change in mostly-replete AITD trials | Effect clearest in documented Se deficiency (serum Se <70–90 ng/mL); minimal in US/European subjects with adequate selenium. |
| **Iron (ferrous sulfate, ferrous bisglycinate), if deficient** | 30–65 mg elemental Fe/d to ferritin >50 ng/mL | If iron-deficient with low FT3: FT3 ↑ modestly with repletion | 3–6 mo | [Cohort] | Zimmermann 2002 *Am J Clin Nutr*; Eftekhari 2006 *Public Health Nutr* | TPO is iron-dependent (heme cofactor); deficiency impairs hormone synthesis. Effect requires baseline iron deficiency. |
| **Zinc (15–30 mg/d), if deficient** | 15–30 mg/d | FT3 may ↑ in zinc-deficient subjects; zinc is structural cofactor for TR-α/β receptors and may modulate D2 | 8–12 wk | [1-Trial] | Mahmoodianfard *J Am Coll Nutr* 2015 (zinc + selenium in obese hypothyroid women, n=68); Maxwell *Br J Med Med Res* 2007 | Effect modest; combine with selenium. |
| **Liothyronine (L-T3, Cytomel)** [Rx] | 5–25 µg/d | FT3 ↑ predictably (T3 t½ ~1 day); supraphysiologic peaks 2–4 h post-dose; trough often subnormal on once-daily dosing | 1–2 wk steady state | [RCT] | Jonklaas ATA *Thyroid* 2014 review; Bunevicius *NEJM* 1999 (n=33 LT4 vs LT4+LT3 crossover) | Not first-line per ATA. | **Combination LT4 + LT3** [Rx] | 4:1 to 14:1 T4:T3 ratio | FT3 normalizes mid-range while keeping FT4 normal; TSH stays in target | 4–8 wk | [RCT/Meta] | Bunevicius 1999 *NEJM*; Grozinsky-Glasberg *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2006 (meta of 11 RCTs — no overall benefit on QoL); Akirov *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2021 review | Most meta-analyses show no overall QoL benefit; subgroup with persistent symptoms on LT4 monotherapy + DIO2 Ala/Ala may benefit (TRUE-T3 trial pending). |
| **NDT (desiccated thyroid extract)** [Rx] | Variable T4+T3 per grain | FT3 ↑ with supraphysiologic 2–4 h peaks | 4–8 wk | [RCT] | Hoang *JCEM* 2013 crossover (n=70: NDT vs LT4) | ATA does not recommend NDT first-line. T4:T3 ratio ~4:1 is supraphysiologic. |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** (when FT3 is genuinely low with normal TSH/FT4):
  - **Increase energy availability / carbohydrate intake** if there is any hint of caloric deficit, low body fat, or high training load. This is the single largest reversible cause of low FT3 in healthy young athletes/optimizers.
  - If energy is adequate: check ferritin (target >50 ng/mL) and serum selenium (>100 ng/mL).
- **Best stack of 3** (for documented low FT3 with normal TSH/FT4 in a non-NTIS context):
  1. **Restore energy availability** (≥30 kcal/kg FFM/day, sufficient CHO).
  2. **Selenium 200 µg/d × 12 wk** if Se status unknown or deficient (Bianco 2019 mechanism; Bonfig 2007 RCT).
  3. **Iron repletion** to ferritin 50–100 ng/mL if iron-deficient (Zimmermann 2002).
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for a healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (FT3 3.2 pg/mL): ≤ 0.3 pg/mL change from any non-pharmacological intervention. FT3 is regulated tightly by TSH feedback in euthyroid.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for a lean over-trained 20yM (FT3 2.4 pg/mL, low normal): with energy restoration alone, FT3 commonly normalizes 0.4–0.8 pg/mL within 8–12 wk; selenium adds modestly if deficient.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary**:
  - **Documented overt hypothyroidism (TSH high, FT4 low)**: LT4 monotherapy first; consider LT4+LT3 only if persistent symptoms after 6+ months on optimized LT4 (TSH 0.5–2.5) and FT3 remains low-normal (Jonklaas ATA 2014; Hennessey 2019).
  - **DIO2 rs225014 Ala/Ala carriers with persistent symptoms on LT4**: trial of LT4+LT3 reasonable per Peeters 2017 evidence; not guideline-mandated.
  - **Hyperthyroid (high FT3/FT4, suppressed TSH)**: methimazole/PTU + propranolol; definitive therapy (RAI/surgery) for relapse.
  - **NTIS in critical illness**: do not treat with T3/T4 — no outcome benefit and possible harm (RCT evidence from cardiac surgery and burn cohorts).

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- Lean SA endurance athletes are at elevated risk of "low-T3 athlete" phenotype due to the combination of low body fat, vegetarian diets prone to iron deficiency, and high training load relative to caloric intake.
- Iron deficiency is the most frequent treatable cause of low FT3 in SA vegetarians; check ferritin first.
- DIO2 rs225014 allele frequency is similar across major populations (~30–40% Ala in most ancestries).
- No SA-specific RCT evidence for selenium repletion in healthy euthyroid; default to general-population evidence.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **DIO2 rs225014 (Thr92Ala)**: Ala/Ala homozygotes (~12% Europeans) have impaired peripheral T4→T3 conversion → may have low-normal FT3 with normal TSH/FT4 on LT4 monotherapy and report persistent symptoms (Peeters *JCEM* 2017;102:1623). Native (untreated) FT3 effects are smaller.
- **SBP2 (SECISBP2) mutations**: rare; impair selenoprotein synthesis → impaired D1/D2 → low FT3, low T4, high rT3 (Schoenmakers *J Clin Invest* 2010).
- **MCT8 (SLC16A2) mutations**: T3 transport defects; severe phenotype (Allan-Herndon-Dudley syndrome).
- **DIO1 polymorphisms**: rs2235544, rs11206244 — modulate T3:T4 ratio; population effects subtle.

**Drug confounders:**
- **Biotin ≥ 5 mg/d** → falsely **elevated** FT3 on competitive assays. Stop ≥72 h before draw.
- **Propranolol, nadolol** (non-selective β-blockers) → D1 inhibition → FT3 ↓ 10–20%, rT3 ↑.
- **High-dose glucocorticoids** (prednisone ≥20 mg/d) → D1 inhibition → FT3 ↓.
- **Amiodarone** → 37% iodine; D1 inhibition; FT3 ↓ acutely; rT3 ↑.
- **Phenytoin, carbamazepine** → induce T4 catabolism; FT3 may ↓ in chronic users.
- **Salicylates (high-dose), furosemide** → displace T3 from TBG; analog assays can mislead.
- **Estrogens (OCP, HRT, pregnancy)** → ↑ TBG → ↑ total T3; FT3 typically unchanged.

**Lifestyle / timing artifacts:**
- **Caloric deficit, fasting, low-CHO** → FT3 ↓ via D1 downregulation; the dominant non-pathological cause of low FT3 in healthy people.
- **Acute illness** → NTIS; FT3 ↓ first.
- **Heavy endurance training + low energy availability** → "low-T3 athlete."
- **Cold exposure / acute stress** → small acute FT3 ↓; resolves within hours.
- **Diurnal**: FT3 has mild diurnal rhythm following TSH (lower mid-afternoon).

**Assay limitations:**
- Analog FT3 immunoassays have inter-platform CV 15–25% (Stockigt *Best Pract Res Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2001) — never compare absolute FT3 across labs/platforms.
- Equilibrium dialysis FT3 + LC-MS/MS is gold standard but rarely available.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **The "FT3/rT3 ratio < 20 → treat with T3" framework** in ambulatory healthy adults: biologically plausible, no RCT outcome data (Halsall & Oddy *Ann Clin Biochem* 2021).
- **Whether DIO2 genotype-guided combination LT4+LT3 therapy improves outcomes**: mixed evidence; Wouters 2017 small RCT positive in Ala carriers; Carlé 2017 *JCEM* larger negative. ATA 2014 does not endorse genotype-guided therapy.
- **Optimal FT3 within reference range for outcomes**: low-normal FT3 has been associated with reduced cognition (Kalmijn *Clin Endocrinol* 2000) and adverse cardiac outcomes in heart failure (Iervasi *Circulation* 2003); whether *raising* FT3 in euthyroid asymptomatic adults improves outcomes is unproven.
- **Selenium dose-response** for FT3: most RCTs use 200 µg/d; dose-finding studies (50 vs 100 vs 200 µg) are limited.
- **Ashwagandha** for FT3 elevation in SCH (Sharma 2018): single small trial, not adequate to recommend.
- **Whether "low-T3 athlete" is harmful or adaptive** is debated; no RCT has tested whether intervening in subclinical low-T3 in healthy lean athletes improves outcomes.
