# TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone) — Intervention Research

> TSH is **not a direct lifestyle target** in healthy euthyroid adults — it is the integrator of underlying thyroid axis status and is dominated by autoimmune disease, iodine status, and pharmacology. The single highest-leverage move for an out-of-range TSH is the diagnostic workup (TPO Ab, FT4, TRAb if low) — *not* a supplement. The most common reason an "optimizer's" TSH is abnormal is **biotin assay interference**, which is fixed by stopping biotin 72 h before draw, not by any biological intervention. For TSH inside the lab range, lifestyle moves it ≤ 0.5 mIU/L; the mortality-nadir target band is **1.9–2.9 mIU/L** (Xu 2023, IPD meta-analysis n>134 000).

**Cross-link:** Biology, reference ranges, diurnal variation, biotin-artifact pattern, and pre-test prep are in `../../thyroid_panel.md` §1, §6, §9. Drug-class effects on TSH (levothyroxine, amiodarone, lithium, glucocorticoids, dopamine, beta-blockers, metformin) are summarized in `../../effect_sizes_endocrine.md` "TSH" and "Drug Class Reference Table." ## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs with consistent direction |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT (≥100 participants or pivotal design) |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology / physiology extrapolation |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial worth knowing but not yet replicated |

Population caveat: most cited evidence is from European-ancestry adults aged 30+ with elevated baseline TSH or established AITD. Magnitudes in a euthyroid 20yM at baseline TSH 1.5 will be ~30–60% of cited effects (floor/ceiling shrinkage).

## 1. Foods that LOWER TSH (or normalize an elevated TSH)

In a euthyroid person, "lowering TSH" is mostly the wrong frame — TSH 1.0–2.5 is mortality-optimal and pushing it lower has no evidence base. The foods below either **correct iodine deficiency** (the only food-based intervention with documented TSH effect) or **provide cofactors (selenium, iron) for thyroid hormone synthesis/conversion** that indirectly normalize an elevated TSH driven by deficiency.

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Iodized salt** (in iodine-deficient regions) | ~150 µg iodine/day (RDA) | TSH normalizes from elevated baseline if hypothyroidism was iodine-driven; in iodine-replete adults: **no effect on TSH** | 4–8 wk | [Cohort/RCT] | Zimmermann *Endocr Rev* 2009; WHO global iodine guidance | In iodine-replete countries (US, most of Europe), incremental iodine has no TSH benefit and risks Wolff-Chaikoff rebound or AITD trigger (see §2). |
| **Sea fish (cod, haddock, pollock, sardines)** | 2–3 servings/wk | Provides ~75–150 µg iodine/serving; corrects deficiency-driven TSH elevation | 4–8 wk | [Cohort] | EFSA 2014 iodine intake; Bath & Rayman *Br J Nutr* 2018 | Salmon is lower in iodine (~30 µg/serving) than white fish. Mercury-low species preferred (sardines, pollock). |
| **Dairy (milk, yogurt)** | 1–2 servings/d | Provides 50–100 µg iodine/serving (from iodophor sanitizers in milking equipment + cattle feed); fills iodine gap | 4–8 wk | [Cohort] | Bath 2014 *Br J Nutr*; UK dairy iodine surveys | Iodine content varies seasonally (winter > summer in dairy). Plant-milk substitutes are essentially iodine-free. |
| **Eggs** | 2–3/d | ~25 µg iodine/large egg (yolk concentrated); modest contribution to RDA | Weeks | [Mechanism] | USDA FoodData Central | Modest; not a primary iodine source. |
| **Brazil nuts** (selenium) | **1–2 nuts/day** (≈ 70–90 µg Se each) | TSH: **no direct effect in euthyroid**; in selenium-deficient AITD patients, repletion may modestly normalize TSH via D2 restoration in pituitary | 8–12 wk | [Mechanism/1-Trial] | Thomson *Am J Clin Nutr* 2008 (Brazil nut selenium repletion); Huwiler 2024 meta confirms no TSH effect in selenium-replete AITD | Selenium content varies 10-fold by soil (Bolivian/Brazilian Amazon nuts can deliver 200+ µg/nut; risk of selenosis). Cap at 2/day. Selenium does **not** reduce TSH in a euthyroid 20yM. |
| **Oysters / shellfish** | Modest serving 1–2×/wk | Iodine + zinc + selenium combined cofactors; corrects deficiency-driven TSH elevation | Weeks | [Mechanism] | USDA FoodData Central; trace mineral nutrition reviews | Allergens; mercury/cadmium in some shellfish. |
| **Fortified breakfast cereals** | 1 serving | Variable iodine and selenium; mainly relevant for vegan/vegetarian iodine intake | Weeks | [Cohort] | EFSA 2014 | Varies by brand; check label. |
| **Liver (beef, chicken)** | 1–2 servings/mo | Iron + selenium + B12 cofactors for thyroid hormone synthesis (TPO is iron-dependent) | 1–3 mo | [Mechanism] | Zimmermann *Am J Clin Nutr* 2002 (iron and TPO); Bianco *Endocr Rev* 2019 | Iron repletion only normalizes TSH if iron deficiency was the proximate cause of impaired hormone synthesis. |
| **Lentils, beef, spinach (iron sources)** | Daily | Iron repletion: in iron-deficient subjects with concomitantly elevated TSH, ferritin repletion can lower TSH ~0.5–1.0 mIU/L | 3–6 mo | [Cohort] | Zimmermann 2002; Eftekhari *Public Health Nutr* 2006 | Effect requires baseline iron deficiency (ferritin <30 ng/mL); no benefit if iron-replete. |

## 2. Foods that RAISE TSH (or push it out of optimal band)

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Kelp / seaweed (kombu, wakame, hijiki)** | 1 serving kombu can deliver **1 000–2 500 µg iodine** (10–25× RDA) | TSH **transiently ↑** via Wolff-Chaikoff (acute iodine excess inhibits organification → feedback TSH rise); chronic excess can precipitate clinical hypothyroidism in susceptible individuals | Days–weeks | [Cohort/Case series] | Teng et al. *NEJM* 2006;354:2783 (3-region China cohort: excessive iodine → 5-yr cumulative subclinical hypothyroidism 2.9% vs 0.2% in mildly deficient); Eliason 1998 case reports | The single most likely *food* explanation for an unexplained TSH rise in a healthy person who recently started "thyroid support" supplements or sushi habits. |
| **Iodine-fortified salt at gross excess** | >1 mg/day total iodine | TSH ↑ via same mechanism | Weeks | [Cohort] | Teng 2006 *NEJM*; Pearce 2013 ETA review | Standard iodized salt at culinary use is safe (~100–200 µg/day). |
| **Excessive cruciferous vegetables (raw, very high quantity)** | Multiple kg/day raw cabbage / kale (extreme intakes only) | Goitrogenic glucosinolates → mild TSH ↑ in iodine-deficient subjects only; no effect in iodine-replete | Months | [Mechanism] | Felker *Nutr Rev* 2016 (review); historical "cabbage-eater goiter" reports | **Largely a non-issue with normal iodine status.** Cooking inactivates >90% of goitrogenic compounds. Do not avoid cruciferous vegetables for TSH. |
| **Cassava (raw, in iodine-deficient regions)** | Staple-level intake | Cyanogenic glycosides → SCN⁻ inhibits NIS iodide uptake → TSH ↑ | Months | [Cohort] | Delange *Bull WHO* 1994 (African cassava-related goiter belt) | Not relevant in non-cassava-staple diets. |
| **Soy (raw / unfermented, very high doses)** | >100 g/d soy protein isolate | TSH may ↑ modestly in subclinical hypothyroid; in euthyroid: no clinically significant effect | Weeks | [RCT] | Sathyapalan *JCEM* 2011 (n=60 SCH; soy protein isolate 30g vs 2g phytoestrogens → 3-fold higher progression to overt hypothyroidism in high-soy arm); Messina 2006 review | Standard soy intake (tofu, edamame, soy milk 1–2 servings/d) does not move TSH in euthyroid subjects. Main clinical issue is **levothyroxine absorption** — separate dosing by ≥4 h. |
| **Alcohol (chronic heavy)** | >3 drinks/d sustained | TSH **↓** modestly (chronic alcohol suppresses HPT axis at hypothalamic level); pattern may mimic central hypothyroidism on chronic heavy use | Months | [Cohort] | Hegedüs *Acta Endocrinol* 1988; multiple cohort studies | Acute moderate intake: minimal effect. Heavy chronic use → low T3, low TSH, low FT4 pattern resolving with abstinence. |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** (in a healthy euthyroid adult): **Standardize the pre-test environment** — stop biotin 72 h prior, draw 7–9 AM fasting, avoid recent acute illness/intense exercise/cold plunge in the prior 24 h, avoid testing during caloric deficit. This eliminates the dominant *measurement* source of out-of-range TSH in optimizers. There is no single supplement or food that meaningfully moves TSH within the reference range in a euthyroid 20yM.
- **Best stack of 3** (for someone with elevated TSH + TPO+ subclinical hypothyroidism, evaluating before pharmacotherapy):
  1. **Selenium 200 µg/d selenomethionine × 6 mo** — primarily TPO Ab ↓ 20–55%; TSH unchanged in most trials but secondary effect possible (Huwiler 2024 Meta).
  2. **Vitamin D3 to 25(OH)D 40–60 ng/mL** — small TSH effect inconsistent; replete for autoimmune-modulation and general health (Jiang 2022 meta).
  3. **Iron repletion to ferritin 50–100 ng/mL** if iron-deficient (Zimmermann 2002).
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for a healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (TSH 1.5, antibodies negative): ≤ 0.3 mIU/L change from any non-pharmacological intervention. TSH is range-bound by a tightly regulated negative feedback loop in healthy individuals.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for someone with elevated baseline (TSH 5–9, TPO+, no overt symptoms): with selenium + D + iron stack, expect TSH movement of 0–1 mIU/L; 30–50% chance TSH normalizes spontaneously over 6–12 months even without intervention (regression-to-mean + spontaneous TPO antibody fluctuation). With levothyroxine: TSH moves predictably to target within 6–12 wk.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary**:
  - TSH > 10 mIU/L (any age, any FT4) → treat with LT4 (Garber 2012; Jonklaas 2014).
  - TSH 4.5–10 + symptomatic + TPO+ + young (<65) → trial of LT4, reassess after 3–6 mo.
  - TSH 4.5–10 + age >65–70 + asymptomatic → **do not treat** (TRUST trial Stott 2017 *NEJM* — zero symptom benefit; Mooijaart 2019 *JAMA* — TRUST + IEMO80+ pooled n=638, no benefit).
  - Suppressed TSH (<0.1) + high FT4/FT3 + clinical hyperthyroidism → workup for Graves'/toxic nodule; antithyroid drugs (methimazole) or definitive therapy (radioactive iodine, surgery) per ATA 2016 hyperthyroidism guidelines (Ross *Thyroid* 2016).
  - The **TRUST trial** (Stott et al. *NEJM* 2017;376:2534, n=737, mean age 74.4, mean TSH 6.4) is the definitive null trial for treating mild SCH in older adults: TSH normalized 5.5 → 3.6 mIU/L on LT4 vs placebo, but **zero benefit** on hypothyroid symptom or tiredness scores at 1 year. This drives the current conservative stance for mild SCH in non-symptomatic adults.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- South Asian populations have **higher Hashimoto prevalence** in regional studies (particularly South Indian, Sri Lankan cohorts; Usha Menon *Thyroid Res Pract* 2011 review). Mechanism mixed iodine-status (some regions historically deficient; iodization variable in supply chain) + genetic susceptibility (HLA-DR3/DR5 similar across ancestries).
- Vitamin D deficiency is **extremely common in SA populations** even in temperate climates (darker skin + limited sun + dietary patterns) and may contribute to autoimmune susceptibility — replete to 30–50 ng/mL regardless of TPO Ab status.
- Iron deficiency is more common in SA vegetarians; check ferritin if TSH is mildly elevated and pursuit of cause is indicated.
- **TSH reference ranges have been argued to differ slightly by ancestry**; Surks & Hollowell *JCEM* 2007 showed Black Americans have lower mean TSH than White Americans even after adjusting for TPO; data for SA populations are sparse but Indian reference data (Marwaha 2008 *Clin Endocrinol*) show similar ranges to NHANES.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **DIO2 rs225014 (Thr92Ala)**: Ala/Ala homozygotes (~12% of Europeans) have impaired peripheral T4→T3 conversion → may have low-normal FT3 with normal TSH/FT4 on LT4 monotherapy and report persistent symptoms (Peeters *JCEM* 2017;102:1623; Bianco *Endocr Rev* 2019). Not relevant to native TSH levels in untreated euthyroid.
- **PDE8B rs4704397**: GWAS variant explains ~2% of population TSH variance (Porcu *PLoS Genet* 2013). Functional impact: minimal, not actionable.
- **THRB mutations**: rare resistance to thyroid hormone β (RTHβ); pattern is high TSH + high FT4 (Dumitrescu/Refetoff *Endocr Rev* 2013); ~1 in 20 000–40 000.
- **TPO and TG SNPs**: confer modestly increased AITD susceptibility but are not routinely tested.

**Drug confounders (most important first):**
- **Biotin ≥ 5 mg/d** → falsely LOW TSH on streptavidin-biotin sandwich assays (Roche, Abbott, Beckman, Ortho). Stop ≥72 h before draw.
- **Levothyroxine** absorption blocked by: calcium carbonate, ferrous sulfate, PPIs (raise gastric pH), aluminum hydroxide antacids, sucralfate, cholestyramine, soy protein isolate, coffee, fiber supplements, espresso. Take LT4 fasted ≥30–60 min before food/coffee, ≥4 h apart from these (Liwanpo & Hershman *Best Pract Res Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2009).
- **Lithium** → inhibits thyroid hormone release; ~20–30% of chronic users develop hypothyroidism; TSH ↑.
- **Amiodarone** → 37% iodine by weight; D1 inhibition; TSH rises within 48 h, peaks ~10× baseline ~10 days; complex AIT (type 1 vs type 2) management per Bartalena *Eur Thyroid J* 2018.
- **Glucocorticoids (prednisone ≥20 mg/d)** → suppress TRH → TSH ↓; D1 inhibition → T3 ↓, rT3 ↑.
- **Dopamine / dopamine agonists** (cabergoline, bromocriptine): suppress TSH directly at pituitary; common ICU artifact.
- **Estrogens (OCP, HRT, pregnancy)** → ↑ TBG → ↑ total T4/T3 with free fractions usually preserved; pregnancy-specific TSH ranges (T1: 0.1–2.5; T2: 0.2–3.0; T3: 0.3–3.5) per ATA 2017 (Alexander *Thyroid* 2017).
- **Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (sunitinib, sorafenib, axitinib)** → drug-induced hypothyroidism in ~20–50% (Torino *J Clin Oncol* 2009).
- **Beta-blockers (esp. propranolol)** → D1 inhibition → modest T3 ↓, rT3 ↑; TSH unchanged.
- **Metformin** → TSH ↓ ~0.4 mIU/L in some hypothyroid patients on LT4; mechanism unclear (Lupoli *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2014).
- **SSRIs (sertraline)** → modest TSH ↑ in some hypothyroid patients on LT4 (de Carvalho *Br J Clin Pharmacol* 2009); minor.
- **Statins**: no consistent direct TSH effect; rare cases of statin-induced thyroiditis in literature.
- **Iodinated contrast media (CT contrast)** → Wolff-Chaikoff with TSH ↑ for weeks; can precipitate hypothyroidism in AITD subjects.

**Lifestyle / timing confounders:**
- **Diurnal rhythm**: TSH peaks 02:00–04:00, nadirs 16:00–20:00; magnitude 24–30% lower at noon vs 08:00, up to 65% in some individuals (Russell *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2008; Roelfsema *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2014). **Always serial-draw at the same time of day.**
- **Acute illness (recent 2 wk)**: NTIS; defer testing.
- **Recent intense exercise / cold plunge**: acute TSH ↑; defer 4–24 h.
- **Heterophile antibodies / HAMA / anti-streptavidin IgM**: spurious TSH elevation; cannot be "washed out" — requires assay change or PEG precipitation.

**Population artifact:**
- **Age**: TSH distribution shifts ~0.3 mIU/L higher per decade after age 30 (Surks & Hollowell *JCEM* 2007); a TSH of 5.5 in an 80yM may be physiologic and harmful to "treat" toward 2.0.

---

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Does anything other than levothyroxine reliably move an elevated TSH back into mortality-optimal range?** No clear evidence base. Selenium, myo-inositol, and vitamin D have mostly null TSH effects in meta-analyses.
- **Is mortality-nadir TSH 1.9–2.9 (Xu 2023) causally protective, or does it reflect underlying healthy phenotypes?** Unclear; the IPD meta-analysis is observational. No RCT has tested whether driving TSH from 3.5 → 2.0 in a healthy adult improves outcomes — and the Stott 2017 TRUST trial argues *against* benefit in mild SCH.
- **Ashwagandha for SCH (Sharma 2018)**: single small unreplicated RCT showing TSH ↓ ~17% — promising, but not adequate to recommend; should be replicated in 2–3 independent trials before guideline-level adoption.
- **Myo-inositol + selenium (Nordio 2017)**: single industry-funded trial; needs independent replication.
- **Does 23-and-Me-derived DIO2 genotype meaningfully predict who benefits from T4+T3 combination?** Mixed evidence; Wouters 2017 *JCEM* small RCT suggested benefit in Ala carriers; larger trials (Carlé 2017 *JCEM*) negative. ATA 2014 does not endorse genotype-guided combination therapy.
- **"FT3/rT3 ratio < 20 → supplement T3" framework**: biologically plausible but lacks RCT outcome data for ambulatory healthy people (Halsall & Oddy *Ann Clin Biochem* 2021).
- **Optimal TSH range in a 20yM** (vs the older-cohort-derived 1.9–2.9 nadir): direct evidence in young adults is sparse. Most safe to interpret 1.0–2.5 as the synthesis target.

## Source format conventions

- Primary citations preferred: PMID, journal+year+vol+page. Reviews and guidelines (ATA, AACE/ACE, ETA, Cochrane) cited where they consolidate evidence.
- Functional-medicine / commercial / blog sources are **not** cited; where their framings are mentioned (e.g., "FT3/rT3 ratio") they are explicitly labeled as functional-medicine framings without endorsed evidence base.
- For each effect-size claim a direction (↑/↓/unchanged) and magnitude (mIU/L, %) are given. Where ranges are wide, this is flagged in the caveats.
