# Lipoprotein(a) — Lp(a) — Intervention Research

> **The honest summary: Lp(a) is ~80–90% genetically determined and essentially does not move with lifestyle.** No food, supplement, exercise pattern, or sleep intervention has a documented effect ≥10% with replication. The handful of pharmacologic agents that move Lp(a) meaningfully are PCSK9 inhibitors (~25–30%), niacin (~20–25%), lipoprotein apheresis (~30% time-averaged), and the new RNA-targeted siRNA/ASO drugs in Phase 3 (80–95%). Statins do **not** lower Lp(a) and may slightly raise it. Saturated-fat reduction *raises* Lp(a) by ~10–25% — the only reproducible dietary effect, and it is in the wrong direction. **For a 20-year-old male: measure once, treat all other modifiable CV risk to the floor, and wait for the RNA drugs to arrive (Lp(a)HORIZON read-out 2025–2026; OCEAN(a) ~2027).**

**Cross-link:** Reference biology / clinical interpretation lives in `../../heart_lipids_advanced.md` §8 and intervention overview in `../../effect_sizes_cardiovascular.md` §Lp(a). This file is INTERVENTION-only — what moves the marker, by how much, in how long, with what evidence.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs with consistent direction |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT (≥100 participants or pivotal design) |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational; can't establish causality |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization — genetic proxies estimating causal effect |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology / physiology extrapolation |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial worth knowing but not yet replicated |

**Floor caveat for Lp(a) is unique:** floor effects are not the issue here — the issue is *ceiling*. Even maximal lifestyle effort moves Lp(a) <10% in most people. The intervention magnitudes in this file are therefore mostly null or small. Document this honestly.

## 1. Foods that LOWER Lp(a) (or improve direction)

Honest evidence-base summary: there is no replicated dietary intervention that lowers Lp(a) by a clinically meaningful amount in healthy individuals. Most "Lp(a) diets" in popular media cite mechanism studies or single trials in high-baseline patients. The handful of foods below have *some* signal — almost all small, often non-significant, and sometimes confounded.

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Flaxseed (ground) | 30–40 g/d ground or as baked product | Lp(a) ~ −14% vs. wheat control (one RCT); meta-analysis: −2.06 mg/dL (~−7%); 2024 GRADE meta-analysis SMD −0.34 | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Bloedon et al. *J Am Coll Nutr* 2008 (PMID 18460483); Sahebkar et al. *Phytother Res* 2020 (PMID 32073724); 2024 GRADE meta-analysis (PMID via ScienceDirect S1098882324000662) | Effect is small in absolute terms; mostly demonstrated in dyslipidemic adults, transportability to a young metabolically-clean male is unclear. Mechanism uncertain (lignans? ALA? fiber?). |
| Filter-brewed coffee (substituted for boiled/unfiltered) | Switch from ≥3 cups/d boiled coffee to filter | Lp(a) ~ −5–11 mg/dL vs. boiled (small studies); ~ −20–30% in heavy boiled-coffee drinkers | 4–8 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Urgert et al. *Eur J Clin Nutr* 1997 (PMID via Nature 1600414, four pooled RCTs); cross-sectional Stensvold *Atherosclerosis* 1996 (PMID 9010383) | Diterpene cafestol/kahweol in unfiltered coffee raises Lp(a). Use a paper filter, French press / espresso / Turkish are diterpene-rich. Clinically tiny but free. |
| Pistachios | 56–84 g/d (2–3 oz) for 4 wk | Lp(a) directionally ↓ (~−5%) in one trial; non-significant | 4 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Hernández-Alonso et al. small trial, *J Nutr* 2014 — non-significant for Lp(a), included in nut–lipid reviews | Likely a noise finding. Do not over-interpret. |
| Almonds | 50 g/d for 4–12 wk | No significant effect on Lp(a) in pooled data; some directional ↓ | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Berryman et al. almond meta-analysis 2011, J Am Heart Assoc; reviews in *Lifestyle and Lp(a)*, Stoll et al. *J Clin Med* 2024 (PMID via MDPI 13/3/751) | Clinically null for Lp(a). Almonds have other lipid benefits (LDL-C ↓ 4–5%). |
| Brazil nuts | 1–4 nuts/d (selenium load) | Mechanism-only; no Lp(a) RCT data | — | **[Mechanism]** | — | No reliable evidence for Lp(a) effect. |
| Dark cocoa / cocoa polyphenols | 25–40 g cocoa/d | No significant Lp(a) effect | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Hooper et al. cocoa lipid meta-analyses, *AJCN* 2012 | Affects flow-mediated dilation; not Lp(a). |
| Whole-food plant-based diet (Esselstyn / Ornish style) | Strict ad libitum | Lp(a) usually unchanged or slightly ↑ (since SFA falls) | 12–52 wk | **[Cohort]** | Ornish et al. lifestyle reversal cohort, multiple papers; reviewed in Enkhmaa et al. *Nutrients* 2020 (PMC7400957) | The reduction in saturated fat in plant-based diets typically *raises* Lp(a) ~10–25%. The diet helps everything else (LDL-C, ApoB, TG) but Lp(a) is unfavorable or null. |

**Honest take:** Of the foods above, only flaxseed has a meta-analytic signal, and the magnitude (~7–14%) is far below the ~50–60% reduction needed to meaningfully reduce CV risk in someone with high Lp(a). Foods are not the lever for Lp(a).

## 2. Foods that RAISE Lp(a) (or worsen direction)

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Saturated-fat REDUCTION (i.e., low-SFA diet) | Diet shift from ~16% SFA → 6% SFA (DASH-style) over 5 wk | Lp(a) **+24%** in one feeding RCT (African Americans); pooled meta-analysis: SFA reduction → Lp(a) ↑ ~10–25% across trials | 5 wk | **[Meta] + [RCT]** | Faghihnia et al. GET-READI feeding trial *J Lipid Res* 2023 (PMID 37482217); Law et al. SFA→Lp(a) meta-analysis *AJCN* 2024 (PMID 38964657); Krauss et al. earlier work | Counter-intuitive but well-replicated. Mechanism unclear; possibly LDL-receptor density changes alter Lp(a) clearance. **Practical consequence**: if Lp(a) is high and you're already on a low-SFA diet for LDL-C reasons, the LDL-C benefit dominates the Lp(a) penalty. Do not abandon SFA reduction to lower Lp(a). |
| Trans fatty acids (industrial, partially hydrogenated oils) | ~3–8% of energy | Lp(a) ↑ ~5–15% vs. cis-monounsaturated control | 3–6 wk | **[RCT]** | Mensink et al. *NEJM* 1990 and *Arterioscler Thromb Vasc Biol* 1997 (PMID 2374566); Nestel review *J Lipid Res* 1992 | Industrial trans fats are now banned in most jurisdictions. Naturally-occurring ruminant trans (CLA, vaccenic acid in dairy/beef) at typical intakes do **not** raise Lp(a). Avoid old margarines, ultra-processed fried foods from countries without trans-fat bans. |
| Boiled / unfiltered coffee (French press, espresso, Turkish, Scandinavian boiled) | ≥3 cups/d | Lp(a) ↑ ~25–35% vs. filter coffee in chronic consumers | 8–12 wk | **[Cohort] + [RCT]** | Stensvold et al. *Atherosclerosis* 1996 (PMID 9010383); Urgert et al. *Eur J Clin Nutr* 1997 (PMID via Nature 1600414) | Diterpenes cafestol and kahweol are the agents. Paper-filter coffee removes them. |
| Alcohol (heavy / chronic) | >3 drinks/d | Modest Lp(a) ↑ in some studies; mostly null | — | **[Cohort]** | Mixed; not a primary driver | Alcohol's main lipid effect is HDL-C ↑ and TG ↑, not Lp(a). |

**Honest take:** The two reproducible "Lp(a)-raising" dietary factors are SFA reduction (paradoxical, ~+10–25%) and unfiltered coffee diterpenes (~+25–35%). Neither finding implies "eat more saturated fat for your Lp(a)" — the LDL-C/ApoB consequence outweighs the Lp(a) benefit by an order of magnitude.

## 3. Supplements

### Lowering / improving

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Smoking modestly raises it but smoking cessation does not reliably reverse the elevation — yet quitting smoking is the single most important behavioral intervention for someone with high Lp(a) because the *combination* of high Lp(a) + smoking has multiplicative CV risk.

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** (current era, FDA-approved): **PCSK9 inhibitor (evolocumab or alirocumab)**, ~25–30% Lp(a) reduction at 12 weeks. Indicated only in patients with established ASCVD or familial hypercholesterolemia at present; Lp(a) is not yet a stand-alone PCSK9i indication.
- **Highest-leverage single intervention (pipeline, not yet approved)**: **Olpasiran** (siRNA, q12wk SC) or **lepodisiran** (siRNA, single annual dose) — 90–95% Lp(a) reduction. Awaiting outcome-trial readouts (OCEAN(a) ~2027; ACCLAIM-Lp(a) enrolling).
- **Best stack of 3 (lifestyle, current era)**: There is no useful 3-stack. Lifestyle effects are individually <10% and additive effects are unproven. The realistic stack for a healthy 20yM is: (1) **filter your coffee** if you drink it, (2) **don't smoke**, (3) **drive ApoB to <60 mg/dL via diet, exercise, and statin if needed**. Item (3) does not lower Lp(a) but reduces the additive ASCVD risk that high Lp(a) imposes — this is the *only* leverage you have until the siRNAs arrive.
- **Realistic 12-week delta for a healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (Lp(a) <30 nmol/L)**: **~0%**. Lifestyle does not move it. Variability between draws (~10%) will swamp any signal.
- **Realistic 12-week delta for someone with elevated baseline (Lp(a) >125 nmol/L)**: with maximal lifestyle effort + filter coffee + flax, **~−5 to −10%** (often within assay noise); with PCSK9i added, **~−25–35%**; with niacin added (not recommended), **another −15–20%**. None of these reach the 65–100 mg/dL absolute reduction Burgess et al. (*JAMA Cardiol* 2018 MR) estimate is needed for clinically meaningful CHD risk reduction.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary**: Lp(a) ≥125 nmol/L (≥50 mg/dL) is the NLA/EAS high-risk threshold. At this level, the relevant intervention is to (a) treat all *modifiable* CV risk maximally (ApoB <60, BP <120/80, HbA1c <5.4, no smoking, regular exercise, optimal sleep) and (b) wait for the RNA-targeted Lp(a) drugs. The pipeline is the answer, not lifestyle.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- **South Asians have median Lp(a) ~31 nmol/L vs. White ~19** (Patel et al. *Circulation* 2021 multi-ethnic Lp(a) distribution). The 20–25% prevalence of "high Lp(a) >50 mg/dL" in NW European cohorts may rise to ~30–35% in South Asians.
- The threshold for "elevated" is set against a Caucasian reference. Some lipidologists argue for ethnic-specific thresholds; consensus is not yet there.
- **Black individuals have median Lp(a) ~75 nmol/L** — the highest of any major ancestry group — yet do not show proportionally elevated CHD risk per nmol/L. The KIV-2 isoform-specific risk modifier may differ by ancestry.
- **For a lean SA 20yM**: Lp(a) is the single biomarker where ancestry modifies the prior probability of finding high values most strongly. Test once at age 20+; if elevated, the lifestyle interventions are still null but the family screening cascade and ApoB-aggressive-management strategy is high-value.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

- **Genetic modifiers (the dominant 80–90% of Lp(a) variance)**:
  - *LPA* gene **KIV-2 copy number variation**: fewer KIV-2 repeats → smaller apo(a) isoform → higher plasma Lp(a) **and** higher per-mg risk (Erqou et al., *JAMA* 2009). 23andMe and standard SNP arrays do *not* directly genotype KIV-2 CNV.
  - SNPs **rs10455872** and **rs3798220** (in *LPA* intron 25 and exon 38, respectively) — both tag the small-isoform haplotype. rs10455872 G allele → ~3-fold higher Lp(a), OR ~1.7–1.9 for CHD per allele (Clarke et al. *NEJM* 2009 PROCARDIS). These ARE on standard SNP arrays and reportable from 23andMe raw data.
  - **APOE genotype** (ApoE2/E3/E4): does not directly affect Lp(a), but APOE4 carriers benefit more from PCSK9i Lp(a) reduction in some sub-analyses.
- **Drug interactions / artifacts**:
  - **Statins**: do *not* reduce Lp(a); some trials show +5–15% increase. Do not expect Lp(a) drop on starting/intensifying statin.
  - **Estrogen (oral)**: lowers Lp(a) ~20–30%; **transdermal** does not.
  - **Tamoxifen, raloxifene (SERMs)**: small Lp(a) ↓ (~10–15%).
  - **Growth hormone**: variable effects.
  - **Aspirin**: no documented Lp(a) effect.
  - **Hormonal contraceptives (oral)**: small Lp(a) ↓ (~10%) in some studies.
  - **Anabolic steroids**: dramatically lower Lp(a); also crash HDL and worsen everything else.
- **Confounding lifestyle / clinical factors**:
  - **Acute illness / sepsis / surgery**: transient Lp(a) elevation (acute-phase reactant). Wait 4–6 weeks after recovery before retesting.
  - **Pregnancy**: Lp(a) doubles during pregnancy and returns to baseline ~6 weeks postpartum.
  - **Renal failure (CKD stage 3–5)**: Lp(a) approximately doubles; nephrotic syndrome elevates it further.
  - **Hypothyroidism**: modest Lp(a) elevation; treatment normalizes.
  - **Menopause**: Lp(a) ↑ ~10–20% with loss of endogenous estrogen.
- **Timing artifacts**:
  - **Fasting**: NOT required. Lp(a) varies <5% fasting vs. fed (Ledford et al. and others). Function Health does not require fasting.
  - **Time of day**: minor diurnal variation; not clinically meaningful.
  - **Recent exercise**: acute exercise does not move Lp(a) within 24–48 hr.
- **Assay confounders (CRITICAL)**:
  - **Mass-based (mg/dL) vs. molar (nmol/L)** assays disagree systematically based on apo(a) isoform. Older mass-based ELISAs over-count large apo(a) isoforms (which carry less risk per mg) and under-count small isoforms (which carry more risk per mg).
  - **Modern isoform-insensitive (Denka-style) molar assays in nmol/L** are the standard. Function Health (Quest backend) uses these.
  - **Do NOT convert mg/dL to nmol/L via a fixed factor**. The actual ratio varies 1.82–3.64 across isoforms (NLA 2024). Track within the same assay.
  - **Recent 2024 data (Schmidt et al., STAR-Lp(a))** suggest Lp(a) is *not* as stable as classically taught; ~10–20% intra-individual variability over years has been observed. Repeat testing reasonable in patients near decision thresholds.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Is the SFA → Lp(a)↑ effect real and direction-stable across populations?** GET-READI was African American; the meta-analytic effect (~+10–25%) is consistent but most trials are short (5–12 weeks). Long-term effects unknown.
- **Does flaxseed actually move Lp(a) clinically?** Three meta-analyses say yes (~−7 to −14%); none has shown CV-event reduction attributable to the Lp(a) change.
- **Does L-carnitine net-benefit or net-harm CV outcomes?** Lp(a) ↓ ~9 mg/dL is real; TMAO elevation may neutralize or reverse the benefit. No outcome trial.
- **Is endurance training Lp(a)-raising clinically meaningful?** A 2-fold increase in marathon training cohorts has been replicated but the magnitude is modest in absolute terms and the broader CV benefit of endurance training overwhelms it.
- **What absolute Lp(a) reduction is needed for outcome benefit?** Burgess et al. MR estimate is 65–100 mg/dL (~150–250 nmol/L). This is why Lp(a)HORIZON, OCEAN(a), and ACCLAIM-Lp(a) all enroll patients with Lp(a) >70 mg/dL — the absolute reduction at lower baselines is too small to detect outcome differences in feasible trial sizes.
- **Will PCSK9 inhibitors get an Lp(a)-only indication?** Currently they require ASCVD or FH. The FOURIER Lp(a) sub-analysis suggested Lp(a) reduction contributed independently to CV benefit, but no regulatory body has approved PCSK9i for Lp(a) elevation alone.
- **Obicetrapib (CETP inhibitor) Lp(a) effect**: Phase 3 BROADWAY/BROOKLYN/TANDEM data suggest ~37–45% Lp(a) reduction in patients with baseline Lp(a) 50–150 nmol/L. If the outcome data confirms benefit and the FDA approves, this could be the first oral non-RNA Lp(a)-lowering therapy.
- **Is the Lp(a) effect on aortic stenosis modifiable?** Mendelian randomization says yes (causal). No outcome trial has yet shown that lowering Lp(a) prevents aortic stenosis progression — Lp(a)HORIZON includes aortic stenosis as a secondary endpoint.
- **Periodic Lp(a) re-testing**: classical teaching is "test once for life." Schmidt et al. (STAR-Lp(a) 2024) suggest periodic retesting may be warranted in patients near thresholds. The NLA 2024 update is cautious: "consider repeat testing in select circumstances" (kidney disease, post-menopause, after major weight change).
