# Calcium (serum, total) — Intervention Research

> Serum total Ca is **extremely tightly regulated by PTH and 1,25(OH)2-vitamin D**. It is one of the LEAST diet-responsive biomarkers — even high-dose Ca supplementation only raises serum Ca 0.1–0.2 mg/dL transiently. The optimal range is ~9.0–10.0 mg/dL (corrected for albumin). **High Ca ≥ 10.5 → think primary hyperparathyroidism (HPT), malignancy (PTHrP), sarcoidosis, vitamin D toxicity, hyperthyroidism, lithium, thiazides, milk-alkali, immobilization.** **Low Ca → vitamin D deficiency (corrected/ionized), hypoparathyroidism (post-thyroidectomy, autoimmune), severe Mg deficiency, CKD, malabsorption, acute pancreatitis (Ca-soap formation), tumor lysis.** In healthy people, the calcium "lever" is mostly **vitamin D status** (D deficient → ionized Ca low, corrected total may appear normal due to albumin binding). Ca supplementation >1,000 mg/d in non-deficient adults: kidney stone risk + possible CV signal without bone benefit (Bolland 2010 *BMJ* meta-analysis).

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology/physiology extrapolation |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial |

Population caveat: most evidence on Ca metabolism comes from osteoporosis trials in older adults, hyperparathyroidism cohorts, and CKD-mineral-bone literature. In healthy young men with normal PTH/D, serum Ca essentially doesn't move with normal dietary modulation.

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** for low corrected Ca: **vitamin D repletion** (2,000–5,000 IU/d D3 if 25-OH-D <30 ng/mL). Effect: serum corrected Ca ↑ 0.1–0.3 mg/dL over 4–12 weeks; PTH falls.
- **Highest-leverage for high serum Ca**: rule out primary hyperparathyroidism (PTH-driven), malignancy (PTHrP), sarcoidosis (1,25-D production), thiazide use, vitamin D toxicity (rare), lithium, milk-alkali, immobilization. **Treat the cause**, not the number.
- **Best stack of 3 to maintain serum Ca in normal range (9.0–10.0):**
  1. Adequate vitamin D status (25-OH-D 30–60 ng/mL)
  2. Adequate dietary Ca (1,000–1,200 mg/d) without excess supplementation (>1,000 mg/d supplemental)
  3. Adequate Mg status (Mg deficit causes refractory hypocalcemia via PTH resistance)
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for healthy 20yM near optimal baseline (Ca 9.6): essentially zero — tightly defended.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for low baseline (Ca 8.6 with vit D 18 ng/mL): vit D 5,000 IU/d → vit D 35 ng/mL, Ca 9.4 within 8–12 weeks.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:**
  - Hypercalcemia: bisphosphonates (zoledronate, pamidronate), calcitonin (acute), cinacalcet (HPT), denosumab, dialysis (severe), saline + furosemide.
  - Hypocalcemia: oral calcium + active vitamin D (calcitriol if 1α-hydroxylation defective); IV Ca gluconate for symptomatic.
  - Surgery for primary HPT (parathyroidectomy) — drops Ca 0.5–1.5 mg/dL toward normal.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- **Vitamin D deficiency is endemic in South Asians** (50–90% prevalence depending on region; Ritu G & Gupta A, *Nutrients* 2014;6:729). Higher melanin reduces UV-driven vit D synthesis; cultural sun avoidance; vegetarian diet low in D-rich foods.
- **Lean SA males with low vit D often have low ionized Ca (corrected total may appear normal)** with elevated PTH — secondary hyperparathyroidism. Replete vit D first.
- **Lower dietary Ca intake** is common in SA vegetarian diet — average 400–600 mg/d vs RDA 1,000 mg/d (Harinarayan CV et al., *Nutrients* 2007). Consider increasing dairy, leafy greens, sesame, tahini, fortified plant milks.
- *CASR* (calcium-sensing receptor) variants — modest effect on PTH set-point in some populations.
- *VDR* (vitamin D receptor) variants — modest effect on Ca/D handling; mostly studied in osteoporosis/CKD-MBD.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Critical interpretation rules:**
- **Total Ca is albumin-dependent.** Corrected Ca = total Ca + 0.8 × (4.0 − albumin g/dL). If albumin <3.5, correct before interpreting.
- **Ionized Ca is the physiologically active fraction** (~50% of total). Acute alkalosis (hyperventilation, vomiting) shifts Ca onto albumin → low ionized despite normal total.
- **Always pair high Ca with PTH** — high Ca + high PTH = primary HPT (or familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia, FHH); high Ca + low/normal PTH = malignancy (PTHrP), vit D toxicity, sarcoidosis, milk-alkali, hyperthyroid.
- **Always pair low Ca with PTH and Mg** — low Ca + high PTH = vit D deficient or CKD; low Ca + low PTH = hypoparathyroidism (post-thyroidectomy, autoimmune); low Ca + normal/inappropriate PTH + low Mg = PTH resistance (Mg deficiency).

**Pre-analytic confounds:**
- **Tourniquet >1 min** → Ca ↑ small (hemoconcentration).
- **Postprandial draw** → Ca ↑ 0.1–0.2 mg/dL transient (esp. dairy, Ca-fortified foods).
- **Tube standing time, hemolysis** → minor effects.
- **Pregnancy** → corrected Ca ↓ 0.1–0.2 mg/dL (hemodilution); ionized Ca ↔.

**Drug interactions:**
- **Raise Ca**: thiazides (DCT Ca reabsorption), lithium (PTH set-point), vit A toxicity, vit D toxicity, calcium supplements (transient), aluminum antacids, milk-alkali, recombinant PTH (transient peaks), recombinant calcitriol.
- **Lower Ca**: bisphosphonates, denosumab, calcitonin, cinacalcet, calcimimetics, glucocorticoids, IV fluids + furosemide (acute Rx hypercalcemia), citrate (massive transfusion), foscarnet.

**Genetic modifiers:**
- *CASR* (calcium-sensing receptor) — gain-of-function (autosomal dominant hypocalcemia); loss-of-function (FHH = familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia, mimics primary HPT but no surgical benefit).
- *PTH*, *PTHR1* — rare hypoparathyroidism / pseudohypoparathyroidism syndromes.
- *VDR* polymorphisms — modest Ca/D handling effects.
- *CYP24A1* — vit D inactivation; mutations → hypercalcemia in infancy.
- *CYP2R1*, *CYP27B1* — vit D activation enzymes.

**MEN1 / MEN2A** — multiple endocrine neoplasia syndromes; primary HPT in MEN1, secondary in MEN2A.

**Other clinical contexts:**
- Sarcoidosis / TB / fungal granulomatous disease → ectopic 1,25-D production → hypercalcemia.
- Multiple myeloma, breast/lung/squamous cancers → PTHrP-mediated hypercalcemia.
- Acute pancreatitis → Ca ↓ via fat necrosis Ca-soap formation.
- CKD → Ca ↓ via reduced 1,25-D synthesis and FGF-23 elevation; CKD-MBD.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Optimal serum Ca for long-term outcomes in healthy young men** — most evidence is osteoporosis and CKD-MBD. Healthy 9.0–10.0 mg/dL is standard.
- **CV risk of Ca supplementation** — Bolland 2010 *BMJ* meta showed ~30% MI risk increase with Ca supplements; Lewis JR et al., *J Bone Miner Res* 2015;30:165 disputes; controversy ongoing. Don't supplement Ca >1,000 mg/d unless dietary intake clearly inadequate.
- **Effect of plant-based diets on serum Ca and bone in lean SA young men** — conflicting evidence; depends on Ca intake and vit D status.
- **Long-term safety of vit D >100 ng/mL** — risk of hypercalcemia + nephrocalcinosis at very high levels; Endocrine Society 2011 guideline upper limit 100 ng/mL.
- **Effect of acute exercise on ionized Ca** — modest; not clinically actionable.
- **Magnesium-Ca interaction in healthy young men** — Mg deficit causes PTH resistance and refractory hypocalcemia, but how often this contributes to subtle hypocalcemia in healthy is unstudied.
- **Genetic risk for chronic mild hypocalcemia / hypercalcemia in healthy young men** — minimal data outside rare syndromes.
- **Wearable / home monitoring of Ca status** — none currently available; relies on serum draws.
