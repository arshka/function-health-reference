# Potassium (serum) — Intervention Research

> Serum K is regulated by **aldosterone (renal excretion), insulin/β-adrenergic tone (cellular shift), and acid-base status**. The optimal range is **4.0–4.5 mmol/L** with U-shaped mortality curves outside (Goyal *JAMA* 2012; Krogager *Eur Heart J* 2017). Foods that raise K: potatoes (especially with skin), leafy greens, avocado, banana, beans/lentils, dairy, coconut water, dried fruits, tomato, fish. Drugs that raise K: ACEi/ARB/spironolactone/amiloride/eplerenone/finerenone (RAAS-blocking), trimethoprim, NSAIDs, beta-blockers (mild). Drugs that lower K: thiazide and loop diuretics (potent), beta-2 agonists (cellular shift), insulin, corticosteroids. **#1 cause of "high K" in healthy people = pseudohyperkalemia from in-vitro hemolysis** — fist clenching during draw raises measured K up to 1.0 mmol/L. **Magnesium repletion is mandatory before treating refractory hypokalemia** — Mg deficit causes ROMK-mediated renal K wasting.

**Cross-link:** Reference biology / clinical interpretation lives in `../../kidney_electrolytes_pancreas.md` §7 (Potassium) and §0.2 (pseudohyperkalemia). This file is INTERVENTION-only.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology/physiology extrapolation |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial |

Population caveat: most evidence on dietary K and BP / serum K comes from middle-aged adults with HTN baseline. Effect sizes shrink in lean normotensive young people. Pseudohyperkalemia is the dominant interpretation issue — always consider before chasing real treatments.

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** for persistent low-normal K (e.g., 3.6 mmol/L) in a fitness enthusiast: **adopt a DASH-style dietary pattern** with K ≥3.5 g/d (potatoes, leafy greens, beans, dairy). Effect: serum K ↑ 0.1–0.3 mmol/L; BP ↓ 3–5 mmHg; CV mortality risk decreases.
- **Highest-leverage for an "isolated high K" (5.2 mmol/L) in an asymptomatic young man**: **redraw with proper technique** (no fist clenching, fresh tube, prompt processing). Pseudohyperkalemia is the #1 cause and resolves on redraw.
- **Best stack of 3 for K optimization in healthy 20yM:**
  1. Whole-foods diet rich in K (leafy greens, beans, potatoes, dairy)
  2. Adequate Mg intake (300–400 mg/d) — Mg deficit causes refractory hypokalemia
  3. Avoid stimulant overload (excessive caffeine, β-2 agonists, pre-workout) on draw days
- **Best stack for hypertensive K-deficient adult:** DASH diet + K-enriched salt substitute (if not on RAAS blocker, no CKD) + maintenance of BP 120/80. Combined: K ↑ 0.3–0.5 mmol/L; BP ↓ 7–10 mmHg.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for healthy 20yM near optimal baseline (K 4.2): essentially zero — tightly defended.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for borderline-low (K 3.6) on processed-food diet + heavy caffeine: switch to DASH + reduce caffeine → K ~4.0–4.2 over 4–8 weeks.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:**
  - True hyperkalemia >5.5: K binders (patiromer, SZC), dietary K restriction, dose-reduce RAAS blocker (KDIGO 2024 advises NOT discontinuing).
  - True hypokalemia <3.5: oral KCl 20–60 mmol/d + Mg repletion; address underlying cause (diuretic, hyperaldosteronism, GI loss).
  - Mineralocorticoid blockers (spironolactone, finerenone) for chronic K wasting or HTN with low K.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- SA traditional diet (dal, vegetables, fruits, dairy) is typically K-rich and meets DASH targets even without modification.
- Vegetarian SA males often have higher dietary K than NW European omnivores.
- Lean SA males with low Mg (common, see Mg file) may have refractory hypokalemia even on K supplementation — repleting Mg first is essential.
- SA males with HTN respond well to K-rich DASH-style modification; salt substitute (KCl) is high-value but contraindicated in CKD or RAAS blockade.
- Aldosterone synthase (*CYP11B2*) variants modestly modulate K handling in some SA cohorts.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Critical pre-analytic confounds (#1 source of "abnormal" K reports in healthy people):**
- **Pseudohyperkalemia from in-vitro hemolysis** (K ↑ up to 1.0 mmol/L):
  - **Fist clenching during phlebotomy** (Don BR et al., *NEJM* 1990;322:1290 — PMID 2325721): well-documented +1.0 mmol/L.
  - **Tourniquet >1 min**: hemolysis risk jumps from 1.3% to 20% (Mayo Clinic Labs 2018 review).
  - **Small-bore needle, vacuum pull, shaken tube**: mechanical lysis releases intracellular K (cells contain ~140 mmol/L vs serum ~4).
  - **Tube delay >2 h before centrifugation, cold storage**: K leak from RBCs.
  - Lab note about hemolysis often present; if not, an isolated K elevation in an asymptomatic patient with no ECG change is presumptively pseudohyperkalemia and **should be redrawn** before clinical action (Asirvatham JR et al., *J Clin Med Res* 2013;5:255; Sevastos N et al., *Clin Lab* 2006;52:497).
- **Reverse pseudohyperkalemia** in severe leukocytosis (>500K/µL) or thrombocytosis (>1M/µL): clot releases K from cells in serum sample; plasma sample resolves (request lithium-heparin plasma).
- **Recent intense exercise (within 2 h)**: K spike +0.3–0.8 mmol/L from working muscle.
- **Recent caffeine, β-2 agonist, pre-workout (within 4 h)**: K shift down 0.2–0.5 mmol/L.
- **Recent heavy carb meal**: K shift down via insulin.

**Drug interactions:**
- **Raise K (RAAS blockers + others)**: ACEi, ARB, spironolactone, eplerenone, finerenone, amiloride, triamterene, trimethoprim, NSAIDs, beta-blockers (mild), heparin, calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), digoxin (overdose), succinylcholine (rhabdo).
- **Lower K**: thiazide and loop diuretics, beta-2 agonists, insulin, sodium bicarbonate, K-binders (patiromer, SZC), corticosteroids (high dose, mineralocorticoid effect), licorice (apparent mineralocorticoid excess), amphotericin B, theophylline, caffeine.
- **Refractory hypokalemia → check Mg first.** Mg deficit causes ROMK-mediated renal K wasting; oral/IV K alone won't fix it (Huang & Kuo 2007 *JASN* PMID 17804670).

**Genetic modifiers:**
- *KCNQ1*, *KCNE1* — long QT and modest K handling effects.
- *HSD11B2* — apparent mineralocorticoid excess (severe HTN + low K). Rare.
- *NR3C2* (mineralocorticoid receptor) variants — Liddle syndrome (gain-of-function ENaC; HTN + low K + low renin).
- *SCNN1B*, *SCNN1G* — Liddle syndrome variants.
- *SLC12A3* (NCC) — Gitelman syndrome; *SLC12A1* (NKCC2) — Bartter syndrome (both → K wasting).
- *CACNA1S*, *SCN4A* — periodic paralysis (hyperkalemic and hypokalemic forms).
- No clinically meaningful single-SNP modifier for daily K management in healthy people.

**Acid-base interaction:**
- Acidemia → K ↑ ~0.6 mmol/L per 0.1 pH drop (cellular shift; H+ in, K out).
- Alkalemia → K ↓ similarly.

**Volume status:**
- Effective arterial underfilling (CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome) → secondary hyperaldosteronism → K wasting.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Optimal serum K for healthy young adults** — DASH/Aburto data are middle-aged adults. The U-shaped mortality curve (Goyal 2012, Krogager 2017) is from CV cohorts; whether 4.2 vs 4.4 matters in a healthy 20yM is unclear.
- **Salt substitute (KCl) safety in young adults** — SSaSS (Neal 2021) was elderly Chinese HTN; broad applicability uncertain. Avoid if CKD or RAAS blocker.
- **Magnesium-K coupling in healthy** — well-established mechanism (ROMK), but how often Mg deficit subtly contributes to low-normal K in healthy young men is unstudied.
- **K supplementation vs dietary K** — dietary K (with bicarbonate equivalents) has additional alkalinizing benefit not fully replicated by KCl supplements (Frassetto 2008).
- **Effect of fitness-enthusiast supplements (creatine, BCAA, electrolyte powders) on serum K** — minimal direct data; mostly mechanistic.
- **Pre-analytic standardization protocols** — no universal consensus on exact procedures to minimize pseudohyperkalemia in healthy young athletes.
- **GLP-1 RA and K trajectory in healthy users** — minimal data outside DM trials.
