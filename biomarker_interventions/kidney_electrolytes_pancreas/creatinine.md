# Creatinine (serum) — Intervention Research

> Serum creatinine is **muscle-mass + meat-intake + creatine-supplementation × renal-clearance**. In a healthy young person, "lowering creatinine" is rarely a goal — the marker is mostly a confound for eGFR. The single biggest non-disease modifier in fitness enthusiasts is creatine monohydrate (raises serum Cr ~0.1–0.4 mg/dL with NO change in true GFR). Hydration moves it ~0.05–0.15 mg/dL. Cooked-meat meals raise it transiently 0.1–0.3 mg/dL for 6 h. True kidney-protective interventions (BP control, SGLT2i, ACEi, weight loss) move Cr only modestly in the absence of CKD.

**Cross-link:** Reference biology / clinical interpretation lives in `../../kidney_electrolytes_pancreas.md` §2 (Creatinine) and §0.1 (creatine confound). This file is INTERVENTION-only — what moves the marker, by how much, in how long, with what evidence.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs with consistent direction |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT (≥100 participants or pivotal design) |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational; can't establish causality |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization — genetic proxies estimating causal effect |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology / physiology extrapolation; no direct human RCT for *this specific effect* |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial worth knowing but not yet replicated |

Population caveat: most cited evidence is from NW European or North American adults aged 40+, frequently with CKD. Floor effects shrink magnitudes 30–60% in lean healthy young adults at near-optimal baseline. Cited ranges in healthy young men come primarily from creatine pharmacology trials and metabolic-ward feeding studies.

## 1. Foods that LOWER serum creatinine (or improve direction)

Creatinine is rarely "too high" in a healthy person — for most users this section is "foods that don't artificially raise creatinine on draw day." Foods that genuinely lower steady-state creatinine do so by lowering muscle mass / dietary creatine pool — usually NOT desirable.

| Food / pattern | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Plant-based / vegetarian diet (chronic) | No meat × ≥4 weeks | Serum Cr ↓ 0.05–0.15 mg/dL | 2–4 wk | **[Cohort + RCT]** | Lew CCH et al., *Am J Kidney Dis* 2017;69:751–761 (vegetarian vs animal protein, n=2,346); Kontessis 1990 *Kidney Int* | Reflects loss of pre-formed creatinine in cooked meat + slightly lower muscle creatine pool. eGFR-cr increases by ~3–7 mL/min on plant-only diet. |
| Avoid cooked meat for 12 h pre-draw | Skip cooked beef/pork/chicken evening before fasting draw | Serum Cr ↓ 0.1–0.3 mg/dL vs post-meat draw | 6–12 h | **[RCT]** | Mayersohn M et al., *Br J Clin Pharmacol* 1983;15:227 (PMID 6849778); Preiss DJ et al., *Am J Kidney Dis* 2007;49:48 (PMID 17185144) | Cooking converts ~20–65% of muscle creatine to creatinine; one 225 g cooked-beef meal raises serum Cr by mean 0.23 mg/dL at 3 h. Raw/undercooked meat: minimal. |
| Adequate fluid intake / euvolemia | Maintain euhydration to draw morning (~30–35 mL/kg/d water) | Serum Cr ↓ 0.05–0.15 mg/dL vs dehydrated | Hours | **[Mechanism + Cohort]** | Sontrop JM et al., *Am J Nephrol* 2013;37:434 (PMID 23635803); Clark WF et al., *JAMA* 2018;319:1870 (CKD-WIT, PMID 29801012) | CKD-WIT (n=631) found no eGFR benefit from chronic forced hydration vs ad-lib in early CKD, but acute prerenal Cr falls with rehydration. | Adequate dietary potassium / fruit-vegetable load | ≥4–5 servings produce/d | Serum Cr ↓ minimal directly; eGFR slope preservation | Years | **[Cohort]** | Kim H et al., *Am J Kidney Dis* 2019;74:618; Banerjee T et al., *Kidney Int* 2014;86:1031 | Plant-rich diet reduces urinary acid load → less glomerular stress; effect on serum Cr is small but eGFR slope is preserved. |
| Reducing sodium intake (if BP elevated) | <2.3 g Na/d | Serum Cr ↔; eGFR ↑ 1–4 mL/min via BP and intraglomerular pressure | 4–12 wk | **[RCT]** | He FJ et al., *J Hum Hypertens* 2009;23:363 (Cochrane meta); SPRINT subanalyses | Indirect — moves eGFR via BP, not muscle physiology. |
| Weight loss in obesity (8–10% of body weight) | Caloric deficit | Serum Cr ↓ 0.05–0.10 mg/dL; eGFR ↑ 4–8 mL/min | 6–12 mo | **[RCT]** | Friedman AN et al., *Clin J Am Soc Nephrol* 2013;8:837; Look AHEAD subanalyses; Kjaergaard AD et al. (MR) *J Am Soc Nephrol* 2022;33:1610 | Reduces glomerular hyperfiltration. Lean 20-y men derive minimal benefit. |
| Lower (moderate) protein intake (~0.8–1.0 g/kg/d) | ~80 g/d for 80-kg man vs 200 g | Serum Cr ↓ 0.03–0.08 mg/dL; BUN ↓ markedly | 1–2 wk | **[RCT]** | Knight EL et al., *Ann Intern Med* 2003;138:460 (PMID 12639078); MDRD study group, *NEJM* 1994;330:877 | Dominant effect is on BUN, not Cr. Cr changes are small because muscle mass — not protein intake — sets steady-state production. |

### Lowering serum creatinine

True non-disease lowering of serum Cr (apart from stopping the above) is rare and mostly reflects loss of muscle.

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Discontinuing creatine | Stop × 7–14 d | Serum Cr ↓ 0.1–0.3 mg/dL toward true baseline | 1–2 wk | **[Meta]** | de Souza e Silva 2019; Pinto 2020 | Confirm with cystatin C if Cr remains elevated after washout. |
| Discontinuing trimethoprim/cimetidine/cobicistat | Stop × 3–7 d | Serum Cr ↓ 10–30% toward baseline | 3–7 d | **[RCT]** | Berg 1996; Andreev 1999 | Confirms pseudo-elevation. |
| Omega-3 fish oil (EPA/DHA) | 2–4 g/d | Serum Cr ↔ in healthy; small ↓ in CKD | 8–12 wk | **[RCT]** | Hu J et al., *J Am Soc Nephrol* 2017;28:2207 (meta of n=993) | Effect mostly on uACR (see uACR file), not Cr. |
| **Things claimed but NOT supported** — chanca piedra, parsley tea, "kidney detox" blends | Various | No measurable effect on Cr or true GFR | — | **[no-effect]** | No RCT data; mechanistic claims unfounded | Ignore. |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** for an artifactually high Cr in a fitness enthusiast: **stop creatine monohydrate × 7–14 days and re-draw, OR add cystatin C** (resolves nearly all "Stage 2 CKD" mislabeling in young men). True GFR unchanged.
- **Best stack of 3** to ensure a clean, accurate Cr reading: (1) hold creatine 14 d before draw; (2) avoid cooked meat 12–24 h before draw, fasted morning sample; (3) maintain euhydration (no chronic dehydration, no acute over-loading) and avoid intense lifting 24–48 h prior.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for a healthy 20-year-old male near optimal baseline (Cr 0.95): essentially none — Cr is a structural marker. Stopping creatine will reveal "true" baseline ~0.15 mg/dL lower; that's it.
- **Realistic 12-week delta** for elevated baseline (Cr 1.4 in a lifter on creatine + 200 g protein): stop creatine, hydrate, avoid pre-draw meat → Cr ~1.15–1.20 (no change in true kidney function). If true GFR concern, cystatin C eGFR > 90 confirms muscular pseudo-elevation.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:** never for "high Cr" alone in a healthy person. ACEi/ARB/SGLT2i are reserved for albuminuric or hypertensive CKD; their initiation INTENTIONALLY raises Cr 10–30% short-term as part of the renoprotective hemodynamic effect (KDIGO 2024).

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- Lean SA males often have **lower muscle mass** at any given BMI than NW European norms → baseline Cr typically 0.7–0.95 mg/dL even with normal kidneys; eGFR-cr often ≥110, occasionally appearing "hyperfiltering."
- The CKD-EPI 2021 race-free equation is the current standard (Inker 2021 *NEJM* 385:1737); it removed the Black-vs-non-Black coefficient but was not specifically calibrated against a SA validation cohort. South Asian validation studies (Jessani S et al., *Am J Kidney Dis* 2014;63:49 — Pakistani cohort) show CKD-EPI is reasonable but tends to overestimate GFR ~3–8 mL/min in SA. Cystatin C concordance studies in SA: Kumar V et al., *Clin Chim Acta* 2017;471:11.
- Vegetarian SA males have a slightly lower steady-state Cr (less dietary creatine pool) — typical normal range 0.7–1.0 mg/dL. A "high" Cr 1.2 in a lifelong vegetarian SA male is a stronger flag than the same number in a meat-eating bodybuilder.
- *APOL1* G1/G2 high-risk variants are essentially absent in SA populations (relevant only to West African ancestry).

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

Things that would make a default Cr recommendation WRONG or that cause unexpected swings:

- **Genetic modifiers:**
  - *GATM* / *AGXT2* / *CPS1* variants — modest effects on serum Cr genome-wide (Köttgen A et al., *Nat Genet* 2010;42:376; Pattaro C et al., *Nat Commun* 2016;7:10023). Effect size <0.05 mg/dL per allele; not clinically actionable individually.
  - *UMOD* rs4293393 — affects long-term eGFR slope, not 20-yr-old Cr.
  - *APOL1* G1/G2 — relevant only in West African ancestry; massive CKD risk multiplier.
  - *SLC22A2* (OCT2) variants — modulate tubular Cr secretion; some Asian populations have altered Cr ↔ GFR relationship.
- **Drug interactions creating Cr pseudo-elevation (no true GFR change):** trimethoprim, cimetidine, dolutegravir, cobicistat, fenofibrate, ranolazine, pyrimethamine, dronedarone, salicylate (high-dose). Use cystatin C eGFR to discriminate.
- **Drugs that lower Cr secretion AND true GFR (real effect):** chronic NSAIDs (afferent vasoconstriction); aminoglycosides; cisplatin; tenofovir disoproxil (NOT tenofovir alafenamide).
- **Creatine monohydrate** — single biggest young-male confound. Always ask before interpreting Cr.
- **Cooked meat within 12 h of draw** — false elevation 0.1–0.3 mg/dL.
- **Acute dehydration / fasting** — modest false elevation.
- **Recent intense exercise (24–48 h)** — Cr ↑ via mild rhabdo/CK release.
- **Assay method differences:**
  - **Jaffe (alkaline picrate)**: subject to interferences from glucose, ketones, bilirubin, cephalosporins → can over-read Cr by 5–20%.
  - **Enzymatic IDMS-traceable**: gold standard for current US labs.
  - **Point-of-care creatinine devices**: vary; not interchangeable with central lab values for trend.
- **Sample handling**: prolonged plasma storage at room temp can cause modest Cr drift; usually <5%.
- **Pregnancy** (not relevant to male user but engine flag): Cr drops 0.2–0.3 mg/dL via plasma-volume expansion + true GFR rise.
- **Liver failure**: low Cr from reduced creatine biosynthesis — masks renal dysfunction.
- **Rhabdomyolysis**: massive Cr release with disproportionately low BUN (BUN/Cr <10). CK is the dominant marker.
- **ACEi/ARB initiation**: 10–30% Cr rise is EXPECTED and part of the protective mechanism; do NOT discontinue unless rise >30% or hyperkalemia (KDIGO 2021 BP guideline).
- **SGLT2i initiation**: 5–10% Cr rise in first 2–4 weeks then plateau; long-term slope improves.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Hydration status as a long-term lever for eGFR preservation** — CKD-WIT (Clark 2018) was negative for benefit of forced hydration in early CKD; the effect of chronic euhydration vs chronic mild dehydration on healthy young eGFR slope is poorly characterized.
- **Coffee → renoprotection** — most evidence is observational with residual confounding; MR studies are mixed (Kennedy OJ et al., *BMJ Open* 2020;10:e036466).
- **Plant-based diet → measured GFR change** — MDRD-era studies showed 5–15 mL/min higher GFR on plant diets, but most was muscle / dietary creatinine effect, not true GFR. Few iohexol-clearance studies in young vegetarians exist.
- **Creatine monohydrate long-term renal safety** — the ISSN 2017 position stand and de Souza e Silva 2019 meta find no nephrotoxicity; trials >5 yr are scarce. The recommendation engine should note creatine as confound, not contraindicate it.
- **Cystatin C reference ranges in young muscular males** — undercharacterized; most reference data are from older populations. Inker's 2021 NEJM eGFR-cys equation performs well but absolute cutoffs in lean 20yM under-validated.
- **Effect of intermittent fasting / time-restricted eating on Cr trajectory** — minimal data; some signals of small Cr rise from acute catabolism but no chronic eGFR change.
- **GLP-1 agonists' effect on Cr in non-diabetics** — FLOW (Perkovic 2024) was T2DM cohort. Effect in healthy young users is unknown but presumed modest.

## Source format conventions

- Cite primary literature (PubMed PMID where available) over secondary sources.
- Prefer meta-analyses → large RCTs → cohorts → mechanism papers.
- Use *Journal Year;Vol:Pages* format. Include PMID when available.
- KDIGO 2024 CKD Guideline: *Kidney Int* 2024;105(4S):S117–S314.
- CKD-EPI 2021: Inker LA et al., *N Engl J Med* 2021;385:1737–1749.
