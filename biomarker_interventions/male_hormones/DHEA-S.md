# DHEA-Sulfate (DHEA-S) — Intervention Research

> DHEA-S is **tightly controlled by ACTH** at the adrenal zona reticularis. The dominant lifespan trajectory is age — peak in early 20s, ~1–4% annual decline ("adrenopause"). The main suppressors in a healthy 20-year-old are (a) **exogenous glucocorticoids** (oral, intra-articular, high-dose inhaled, extensive topical) → ACTH suppression → adrenal androgen pathway shutdown; (b) **chronic energy deficiency / RED-S / overtraining**; (c) **CYP17A1 inhibitors** (oral ketoconazole, abiraterone). Supplemental DHEA in eugonadal young men with normal DHEA-S has **no demonstrated benefit and several concerns** (Brown 2003 RCT — raises androgenic metabolites, prostate concerns). DHEA replacement is evidence-supported only for **adrenal insufficiency**.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

Same as other files. **Reference range — age-stratified (LabCorp adult male):**
- 20–29 years: **211–492 µg/dL**
- 30–39 years: 110–510 µg/dL
- 40–49 years: 110–370 µg/dL
- 60–69 years: 30–260 µg/dL

A 20-year-old man is at lifetime peak. "Age-appropriate optimal" sits in the upper portion of the 20–29 range — perhaps 350–500 µg/dL. Values in the lower part of the 20–29 range (e.g. <300 µg/dL) deserve a "why" — chronic stress, RED-S, exogenous glucocorticoid exposure, beginning adrenal insufficiency.

## 1. Foods that RAISE DHEA-S

**There are no validated dietary interventions that meaningfully raise DHEA-S in healthy eugonadal men.** DHEA-S production is ACTH-driven and substrate-saturated under normal conditions; food-level "boosters" are essentially marketing.

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Caloric adequacy (correcting RED-S)** | EA ≥45 kcal/kg LBM/d in chronically underfueled men | DHEA-S recovers from RED-S-suppressed levels | 3–6 months | **[Cohort]** | Mountjoy 2018 IOC RED-S consensus (PMID 29773536); male RED-S literature | Restorative; no benefit in eugonadal men |
| **Mediterranean diet pattern** | Adherence high | Cross-sectional modest higher DHEA-S in Med-diet adherent populations | Years | **[Cohort]** | Med-diet endocrine cohorts; ATTICA | Confounded by overall lifestyle |
| **Adequate fat / cholesterol intake** | ≥30% kcal from fat | Substrate adequacy for steroidogenesis (cholesterol → pregnenolone → DHEA) | Months | **[Mechanism]** | Steroidogenesis substrate physiology | Direct effect not isolated; effect is on the order of correcting deficiency |

## 3. Supplements

### Raising DHEA-S (when appropriate — adrenal insufficiency only)

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **DHEA (oral micronized)** | 25–50 mg/d | DHEA-S **rises markedly** (dose-dependent); 25 mg → ~200 µg/dL serum DHEA-S rise; 50 mg → 300+ µg/dL rise; T may rise modestly via DHEA→T conversion | 4–8 wk | **[RCT]** | Brown 2003 *Fertil Steril* 80:1495 (PMID 14667893; healthy men 18–42, 6 mo); Morales 1994 *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 78:1360 | Raises androgenic metabolites (DHT, androstenedione); E2 also rises (aromatization). | **DHEA (in confirmed adrenal insufficiency)** | 25–50 mg/d | DHEA-S → physiologic range; modest improvements in well-being, mood in some women's trials; men's data sparse | 6 months | **[RCT]** | Arlt 1999 *NEJM* 341:1013 (women with AI); Hunt 2000 (mixed AI); Endocrine Society 2016 AI guideline (DHEA mention as adjunct) | Endocrine Society 2016 AI guideline does not strongly recommend; limited trial benefit in men |
| **Pregnenolone** | 25–50 mg/d | Raises DHEA-S in some old trials; effect inconsistent and small | Weeks | **[1-Trial]** | Old neurosteroid literature; sparse modern RCT | Marketed widely; modest evidence; safety concerns less studied |
| **7-Keto DHEA** | 100 mg 2×/d | Does NOT raise DHEA-S (non-androgenic metabolite); marketed for weight loss without raising androgenic burden | — | **[1-Trial]** | Kalman 2000 *Curr Ther Res*; small weight-loss trials | Different endpoint; not a DHEA-S "booster" |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** for low DHEA-S in young man:
  - **Identify and remove glucocorticoid exposure** (oral, high-dose inhaled, extensive topical, intra-articular) — DHEA-S can recover ~50% of suppression within months of stopping.
  - **Stop chronic opioid** — modest recovery.
  - **Caloric refeeding** if RED-S — DHEA-S recovers over months.
  - **Treat hyperprolactinemia** if present.
  - In **healthy 20yM with low-normal DHEA-S** (250–350) and no removable cause: probably nothing actionable. DHEA replacement is not indicated.

- **Best stack of 3** for restoring DHEA-S:
  1. Address removable cause (exogenous steroid exposure, opioids, RED-S, hyperprolactinemia).
  2. Adequate sleep + caloric availability + stress reduction.
  3. Resistance + aerobic training (modest cross-sectional support).

- **Realistic 12-week delta — healthy lean 20yM with normal DHEA-S (350–500 µg/dL):** essentially stable. No intervention reliably moves DHEA-S in this population.

- **Realistic 12-week delta — RED-S underfueled lean man with DHEA-S 200:** +50–100 µg/dL with refeeding within 3–6 months.

- **Realistic 12-week delta — chronic glucocorticoid user (DHEA-S 50):** with taper + months recovery → +100–200 µg/dL; sometimes incomplete recovery if longstanding exposure.

- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:**
  - **DHEA replacement 25–50 mg/d** in confirmed primary or secondary adrenal insufficiency (Endocrine Society 2016 AI guideline mentions as adjunct, not strong recommendation).
  - **Hydrocortisone replacement** for adrenal insufficiency (the cortisol component, not DHEA per se).
  - **Cabergoline** if hyperprolactinemia is causing DHEA-S suppression.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- DHEA-S reference ranges are largely population-stable across ancestries, but absolute peak values may run modestly lower in SA cohorts (limited population data).
- Lean SA men with chronic visceral adiposity / IR may have lower DHEA-S than expected (insulin → adrenal IR mechanism).
- South Asian cultural prevalence of **non-classical congenital adrenal hyperplasia (21-OH deficiency)** is ~1/100 in some populations (cf. Ashkenazi Jewish 1/100, general 1/1000). Markedly elevated DHEA-S in young SA man should prompt 17-OH progesterone screen.
- Vitamin D deficiency very common in SA — addressing D deficiency is reasonable general intervention but no specific DHEA-S effect demonstrated.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- `CYP17A1` variants — affect adrenal androgen synthesis rate.
- `HSD3B2` — 3β-HSD deficiency (rare CAH variant) → altered DHEA-S/cortisol.
- `CYP21A2` — 21-hydroxylase deficiency (CAH) → can elevate DHEA-S markedly.
- `DHEA-sulfotransferase (SULT2A1)` variants — affect DHEA → DHEA-S conversion.
- `ZNF gene cluster on 17q21` — affects DHEA-S levels in genome-wide studies.

**Drug interactions (lower DHEA-S):**
- Glucocorticoids (oral, intra-articular, high-dose inhaled, extensive topical)
- Ketoconazole (oral, extensive topical)
- Abiraterone (oncology)
- Megestrol (very high-dose, anti-cachexia)
- Mitotane (adrenolytic)
- Etomidate (anesthetic; transient)
- Opioids (chronic)
- Insulin / hyperinsulinemia
- Dopamine agonists (modest, transient)

**Drug interactions (raise DHEA-S):**
- DHEA supplementation (direct)
- ACTH (Cosyntropin stimulation, transient)
- 21-hydroxylase inhibitors (in CAH context)
- Some anti-epileptics (mild)

**Lifestyle confounders:**
- **Age** — dominant variable; use age-stratified ranges.
- **Hour of day** — minimal variation due to long DHEA-S half-life and large reservoir; convenient for measurement.
- **Recent ACTH stimulation** (test, infection, surgery, exercise) — raises transiently.
- **DHEA supplementation** — raises markedly; ASK about supplements.
- **Glucocorticoid exposure** including topical/inhaled — suppresses.
- **RED-S / underfueling** — suppresses.

**Assay considerations:**
- DHEA-S immunoassay generally reliable; less platform variability than thyroid assays.
- LC-MS/MS available for steroid panels (more accurate for low values).
- Biotin: high-dose biotin can interfere with some DHEA-S assays. Hold ≥48 h before draw.

**Important pattern recognition:**
- **DHEA-S markedly high (>700 µg/dL) in young man** → rule out non-classical CAH (17-OH progesterone) or adrenal tumor (imaging).
- **DHEA-S low (<200 µg/dL in young man) + low AM cortisol** → adrenal insufficiency or chronic exogenous glucocorticoid exposure.
- **DHEA-S low + normal cortisol** → can reflect chronic stress/RED-S/opioid use; less ominous than combined deficit.
- **DHEA-S low + high prolactin** → consider prolactinoma's secondary adrenal androgen suppression.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **DHEA supplementation in healthy young men with low-normal DHEA-S** — no demonstrated benefit; potential harms (androgenic side effects, prostate growth concerns); guidelines do not endorse.
- **Whether age-related DHEA-S decline contributes meaningfully to "andropause" symptoms** — debated; DHEA replacement RCTs in older men have shown inconsistent effects on well-being.
- **Cortisol/DHEA-S ratio as longitudinal optimization marker** — biologically plausible (epigenetic clock literature) but no RCT showing intervention to improve ratio durably improves outcomes.
- **"Adrenal fatigue" framework** — Endocrine Society 2016 statement explicitly rejects as a clinical entity. The phenotype described is real (chronic stress, RED-S, exogenous steroid exposure, sleep restriction) but mechanistic framing is wrong.
- **Whether ashwagandha specifically raises DHEA-S** (vs. its established cortisol-lowering effect) — studied less; effect inconsistent.
- **Pregnenolone supplementation effect on DHEA-S** — old neurosteroid literature, sparse modern RCT data.
- **Topical ketoconazole shampoo's chronic systemic DHEA-S effect** — at standard 2% rinse-off doses, probably negligible; extensive cream use to large body surface may matter.
