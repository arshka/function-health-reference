# Total Testosterone — Intervention Research

> Highest-leverage moves: **sleep extension to 8 h** (recovers 10–15% of T lost to chronic restriction; Leproult 2011), **fat loss in obese baseline** (~+84 ng/dL per 10 kg lost; Grossmann 2013 meta), **eliminate opioids/heavy alcohol** (chronic opioids halve T; Bawor 2015), and **deficiency-correction only** for vitamin D and zinc. The "T-booster" supplement category — D-aspartic acid, tribulus, fenugreek, most herbal blends — is largely null in healthy eugonadal men. In a healthy lean 20yM near upper-normal baseline, lifestyle "optimization" rarely adds more than 30–80 ng/dL.

**Cross-link:** Reference biology / clinical interpretation lives in `../../hormones_male_panel.md` §1 and `../../effect_sizes_endocrine.md` (Total Testosterone section). This file is INTERVENTION-only.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs with consistent direction |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT (≥100 participants or pivotal design) |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational; can't establish causality |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization — genetic proxies estimating causal effect |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology / physiology extrapolation; no direct human RCT for this specific effect |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial worth knowing but not yet replicated |
| **[no-effect]** | Trials demonstrate null result; commonly marketed but unsupported |

Population caveat: most cited evidence is from European-ancestry men aged 30+ with elevated baseline (low T, obese, or hypogonadal). Floor/ceiling effects shrink magnitudes ~30–60% in lean healthy young adults at near-optimal baseline. South Asian / lean-male specific data flagged where it exists.

## 1. Foods that RAISE total testosterone (or improve direction)

The food-level effect on baseline T in healthy eugonadal men is small. Most "T-supportive" foods work through (a) supplying micronutrient cofactors (zinc, magnesium, selenium, vitamin D), (b) caloric adequacy / fat-fraction, or (c) reducing aromatization via fat-mass loss. There is no single food that reliably raises T in a replete young man by more than ~10%.

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Eggs (whole, including yolk)** | 3 whole eggs/day | Total T **+~10–15%** in resistance-trained men vs. egg-white-only at matched protein | 12 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Bagheri et al. 2021, *Food Funct* 12:8919 (n=30 resistance-trained men, RCT) | Mechanism: dietary cholesterol is the substrate for steroidogenesis; effect attenuated in caloric surplus. Independent replication absent. |
| **Oysters & shellfish (zinc-rich)** | 6 oysters (~30–50 mg zinc) | If zinc-deficient: total T **+25–90%** over 3–6 months of repletion. If replete: **null**. | 3–6 months | **[1-Trial]** | Prasad 1996, *Nutrition* 12:344 | Effect is repletion, not pharmacology. Confirm baseline serum/RBC zinc before assuming benefit. |
| **Beef / red meat (zinc, B12, sat fat substrate)** | 100–200 g/day in mixed diet | T effect: small **positive** vs. low-fat vegan (~SMD +0.38 in Whittaker 2021 favoring higher-fat). Magnitude ~50–80 ng/dL transition between low-fat to mixed Western diet. | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Whittaker & Wu 2021, *J Steroid Biochem Mol Biol* 210:105878 (PMID 33741447) | Effect attributable to dietary fat fraction (≥30–40% kcal), not red meat per se. Replicable in fish/dairy fat as well. |
| **Brazil nuts (selenium)** | 2–4 nuts/day (~150–300 µg Se) | Selenium repletion modestly supports steroidogenesis (Se cofactor in 5α-reductase, deiodinases); T direct effect **~null** in replete men. | Weeks | **[Mechanism]** | Bianco 2019, *Endocr Rev* 40(4):1000 (PMC6668716); selenoenzyme physiology | Excess selenium (>400 µg/d) → selenosis. Avoid daily 4 brazil nuts long-term. |
| **Fatty fish / EPA-DHA-rich diet** | 250 g salmon 2×/wk | Total T: small positive, ~+5–10% in some intervention trials; mechanism partly via reduced inflammation/cortisol. | 8–12 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Abbott 2020, *Asian J Androl* 22:609 (n=38, fish oil vs. placebo, total T +71 ng/dL); contrasted by null trials in non-obese | Effect plausible only in inflammation/obesity baseline; null in lean healthy men. |
| **Pomegranate juice** | 8 oz/day | Total T **+24%** in salivary testosterone (Al-Dujaili 2012, n=60); serum T not robustly replicated | 2 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Al-Dujaili & Smail 2012, *Endocr Abstr* 28 P313 (conference abstract); not peer-reviewed full paper | **Conference abstract only.** Salivary T ≠ serum T. Do not over-rely. |
| **Allium (garlic, onions)** | Garlic 0.8 g/kg/day (rat data) | Animal data show T elevation; **no human RCT data** of meaningful effect. | — | **[Mechanism]** | Oi 2001, *J Nutr* 131:2150 (rat study) | Marketed as "T-food" without human evidence. **[no-effect]** in humans until proven otherwise. |
| **Cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cabbage)** | 500 g/day | Indole-3-carbinol / DIM may modulate E2 metabolism (favor 2-OH over 16α-OH pathway); secondary modest T preservation in obese men via lower aromatization | Months | **[Mechanism]** | Bradlow 1995, *Carcinogenesis*; DIM E2 metabolism studies | Direct T effect not demonstrated; mechanism is via E2 modulation. |
| **Olive oil (extra-virgin)** | 25–40 mL/day Mediterranean pattern | Total T modestly **higher** vs. corn oil substitution in small RCT (Derouiche 2013, +17% T at 3 wk in n=25) | 3 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Derouiche et al. 2013, *Nat Prod Res* 27:1953 | Small Moroccan trial; not replicated; effect possibly via reduced oxidative steroidogenic stress. |
| **Caloric adequacy (energy availability ≥45 kcal/kg LBM/d)** | Stop chronic underfueling | T recovers from RED-S-suppressed levels by **+30–50%** over 3–6 months when EA restored | 3–6 months | **[Cohort]** | Mountjoy 2018 IOC RED-S consensus (PMID 29773536); Hooper 2017 male endurance studies | Underfueling is the dominant lifestyle T suppressor in lean active young men; refeed alone often eliminates "low T" phenotype |
| **Mediterranean diet (overall pattern)** | Adherence score high vs. low | Total T modestly higher cross-sectionally in Med-diet adherent men; intervention data modest | Months–years | **[Cohort]** | Salas-Salvadó 2019; ATTICA cohort | Mostly cross-sectional; intervention-grade evidence weak. |

## 2. Foods that LOWER total testosterone (or worsen direction)

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Low-fat diet (vs. higher-fat isocaloric)** | <20% kcal from fat | Total T **−0.52 SD (~60 ng/dL)** in Western men; pooled SMD = −0.38 vs. moderate-fat | 4–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Whittaker & Wu 2021, *J Steroid Biochem Mol Biol* 210:105878 (6 studies, n=206) | Effect smaller in East/South Asian men. Direction is real; absolute magnitude uncertain. |
| **Alcohol — chronic heavy use** | ≥3–4 drinks/day chronically | Total T **−~15–25%**; free T −10%; E2 +7.65 pg/mL | Weeks–months | **[Meta]** | Santi et al. 2024, *Andrology* 12:626 (21 studies, n=10,199; T MD −4.02 nmol/L) | Acute single-session effect minimal; chronic effect dose-dependent; ≤7 units/wk → no clear effect |
| **Soy isoflavones — supraphysiologic intake** | Whole-food soy 25 g protein/day | Total T meta-analysis: **null** (Hamilton-Reeves 2010 meta); only at very high isoflavone doses (>100 mg/d) modest T reduction | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Hamilton-Reeves et al. 2010, *Fertil Steril* 94:997 (PMID 19524224; 15 studies, no significant T reduction) | "Soy is feminizing" myth largely refuted. Reasonable dietary soy intake has **no measurable T effect** in men. |
| **Trans fats / processed oils** | Hydrogenated oils habitually | Cross-sectional: trans-fat intake inversely correlates with T in NHANES; magnitude ~−15% across quartiles | Years | **[Cohort]** | Mínguez-Alarcón 2017, *J Nutr* 147:1375 | Confounded by overall diet quality; mechanistic plausibility (membrane lipid composition affects steroidogenic enzyme function). |
| **Licorice (glycyrrhizin)** | 7 g/day glycyrrhetinic acid | Total T **−26–35%** (Armanini 2003); inhibits 17,20-lyase | 1 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Armanini et al. 2003, *N Engl J Med* 348:2116 (n=7); also Sakamoto 1988 *Endocrinol Jpn* | Real, replicable effect at high doses (>500 mg glycyrrhizin/day); "DGL" deglycyrrhized licorice does not have this effect. |
| **Spearmint tea** | 2 cups/day | Total T **−~25%** in PCOS women; data in men sparse but mechanism (anti-androgen, 5α-reductase inhibition) extrapolates | 30 days | **[1-Trial]** | Akdoğan 2007, *Phytother Res* 21:444 (women, n=21) | Avoid as habitual drink if T optimization is goal; chamomile / green tea are safer alternatives. |
| **Excess sugar / refined carbs (acute)** | 75 g glucose load | Total T **−25%** within 2 h post-glucose load in healthy men (Caronia 2013) | 2 h | **[RCT]** | Caronia et al. 2013, *Clin Endocrinol* 78:291 (n=74, OGTT) | Transient — recovers by 4 h. Real but probably not clinically actionable beyond avoiding measurement after carb load. |
| **Ultra-processed food pattern** | High UPF intake quartile | Cross-sectional: UPF intake inversely correlates with T in young men (NHANES) | Years | **[Cohort]** | Lopez 2024, *J Nutr* (NHANES analysis) | Confounded by adiposity; effect mediated mostly by weight gain → aromatization. |
| **Low-protein diet (<0.8 g/kg)** | <60 g/d protein in adult man | SHBG ↑, free T ↓; total T mildly decreased in some trials | Weeks | **[1-Trial]** | Anderson 1987, *Metabolism* 36:1031 (low- vs. high-protein crossover in lean men) | Affects free T more than total T via SHBG. |
| **Mint / peppermint (large doses)** | Concentrated mint extract | Anti-androgenic in animal models; sparse human data in men | — | **[Mechanism]** | Akdoğan 2004 rat data; Vandergrift 2018 review | Casual culinary mint = irrelevant. |

### Improving (when applicable)

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| **Zinc (in zinc-deficient men only)** | 30 mg/d elemental × 3–6 mo | Total T **+92%** in marginally deficient elderly (8.3→16.0 nmol/L) | 3–6 months | **[1-Trial]** | Prasad et al. 1996, *Nutrition* 12:344 (PMID 8875519) | Replete young men: no effect. | **Magnesium (in deficient/active men)** | 10 mg/kg/d (~750 mg) × 4 wk | Total T **+24%** in tae kwon do athletes (Cinar 2011); effect smaller in sedentary | 4 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Cinar et al. 2011, *Biol Trace Elem Res* 140:18 (n=30 young athletes) | Population-deficient (active sweating) athletes; null in replete sedentary men. | **Tongkat ali (Eurycoma longifolia)** | 200 mg standardized/d | In LOH men: T-normalization rate 35.5%→90.8%; meta-analytic SMD **+1.35 (95% CI 0.57–2.14)** | 4 wk | **[Meta]** | Tambi et al. 2012, *Andrologia* 44:226; meta PMC9415500 (2022) | Effect in **hypogonadal/LOH** men, NOT eugonadal young men. |---|---|---|---|---|

## 4. Activities & Behaviors

| Activity | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sleep extension (correcting restriction)** | 5 h → 8 h/night | Total T **+10–15%** recovery; effect concentrated 2–10 PM | 1 wk | **[RCT]** | Leproult & Van Cauter 2011, *JAMA* 305:2173 (PMID 21632481; n=10 healthy men age ~24, crossover) | Not a "boost above baseline" — recovery from debt. Already-rested men gain little. **Single highest-leverage cheap intervention.** |
| **Sleep restriction (worsener)** | 5 h/night × 1 wk | Total T **−10–15%** | 1 wk | **[RCT]** | Same as above | Reversible on restoration. |
| **Weight loss in obese baseline** | ~10 kg loss via caloric deficit | Total T **+~84 ng/dL** (+2.9 nmol/L) per 10 kg | 3–6 months | **[Meta]** | Grossmann & Matsumoto 2013, *Eur J Endocrinol* 168:829 (PMID 23482592; 44 studies, n>1,700) | Floor effect: lean men gain almost nothing. |
| **Bariatric surgery (severe obesity)** | RYGB / sleeve | Total T **+~250 ng/dL** (+8.7 nmol/L) | 6–12 months | **[Meta]** | Same Grossmann meta | Largest single lifestyle/surgical move for hypogonadal obese. |
| **Resistance training (acute spike)** | Multi-set compound session | Total T **+20–35%** acutely; returns to baseline within 60–120 min | 15–30 min post | **[Meta]** | Vingren et al. 2010, *Sports Med* 40:1037 | Acute only — **does NOT raise baseline T** in eugonadal trained men long-term. |
| **Resistance training (chronic, baseline)** | 12-wk progressive RT in untrained | Baseline total T **unchanged** in most replete trained men; modest +5–8% in some untrained populations | 12 wk | **[Meta]** | Vingren 2010 review; Hayes 2010 *Br J Sports Med* | The most over-claimed effect. RT raises strength, body composition, mood — but not chronic T in normal men. |
| **HIIT / sprint training** | 30 s × 6 sprints, 3×/wk | Acute T spike similar to RT; baseline effect ~null | Weeks | **[1-Trial]** | Hackney 2008; Trapp 2008 | Same caveat as RT. |
| **Endurance overtraining / high-volume running** | >80 km/wk at high effort | Total T **−30–40%** vs. sedentary controls | Months–years | **[Cohort]** | Hackney et al. 2003, *Eur J Appl Physiol* 89:198; Hackney 2020 (PMC5988228, "exercise-hypogonadal male condition") | Confounded by RED-S. Moderate endurance (3–5 h/wk) NOT suppressive. |
| **Sauna (Finnish, dry heat)** | 80–100°C, 20 min, 4×/wk | Acute cortisol/T spike; chronic baseline T **largely unchanged** in healthy men | Weeks | **[Cohort]** | Pilch 2014, *J Hum Kinet* 40:85 (acute changes); Laukkanen 2018 cardiovascular benefits | Sauna sells well as "T booster" — direct evidence weak. Indirect via stress reduction / sleep. |
| **Cold water exposure / ice baths** | 14°C × 15 min daily | Acute cortisol spike; T effect **null** in chronic studies | Weeks | **[1-Trial]** | Šrámek 2000 *Eur J Appl Physiol* 81:436 (cortisol/NE acute); no chronic T data | Marketed widely; specific T evidence absent. |
| **Stop heavy alcohol** | Cessation from ≥4 drinks/d baseline | Total T **+15–25%** recovery | 4–12 wk | **[Meta — extrapolated]** | Inferred from Santi 2024 (heavy drinkers vs. abstinent meta) | Real, replicable in addiction-medicine populations. |
| **Smoking** | Cessation from chronic smoker | Total T paradoxically **slightly higher** in current smokers (cross-sectional); cessation may slightly lower T | Months | **[Cohort]** | Wang 2013, *Andrology* 1:917 (NHANES) | Don't keep smoking for T — net cardiovascular harm dwarfs any T effect. |
| **Caffeine (chronic moderate)** | 200–400 mg/d | T effect modest, inconsistent across studies; small acute rise post-RT with caffeine | Acute | **[1-Trial]** | Beaven 2008 *Eur J Appl Physiol* 102:317 | Not a clinically meaningful T modulator. |
| **Hydration / dehydration** | Acute 2.5% body mass loss | Cortisol ↑, T ↓ acutely (~−5–10%) during dehydration | Hours | **[1-Trial]** | Judelson 2008 *J Appl Physiol* 105:816 | Easily corrected; pre-draw hydration matters. |
| **Time-restricted eating / 16:8** | 8-h feeding window | Total T modestly **decreased** in TRF studies of resistance-trained men (Moro 2016 −16% T) | 8 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Moro et al. 2016, *J Transl Med* 14:290; Stratton 2020 partial replication | Energy-deficit confounded; not a recommended T strategy in athletes. |
| **Prolonged fasting (>24 h)** | Multi-day fast | Acute T **−~20–30%** | Days | **[1-Trial]** | Cangemi 2010 cohort | Reverses on refeed. |
| **Sexual activity / abstinence** | 21-day abstinence | Single trial showed +~145% transient T spike on day 7 of abstinence (Jiang 2003); other trials null | Days | **[1-Trial]** | Jiang et al. 2003, *Zhonghua Nan Ke Xue* 9:467 | Tiny effect or noise; do not act on this. |
| **Stress reduction (meditation, breath work)** | 8-wk MBSR | T direct effect minimal; cortisol modest reduction → indirect modest T support | 8 wk | **[1-Trial]** | MacLean 1997 *Behav Med*; Khalsa 2008 | Effect small; primary value via cortisol/stress not T directly. |
| **Outdoor sun exposure (vit D pathway)** | Routine UV exposure | Indirect via vitamin D status; magnitude as in vit D trial | Months | **[Mechanism]** | Pilz 2011 + sun-exposure cohorts | Primary mechanism = D synthesis. |
| **Anabolic steroid use (raising)** | Any exogenous androgen | Total T supraphysiologic; LH/FSH suppressed; SHBG suppressed | Weeks | **[Mechanism]** | Universal observation | The **only** intervention that produces T levels >1000 ng/dL reliably. Comes with full HPG suppression, fertility loss, CV risk. |

## 5. Wearable-Trackable Metric Relationships

| Wearable metric | Direction of relationship | Magnitude / effect size | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sleep duration (nightly)** | Direct positive (up to ~7.5–8 h, then plateau) | T drops ~10–15% per 3 h chronic restriction | **[RCT]** | Leproult 2011 | Causal; tightest correlate |
| **Sleep regularity (variability index)** | Higher regularity → modestly higher T | Cross-sectional ~5–10% T difference between high/low SRI | **[Cohort]** | Lim 2024 *J Clin Sleep Med* (sleep regularity index cohort); UK Biobank actigraphy | Mechanism via circadian alignment |
| **REM sleep proportion** | Positive — REM coincides with morning T peak | LH pulses + T pulses cluster in REM | **[Mechanism]** | Luboshitzky 2001 *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 86:1134 | Strong mechanism, modest effect size |
| **Sleep onset time (chronotype)** | Late chronotype → modestly lower morning T cross-sectionally | ~5–10% delta | **[Cohort]** | Patterson 2017 cross-sectional | Confounded by overall lifestyle |
| **VO₂max** | Weak positive cross-sectionally | r = 0.10–0.15 with T | **[Cohort]** | Kraemer 2017 *Compr Physiol*; multiple aerobic studies | Modest; high VO₂max in endurance overtrainers reverses |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** (ceiling effect, magnitude, time):
  - In an obese man: **weight loss** to BMI <27 → +150–250 ng/dL over 6–12 months.
  - In a sleep-deprived man: **sleep extension to 8 h** → +10–15% in 1–2 weeks.
  - In an opioid user: **opioid cessation** → +200+ ng/dL.
  - In a deficient man (zinc/D/Mg): **deficiency correction** → +25–90% (dose-dependent on baseline deficit severity).
  - In a healthy lean man at upper-normal T: **no high-leverage intervention exists** — most "optimization" is marketing.

- **Best stack of 3** (additive, but with diminishing returns ~60–70% of linear sum):
  1. Sleep ≥7.5 h with regular schedule.
  2. Body composition target (≤18–20% body fat for healthy young man).
  3. Eliminate suppressors (alcohol ≤7 units/wk, no opioids, no chronic glucocorticoids, no AAS).
  - Add for deficient subset: vitamin D to 30–50 ng/mL; zinc to mid-normal RBC zinc.

- **Realistic 12-week delta — healthy lean 20yM at near-optimal baseline (T ~600–700 ng/dL):** +0 to +50 ng/dL with optimized lifestyle. Most variance is assay/diurnal noise.

- **Realistic 12-week delta — overweight man with elevated baseline confounders (T 320–450 ng/dL):** +100–250 ng/dL with sleep + 5–10 kg fat loss + alcohol cessation + deficiency repletion. Larger if obese (BMI ≥30).

- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:**
  - **Confirmed hypogonadism** (Endocrine Society 2018: total T <264 ng/dL on 2 morning fasted samples + symptoms) AFTER lifestyle optimization → consider TRT or SERM (clomid/enclomiphene).
  - Clomiphene 25 mg/d → +273 ng/dL (Huijben 2022 meta) — preserves fertility; first-line in young men wanting to preserve sperm.
  - Testosterone cypionate 100 mg/wk IM → trough 600–900 ng/dL — suppresses fertility.
  - HCG 500–1,000 IU 2–3×/wk → preserves intratesticular T, used adjunctively.
  - **Do NOT pharmacologically "boost" T in a healthy eugonadal man** (>300 ng/dL with no symptoms). Risks: erythrocytosis, suppressed fertility, lifelong dependence.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- South Asian men have **lower SHBG set-point** at any given total T vs. European-ancestry men — total T 350–450 ng/dL with normal free T is common and not pathologic. (McTernan 2007 PMID 17900299; Bhatt 2010 PMID 20550541.)
- **Always compute free T (Vermeulen)** before concluding low T in an SA man with borderline total T.
- Insulin resistance is more prevalent at lower BMI in SAs, suppressing SHBG further. SHBG <20 nmol/L in lean SA man is a **metabolic** flag, not a hormonal one.
- The Whittaker 2021 low-fat-diet meta showed smaller magnitude in East/South Asian men — diet-fat-fraction interventions less likely to move T meaningfully in SA men.
- Vitamin D deficiency is extremely common in SA populations (darker skin + low UV exposure); deficiency correction is one of few generalizable interventions with real evidence in this population.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- `SHBG rs6258` (Ser156Thr → lower SHBG, lower total T but normal free T)
- `SHBG rs12150660` (intron 1; affects SHBG set-point and free:total T)
- `CYP19A1` variants (aromatase activity → affect T→E2 conversion)
- `CYP17A1` (steroidogenesis rate-limiting)
- `HSD3B2` (steroidogenesis)
- `AR CAG repeat length` (longer CAG → less androgen-receptor sensitivity → compensatory higher T in some men)
- `LHCGR variants` (Leydig sensitivity to LH)

**Drug interactions:**
- Opioids → secondary hypogonadism (any µ-agonist; class effect)
- Glucocorticoids (oral, high-dose inhaled, intra-articular) → HPG suppression
- Anti-epileptics (phenytoin, carbamazepine, valproate) → SHBG ↑, free T ↓
- Ketoconazole (oral) → CYP17A1 inhibition → T ↓ acutely
- Spironolactone → AR antagonism (functional anti-androgen)
- Statins → modest T decrease
- SSRIs → modest T decrease in some; sexual side effects independent
- Cimetidine (high-dose) → AR antagonism
- Finasteride/dutasteride → DHT down 65–90%, compensatory T up
- Marijuana (heavy chronic) → modest T decrease

**Lifestyle confounders (assay/timing artifacts):**
- **Time of day:** T peaks 5:30–8 AM, drops 25–30% by evening. Always draw before 10 AM.
- **Fasting:** OGTT/glucose load drops T 25% acutely; fast 8–12 h before draw.
- **Acute illness/fever:** T transiently suppressed 30–50%; defer panel by 1–2 weeks post-illness.
- **Recent intense exercise:** acute spike or drop depending on volume; rest 24 h before draw.
- **Recent ejaculation:** small effect, probably ignorable.
- **Biotin** (≥5,000 µg/d): direct competitive immunoassays for T may show **falsely elevated** results. Hold biotin ≥48–72 h before draw. (AACC Guidance.)

**Assay considerations:**
- Direct immunoassay T overstates at low concentrations and cross-reacts with DHT, DHEA, androstenedione. **LC-MS/MS is gold standard** (Endocrine Society 2018, JCEM 103:1715).
- LabCorp 070282 = LC-MS/MS Total + Free (equilibrium dialysis send-out).
- Standard reference range 264–916 ng/dL (Travison 2017 harmonization).
- **Repeat any abnormal result** with second AM fasting draw before acting (Endocrine Society 2018) — ~30% of "low" first reads normalize on retest.

**Conditions that mimic low T:**
- Recent acute illness, surgery, fever
- Severe sleep restriction (transient)
- Intense exercise within 24 h (variable direction)
- High SHBG state (anorexia, hyperthyroidism, anti-epileptic) → low total T but normal free T
- AAS withdrawal (post-cycle, recovery phase)

**Conditions that mimic high T:**
- Biotin assay artifact
- Exogenous androgen use (history must be elicited)
- Leydig cell tumor (rare; testicular ultrasound if T persistently high without supplementation)
- Familial / idiopathic high-normal

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Vitamin D dose-response above replete:** Pilz 2011 showed +25% T at 12 mo in deficient men; whether D >50 ng/mL adds further T benefit is **not established** (negative trials in replete men).
- **Ashwagandha replicability:** All positive ashwagandha-T trials are <100 men, mostly industry-adjacent, run by overlapping authors. Awaiting independent large RCT.
- **Tongkat ali generalizability:** Effect size demonstrated in LOH/aging men; generalization to healthy young men lacks evidence.
- **Sauna / cold-exposure direct T effect:** Marketed widely; trial-quality evidence near-absent; effects probably indirect via sleep/stress.
- **Time-restricted eating in lean trained men:** Single trial showed T −16% (Moro 2016); whether reflects energy deficit or TRE per se unclear.
- **CBD / cannabinoids on T:** Mixed; chronic heavy cannabis modestly suppresses; light/occasional probably neutral or slight positive. RCT data weak.
- **Microplastic / phthalate exposure:** Cross-sectional negative T associations (Meeker 2010); intervention data absent.
- **Berberine / metformin in non-diabetic optimization:** Limited evidence on T in eugonadal non-diabetic men.
- **Whether "optimal" T differs by ancestry:** Travison harmonization combined European/American cohorts; SA-specific reference ranges underdeveloped.
