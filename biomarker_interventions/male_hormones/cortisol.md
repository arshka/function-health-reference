# AM Cortisol — Intervention Research

> Single-point AM serum cortisol has **CVI ~50%** — it is reliable for **ruling out adrenal insufficiency (<3 µg/dL) or screening Cushing's (>25 µg/dL)** but unreliable for fine "optimization" tracking. The dominant biology is the **diurnal rhythm**: peak ~30 min post-wake (Cortisol Awakening Response, CAR), nadir near midnight. Most "lifestyle interventions to lower cortisol" actually shift the **diurnal slope** (flatten or steepen) more than the AM peak; salivary cortisol × 4 timepoints is the better tool. The main modifiable AM-cortisol-lowering interventions are **sleep, alcohol cessation, glucocorticoid avoidance, and stress reduction**. Ashwagandha is the only supplement with consistent RCT-level cortisol-reduction evidence in chronically stressed populations (~−14–28%).

## Evidence-Grading Legend

Same as other files. **Reference range (8 AM serum cortisol):** ~5–25 µg/dL (138–690 nmol/L); commonly cited 6.7–22.6 µg/dL.

**Clinical thresholds:**
- AM cortisol >14–15 µg/dL essentially rules out adrenal insufficiency.
- AM cortisol <3 µg/dL suggests adrenal insufficiency.
- AM cortisol 3–14 µg/dL with symptoms → ACTH stim test.
- After 1 mg overnight dexamethasone suppression test (DST), AM cortisol <1.8 µg/dL rules out Cushing syndrome (sensitivity ~95%).

**Optimal AM cortisol for healthy 20yM:** 10–20 µg/dL with intact diurnal decline. **U-shaped** — both pathologically high and pathologically low are bad.

## 1. Foods that LOWER AM cortisol (or improve direction)

**Note on direction:** "Lowering cortisol" in eugonadal healthy men is not always a goal. The goal is **maintaining the proper diurnal pattern** (high AM, low evening). Most foods listed lower cortisol modestly under chronic stress — useful when cortisol is pathologically elevated, less relevant in healthy 20yM with normal AM cortisol.

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Fatty fish (omega-3 EPA/DHA)** | 250–500 mg EPA/DHA daily, or 2× weekly fatty fish | Cortisol modest **↓ ~10–20%** under stress; effect best documented in salivary cortisol response to acute stress test | 4–12 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Delarue 2003 *Diabetes Metab* 29:289 (n=7, fish oil + mental stress, cortisol response −20%); Madison 2021 *Mol Psychiatry* 26:3034 (CRP/IL-6 + stress reactivity) | Effect on stress-reactive cortisol > on baseline AM cortisol |
| **Dark chocolate (high cocoa, low sugar)** | 40 g 70%+ cocoa daily | Cortisol modest **↓** in chronically stressed populations; salivary cortisol AUC reduced ~15% | 2 wk | **[1-Trial]** | Wirtz 2014 *Br J Nutr* 111:653; Tsang 2019 systematic review | Cocoa flavanol mechanism; effect modest |
| **Mediterranean diet pattern** | High adherence | Modest cross-sectional cortisol-lowering vs. Western diet | Months | **[Cohort]** | Med-diet cortisol cohorts | Confounded |
| **Adequate carbohydrate (vs. severe restriction)** | ≥3 g/kg/d carbs | Prevents stress-induced cortisol elevation; cortisol ~10–15% lower vs. very low-carb diet | Days–weeks | **[1-Trial]** | Anderson 1987; Volek 2002 (KD vs higher carb) | Severe carb restriction transiently raises cortisol |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention** for elevated AM cortisol or disrupted diurnal pattern:
  - **Sleep extension to 7.5–8 h/night with consistent timing** — restores diurnal slope, normalizes evening cortisol.
  - **Identify and remove glucocorticoid exposure** for low AM cortisol.
  - **Alcohol cessation** for chronic heavy drinkers.
  - **Stress reduction (MBSR, breath, meditation)** for chronically stressed populations.
  - **Treat sleep apnea (CPAP)** if OSA contributing.

- **Best stack of 3** for cortisol normalization:
  1. Sleep ≥7.5 h, regular schedule, morning bright light exposure.
  2. Stress reduction practice (10+ min daily breath/meditation/yoga).
  3. Address modifiable confounders (alcohol ≤7 units/wk, no chronic glucocorticoids, manage caffeine timing).
  - Add for chronically stressed: ashwagandha 300–600 mg/d (the only consistent supplement) for 8–12 weeks.

- **Realistic 12-week delta — healthy lean 20yM with normal AM cortisol (10–20 µg/dL) and intact diurnal:** essentially stable. Single-point AM cortisol CVI ~50%, so values can shift ±20–30% randomly across draws.

- **Realistic 12-week delta — chronically stressed man with elevated AM cortisol (~25–30 µg/dL):** −3–5 µg/dL with stress reduction + sleep + lifestyle changes; ashwagandha may add −2–4 µg/dL.

- **Realistic 12-week delta — sleep-restricted shift worker:** sleep restoration → diurnal slope steepens within weeks; AM peak less variable.

- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:**
  - **Hydrocortisone replacement** for confirmed adrenal insufficiency (15–25 mg/d split AM/midday).
  - **Ketoconazole / metyrapone / mitotane** for confirmed Cushing's syndrome (specialist management).
  - **CPAP** for OSA-driven HPA dysregulation.
  - **Cabergoline** for prolactin-driven cortisol abnormalities (rare).

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- AM cortisol reference ranges are largely population-stable (5–25 µg/dL).
- South Asian populations may show higher diurnal cortisol variability and modestly higher AM cortisol in cross-sectional cohorts (cultural / urban stress confound).
- Vitamin D deficiency very common in SA — generally addressed for other reasons; specific cortisol effect minimal.
- **OSA is more prevalent at lower BMI in SA men** due to craniofacial structure — CPAP indication should be considered earlier in lean SA man with daytime sleepiness or morning headache + elevated AM cortisol.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- `NR3C1` (glucocorticoid receptor) variants — affect tissue glucocorticoid sensitivity; rare polymorphisms (e.g., ER22/23EK) → relative GC resistance.
- `CRH` and `CRHR1` variants — affect HPA reactivity to stress.
- `FKBP5` variants — modulate GR sensitivity; associated with stress-related disorders and altered cortisol response.
- `CYP11B1` — 11β-hydroxylase; congenital deficiency → CAH variant.
- `HSD11B2` (11β-HSD2) — variants affect tissue cortisol-cortisone conversion.

**Drug interactions (raise cortisol):**
- Alcohol (acute and chronic)
- Caffeine (acute, in caffeine-naive)
- Estrogens / OCPs (raise CBG → raise total cortisol with normal free)
- Pregnancy (raise CBG)
- Licorice (real, glycyrrhizin) — potentiates cortisol effect
- Some antidepressants (mirtazapine raises cortisol acutely)
- Sympathomimetics (pseudoephedrine, amphetamines)

**Drug interactions (lower endogenous cortisol):**
- Exogenous glucocorticoids (oral, intra-articular, high-dose inhaled, extensive topical)
- Ketoconazole (oral therapeutic)
- Etomidate (anesthetic)
- Mitotane (adrenolytic)
- Megestrol / cyproterone (high-dose, rare)
- Opioids (acute may raise; chronic effect variable)

**Pre-analytical / lifestyle confounders — CRITICAL:**
- **Time of draw** — biggest single factor. AM 8–9 AM = peak; values at noon or evening ≠ comparable.
- **Recent acute stress** (psychological or physical) — venipuncture itself can raise.
- Standard pre-draw guidance: 20–30 min seated rest, no caffeine ≥4 h, no intense exercise ≥24 h.
- **Recent meal** — large meal can cause modest postprandial rise.
- **Sleep deprivation, shift work, jet lag** — alter the diurnal pattern; AM may not represent normal peak.
- **Pregnancy** (irrelevant in men) raises CBG → raises total cortisol.
- **Estrogen therapy** raises CBG.
- **Infection / illness** raises cortisol.
- **Caffeine within 2–4 h** of draw modestly raises.
- **Hypoglycemia** raises cortisol acutely.
- **Strenuous exercise** within prior hour raises.
- **Glucocorticoid drugs** can suppress endogenous production for **weeks** after stopping.

**Assay considerations:**
- Standard immunoassay AM cortisol generally reliable for screening purposes.
- LC-MS/MS available for steroid panels.
- Salivary cortisol for diurnal pattern assessment (4 timepoints: wake, +30, noon, bedtime) — the best tool for HPA-axis dynamics, much more informative than single-point serum AM.
- **Within-person CVI ~50%** for serum cortisol — single value should NOT drive longitudinal "trend" decisions without controlling pre-draw conditions tightly.
- Biotin: high-dose biotin can interfere with some cortisol assays. Hold ≥48 h.

**Important pattern recognition:**
- **AM cortisol >25 µg/dL** + symptoms (truncal obesity, abdominal striae, easy bruising, glucose intolerance) → screen Cushing's: 1 mg overnight DST, late-night salivary × 2, or 24-h urine free cortisol × 2. Random AM serum alone ≠ Cushing's screening test.
- **AM cortisol <5 µg/dL** → highly suggestive of adrenal insufficiency or HPA suppression. ACTH stim test. Check for exogenous steroid exposure (oral/inhaled/topical/intra-articular history).
- **AM cortisol normal but symptoms of stress / fatigue** → consider salivary 4-point cortisol for diurnal slope assessment.
- **AM cortisol elevated + low ACTH** → consider primary adrenal hypercortisolism (adrenal adenoma) or exogenous glucocorticoid exposure.
- **AM cortisol low + elevated ACTH** → primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's).
- **AM cortisol low + low ACTH** → secondary or tertiary adrenal insufficiency.

**"Adrenal fatigue" framework note:**
The functional medicine framing of "cortisol slope" / cortisol awakening response / DUTCH test as a "stress phenotype" tool can be useful descriptively but: (a) salivary cortisol and DUTCH test results are NOT validated for diagnosing pathologic Cushing's or adrenal insufficiency; (b) "adrenal fatigue" diagnosis is **NOT recognized by mainstream endocrinology** (Endocrine Society explicitly rejected as a clinical entity in 2016 statement). Use multiple measurements (salivary CAR + diurnal slope) for HPA-axis dynamics, not single AM serum cortisol.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Single-point AM serum cortisol's CVI ~50%** — limits longitudinal tracking utility. Salivary 4-point or hair cortisol (chronic exposure marker) more informative for "stress phenotype" tracking.
- **Hair cortisol** as a 1–3 month chronic-exposure biomarker — emerging evidence supports it as a chronic stress marker but assay standardization variable.
- **DUTCH test** (dried urine total cortisol and metabolites) — popular in functional medicine; validation for clinical decision-making weaker than salivary 4-point.
- **Whether CAR augmentation by morning light exposure has long-term cardiometabolic benefit** — mechanistically plausible, RCT outcome data sparse.
- **Ashwagandha effect size in healthy low-stress 20yM** — effect mostly demonstrated in self-reported stressed populations; magnitude in healthy young men with normal cortisol likely smaller.
- **Phosphatidylserine effect on chronic AM cortisol vs. acute stress reactivity** — most data are on acute stress test response; chronic AM data sparse.
- **Whether stress-reduction intervention durably normalizes diurnal slope** — single short trials show effect; long-term (>1 year) maintenance data sparse.
- **Cortisol/DHEA-S ratio as longitudinal optimization marker** — biologically plausible; epigenetic clock literature supports; intervention RCTs sparse.
- **Whether mild AM cortisol elevations (~25–30 µg/dL) without Cushing's features have actionable clinical meaning** — debated; some evidence of cardiometabolic risk at upper end of normal range.
- **Intermittent fasting / TRE effect on cortisol pattern** — short trials show modest effects; long-term effect on diurnal architecture inconclusive.
- **Acute stress hormone response to exercise vs. baseline AM cortisol** — acute spikes are normal physiology; chronic baseline elevation = signal.
