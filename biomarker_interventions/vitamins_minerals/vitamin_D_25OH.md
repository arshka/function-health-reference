# Vitamin D (25-OH) — Intervention Research

> Vitamin D3 supplementation is the only reliable lever for raising 25-OH in temperate-latitude or pigmented-skin populations. Rule-of-thumb at deficient baseline: ~+1 ng/mL per 100 IU/d D3 (curvilinear — flatter at higher baseline). 2000 IU/d D3 raises 25-OH by ~15–20 ng/mL in 8–12 wk. D3 is ~1.7–2× more potent than D2 (Tripkovic 2012 meta). Sun is functionally unavailable to South Asian skin-type V at >35°N for ≥6 months/year. VITAL (n=25,871, 2000 IU/d D3) was null for cancer/CVD in a replete-baseline US population — supplementation is for sufficiency, not pharmacology.

## Evidence-Grading Legend

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[Meta]** | Replicated meta-analysis of ≥5 RCTs with consistent direction |
| **[RCT]** | Single high-quality RCT (≥100 participants or pivotal design) |
| **[Cohort]** | Prospective observational; can't establish causality |
| **[MR]** | Mendelian randomization |
| **[Mechanism]** | Pharmacology / physiology extrapolation |
| **[1-Trial]** | Single small trial worth knowing but not yet replicated |

## 1. Foods that RAISE 25-OH (improve direction)

Dietary vitamin D is *intrinsically limited*: even a maximalist Western diet provides only ~200–400 IU/d, vs. supplementation doses of 1000–4000 IU/d. The food table is largely informational — for repletion, supplementation is required.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| Fatty fish — sardines (canned with bones) | 100 g, 3×/wk | ~270 IU D3 per 100 g; 25-OH ↑ ~3–5 ng/mL marginally additive over months | 8–12 wk | **[Cohort]** | USDA FDC; observational dietary surveys | Mostly relevant as a regular diet piece, not as a repletion strategy |

## 2. Foods/exposures that LOWER 25-OH

| Food/exposure | Pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strict vegan + indoor lifestyle | No animal D, no UVB exposure | 25-OH typically <15 ng/mL without supplement | Months | **[Cohort]** | EPIC-Oxford vegan cohort (Crowe 2011 *Eur J Clin Nutr*, PMID 20354468) | Lichen-derived D3 supplements solve this; mechanism is intake/synthesis deficit |
| High BMI / obesity | BMI >30 vs <25 | 25-OH 5–10 ng/mL lower at any given intake; supplementation response 30–50% blunted | Sustained | **[Meta]** | Pereira-Santos 2015 *Obes Rev* (PMID 25688659); VITAL subgroup analysis | Volumetric dilution into adipose; obese individuals need 2–3× higher maintenance dose |
| Sunscreen SPF 15+ used daily | All-skin coverage | UVB-mediated D3 synthesis ↓ ~93–98% | Acute | **[RCT, Mechanism]** | Matsuoka 1987 *Arch Dermatol*; Holick 2007 *NEJM* | Real-world use is rarely complete coverage — net effect smaller; do not stop sunscreen for D status |

| Glucocorticoids (chronic prednisone ≥5 mg/d) | Sustained | ↑ catabolism via CYP24A1 induction; 25-OH and bioavailability ↓ | Months | **[Cohort]** | Skversky 2011 *JCEM* (PMID 21795452) | Add to baseline osteoporosis risk on chronic steroids — D + Ca standard of care |

| HIV antiretrovirals (efavirenz) | Chronic | Induce CYP24A1 → 25-OH ↓ 5–10 ng/mL | Months | **[Cohort]** | Welz 2010 *AIDS*; Brown 2010 *AIDS Reader* | Higher D dose needed in HIV+ on EFV regimens |

## 3. Supplements

### Improving / repleting

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cholecalciferol (D3) | 1000 IU/d | 25-OH ↑ ~8–12 ng/mL from baseline ~15 ng/mL | 8–12 wk | **[Meta]** | Heaney 2003 *Am J Clin Nutr*; Gallagher 2014 *JCEM* (PMID 24878432) | Curvilinear: baseline 10 ng/mL → +11 ng/mL; baseline 30 ng/mL → +8 ng/mL |
| Cholecalciferol (D3) | 2000 IU/d | 25-OH ↑ ~15–20 ng/mL at steady state | 10–14 wk | **[Meta]** | EU meta of 49 trials (*Nutrients* 2023, PMID 37111223): pooled +36 nmol/L (~14.4 ng/mL); VITAL pharmacokinetics | This is the IOM upper end of "tolerable" daily and the practical maintenance dose for most adults |
| Cholecalciferol (D3) | 4000 IU/d | 25-OH ↑ ~25–35 ng/mL | 12–16 wk | **[RCT]** | Garland 2011 *Anticancer Res*; Heaney dose-response | IOM UL for adults; Endocrine Society 10,000 IU/d UL for short-term repletion |
| Endocrine Society loading | 50,000 IU D3 weekly × 8 wk → 1500–2000 IU/d maintenance | 25-OH ↑ ~30–40 ng/mL from deficient baseline; final mean ~47 ng/mL | 8 wk | **[GL, RCT]** | Endocrine Society *JCEM* 2011 (PMID 21646368); PMC2683376 | Fastest reliable repletion protocol; standard clinical practice |
| Ergocalciferol (D2) | Same IU as D3 | 25-OH response ~50–60% of equivalent D3 dose | Same | **[Meta]** | Tripkovic 2012 *Am J Clin Nutr* (PMID 22552031) — D3 was 87% more potent than D2 | US prescription default is D2 50,000 IU; pharmacy substitution may reduce response. Request D3 explicitly. |
| Calcifediol (25-OH-D3) | 0.266 mg/wk (oral) | Bypasses hepatic 25-hydroxylation; raises 25-OH 2–3× faster than equivalent D3 dose | 4–6 wk | **[RCT]** | Quesada-Gomez 2018 *J Steroid Biochem* (PMID 28412412); Pérez-Castrillón 2021 (PMID 34036430) | Useful in malabsorption, hepatic disease, or when fast repletion needed; available in EU; rarely used in US |
| Calcitriol (1,25-(OH)₂-D3) | 0.25–0.5 µg/d (Rx) | Active hormone, bypasses both hydroxylations; does NOT raise serum 25-OH; raises serum Ca | Days | **[RCT]** | Standard renal pharmacology | Reserved for CKD/hypoparathyroidism; not for 25-OH optimization; high hypercalcemia risk |
| D3 + magnesium glycinate | D3 + 250–400 mg Mg/d | Magnesium is a cofactor for CYP2R1 (25-hydroxylase) and CYP27B1 (1α-hydroxylase); deficiency blunts D3 response | Concurrent | **[RCT — modest]** | Deng 2018 *Am J Clin Nutr* (PMID 29794773); Uwitonze 2018 *J Am Osteopath Assoc* | Effect size not well-quantified; relevant if Mg simultaneously deficient (common in SA, low-dairy diets) |
| D3 + vitamin K2 (MK-7) | D3 + 100–200 µg/d MK-7 | Does NOT change 25-OH response. | D3 + calcium combo supplement | 1000 IU D + 1000–1200 mg Ca/d | 25-OH ↑ as expected from D3 alone; CV-event safety: WHI 22-yr follow-up (2024) showed 6% higher CV death (borderline sig) in Ca+D arm | Years | **[Meta, RCT]** | WHI long-term (Cauley 2024 *Ann Intern Med*); Bolland 2010 *BMJ* (PMID 20671013) | Take Ca with food, split doses, prefer dietary Ca; D3 alone does not carry the signal |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention:** Oral D3 2000 IU/d for maintenance, 50,000 IU/wk × 8 wk for repletion from deficient baseline. This is the *only* reliable lever for SA and temperate-latitude populations.
- **Best stack of 3:**
  1. D3 2000–4000 IU/d (the dominant lever)
  2. Magnesium glycinate 250–400 mg/d (cofactor for hydroxylation; commonly co-deficient)
  3. Outdoor activity in summer when latitude/skin type allows (additive 2–5 ng/mL)
  - *(K2 MK-7 100 µg/d is reasonable as a 4th if also supplementing calcium, but evidence for hard outcomes is null/mixed.)*
- **Realistic 12-week delta for healthy 20yM at near-optimal baseline (25-OH ~30 ng/mL):** +5–10 ng/mL with 2000 IU/d D3.
- **Realistic 12-week delta for someone with deficient baseline (25-OH <20 ng/mL):** +20–30 ng/mL with loading dose protocol; +15–20 ng/mL with 2000 IU/d steady; +25–35 ng/mL with 4000 IU/d.
- **Where pharmacotherapy becomes necessary:** Severe malabsorption (post-bariatric, celiac with persistent enteropathy, cystic fibrosis), CKD (use calcitriol/alfacalcidol — active forms bypass renal 1α-hydroxylase failure), or severe hepatic disease (use calcifediol to bypass 25-hydroxylation).

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- **Near-universal deficiency in SA populations regardless of latitude.** UK Biobank analysis of 6,433 SA adults: 72% with 25-OH <10 ng/mL in winter; >80% deficient (<12 ng/mL) overall **[Cohort — PMC7844605]**. Indian urban cohort data (Marwaha 2005 *Am J Clin Nutr*): mean 25-OH 8–10 ng/mL in young SA adults despite tropical latitude — driven by pigmentation, dress, indoor work, low dairy intake.
- **Dosing implication:** SA individuals need approximately *double* the D3 dose vs European-descent adults to achieve the same 25-OH target. Empirical maintenance for healthy SA adult: 2000–4000 IU/d D3.
- **Magnesium co-deficiency is common** in low-dairy, low-leafy-green SA diets — co-supplement Mg 250–400 mg/d glycinate when starting D3.
- **GC gene variants** (rs4588, rs7041) common in SA — affect vitamin D binding protein → altered free/bioavailable 25-OH at same total 25-OH. May warrant targeting upper-normal range (40–50 ng/mL) rather than just "≥30 ng/mL."

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **GC rs4588, rs7041** (vitamin D-binding protein) — T allele of rs4588 binds 25-OH more tightly → lower bioavailable D at same total 25-OH; carriers may need higher target.
- **CYP2R1 rs10741657** — hepatic 25-hydroxylase; minor allele → less efficient conversion of D3 to 25-OH; need higher dose. **[GWAS — Wang 2010 *Lancet* (PMID 20541252)]**
- **DHCR7 rs12785878** — affects skin 7-dehydrocholesterol → D3 conversion under UV; minor allele lowers cutaneous synthesis efficiency.
- **VDR rs2228570 (FokI), rs1544410 (BsmI), rs731236 (TaqI)** — receptor variants; tissue-level D activity differs even at same circulating 25-OH. Clinical implication uncertain.
- **CYP24A1 loss-of-function** — rare; impairs catabolism → can cause hypercalcemia at standard D doses.

**Drug interactions:**
- **Anticonvulsants (phenytoin, phenobarbital, carbamazepine, primidone)** — induce 25-OH catabolism → 30–50% lower 25-OH at same intake. Need 2–3× standard dose.
- **Glucocorticoids (chronic ≥5 mg prednisone)** — induce CYP24A1 catabolism + impair calcium absorption.
- **Cholestyramine, colestipol, orlistat** — bind dietary fat → block fat-soluble vitamin absorption. Take D ≥2 h apart.
- **Mineral oil laxatives** — same mechanism.
- **Thiazide diuretics + high-dose D + Ca** — risk of hypercalcemia (thiazides reduce renal Ca excretion).
- **Digoxin + D-induced hypercalcemia** — increases digoxin toxicity risk.
- **HIV antiretrovirals (efavirenz)** — induce CYP24A1 → lower 25-OH.
- **Statins** — no consistent effect on 25-OH (some early reports of statin-mediated D production were not replicated).

**Lifestyle confounders:**
- **Adiposity** — BMI >30 → 25-OH 5–10 ng/mL lower; supplement response 30–50% blunted.
- **Skin pigmentation** — Fitzpatrick V/VI need 3–5× UVB exposure for same synthesis; in temperate latitudes essentially relies on supplement.
- **Sunscreen use** — SPF 15+ blocks 93–98% of UVB-mediated D synthesis (theoretical; real-world coverage is incomplete, so net effect smaller).
- **Latitude** — >35° N or S, no D3 synthesis Oct–Mar regardless of skin type.
- **Indoor occupation** — eliminates incidental UVB exposure even in summer.

**Timing artifacts:**
- 25-OH has a long half-life (~2–3 weeks) — single-day fluctuations are small. No diurnal variation.
- Acute illness can suppress 25-OH ("anergic" inflammation effect, ~10–20% lower); re-test when well.
- Recent loading dose can transiently elevate 25-OH for weeks.
- Assay variability: LC-MS/MS is gold standard; immunoassays underestimate D2, sometimes overestimate D3 — switching labs can produce 5–15% spurious changes.

**Acute illness / inflammation:**
- COVID, sepsis, acute trauma transiently lower 25-OH 5–15 ng/mL — re-test after recovery.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **The "optimal" 25-OH target.** Endocrine Society 2011 said ≥30 ng/mL; IOM 2011 said ≥20 ng/mL is sufficient for skeletal endpoints; Endocrine Society 2024 update stepped back from a hard 30 ng/mL target and now provides population-specific guidance. Mortality U-shape data (Durup 2012 PMC3413162) suggest very high 25-OH (>100 ng/mL) may not be benign. Personal optimum likely 30–50 ng/mL; pushing >60 ng/mL has no proven benefit.
- **Vitamin K2 (MK-7) co-supplementation for arterial calcification prevention.** Mechanistically appealing (matrix Gla protein activation), but the AVADEC RCT (Lindholt 2024, n=389, 720 µg MK-7 + 25 µg D over 24 mo) was *negative* for aortic valve calcification progression. The K2 + D combo lowered dp-ucMGP (the intermediate biomarker) but did not move the hard imaging endpoint. The evidence for K2 reducing vascular calcification is weaker than commonly marketed.
- **VITAL paradox.** Manson 2019 (n=25,871, 2000 IU/d D3 × 5 yr): null for primary cancer, CVD, fracture endpoints in a generally-replete US population. Subgroup analyses suggested possible benefit in low-baseline subjects but were exploratory. Interpretation: D supplementation works to *raise 25-OH* and prevent deficiency syndromes; it does not appear to be a primary CV/cancer prevention agent in already-replete populations.
- **Vitamin D and respiratory infection.** Martineau 2017 *BMJ* (PMID 28202713; IPD meta of 25 RCTs, n=10,933): daily/weekly D supplementation reduced acute respiratory infection (OR 0.88; 95% CI 0.81–0.96), with the strongest effect in deficient subjects (<10 ng/mL: OR 0.30). Bolus dosing was *not* protective. Subsequent updates (Jolliffe 2021 *Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol*, PMID 33798465) attenuated the effect in larger pooled data; Martineau 2024 update further refined. Effect is real but modest.
- **MTHFR-style "you must take K2 with D3" claims.** Marketing-driven; the human RCT data don't support a meaningful K2 *requirement* for D3 to be effective at raising 25-OH or for skeletal/CV outcomes. K2 at 100–200 µg/d MK-7 is safe and mechanistically reasonable but not essential.
- **Calcifediol vs cholecalciferol for routine repletion.** Calcifediol raises 25-OH 2–3× faster but is more expensive and sparingly available in the US. For most patients, oral D3 is adequate. Calcifediol is the right choice in malabsorption, hepatic disease, or when very fast repletion is needed.
- **D2 vs D3 in real-world adherence.** D2 is the US prescription default (50,000 IU ergocalciferol). It works — just less efficiently per IU. If a patient is on prescription D2 and tolerating it, switching to D3 may raise 25-OH ~25–40% with no other change.
- **K2 forms (MK-4 vs MK-7).** MK-7 has longer half-life (3 days vs 1 hour); most modern RCTs use MK-7. MK-4 (high-dose 45 mg/d) is the Japanese osteoporosis treatment dose — different evidence base.
