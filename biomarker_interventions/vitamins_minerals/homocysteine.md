# Homocysteine — Intervention Research

> Folic acid 0.5–5 mg/d lowers Hcy ~25%; folic acid + B12 lowers ~33% (HCLT 1998 meta of 12 RCTs, n=1114). Riboflavin 1.6 mg/d adds another 2.2 µmol/L reduction in MTHFR C677T TT homozygotes (McNulty 2006 *Circulation*). Betaine (TMG) 3–6 g/d adds 10–20% on top. **The CV paradox: despite reliably lowering Hcy, B-vitamin therapy does NOT reduce CV events** (HOPE-2, NORVIT, VITATOPS, SEARCH, FACIT — Clarke 2010 meta of 8 RCTs, n=37,485: no CV/cancer/mortality benefit). MR data (Yuan 2021 *BMC Med*) suggest a small causal effect on stroke (OR ~1.11/SD) but not coronary disease. Best framing: treat elevated Hcy as a *signal* of B-vitamin or methylation status inadequacy, NOT as a CV target.

## 1. Foods that LOWER Hcy

Hcy is lowered by interventions that supply folate, B12, B6, riboflavin (B2), betaine (trimethylglycine), and choline — i.e., the methylation/transsulfuration cofactors.

| Food | Dose / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Leafy greens (spinach, asparagus, broccoli) | 2 servings/d | Hcy ↓ 1–2 µmol/L over 4–8 wk in folate-deficient adults | 4–8 wk | **[Cohort, RCT]** | Multiple Mediterranean diet RCTs; Bønaa NORVIT supportive data | Mechanism: dietary folate; works only if baseline folate is suboptimal |
| Beef liver | 100 g, weekly | Folate + B12 + B6 in single serving; Hcy ↓ measurable in deficient individuals | 4–8 wk | **[Food composition]** | USDA FDC | Vitamin A burden — limit to weekly |

## 3. Supplements

### Lowering

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Folic acid | 0.5–5 mg/d | Hcy ↓ ~25% (3 µmol/L from baseline ~12 µmol/L) | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | Homocysteine Lowering Trialists' Collaboration — *BMJ* 1998 (PMID 9569395, n=1114, 12 RCTs) | Effect plateaus near 0.5 mg/d; higher doses do not produce meaningfully more Hcy reduction |
| Folic acid + vitamin B12 | 0.5–5 mg FA + 0.5 mg B12 | Hcy ↓ ~33% (12 → ~8–9 µmol/L) | 4–8 wk | **[Meta]** | HCLT 1998 (PMID 9569395) | B12 contributes additional ~7% beyond folate alone |
| L-methylfolate (5-MTHF) | 400–1000 µg/d | Hcy reduction comparable to equivalent folic acid in CC/CT; possibly modestly better in TT | 4–8 wk | **[RCT]** | Anderson 2013 (PMID 23456769); Lamers 2006; Greek RCT 2024 (PMID 38056998) | The "MTHFR demands methylfolate" claim is overstated for hard outcomes |
| Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine, P5P) | 16.5 mg/d | No additional Hcy reduction beyond folate ± B12 in fasting Hcy | — | **[Meta]** | HCLT 1998 (PMID 9569395) | B6 is relevant for the *methionine-load* response (transsulfuration via CBS), not fasting Hcy |
| Riboflavin (B2) | 1.6 mg/d (in MTHFR 677TT) | Hcy ↓ additional ~2.2 µmol/L specifically in MTHFR TT homozygotes who are B2-deficient | 12 wk | **[RCT]** | McNulty 2006 *Circulation* (PMID 16380552); McNulty 2017 *Mol Aspects Med* | B2 is FAD cofactor for MTHFR; deficiency in TT individuals amplifies Hcy. Not relevant if B2 replete |
| Betaine (trimethylglycine, TMG) | 3–6 g/d | Hcy ↓ ~10–20% via BHMT remethylation; additive to folate | 4–8 wk | **[RCT]** | Olthof 2003 *Am J Clin Nutr* (PMID 12936924); Schwab 2002 *Eur J Clin Nutr* | Useful adjunct in MTHFR TT or refractory Hcy elevation; effect partly independent of folate/B12 status |
| Choline (precursor to betaine) | 500–2000 mg/d | Indirect Hcy reduction via betaine pathway; smaller direct effect | 8–12 wk | **[RCT]** | Olthof 2005 *Am J Clin Nutr* | Choline at high doses raises TMAO (microbial metabolite); CV-relevance contested |
| Multivitamin (folate-containing) | Standard B-complex | Hcy ↓ 5–15% in mildly deficient adults | 4–8 wk | **[RCT]** | NHANES post-fortification; multiple MVI trials | Maintenance-level intervention |
| N-acetylcysteine (NAC) | 600–1800 mg/d | Hcy ↓ ~25% in some uremic / CKD trials; provides cysteine, may reduce Hcy via direct disulfide exchange | Days–weeks | **[RCT]** | Ventura 1999 *Am J Clin Nutr*; CKD-specific data | Mostly studied in CKD/dialysis; effect in healthy adults less robust |

### Forms / claims with weak or no support

| Claim | Reality | Source |
|---|---|---|
| "Lower Hcy to prevent heart attack" | RCTs (HOPE-2 n=5522; NORVIT n=3749; VITATOPS n=8164; SEARCH n=12,064) lowered Hcy 25–33% but did NOT reduce CV mortality or major events. | Clarke 2010 *Arch Intern Med* meta of 8 RCTs n=37,485 (PMID 20937919); HOPE-2 *NEJM* 2006 (PMID 16531614) |
| "Mega-dose B-vitamin therapy is the cure for elevated Hcy" | Effect plateaus near standard supplemental doses; mega-dose adds little Hcy reduction and raises Brasky 2017 lung cancer signal in male smokers | Brasky 2017 *J Clin Oncol* (PMID 28829668) |
| "Methylfolate is required for any MTHFR variant" | Modest folate-status difference; folic acid still works to lower Hcy in TT | CDC; Anderson 2013 |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention:** Folic acid 400–800 µg/d (or methylfolate equivalent). Achieves ~25% Hcy reduction within 4–8 weeks. Add B12 1000 µg/d for additional ~7% reduction.
- **Best stack of 3:**
  1. Folic acid 400–800 µg/d (or L-methylfolate if MTHFR TT genotype known)
  2. Vitamin B12 1000 µg/d (oral cyano- or methylcobalamin)
  3. For MTHFR 677TT specifically: add riboflavin 1.6 mg/d (additional ~2.2 µmol/L) and consider betaine 3 g/d for refractory cases
- **Realistic 12-week delta from baseline ~12 µmol/L (typical Western mid-elevated):** Down to ~8 µmol/L on folate + B12.
- **Realistic 12-week delta from baseline ~20 µmol/L (deficient):** Down to ~10 µmol/L on folate + B12 + B2 (if TT).
- **Where pharmacotherapy / Rx becomes necessary:**
  - Severe Hcy elevation (>30 µmol/L) refractory to standard B vitamins → check for B12-mutase inborn error, CBS deficiency (homocystinuria), severe renal disease
  - Homocystinuria (CBS deficiency) — Rx pyridoxine 100–500 mg/d; if non-responsive, low-methionine diet + betaine 6 g/d ± cysteine
  - CKD stage 4–5 — folate + B12 supplementation lowers Hcy partially but does not normalize; main intervention is renal replacement

**Critical framing note:** Hcy reduction with B vitamins improves the *biochemical* marker but does **NOT reduce CV events** in unselected populations. The clinical value of Hcy lowering lies in:
1. Confirming adequate B vitamin status
2. Possible small reduction in stroke risk (MR data; HOPE-2 stroke subgroup signal)
3. Possible slowing of brain atrophy in mild cognitive impairment with elevated Hcy (Smith 2010, VITACOG, n=271)
4. Resolving symptoms of underlying B12 / folate deficiency

It is NOT a CV-prevention strategy. The recommendation engine must not over-promise here.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- Vegetarian Indian populations have higher mean Hcy (often 15–25 µmol/L) due to widespread B12 deficiency. Refsum 2001 (PMID 11470727): mean Hcy in Indian Hindu vegetarians substantially higher than meat-eaters; correctable with B12 supplementation.
- MTHFR C677T T allele frequency is *lower* in SA populations (~5–10%) than European (~30%) — the methylfolate question is even less pressing.
- However, MTHFR A1298C is more variable across SA subpopulations.
- Yajnik Pune Maternal Nutrition Study: high folate + low B12 in pregnant SA women is associated with offspring metabolic risk — argues for combined folate + B12 supplementation in vegetarian SA women planning pregnancy.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **MTHFR C677T (rs1801133)** — TT homozygotes: +2–3 µmol/L higher Hcy; especially elevated if B2 or folate co-deficient.
- **MTHFR A1298C (rs1801131)** — partial activity loss; smaller effect.
- **CBS (cystathionine β-synthase)** — homozygous LoF causes homocystinuria (severe Hcy >100 µmol/L, ectopia lentis, marfanoid habitus, thrombosis). Rare. Heterozygotes have mildly elevated Hcy.
- **MTRR rs1801394** — methionine synthase reductase; affects B12 cofactor cycling.
- **MTR rs1805087** — methionine synthase activity.
- **TCN2 variants** — transcobalamin 2 (B12 transport).
- **BHMT, BHMT2** — betaine remethylation pathway; modulate response to betaine supplementation.
- **CTH (cystathionine γ-lyase)** — transsulfuration pathway.

**Drug interactions / drugs that RAISE Hcy:**
- **Methotrexate** — folate antagonist; routine MTX use raises Hcy ~5–15%.
- **Phenytoin, phenobarbital, primidone, carbamazepine** — antiepileptics deplete folate.
- **Sulfasalazine, trimethoprim, pyrimethamine, triamterene** — folate antagonists.
- **Metformin** — long-term use lowers B12 → raises Hcy modestly.
- **PPIs, H2 blockers** — long-term use lowers B12 → raises Hcy.
- **Levodopa** — methylation of L-dopa consumes methyl groups → raises Hcy (Müller 2014); may benefit from methylfolate + B12 + B6 in Parkinson's patients on levodopa.
- **Theophylline, fibrates (fenofibrate)** — modest Hcy elevation.
- **Cyclosporine** — raises Hcy in transplant patients.
- **Niacin (high-dose 1500–3000 mg)** — raises Hcy modestly.

**Lifestyle confounders:**
- **Heavy alcohol** — major Hcy elevator (folate + B12 + B6 depletion).
- **Smoking** — modest Hcy ↑.
- **Heavy coffee (>6 cups/d)** — modest Hcy ↑.
- **Vegetarian/vegan diet** — Hcy elevation via low B12 is the dominant pattern.
- **Aging** — Hcy rises ~1–2 µmol/L per decade after age 40, partly from declining renal function and atrophic gastritis.
- **Hypothyroidism** — Hcy ↑ ~2–3 µmol/L in untreated hypothyroidism; resolves with levothyroxine.
- **Renal insufficiency (eGFR <60)** — Hcy ↑ 1–3 µmol/L per 10 mL/min decline; CKD5 typically Hcy >20 µmol/L. B vitamins partially lower but do not normalize.

**Timing/assay artifacts:**
- **Fasting Hcy** is the standard; non-fasting can be slightly higher.
- **Recent methionine-rich meal** can raise Hcy 20–30% acutely — fast 8 h before draw.
- **Hemolysis** falsely elevates Hcy as RBCs leak intracellular Hcy.
- **Sample handling** — Hcy continues to be released from RBCs in unprocessed blood at room temperature; specimen must be centrifuged within 1 h of draw or kept cold to avoid spuriously high readings.
- **Recent vitamin supplementation** lowers Hcy by 5–25% — record current supplements at time of draw.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **The Hcy-CVD paradox.** Observational data for decades showed elevated Hcy as a CV risk factor; MR data suggest modest causal contribution to stroke specifically. Yet B-vitamin RCTs that lowered Hcy by 25–33% did NOT reduce CV events. Most likely explanation: Hcy is a *marker* of broader vascular and methylation dysfunction, not a primary driver. Aggressive Hcy lowering without addressing the underlying biology produces no CV benefit. The recommendation engine must communicate: lowering Hcy is good for confirming nutrient adequacy, not for preventing heart attacks.
- **Stroke specifically.** HOPE-2 secondary analysis showed possible stroke risk reduction (RR 0.75 in non-fatal stroke) with B-vitamin therapy. Mendelian randomization (Yuan 2021 *BMC Med*) showed Hcy causally raises ischemic stroke risk (OR ~1.11/SD) but not coronary disease. The stroke signal is modest and population-dependent.
- **Cognitive decline.** VITACOG (Smith 2010 PLOS, n=271) showed B vitamins (folate + B12 + B6) slowed brain atrophy ~30% over 2 yr in elderly with mild cognitive impairment AND elevated Hcy. Subsequent trials in unselected MCI populations (Chen 2016) less clear. The signal is real but only in elevated-Hcy subjects.
- **Optimal Hcy target for healthy adults.** "Normal" upper limit varies by lab (12–15 µmol/L). Many functional medicine practitioners target <8 µmol/L; this is achievable with adequate folate + B12 in young healthy adults but the evidence for incremental health benefit below 8 µmol/L is absent.
- **Methylfolate vs folic acid vs folinic acid for Hcy.** Greek RCT 2024 (PMID 38056998) suggested folinic acid > methylfolate for Hcy reduction in CT genotype. Larger-scale comparisons absent. The form of folate is much less important than total folate adequacy.
- **Betaine supplementation outside MTHFR TT or homocystinuria.** Effective in lowering Hcy but no RCT evidence for hard outcomes. Useful adjunct for refractory Hcy elevation.
- **Coffee–Hcy relationship.** Mechanism debated; chlorogenic acid is plausible mediator. Effect modest; no need to recommend coffee restriction to general adults.
