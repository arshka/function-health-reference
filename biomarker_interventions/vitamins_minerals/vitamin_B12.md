# Vitamin B12 (Serum Cobalamin) — Intervention Research

> Animal foods are essentially the only natural source. Oral cyanocobalamin or methylcobalamin ≥500–1000 µg/d is equivalent to IM injection for normalizing serum B12 and MMA in non-pernicious-anemia deficiency (Vidal-Alaball Cochrane 2005; Kuzminski 1998 *Blood*). Mass-action diffusion absorbs ~1–2% of any oral dose independent of intrinsic factor — at 1000 µg/d, that's ~10–20 µg absorbed daily, adequate even for IF-deficient patients in most cases. Methylcobalamin's clinical superiority over cyanocobalamin in healthy adults is not supported. Metformin and chronic PPIs lower B12 absorption ~10–30%.

## 1. Foods that RAISE B12

B12 is synthesized only by bacteria/archaea. Plants contain essentially zero bioactive B12. Animal products concentrate microbial B12 from gut/rumen synthesis. Dose context: RDA = 2.4 µg/d adults; bioavailability of food B12 is ~50% (limited by intrinsic factor saturation per meal at ~1.5–2 µg).

| Clams | 100 g cooked | ~84 µg B12 | Per meal | **[Food composition]** | USDA FDC | Among the highest seafood B12 |
| Sardines (canned) | 100 g | ~9 µg B12 | Per meal | **[Food composition]** | USDA FDC | Excellent regular source; also rich in D, omega-3, calcium |

| Food / pattern | Direction & magnitude | Time to effect | Evidence | Source | Caveats |

## 3. Supplements

### Improving / repleting

| Supplement | Dose | Direction & magnitude | Time to peak | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Oral cyanocobalamin | 1000 µg/d | Serum B12 ↑ from <200 pg/mL to >400 pg/mL within 4 wk; MMA ↓ ~33% from baseline (Eussen dose-finding) | 2–4 wk (serum); 4–8 wk (MMA) | **[RCT]** | Kuzminski 1998 *Blood* (n=33, oral 2000 µg/d ≡ IM 1000 µg/mo for serum B12 and MMA at 4 mo); Eussen 2005 *Arch Intern Med* (PMID 15911731) | Mass-action diffusion absorbs ~1–2% of dose independent of intrinsic factor; at 1000 µg/d, 10–20 µg/d absorbed — adequate for repletion in non-pernicious deficiency |
| Oral cyanocobalamin | 500 µg/d | MMA ↓ ~33% from deficient baseline; serum B12 normalizes | 8–16 wk | **[RCT]** | Eussen 2005 (PMID 15911731) — dose-finding trial in 120 older adults at doses 2.5, 100, 250, 500, 1000 µg | Optimal cost-benefit dose for older adults with mild deficiency |
| Oral cyanocobalamin | 100 µg/d | MMA ↓ ~16–23%; partial normalization only | 12–16 wk | **[RCT]** | Eussen 2005 | Standard "geriatric" supplement dose; insufficient for repletion from frank deficiency |
| Oral cyanocobalamin | 6–25 µg/d (multivitamin dose) | Maintains adequacy if baseline replete; insufficient for repletion | Months | **[RCT]** | Multiple multivitamin trials | This is what most multivitamins contain — maintenance only |
| Oral methylcobalamin | 1000–2000 µg/d | Serum B12 and MMA response equivalent to cyanocobalamin in healthy individuals | Same | **[Meta]** | Network meta-analysis PMC11128391 (2024); comprehensive review PMC12681447 (2025) | The methylcobalamin "more bioavailable/active" marketing is not supported in healthy adults; both are dephosphorylated and re-synthesized intracellularly to active forms |

## 5. Wearable-Trackable Metric Relationships

B12 status is **not directly trackable via wearables**. Indirect signals:

| Wearable metric | Direction | Magnitude | Evidence | Source | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Heart rate / HRV in severe B12 deficiency | Tachycardia from anemia; HRV reduced if neurological involvement (autonomic) | Variable | **[Mechanism]** | Standard megaloblastic anemia clinical features | Late-stage; not a screening signal |
| Sleep quality | Possibly worse in B12 deficiency (mood, fatigue, restless legs) | Weak association | **[Cohort]** | Some sleep clinics screen B12 in restless legs syndrome (Allen 2013 *Sleep Med Clin*) | Not specific |
| Step count / activity | Reduced when B12 deficiency causes fatigue, paresthesias, gait ataxia | Late stage | **[Cohort]** | — | Not useful for early detection |

The clinical signal for B12 deficiency is in lab values and symptom history (paresthesias, gait imbalance, glossitis, fatigue, mood/cognitive changes), not in wearable data.

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention:** Oral cyanocobalamin or methylcobalamin 1000 µg/d. This dose reliably repletes deficiency in non-pernicious-anemia patients via mass-action diffusion.
- **Best stack of 3:**
  1. Oral B12 1000 µg/d (the dominant lever)
  2. Concurrent folate adequacy (avoid B12-deficiency masking by high-dose folate alone — see Lindenbaum 1988)
  3. Address the upstream driver: dietary animal foods OR ongoing supplement, plus address any contributing meds (PPI, metformin)
- **Realistic 12-week delta for healthy 20yM at adequate baseline (>400 pg/mL):** No meaningful change with supplementation; no deficit to correct.
- **Realistic 12-week delta for someone with deficient baseline (<200 pg/mL):** Serum B12 → 400–800 pg/mL within 4–8 wk on 1000 µg/d oral; MMA normalizes by 8–12 wk if no renal disease.
- **Where pharmacotherapy / IM becomes necessary:** Pernicious anemia (autoimmune IF deficiency) — most guidelines still default to IM 1000 µg cyano or hydroxo (q monthly maintenance), though Vidal-Alaball Cochrane 2005 found oral 1000–2000 µg/d equivalent. Acute neurologic deficiency (subacute combined degeneration, optic atrophy) — IM preferred for fastest tissue loading, then oral maintenance. Total gastrectomy or terminal ileal resection — IM preferred. Severe malabsorption (Crohn's with extensive ileal involvement). Nitrous oxide–induced acute neurological deficit — IM hydroxocobalamin urgently.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- **B12 deficiency prevalence in vegetarian Indian populations: 47–70%** **[Cohort — Antony 2003 *Am J Clin Nutr* (PMID 12816768); Refsum 2001 *Am J Clin Nutr* (PMID 11470727); Pasricha 2008]**
- Even non-vegetarian SA populations have ~20–30% deficiency due to low overall meat consumption and processing/cooking effects (Yajnik 2014).
- **Practical rule:** Any SA male eating predominantly vegetarian diet should supplement B12 regardless of serum B12 — serum B12 misses functional deficiency (check MMA), and B12 has no known toxicity at supplemental doses.
- **Maternal B12 deficiency in SA women** linked to offspring insulin resistance and adiposity (Pune Maternal Nutrition Study, Yajnik 2008 *Diabetologia*) — relevant for family planning conversations.
- **FUT2 secretor status:** Non-secretors (~20% population, varies by ancestry) have lower baseline serum B12 due to altered IF function — more reliant on supplement for adequate status.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **FUT2 rs601338** (secretor status) — non-secretors (AA, ~20%) have ~10–20% lower serum B12 at any given intake; may need higher target.
- **MTRR rs1801394** (methionine synthase reductase) — affects B12 recycling; TT homozygotes may need higher functional B12 to maintain methylation.
- **MTR rs1805087** (methionine synthase) — affects B12 utilization directly.
- **TCN1, TCN2 variants** (transcobalamin) — affect B12 transport; rare clinically significant variants.
- **CBLA, CBLB (MMUT, MMAA, MMAB)** — methylmalonic acidemia; rare; inborn errors that prevent MMA normalization with B12 supplementation.

**Drug interactions:**
- **Metformin** — chronic use lowers B12 ~10–30%; OR for deficiency 1.5–2.0 in diabetic populations **[Meta — Liu 2014 *PLOS One*; de Jager 2010 *BMJ*]**. Mechanism: blocks ileal IF-B12 complex uptake (calcium-dependent). High-dose oral B12 1000 µg/d overcomes via passive diffusion.
- **PPIs (omeprazole, esomeprazole, etc.)** — chronic use ↑ B12 deficiency risk (OR 1.42; 95% CI 1.16–1.73 in PMID 37060552 meta of 25 studies, n=30,922). Mechanism: gastric acid needed to cleave B12 from food protein. **Does NOT affect supplement B12** (already free) — fix is to switch B12 source from food to supplement.
- **H2 blockers (ranitidine, famotidine)** — same mechanism as PPI, smaller effect.
- **Colchicine** — chronic use → ileal B12 malabsorption.
- **Cholestyramine, neomycin** — bind/prevent absorption.
- **Aminosalicylates, methotrexate** — folate antagonism can secondarily affect B12 cycling.
- **Nitrous oxide (N₂O)** — irreversibly oxidizes Co(I) in methylcobalamin → inactivates methionine synthase. Important: a single dental N₂O exposure in a B12-deficient patient can precipitate acute neurological deficit. Check B12 before any planned anesthesia in older or vegetarian patients.

**Lifestyle confounders:**
- **Vegetarian/vegan diet** — by far the dominant non-medical driver.
- **Heavy alcohol use** — paradoxically can elevate serum B12 (acute hepatic release) while functional B12 is depleted; check MMA.
- **Aging** — atrophic gastritis prevalence rises with age (~20% by 60, ~40% by 80) — reduced gastric acid → reduced food B12 cleavage. Same fix as for PPI: switch to supplement.

**Timing/assay artifacts:**
- **Serum B12 in pregnancy** — falls 20–30% by third trimester from hemodilution, not true deficiency.
- **Recent IM B12 dose** — serum B12 transiently very high (>1500 pg/mL) for weeks; not informative.
- **Holotranscobalamin (active B12)** is a more sensitive early marker than total serum B12; not commonly available in routine panels but available via Quest/Labcorp.
- **Falsely elevated serum B12** — myeloproliferative disorders (CML, polycythemia vera), liver disease, renal failure, B12-binding antibodies → high serum B12 with functional deficiency; check MMA.
- **Biotin assay interference** — high-dose biotin (>5 mg/d) can falsely elevate B12 on many immunoassays. Stop biotin ≥2 days before draw.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Does methylcobalamin offer any clinical advantage in MTHFR variants or "methylation issues"?** RCT data say no for serum B12 / MMA / Hcy in healthy adults. Marketing claim is over-extrapolated from biochemistry. Possible minor advantage in specific cobalamin metabolism disorders (cblE, cblG) but not in normal adults with MTHFR C677T.
- **Optimal target for serum B12 in older adults.** Standard "normal" lower bound (~200 pg/mL) misses functional deficiency in many. Many clinicians target >400 pg/mL with concurrent MMA <0.27 µmol/L. Some (Smith & Refsum) argue for >500 pg/mL in older adults to reduce cognitive decline risk — supportive RCTs (Hooshmand 2016 *JAMA Psychiatry* in mild cognitive impairment) show B vitamin supplementation slows brain atrophy in homocysteine-elevated subjects. Not yet definitive.
- **Brasky 2017 high-dose B12 + B6 lung cancer signal in male smokers.** *J Clin Oncol* 2017 (PMID 28829668): VITAL cohort observational analysis found high-dose B6 (>20 mg/d × 10 yr) and B12 (>55 µg/d × 10 yr) associated with ~30–40% higher lung cancer risk in male smokers. *Not seen in non-smokers, not seen in women.* Mechanism unclear; may be confounded. Practical implication: avoid mega-dose B6/B12 (>5 mg or >100 µg long-term respectively) in male smokers without clear deficiency.
- **B vitamin therapy and CVD.** HOPE-2, NORVIT, VITATOPS (folate + B12 + B6 in high-risk patients) lowered Hcy by 25–33% but did NOT reduce coronary events. Stroke risk may be modestly reduced (Yuan 2021 MR). For B12 specifically, the supplementation indication is functional B12 status, not cardiovascular prevention.
- **B12 + cognition.** Observational links between low B12 and dementia exist but RCT evidence for B12 supplementation slowing dementia in already-replete subjects is weak. The strongest signal is in homocysteine-elevated subjects with mild cognitive impairment (Smith 2010 PLOS, Douaud 2013 PNAS — VITACOG: B12+folate+B6 slowed brain atrophy ~30% in 2 yr in elevated-Hcy MCI subjects).
- **Pernicious anemia: oral vs IM.** Vidal-Alaball Cochrane 2005 found oral 1000–2000 µg/d *equivalent* to IM in PA patients for serum B12 and MMA at 4 mo. Despite this, many practitioners default to IM out of habit. Oral is reasonable first-line for compliant adult PA patients without acute neurologic deficits.
