# Magnesium (RBC) — Intervention Research

> RBC magnesium is the panel marker for tissue Mg status. Serum Mg is tightly buffered (~99% intracellular) and a poor indicator. Oral Mg 300–400 mg/d in any soluble form (glycinate, citrate, malate, threonate) raises serum Mg by ~0.05 mmol/L (+0.12 mg/dL) and RBC Mg correspondingly over 8–12 weeks. Mg oxide is poorly absorbed (~4–5%) — mostly a laxative, not a repletion strategy. Form choice matters less than total elemental dose and adherence; glycinate has best GI tolerance, threonate uniquely crosses BBB (relevant for cognition, less so for RBC Mg). PPIs and loop/thiazide diuretics are the dominant drug-driven depleters.

**Cross-link:** Biology and clinical interpretation in `../../nutrients_vitamins_minerals.md` (Magnesium section). Existing intervention table in `../../effect_sizes_nutrients.md` (Magnesium, lines ~270–303). Note: most RCTs measure serum Mg (the easier assay); RBC Mg responds in parallel but with somewhat slower kinetics (RBC turnover ~120 d).

## 1. Foods that RAISE Mg

Adult RDA: 400–420 mg men, 310–320 mg women. NHANES data: ~50% of US adults consume below RDA. Mg in plant foods is bound to chlorophyll (leafy greens) and can be partially inhibited by phytate (whole grains, legumes — phytate also blocks Mg). Bioavailability ~30–50% from typical mixed diet.

### Forms / claims with weak or no support

| Claim | Reality | Source |
|---|---|---|
| "Transdermal Mg (Mg oil sprays, Epsom baths) raises Mg as effectively as oral" | Skin Mg absorption is minimal in controlled studies | Gröber 2017 *Nutrients* (PMID 28788060) |

## 6. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Stack

- **Highest-leverage single intervention:** Oral Mg glycinate or citrate, 300–400 mg elemental/d, for 8–12 weeks. Add Mg-rich mineral water (1 L/d) as a low-side-effect adjunct.
- **Best stack of 3:**
  1. Mg glycinate 200–400 mg elemental/d (the dominant supplement lever)
  2. Dietary leafy greens, nuts, seeds, legumes (sustainable food-source baseline)
  3. Mineral water (1 L/d Gerolsteiner-class) — add 50–100 mg/d background with no GI effect
  - *(For cognitive/brain-specific goals: substitute Mg threonate 1.5–2 g/d for glycinate; for systemic Mg, glycinate is superior.)*
- **Realistic 12-week delta from baseline serum Mg ~0.75 mmol/L (low-normal):** Serum Mg → ~0.85 mmol/L; RBC Mg ↑ proportionally with ~3-month lag.
- **Realistic 12-week delta from frank deficiency (serum Mg <0.7 mmol/L, RBC Mg <4.5 mg/dL):** Serum to mid-normal in 8–12 wk on 400 mg/d glycinate; RBC Mg full repletion ~16–24 wk.
- **Where pharmacotherapy / IV becomes necessary:**
  - Severe symptomatic hypomagnesemia (tetany, seizures, refractory hypokalemia/hypocalcemia, torsades) — IV MgSO₄ 1–2 g over 15 min, then continuous infusion.
  - Eclampsia/preeclampsia — IV MgSO₄ standard.
  - Refractory hypomagnesemia in PPI users despite oral repletion — discontinue PPI if possible; switch to H2 blocker; consider chronic IV.
  - Inherited Mg-wasting (TRPM6 mutations, Gitelman syndrome) — chronic high-dose Mg + K replacement.

## 7. South Asian / lean-male-specific notes

- SA diets are often suboptimal in Mg: low nut/seed intake in many SA cuisines, lower leafy green diversity, high-phytate legumes/whole wheat reducing bioavailability. Estimated SA dietary Mg intake ~200–280 mg/d vs RDA 400 mg/d.
- T2DM prevalence is elevated in SA populations and *itself* depletes Mg via osmotic diuresis. SA male with insulin resistance or T2DM should treat Mg deficiency as likely and supplement proactively.
- **Practical recommendation for SA male:** baseline Mg glycinate 200–400 mg/d if dietary Mg intake is below RDA; specifically reasonable if T2DM, hypertension, or restless legs are present.

## 8. Interactions & Confounders for Recommendation Engine

**Genetic modifiers:**
- **TRPM6 rs3750425, TRPM7** — Mg-selective transporter channels; loss-of-function variants → familial hypomagnesemia. Common variants associated with serum Mg in GWAS; effect modest individually.
- **SLC41A1 rs708727** — solute carrier for Mg; common variant linked to serum Mg in GWAS.
- **CNNM2** — Mg cation transporter; loss-of-function variants → renal Mg wasting.
- **CLDN16, CLDN19** — claudin tight-junction proteins; mutations cause familial hypomagnesemia with hypercalciuria.

**Drug interactions / drugs that LOWER Mg:**
- **Proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole, esomeprazole, etc.)** — chronic use ↓ intestinal Mg absorption; symptomatic hypomagnesemia in ~5% of long-term users. FDA safety communication 2011. Mg supplementation may not fully replete; consider switching to H2 blocker if persistent.
- **Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide)** — block Mg reabsorption in thick ascending limb; significant urinary Mg loss.
- **Thiazide diuretics** — moderate urinary Mg loss; less than loops but additive over years.
- **Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin)** — tubular Mg wasting.
- **Cisplatin** — major Mg wasting; routine Mg replacement required.
- **Cyclosporine, tacrolimus** — tubular Mg wasting; common post-transplant.
- **Amphotericin B** — tubular damage.
- **Foscarnet, pentamidine** — tubular wasting.
- **Insulin (high doses, DKA treatment)** — drives Mg intracellularly; serum Mg falls during DKA correction.

**Drug interactions / drugs Mg interferes with:**
- **Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate)** — Mg supplements (and any divalent cation) reduce absorption; separate by ≥2 hours.
- **Tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin)** — Mg chelates antibiotic; reduces antibiotic absorption substantially. Separate by ≥4 hours (tetracycline) or 2 hours (fluoroquinolone).
- **Levothyroxine** — Mg can reduce absorption; separate by ≥4 hours.
- **Iron, calcium supplements** — modest competition; ideally take separately.

**Lifestyle confounders:**
- **Heavy alcohol** — major Mg depleter via tubular wasting.
- **Diabetes** — osmotic diuresis depletes Mg.
- **Endurance athletes** — sweat losses; higher requirement.
- **Chronic stress / cortisol elevation** — increased urinary Mg loss.
- **Aging** — reduced absorption efficiency, often higher PPI/diuretic exposure.
- **Pregnancy/lactation** — increased requirement; hemodilution lowers serum Mg.

**Timing/assay artifacts:**
- **Serum Mg** is tightly buffered; can be normal even with significant intracellular depletion.
- **RBC Mg** reflects ~120-day intracellular pool; better long-term marker but not routinely available everywhere.
- **Ionized Mg** (gold standard) is rarely available; ionized correlates with serum total but with measurable discordance in low-protein states.
- **Hemolysis** falsely elevates RBC Mg in some processing; samples need careful handling.
- **Diurnal variation** small for serum Mg.
- **Recent supplement dose** transient post-dose serum spike; fasting morning draw preferred.

**Acute illness / inflammation:**
- Sepsis, acute MI, surgery — serum Mg often falls due to redistribution; correct before extubation/discharge.

## 9. Open questions / weak evidence

- **Optimal RBC Mg target.** Reference range typically 4.2–6.8 mg/dL or 1.7–2.4 mmol/L depending on lab. The lower end of "normal" may be associated with higher CV/diabetes risk in some cohorts (Gröber 2015 *Nutrients*); whether targeting upper-normal RBC Mg improves outcomes RCT-prospectively is unestablished.
- **Form selection beyond bioavailability.** Mg glycinate, citrate, malate, threonate — all elevate Mg status; specific forms may have additional non-Mg effects (glycine: GABA-related calming; malate: Krebs intermediate; threonate: brain Mg). Form-vs-form RCTs comparing functional endpoints are limited.
- **Mg threonate for cognitive improvement.** Recent RCTs (PMID 41601871 — n=100, 6 wk; older adult RCT 12 wk) suggest measurable improvements in cognitive performance and sleep. Effect sizes modest. Mechanism (BBB transit) is robust in animal models. The "7.5 year cognitive age reduction" headline is industry-funded — interpret cautiously. Replication needed.
- **Mg supplementation for primary CV prevention.** Mg supplementation lowers BP modestly (Zhang 2016 *Hypertension* meta of 34 trials: SBP −2.0 mmHg, DBP −1.7 mmHg, n=2028) and may reduce AF burden (observational + small RCTs). No mega-RCT yet shows MACE reduction. Reasonable adjunct, not standalone CV intervention.
- **Mg for migraine.** AAN level B recommendation for prevention (400 mg/d). Effect modest; not as strong as CGRP inhibitors but cheap and safe.
- **Mg for type 2 diabetes prevention and control.** Cohort data (NHANES, EPIC) show inverse Mg intake/T2DM relationship; small RCTs (Guerrero-Romero 2008; Rodríguez-Morán 2003) show improved insulin sensitivity in deficient subjects. Mechanistic plausibility strong; large preventive RCTs absent.
- **Transdermal Mg (oils, baths, sprays).** Marketing claims absorption through skin; controlled studies show minimal absorption (Gröber 2017). The "Epsom bath benefits" are mostly from warm bath relaxation, not Mg uptake.
- **Mg + vitamin D coupling.** Mg is a cofactor for CYP2R1 (D 25-hydroxylation) and CYP27B1 (1α-hydroxylation). Co-supplementation theoretically improves D response. Deng 2018 *Am J Clin Nutr* (PMID 29794773) supports the relationship; magnitude in non-deficient subjects modest. Reasonable to co-supplement when starting D3.
