# Autoimmunity Panel — Reference Guide for a 20-Year-Old Male

Markers covered:
1. Antinuclear Antibodies (ANA) Screen
2. Antinuclear Antibodies (ANA) Titer
3. Antinuclear Antibodies (ANA) Pattern
4. Rheumatoid Factor (RF)

This guide is built from primary literature (Tan 1997, Satoh 2012, Mahler 2019, Aringer/EULAR-ACR 2019, Aletaha/ACR-EULAR 2010) and current laboratory practice references (ACR, ARUP, Mayo, Labcorp, ICAP). The most important practical message up front: **isolated low-titer ANA positivity in an asymptomatic young person is common (≈13–32% of "healthy" individuals depending on cutoff) and rarely indicates disease.** RF positivity in healthy young adults is also relatively non-specific. These tests were designed as confirmatory aids in patients with clinical features suggestive of autoimmune disease — using them as untargeted screening generates many false positives.

---

## 1. The ANA workflow: Screen → Titer → Pattern

Most modern panels (including Function Health) report ANA as three linked outputs:

- **Screen** — qualitative result (positive/negative) at the laboratory's screening dilution (commonly 1:40 or 1:80)
- **Titer** — if positive, the highest serial dilution (1:80, 1:160, 1:320, 1:640, 1:1280, 1:2560+) at which the antibody is still detectable. Each step is a 2-fold dilution; higher titer = more autoantibody
- **Pattern** — if positive, the morphology of nuclear/cytoplasmic staining on HEp-2 cells (homogeneous, speckled, nucleolar, centromere, cytoplasmic, etc.). Pattern hints at the target antigen

A negative screen ends the workflow. A positive screen reflexes to titer + pattern, which together — combined with clinical context — direct any next-step antibody panels (anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm, ENA, anti-CCP, etc.).

### What's actually being measured

**ANA gold standard: indirect immunofluorescence (IIF) on HEp-2 cells.** Patient serum is layered onto fixed HEp-2 cells (a human laryngeal carcinoma cell line with prominent nuclei, abundant nuclear antigens, and visible mitotic figures). Bound autoantibodies are detected with a fluorescent anti-human IgG secondary. Positivity is read by a trained microscopist (or automated reader) at a screening dilution; positives are titered serially. The American College of Rheumatology and the 2019 EULAR/ACR SLE classification criteria explicitly endorse HEp-2 IIF as the reference method (Aringer 2019; ACR position statement). [PMC: ACR position on ANA testing](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4204803/)

**Alternative platforms: ELISA, multiplex bead immunoassay (ALBIA, e.g. BioPlex), chemiluminescence.** These use a panel of pre-coated recombinant or purified antigens (typically ~8–17 antigens). They are higher-throughput and cheaper, but they will **miss autoantibodies whose target antigens are not on the panel**. A 2025 Frontiers review notes solid-phase assays show only weak agreement with IIF (κ 0.45–0.55) and can return false negatives when relevant epitopes are conformational or absent from the bead set. Some labs run a hybrid: solid-phase screen with reflex to IIF on positives, or vice versa. [Frontiers 2025: Alternatives to HEp-2 IIF](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/immunology/articles/10.3389/fimmu.2025.1669124/full)

**Practical implication:** Function Health uses HEp-2 IIF (the gold standard). A negative screen on this method is more reassuring than a negative screen on ELISA-only platforms.

**RF: typically nephelometry or turbidimetry, occasionally ELISA.** What's measured is predominantly the **IgM autoantibody directed against the Fc portion of human IgG**. RF can also exist as IgG, IgA, IgE, and IgD, but the standard "RF" assay reports IgM (or total) RF. Results are reported in IU/mL (calibrated to a WHO standard) or U/mL (kit-specific). [StatPearls: Rheumatoid Factor](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK532898/)

## 2. Physiological function and mechanism

### ANA — what the antibody is targeting

ANA is a category, not a single antibody. It captures autoantibodies against antigens located in (or, by extension, near) the cell nucleus: **double-stranded DNA, single-stranded DNA, histones, nucleosomes, the Smith (Sm) snRNP complex, U1-RNP, SS-A/Ro, SS-B/La, Scl-70 (topoisomerase I), centromeric proteins, RNA polymerases, Jo-1 (histidyl-tRNA synthetase), and many more.** ICAP recognizes 30 distinct HEp-2 immunofluorescence patterns split into nuclear (15), cytoplasmic (9), and mitotic (5) groups, plus negative. [ICAP / anapatterns.org](https://www.anapatterns.org/)

The pattern of staining reflects which antigen (or family of antigens) the antibodies are binding. Some patterns are tightly linked to specific diseases; others are non-specific.

Why these antibodies form is incompletely understood. Leading hypotheses include impaired clearance of apoptotic cells (exposing nuclear material to the immune system), molecular mimicry (a viral/bacterial epitope resembling a self-antigen), defective B-cell tolerance checkpoints, and environmental triggers (UV, drugs, smoking, silica). Hormonal factors play a role in the female predominance of most ANA-associated diseases.

### RF — what the antibody is targeting

RF is an **autoantibody directed against the Fc fragment of host IgG**. It is most abundantly produced by B cells in the inflamed RA synovium, but is also generated by peripheral B cells in many other contexts (chronic infections, other autoimmune diseases, normal aging). When RF binds IgG, it forms immune complexes that fix complement, drive synovial inflammation in RA, and (in cryoglobulinemia and endocarditis) deposit in vessel walls. [Wikipedia: Rheumatoid factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheumatoid_factor)

Functionally, low-level IgM RF appears to play a role in **normal IgG homeostasis and immune-complex clearance**, which is part of why it's detectable in healthy individuals — it's not strictly pathological in isolation. [PMC: RF IgM and IgG homeostasis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9634112/)

## 3. What POSITIVE results indicate

### ANA positive — frame the result by titer first, then pattern

**Titer matters more than the binary positive/negative.** Per Tan et al. 1997 (Arthritis & Rheumatism) — the foundational study of ANA in 125 healthy volunteers ages 20–60 across 15 international labs:

| Titer | Healthy individuals positive |
|---|---|
| 1:40 | 31.7% |
| 1:80 | 13.3% |
| 1:160 | 5.0% |
| 1:320 | 3.3% |

[Tan 1997: Range of ANAs in healthy individuals](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9324014/)

US population-level data (Satoh et al. 2012, NHANES 1999–2004, 4,754 subjects ≥12 years) found overall ANA prevalence by HEp-2 IIF of **13.8%**, with female-to-male ratio rising to ~2:1 in middle age. A follow-up NHANES analysis showed ANA prevalence **rising over time**: 11.0% (1988–1991), 11.5% (1999–2004), 15.9% (2011–2012) — a real epidemiologic increase, not just methodologic drift. [PMC: Increasing prevalence of ANA in the US (Satoh-Dinnoo)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7255943/)

**For a 20-year-old male specifically:** ANA positivity is the *least* common in this demographic. Male, young (20–29), and pre-reproductive epidemiology all push baseline ANA prevalence below the population average. So a positive result in this individual carries somewhat more weight than the same result in, say, a 50-year-old woman — but still, low-titer positivity (1:40, 1:80) usually does not indicate disease.

**General clinical thresholds (modern labs, Aringer 2019):**

- **<1:40** — negative
- **1:40 to 1:80** — borderline; common in healthy people; clinical correlation required
- **1:160** — generally considered the threshold for clinical significance
- **1:320 and above** — increasing likelihood of clinically meaningful autoimmunity
- **1:640 to 1:2560+** — high; strongly suggests an autoimmune connective tissue disease in the right clinical setting

The **2019 EULAR/ACR SLE classification criteria** require a positive ANA at **≥1:80** by HEp-2 IIF (or equivalent solid-phase assay) **at least once** as the entry criterion. The cutoff was chosen because a systematic review of 13,080 SLE patients found ≥1:80 had **sensitivity 97.8% (95% CI 96.8–98.5)** for SLE. Specificity at this cutoff is much lower (~75%) — it's a sensitive screen, not a confirmatory test. [Aringer 2019, EULAR/ACR SLE criteria — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6827566/)

### Patterns and what they suggest

Per ICAP nomenclature (Chan 2015 and subsequent updates):

| Pattern | Common antigens | Disease associations |
|---|---|---|
| **Homogeneous (AC-1)** | dsDNA, histones, nucleosomes | SLE, drug-induced lupus, juvenile idiopathic arthritis, autoimmune hepatitis |
| **Speckled — coarse (AC-5)** | Sm, U1-RNP, SS-A/Ro, SS-B/La, RNA Pol III | SLE, MCTD, Sjögren's, scleroderma, polymyositis |
| **Speckled — fine (AC-4)** | SS-A/Ro, SS-B/La | Sjögren's syndrome, SLE, neonatal lupus |
| **Dense fine speckled (AC-2 / DFS70)** | LEDGF/DFS70 | **Generally NOT autoimmune disease** — see Section 7 |
| **Centromere (AC-3)** | CENP-A, CENP-B | Limited cutaneous systemic sclerosis (CREST), primary biliary cholangitis |
| **Nucleolar (AC-8/9/10)** | RNA Pol I, fibrillarin (U3-RNP), PM-Scl, Th/To | Diffuse systemic sclerosis, scleroderma-myositis overlap |
| **Peripheral / rim** | dsDNA, lamin | SLE (less commonly distinguished from homogeneous on modern reads) |
| **Nuclear dots — few (AC-6/7)** | sp100, p80-coilin | Primary biliary cholangitis |
| **Cytoplasmic — fine speckled (AC-19/20)** | Jo-1 and other tRNA synthetases, ribosomal P, mitochondrial (M2) | Antisynthetase syndrome, polymyositis/dermatomyositis, SLE (anti-ribosomal P), PBC (AMA-M2) |
| **Cytoplasmic — reticular/AMA (AC-21)** | Mitochondrial M2 | Primary biliary cholangitis |

[ICAP: International Consensus on ANA Patterns](https://www.anapatterns.org/) · [Chan 2015 Frontiers: ICAP nomenclature](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/immunology/articles/10.3389/fimmu.2015.00412/full) · [PMC: ANAs in healthy and non-rheumatic disease](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6142026/)

A few patterns deserve special mention:

- **Centromere** — among the highest-relevance patterns; high specificity for limited cutaneous SSc / CREST (60–80% of limited SSc, up to 95% of CREST patients). Rare in healthy individuals. [Scleroderma Education Project — Antibody types](https://sclerodermainfo.org/diffuse-limited-overlap-sine-know-your-terms-and-your-antibody-type-2/)
- **Nucleolar** — strongly associated with systemic sclerosis (especially diffuse), occasionally with malignancy
- **Cytoplasmic patterns** — important: a "negative" ANA *nuclear* read does not rule out antisynthetase syndrome (Jo-1 etc.), because the antigen is cytoplasmic. ASSD patients have cytoplasmic IIF staining in 70–80% of cases. Modern labs typically report cytoplasmic patterns as "ANA-positive, cytoplasmic"; older protocols sometimes reported them as "negative." [J Rheumatol 2017: Cytoplasmic staining as screen for antisynthetase syndrome](https://www.jrheum.org/content/44/2/223)

### RF positive — broader differential than people realize

In RA, RF has **sensitivity 60–80% and specificity 80–85%** in established disease (lower in early disease). [StatPearls: Rheumatoid Factor](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK532898/)

But RF is positive in many other settings:

| Condition | RF prevalence |
|---|---|
| **Rheumatoid arthritis** | 60–80% |
| **Sjögren's syndrome** | 75–95% (often very high titer) |
| **Cryoglobulinemia (esp. type II/III, HCV-associated)** | very high, often markedly elevated titers |
| **Hepatitis C** | 45–70% |
| **Hepatitis B, HIV, EBV, CMV** | variable, lower |
| **Subacute bacterial endocarditis** | up to 50% |
| **Tuberculosis, leprosy, syphilis, parasitic infections** | variable |
| **SLE** | 20–30% |
| **Mixed connective tissue disease** | 50–60% |
| **Scleroderma** | 20–30% |
| **Sarcoidosis, idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis** | variable |
| **Healthy adults <60** | 3–5% |
| **Healthy adults ≥65** | 10–25% (rises further with age, >25% in 85+) |

[PMC: RF Diagnostic and Prognostic Performance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11900400/) · [MDPI Biology: HCV autoantibodies and rheumatologic manifestations](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-7737/10/11/1071)

**Titer matters here too.** A low-positive RF (e.g., 15–30 IU/mL) in a healthy young person carries low predictive value. RF >3× the upper limit of normal scores higher in the 2010 ACR/EULAR RA criteria, and very high titers (e.g., >100 IU/mL) shift the differential toward Sjögren's syndrome or cryoglobulinemia rather than seronegative-spectrum conditions.

A 1992 study quoted in StatPearls found the positive predictive value of RF for RA in a general-population screening context to be only **0.24** — i.e., 76% of unselected positive RFs were "false positive" (no RA on follow-up). This is why the test is poor for screening and useful only in clinically suggestive contexts. [PubMed 1456851: RF predictive value](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1456851/)

## 4. What NEGATIVE results indicate

**ANA negative** — generally reassuring, especially with no clinical features of CTD. But two caveats:

1. **ANA-negative SLE exists in ~1–5% of clinically diagnosed SLE patients** (some series report higher figures, but those mix in SLE-mimics). Most ANA-negative SLE patients have isolated anti-Ro/SSA antibodies that the IIF assay missed because the SS-A antigen is poorly retained on the HEp-2 substrate, or anti-ribosomal P (cytoplasmic). If the clinical picture screams SLE, a negative ANA does not stop the workup. [PMC: ANA-negative SLE — How many and how to identify](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9985371/)
2. **A negative nuclear ANA can miss antisynthetase syndrome** (Jo-1 antibodies are cytoplasmic). If myositis is suspected, ask specifically for cytoplasmic IIF reporting and a myositis-specific antibody panel.

For an asymptomatic 20-year-old male, a negative ANA is the expected and most reassuring result.

**RF negative** — common in healthy young adults and the most likely result for this patient. Note:

1. **RF-negative ("seronegative") RA exists** — about 20–30% of RA patients. In these, anti-CCP (anti-citrullinated protein antibody) is the more sensitive *and* more specific test (sens ~60–70%, spec ~95%). Anti-CCP is **not** in this Function Health panel.
2. Early RA may be RF-negative and seroconvert over months to years.

## 5. Direction of "better" and reference ranges

### ANA

**Direction of better:** undetectable / negative is best. There is no biological "floor of concern" — you cannot have too little ANA. ANA does not have a homeostatic role; it's a marker of B-cell autoreactivity.

**Reference ranges (typical US labs):**

- **Negative**: titer <1:40 (or below screening threshold)
- **Low positive / borderline**: 1:40 to 1:80
- **Moderate positive**: 1:160 to 1:320
- **High positive**: 1:640 and above

The 2019 EULAR/ACR threshold for SLE classification is **≥1:80 at least once** (HEp-2 IIF or equivalent). Many US labs now report 1:80 as the screening cutoff specifically to align with this criterion. [Aringer 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6827566/)

### RF

**Direction of better:** undetectable / negative is best. As with ANA, no floor of concern.

**Reference ranges (commonly reported):**

- **Negative**: <14 IU/mL **or** <20 U/mL (units depend on assay calibration)
- **Borderline**: 14–20 IU/mL
- **Positive**: ≥20 IU/mL (some labs use ≥14 IU/mL)
- **High positive (3× ULN)**: weighted higher in 2010 ACR/EULAR RA criteria; titers in the hundreds raise concern for Sjögren's, cryoglobulinemia, or chronic infection rather than RA alone

Lab-to-lab variability is real — RF assays are not fully harmonized. Compare against the reporting lab's specific reference range.

[Cleveland Clinic: RF reference ranges](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diagnostics/rheumatoid-factor) · [Medscape: RF reference range](https://emedicine.medscape.com/article/2087091-overview)

### Optimal range for a healthy 20-year-old male

- **ANA**: negative (titer <1:40, or <1:80 if that's the screening cutoff). A "borderline" 1:40 with no symptoms is statistically common (≈30% of healthy people) and rarely meaningful in isolation.
- **RF**: negative (<14 IU/mL).

## 6. Specific conditions associated with each marker

### ANA-associated diseases (rough hierarchy of strength)

**Tier 1 — ANA is a classification entry criterion or near-universal:**

- **Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)** — ANA ≥1:80 is the entry criterion (2019 EULAR/ACR). Sensitivity ~97–99%. Specific ANAs in SLE: anti-dsDNA (specific, fluctuates with flares), anti-Sm (~30%, virtually 99% specific). [Aringer 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6827566/)
- **Drug-induced lupus** — ANA positive in essentially all cases; **anti-histone antibodies** present in >95%. Resolves within weeks to months after stopping the drug. [PMC: DILE update on drugs and mechanisms](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7299070/)
- **Mixed connective tissue disease (MCTD)** — high-titer speckled ANA (often >1:1280) with anti-U1-RNP. Anti-RNP is essentially 100% sensitive for MCTD (but also seen in 30–40% of SLE). [PMC: MCTD updated perspectives](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10387239/)
- **Systemic sclerosis (scleroderma)** — ANA positive in >90%. Subset-defining antibodies: anti-centromere (limited cutaneous / CREST), anti-Scl-70/topoisomerase I (diffuse, ILD risk), anti-RNA polymerase III (diffuse, renal crisis, malignancy risk). [PMC: Scleroderma autoantibodies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC165038/)

**Tier 2 — ANA frequently positive, but not diagnostic on its own:**

- **Sjögren's syndrome** — ANA positive in ~70%; key specific antibodies are SS-A/Ro (60–70%) and SS-B/La (40–60%). RF also frequently positive at high titer. [PMC: Sjögren's autoantibodies and classification](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8728464/)
- **Polymyositis / dermatomyositis / antisynthetase syndrome** — ANA may be cytoplasmic (Jo-1 and other tRNA synthetases); a negative *nuclear* ANA does not rule this out. [J Rheumatol 2017](https://www.jrheum.org/content/44/2/223)
- **Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA)** — ANA positivity is a major risk factor for JIA-associated uveitis (45% of oligoarticular JIA with ANA develop uveitis). [PMC: JIA and uveitis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4349966/)
- **Autoimmune hepatitis (type 1)** — ANA + anti-smooth-muscle antibodies; characteristic
- **Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)** — anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA, M2) is the disease-defining antibody; ANA can be positive, with characteristic patterns being "nuclear dots" (sp100), "nuclear rim" (gp210). AMA itself shows up as a *cytoplasmic reticular* pattern on HEp-2 IIF. [Frontiers: Autoantibodies in autoimmune liver disease](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/immunology/articles/10.3389/fimmu.2018.00609/full)
- **Rheumatoid arthritis** — ANA positive in 30–40%, usually low titer; rarely the primary diagnostic marker but can complicate the workup

**Tier 3 — ANA may be positive, but as a non-specific finding:**

- Hashimoto's thyroiditis, type 1 diabetes, multiple sclerosis, vitiligo, autoimmune cytopenias
- Chronic infections — hepatitis C (10–40%), HIV, EBV, parvovirus B19, tuberculosis
- Malignancy (especially lymphoproliferative; nucleolar pattern has some association with leukemia/solid tumor)
- Healthy individuals (13–32% depending on cutoff and population)
- Pregnancy
- First-degree relatives of patients with SLE (familial autoimmunity)

### RF-associated diseases

- **Rheumatoid arthritis** — sens 60–80%, spec 80–85%. Higher titers correlate with more aggressive disease, extra-articular manifestations (rheumatoid nodules, vasculitis, ILD), and worse prognosis.
- **Sjögren's syndrome** — RF often very high titer
- **Cryoglobulinemia (especially HCV-associated mixed cryoglobulinemia)** — RF activity is intrinsic to the cryoglobulin complex
- **Hepatitis C, hepatitis B, HIV** — chronic B-cell stimulation
- **Subacute bacterial endocarditis** — up to 50%
- **SLE, MCTD, scleroderma, dermatomyositis** — variable
- **Sarcoidosis, idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, malignancy**
- **Aging** — independent of disease
- **Healthy adults** — 3–5% under 60, rising to ≥25% in 85+

### Critical companion test for RF: anti-CCP (anti-CCP / ACPA)

**Anti-CCP is NOT in this panel.** This is important. Anti-CCP (anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide antibody, also called ACPA) is **more specific for RA than RF** (specificity ~95% vs. RF's 80–85%) with comparable sensitivity. It can be positive years before RA onset. The 2010 ACR/EULAR RA classification criteria weight high-titer RF *or* anti-CCP equivalently — but anti-CCP is the better discriminator in early or atypical disease. If RF is positive in this patient and there's any clinical concern, **anti-CCP is the next test to order, not a repeat RF**. [MDCalc: 2010 ACR/EULAR RA Criteria](https://www.mdcalc.com/calc/10092/acr-eular-2010-rheumatoid-arthritis-classification-criteria) · [Aletaha 2010 — A&R](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/art.27584)

## 7. Confounders and pitfalls

### ANA confounders

**1. High background prevalence in healthy people.**
Per Tan 1997: ~32% of healthy adults positive at 1:40, ~13% at 1:80. Per Satoh 2012 (NHANES): 13.8% of US adults ≥12 years are ANA-positive. Prevalence is rising. **A low-titer positive ANA is by far the most likely outcome of this test even in completely healthy people**, and that probability rises with age and is roughly 2–3× higher in women than men.

**2. Drug-induced ANA / drug-induced lupus.**
Many drugs cause ANA seroconversion. Classic high-risk drugs:

- **Procainamide** (≈20% develop DILE on long-term use)
- **Hydralazine** (5–10% develop DILE)
- **Isoniazid**
- **Minocycline** (relatively common, often in young people treated for acne)
- **Methyldopa, chlorpromazine, quinidine**
- **TNF inhibitors** (etanercept, infliximab, adalimumab, golimumab, certolizumab) — induce ANA in 20–50% of users; <1% develop clinical DILE
- **Hydrochlorothiazide, terbinafine, statins, propylthiouracil, sulfasalazine, interferon-α, methimazole, carbamazepine, ethosuximide**

Antihistone antibodies are present in >95% of classic DILE (procainamide, hydralazine). In TNF-inhibitor DILE the pattern is different — anti-dsDNA can appear, but lupus-like disease is rare. [PMC: DILE drugs and mechanisms](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7299070/)

**3. Recent infection.**
Viral infections (parvovirus B19, EBV, hepatitis viruses) and chronic infections (TB, HIV) commonly produce transient ANA. A repeat in 3–6 months often shows resolution.

**4. Pregnancy.**
ANA prevalence rises in pregnancy and postpartum.

**5. DFS70 / dense fine speckled pattern — the most important "don't panic" pattern.**
**Anti-DFS70 antibodies (target: LEDGF/p75) are MORE prevalent in healthy individuals than in patients with systemic autoimmune rheumatic disease (SARD).** Mahler et al. found 1.6% of routine ANA cohorts and up to 5% of healthy donors positive for anti-DFS70. Critically, when anti-DFS70 is the *only* ANA reactivity (no anti-dsDNA, no anti-ENA), it is strongly **negative** evidence for SARD — i.e., it suggests the person likely does not have lupus, scleroderma, etc. The DFS70 pattern is now formally part of ICAP (AC-2) and the ACR/EULAR community treats isolated anti-DFS70 as essentially benign. If the report shows a dense fine speckled pattern with negative ENA panel, this is a **non-disease finding**. [Nature Sci Rep 2019, Mahler — DFS70 cohort](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-38686-5) · [Clin Chem 2024: DFS70 clinical utility](https://academic.oup.com/clinchem/article/70/2/374/7469965)

**6. Assay platform mismatch.**
ELISA-only screens can miss DFS70 and other patterns visible on IIF. Conversely, IIF can show patterns that don't have a corresponding bead in a multiplex panel. Mismatched results between assays are common.

**7. Sex and age.**
At every age, women are more likely than men to be ANA-positive. The female:male ratio peaks in the 40–49 age range. Prevalence in both sexes rises with age — most steeply in elderly women.

**8. First-degree relatives of SLE patients.**
Up to 25% have ANA without disease.

### RF confounders

**1. Age.**
The single biggest confounder. Healthy adults: 3–5% positive under age 60; up to 25%+ in those over 85. This is partly real B-cell repertoire drift, partly chronic subclinical infection / inflammation.

**2. Chronic infection.**
Hepatitis C (45–70%), hepatitis B, HIV, EBV, parvovirus, subacute bacterial endocarditis, tuberculosis, leprosy, syphilis, parasitic infection. RF in these settings reflects chronic B-cell stimulation against immune complexes.

**3. Other autoimmune disease.**
Sjögren's (often very high titer), SLE, MCTD, scleroderma, dermatomyositis. The RF here is "real" but non-specific for RA.

**4. Cryoglobulinemia.**
Type II and III cryoglobulins have intrinsic RF activity. Often associated with HCV. Markedly elevated RF in this setting can be a clue.

**5. Pulmonary disease.**
Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, sarcoidosis, silicosis.

**6. Other medical conditions.**
Hyperglobulinemia of any cause, primary biliary cholangitis, cancer (especially lymphoproliferative).

**7. Recent vaccination.**
Some studies show transient RF rises post-vaccination.

**8. Assay variability.**
RF is not fully harmonized internationally. The same serum can give different IU/mL on different platforms. Modest positives (15–25 IU/mL) are particularly susceptible to assay noise.

## 8. Special depth points

### 8a. The 2019 EULAR/ACR SLE classification criteria (Aringer 2019)

These criteria are the current gold standard for classifying SLE. Key features for interpreting an ANA result:

- **Entry criterion**: positive ANA at **≥1:80 on HEp-2 cells** (or solid-phase equivalent) at least once. Without this, SLE is not classified.
- **Weighted point system**: clinical and immunological domains, each scored. Threshold for classification: ≥10 points.
- **Performance** (validation cohort, n=1,270): sensitivity 96.1%, specificity 93.4%.
- ANA at ≥1:80 by itself has sensitivity ~97.8% but specificity only ~75% — i.e., it casts a wide net. The point system narrows it.
- "Classification" criteria are designed for research consistency; they are **not** diagnostic. A clinician can diagnose SLE in a patient who doesn't meet criteria, or decline to diagnose someone who does.

[Aringer 2019, EULAR/ACR SLE criteria — full text PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6827566/)

### 8b. Anti-DFS70 — the "don't panic" pattern

If a young, asymptomatic patient gets a positive ANA with **dense fine speckled (DFS70 / AC-2) pattern, isolated** (i.e., no anti-dsDNA, no anti-ENA, no clinical features), this is:

- More common in healthy individuals than in lupus/scleroderma patients
- Currently used by some labs as an **exclusion marker** for SARD when isolated
- A reasonable end-point — no immediate further workup needed if there are no symptoms

The 2025 Journal of Rheumatology supplement explicitly calls anti-DFS70 a "marker for exclusion of systemic autoimmune rheumatic diseases" when isolated. [J Rheumatol 2025: Anti-DFS70 as exclusion marker](https://www.jrheum.org/content/52/Suppl_1/97.2)

This is the single most important practical point in this report: **a positive ANA with isolated DFS70 pattern is not a disease finding.** Lab reports that don't break out DFS70 separately can mislead patients (and primary care providers) into thinking they have a budding autoimmune condition.

### 8c. What to do with isolated low-titer ANA in a young, asymptomatic person

Per ACR patient guidance and the 2019 EULAR/ACR criteria spirit:

- **Do not** chase further antibody panels in the absence of symptoms
- **Do not** repeat the ANA "to confirm" within weeks — titers fluctuate
- **Do** take a careful symptom history covering: photosensitive rash, oral ulcers, alopecia, Raynaud's phenomenon, dry eyes/dry mouth, joint swelling (vs. mechanical pain), pleuritic chest pain, unexplained fevers, hair loss
- **Do** ensure basic labs are normal: CBC (cytopenias?), urinalysis (proteinuria/hematuria suggesting nephritis?), basic chemistries
- **Do** consider repeating the ANA in 6–12 months if there's any clinical concern; persistent high titers warrant rheumatology referral
- **Do not** interpret a positive ANA as a "diagnosis" — it isn't one

For this patient (20-year-old male, asymptomatic), **a positive ANA at 1:40 or 1:80 with isolated DFS70 pattern is essentially expected and reassuring**. Low-titer positivity at 1:160 with negative ENA panel still has very low pretest probability of disease in this demographic. Persistent high titer (≥1:320) or clinical symptoms shifts the calculus toward rheumatology consultation.

### 8d. RF + anti-CCP + clinical findings — the 2010 ACR/EULAR RA criteria

The 2010 ACR/EULAR criteria are designed to identify patients with early inflammatory arthritis who are at risk of developing persistent erosive disease — emphasizing earlier diagnosis than the 1987 criteria allowed.

Domains and points:

| Domain | Criteria | Points |
|---|---|---|
| **A. Joint involvement** | 1 large joint | 0 |
| | 2–10 large joints | 1 |
| | 1–3 small joints | 2 |
| | 4–10 small joints | 3 |
| | >10 joints (≥1 small) | 5 |
| **B. Serology** | RF and anti-CCP both negative | 0 |
| | Low-positive RF or anti-CCP (≤3× ULN) | 2 |
| | High-positive RF or anti-CCP (>3× ULN) | 3 |
| **C. Acute-phase reactants** | CRP and ESR both normal | 0 |
| | CRP or ESR abnormal | 1 |
| **D. Symptom duration** | <6 weeks | 0 |
| | ≥6 weeks | 1 |

**Threshold for classification: ≥6 of 10 points + at least one swollen joint not better explained by another disease.**

Practical implication for this patient: an isolated low-positive RF, with no joint swelling, no morning stiffness >30 min, and normal inflammatory markers, scores nowhere near the threshold. RF in isolation is not an RA diagnosis. If symptoms ever arise, **anti-CCP is the next-step test** because of its much higher specificity. [Aletaha 2010 — A&R](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/art.27584) · [MDCalc — 2010 ACR/EULAR RA Criteria](https://www.mdcalc.com/calc/10092/acr-eular-2010-rheumatoid-arthritis-classification-criteria)

### 8e. Tests NOT in this panel that become relevant if ANA or RF is positive

If ANA is positive at clinically meaningful titer (≥1:160) or with a worrying pattern, the natural next-step tests are:

- **Anti-dsDNA** — high specificity for SLE; correlates with disease activity (esp. nephritis); fluctuates over time
- **Anti-Sm** — ~30% of SLE patients but ~99% specific; stable over time
- **ENA panel** — typically includes Sm, U1-RNP, SS-A/Ro, SS-B/La, Scl-70, Jo-1, centromere
- **Anti-Scl-70 (topoisomerase I)** and **anti-RNA polymerase III** — scleroderma subset markers
- **Anti-CCP** — for the RA workup if RF is positive or symptoms suggest RA
- **Complement (C3, C4, CH50)** — low in active SLE
- **Antiphospholipid antibodies** — anticardiolipin (IgG/IgM), anti-β2-glycoprotein-I, lupus anticoagulant
- **ANCA (PR3, MPO)** — for vasculitis if clinically suspected; ANCA-associated vasculitis is a separate axis from ANA-associated CTDs and has a different antibody profile
- **Anti-mitochondrial (AMA), anti-smooth-muscle, anti-LKM** — for autoimmune liver disease
- **Anti-thyroid (TPO, Tg)** — for autoimmune thyroiditis
- **Myositis-specific antibodies** (Jo-1, Mi-2, MDA5, TIF1-γ, NXP2, SRP, HMGCR) — if there's clinical concern for inflammatory myopathy

For this asymptomatic 20-year-old male, **none of these reflex tests are warranted unless the ANA is unexpectedly high titer (≥1:320), the pattern is concerning (e.g., high-titer speckled, centromere, nucleolar, peripheral/rim), or symptoms emerge.**

## 9. Bottom-line interpretation framework for this patient

For a 20-year-old male with no autoimmune symptoms, the prior probability of an autoimmune connective tissue disease is **very low** — roughly 0.05–0.1% population-wide for SLE in young men, much lower than in young women. Most autoimmune CTDs have a strong female predominance (SLE 9:1, Sjögren's 9:1, scleroderma 4:1; RA is closer to 3:1).

This means the **positive predictive value** of a low-titer ANA or borderline RF in this individual is even lower than in the general "asymptomatic adult" population — a positive result is almost always a false alarm.

**Decision framework:**

| Result | Interpretation for this patient |
|---|---|
| ANA negative, RF negative | Best case, expected. No further action. |
| ANA 1:40 or 1:80, any pattern except concerning, RF negative | Common (≈13–32% of healthy people). Almost certainly noise. No further action absent symptoms. |
| ANA positive with isolated DFS70 pattern, RF negative | Reassuring — anti-DFS70 in isolation is *negative* evidence for SARD. No further workup. |
| ANA 1:160, homogeneous/speckled, RF negative, no symptoms | Borderline. Monitor symptoms; consider repeat in 6–12 months. Most resolve or remain isolated without disease. |
| ANA ≥1:320, **OR** centromere/nucleolar/cytoplasmic pattern, **OR** any titer with clinical symptoms | Warrants further workup: ENA panel, anti-dsDNA, complement, urinalysis, CBC, and rheumatology referral if any of these are abnormal or symptoms persist. |
| RF positive, low (15–30 IU/mL), no symptoms | Likely false positive. Repeat in 3–6 months if concerned; consider anti-CCP. No diagnosis. |
| RF positive, high (>3× ULN), no symptoms | Worth a rheumatology touchpoint and an anti-CCP test. Differential includes early RA, Sjögren's, cryoglobulinemia, chronic infection (HCV serology reasonable). |
| RF very high (>100 IU/mL) | Think Sjögren's, cryoglobulinemia, HCV, or — less likely — early RA. Workup warranted regardless of symptoms. |

## Summary card

- **For a healthy young male**: baseline prior probability of any ANA-associated CTD is very low. Most autoimmune CTDs are 3–9× more common in women.
- **A positive ANA at low titer (1:40, 1:80) is the single most likely "abnormal" result.** It happens in 13–32% of healthy people. By itself, in the absence of symptoms, it's not a disease.
- **Pattern matters.** Centromere, nucleolar, cytoplasmic, and high-titer speckled patterns deserve attention. Isolated **dense fine speckled (DFS70) pattern is reassuring** — actually associated with NOT having autoimmune disease.
- **Titer matters more than positivity.** ≥1:160 starts to be clinically interesting; ≥1:320 more so. The 2019 EULAR/ACR SLE criterion is ≥1:80 as a screening floor.
- **A positive RF in isolation is also low-information** in a young person. Sensitivity for RA is 60–80%, specificity is only 80–85%. Anti-CCP (not on this panel) is the more specific RA marker.
- **A negative ANA does not 100% rule out lupus** (~5% of SLE is ANA-negative on IIF, often anti-Ro positive). But for an asymptomatic young man, negative is the expected and reassuring result.
- **Don't chase a positive ANA into expensive or invasive workups without symptoms.** Repeat in 6–12 months if concerned; refer to rheumatology only for high titer, concerning pattern, or clinical findings.
