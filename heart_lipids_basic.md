# Heart & Basic Lipids — Reference Report

A comprehensive reference for interpreting Function Health basic lipid + heart inflammation results, intended for a healthy 20-year-old male. Sources are cited inline; preference is given to peer-reviewed literature, AHA/ACC/NLA/ESC guidelines, USPSTF, and lipidology specialists (Sniderman, Ridker, Nordestgaard, Attia).

A core framing point used throughout: **"normal" (population reference range) ≠ "optimal" (range associated with lowest cardiovascular event risk)**. Most lab "normal" ranges are derived from population distributions in countries with high background rates of atherosclerosis. Lipidology guidelines (NLA, ESC/EAS, the 2026 ACC/AHA/Multisociety guideline) and longevity-focused clinicians (Attia, Hyman, Means/Lustig at Levels) generally argue that population-typical lipid values are not biologically optimal.

---

## 1. Total Cholesterol (TC)

### What it is
Total cholesterol is the sum of cholesterol carried by all lipoprotein fractions in plasma: LDL-C + HDL-C + VLDL-C (and IDL, chylomicron remnants, Lp(a), etc.). It is most often calculated, not directly measured. Reported in mg/dL (US) or mmol/L (Europe; conversion factor ~38.6 for cholesterol species). [Source: 2018 AHA/ACC Cholesterol Guideline, *Circulation* — Grundy et al., 2019.]

### Physiological function / mechanism
Cholesterol is a structural component of every cell membrane and the precursor for steroid hormones (cortisol, aldosterone, testosterone, estradiol), bile acids (cholate, chenodeoxycholate), and vitamin D. The liver synthesizes most of the body's cholesterol via HMG-CoA reductase; dietary cholesterol contributes a smaller, variable fraction. Cholesterol does not dissolve in plasma and is therefore transported inside lipoprotein particles (LDL, HDL, VLDL, IDL, chylomicrons, Lp(a)). [Source: Endotext "Guidelines for the Management of High Blood Cholesterol," NCBI Bookshelf NBK305897.]

### What HIGH values indicate
- **Atherosclerotic CVD risk** when the elevation is driven by atherogenic (apoB-containing) lipoproteins. TC alone is a weak surrogate because it lumps together protective HDL-C and atherogenic LDL/VLDL/Lp(a).
- **Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH)**: TC commonly >290 mg/dL in heterozygous FH adults (LDL-C >190 mg/dL is the screening threshold); homozygous FH typically TC >500 mg/dL with childhood-onset disease. [Source: Simon Broome / Dutch Lipid Clinic Network criteria; AHA 2020 statement, JAHA *Diagnosis and Treatment of Heterozygous Familial Hypercholesterolemia*.]
- **Secondary causes**: hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, cholestatic liver disease, anorexia nervosa (paradoxically), pregnancy, certain drugs (cyclosporine, amiodarone, atypical antipsychotics, glucocorticoids, oral retinoids, thiazides at high dose).
- **Diet/lifestyle**: high saturated fat / trans fat, weight gain, sedentary behavior. [Source: AHA Scientific Statement on dietary fats, *Circulation* 2017 (Sacks et al.).]

### What LOW values indicate
- Generally low TC is seen as benign in young people, but **very low TC** has been associated with higher all-cause mortality in observational data — usually because low TC is a marker of underlying disease (cancer, severe liver disease, malabsorption, hyperthyroidism, severe infection, malnutrition) rather than the cause.
- A 12.8 million-adult Korean cohort (Yi et al., *Scientific Reports* 2019) found a **U-shaped** TC–mortality curve: lowest mortality at TC 210–249 mg/dL across most age groups, with elevated mortality at both lower and higher extremes. This U-shape is most pronounced in adults >65 and weakest in young adults; in the 18–34 age group, increases in TC were still associated with progressively higher mortality.
- Genetic causes of very low TC: abetalipoproteinemia (MTP mutations), hypobetalipoproteinemia (APOB mutations) — usually TC <100 mg/dL with neurologic and GI manifestations.

### Direction of "better"
**Mildly U-shaped on all-cause mortality**, but **monotonic for CVD events** when viewed through the apoB lens. For CVD specifically, lower atherogenic-particle exposure is always better; the U-shape on all-cause mortality is largely confounding by reverse causation (illness lowers cholesterol). For a healthy 20-year-old male, lower TC driven by low LDL/non-HDL is favorable; very low TC (<120 mg/dL) without medication should prompt evaluation for hyperthyroidism, malabsorption, or genetic hypobetalipoproteinemia. [Sources: Yi et al. 2019, Sci Rep; *Circulation* 2017 EAS Consensus on LDL causality.]

### Conventional/lab reference range (LabCorp/Quest/AHA)
- **Desirable**: <200 mg/dL
- **Borderline-high**: 200–239 mg/dL
- **High**: ≥240 mg/dL
[Source: American Heart Association, *What Your Cholesterol Levels Mean*; mirrored by NCEP ATP III and used by LabCorp/Quest.]

### Optimal range for a healthy 20-year-old male
- **AHA "desirable"**: <200 mg/dL
- **Mark Hyman / functional medicine**: <200 mg/dL with HDL >60 and TG <100 (whole-panel framing) [Source: drhyman.com, "7 Ways to Optimize Cholesterol"]
- **Attia / Levels framing**: TC is a poor standalone target — focus on **apoB and non-HDL-C**, not TC. Attia explicitly de-emphasizes TC. [Source: peterattiamd.com #229, #238 AMA #43.]
- **Optimal Diagnostics functional range (commonly cited in functional medicine): 160–199 mg/dL**, with caveat that very low TC can signal underlying disease.

### Specific conditions/diseases it can flag
- Familial hypercholesterolemia (heterozygous: TC ~290–500; homozygous: TC >500)
- Hypothyroidism (typically raises TC by 10–30%)
- Nephrotic syndrome
- Cholestatic liver disease (very high; often with elevated Lp-X)
- Anorexia nervosa (paradoxical hypercholesterolemia)
- Polygenic hypercholesterolemia
- For low TC: hyperthyroidism, advanced liver disease, malabsorption, abetalipoproteinemia, severe systemic illness

### Confounders / acute movers
- Recent illness or major stress can transiently *lower* TC (acute-phase response).
- Pregnancy raises TC ~25–50% in second/third trimester.
- 24-hour fasting can *increase* TC, LDL, and HDL acutely (HCPLive, *Cholesterol Levels Rise After a 24-Hour Fast*).
- Posture: standing samples are slightly higher than supine.
- Seasonal: TC is ~5 mg/dL higher in winter.

## 2. LDL Cholesterol (LDL-C)

### What it is
LDL-C is the cholesterol mass carried within low-density lipoprotein (LDL) particles. Most clinical labs **calculate** LDL-C using the Friedewald equation (LDL-C = TC − HDL-C − TG/5), which becomes inaccurate when TG >400 mg/dL. The Martin-Hopkins equation is now used by many labs (including LabCorp's extended panels) for better accuracy at low LDL-C. Direct LDL-C assays exist but are more expensive and less standardized. [Source: Martin SS et al., *JAMA* 2013;310(19):2061-8 — "Comparison of a Novel Method vs the Friedewald Equation for Estimating LDL-C."]

LDL-C measures **cholesterol mass**, NOT particle number. This distinction is critical and is the mechanistic basis for apoB's superiority (see Section 7).

### Physiological function / mechanism
LDL particles are the end product of VLDL→IDL→LDL metabolism in plasma. They deliver cholesterol to peripheral tissues via the LDL receptor (LDLR). LDL particles that infiltrate the arterial intima can be retained by proteoglycans, oxidized, and engulfed by macrophages → foam cells → atherosclerotic plaque. The LDL particle, not its cholesterol content, is what gets trapped — this is the key insight underlying apoB's superiority. [Source: 2017 EAS Consensus statement, *European Heart Journal* — Ference, Ginsberg, Graham et al., "Low-density lipoproteins cause atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease."]

### What HIGH values indicate
- **Atherosclerotic CVD risk** — causally established by Mendelian randomization. The 2017 EAS Consensus concluded "LDL causes ASCVD" with the same level of certainty as smoking causes lung cancer.
- **Cumulative LDL-C exposure** (mg/dL × years) drives risk; lifetime burden matters more than any single measurement. Ference (*JACC* 2012) showed via Mendelian randomization that long-term lower LDL-C from early life produces ~3x the CHD risk reduction per mmol/L compared with statin trials starting in mid-life.
- **Familial hypercholesterolemia**: LDL-C ≥190 mg/dL in adults (or ≥160 mg/dL with family history of premature ASCVD) is highly suggestive; LDL-C >200 mg/dL in someone <20 is "highly suggestive of HeFH" [Source: Family Heart Foundation; AAFP 2024].
- **Secondary causes**: hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, cholestasis, anorexia, pregnancy, certain drugs.

### What LOW values indicate
- For most adults, lower LDL-C means lower CVD risk — Mendelian randomization shows the relationship extends well below the population average.
- **Concerns at very low LDL-C** (<50–70 mg/dL):
  - Observational data (Rist et al., *Neurology* 2019) found women with LDL-C <70 mg/dL had ~2.2x risk of hemorrhagic stroke vs. those with LDL-C 100–130; risk further elevated at <50 mg/dL. [Source: HCPLive summary; original *Neurology* study.]
  - Importantly, RCTs using statins/PCSK9 inhibitors that drive LDL-C below 55 mg/dL have **not** found increased hemorrhagic stroke (meta-analysis in *J Stroke Cerebrovasc Dis* 2021) — suggesting naturally low LDL-C in observational cohorts is a marker of underlying frailty/cancer/liver disease, not the cause of harm.
  - Genetic: PCSK9 loss-of-function carriers have very low LDL-C (sometimes <30 mg/dL) lifelong with reduced CVD risk and no clear adverse effects (Cohen et al., *NEJM* 2006).

### Direction of "better"
**Monotonic — lower is better — for CVD risk in primary prevention**, supported by Mendelian randomization, statin trials, ezetimibe (IMPROVE-IT), and PCSK9i trials (FOURIER, ODYSSEY OUTCOMES). The all-cause mortality benefit plateaus below ~70 mg/dL in trials; CVD event reduction continues to ~20 mg/dL. [Source: 2017 EAS Consensus; Ference et al., *JACC* 2018 Mendelian randomization synthesis.]

### Conventional/lab reference range (NCEP ATP III, mirrored by LabCorp/Quest/AHA)
- **Optimal**: <100 mg/dL
- **Near-optimal**: 100–129 mg/dL
- **Borderline-high**: 130–159 mg/dL
- **High**: 160–189 mg/dL
- **Very high**: ≥190 mg/dL (FH screening threshold)
[Source: AHA / NCEP ATP III; LabCorp test 001172.]

### Optimal range for a healthy 20-year-old male
- **2018 AHA/ACC**: <100 mg/dL is "optimal" for primary prevention; thresholds for action ≥160 (consider statin in young adults with strong family history) and ≥190 (statin recommended). [Source: Grundy et al., *Circulation* 2019.]
- **2026 ACC/AHA/Multisociety Dyslipidemia Guideline**: emphasizes earlier and more aggressive treatment across the life course; LDL-C goal **<100 mg/dL** for borderline/intermediate risk, **<70 mg/dL** for high risk, **<55 mg/dL** for very-high-risk/secondary prevention. Recommends pharmacotherapy in young adults with LDL-C ≥160 mg/dL or strong family history of premature ASCVD. [Source: 2026 ACC/AHA Guideline, *Circulation* CIR.0000000000001423.]
- **2019 ESC/EAS**: LDL-C **<116 mg/dL** for low-risk individuals (SCORE <1%); <100 mg/dL moderate risk; <70 mg/dL high; <55 mg/dL very high. [Source: Mach et al., *Eur Heart J* 2020;41:111-188.]
- **Peter Attia (primordial prevention)**: views LDL-C as a less precise version of apoB; targets translate roughly to apoB <60 mg/dL ≈ LDL-C ~70–80 mg/dL. Argues a 20-year-old at the 50th percentile (LDL-C ~100) with elevated genetic risk should be treated aggressively. [Source: peterattiamd.com #229, "Early and aggressive lowering of apoB."]
- **Mark Hyman (functional)**: ideally <80 mg/dL but with strong emphasis on particle size/number (NMR LDL-P) over LDL-C. [Source: drhyman.com podcast Ep856.]
- **Levels (Means/Lustig)**: aligned with Attia framing — focus on apoB and non-HDL-C; LDL-C alone is inadequate.

### Specific conditions/diseases it can flag
- Familial hypercholesterolemia (LDL-C ≥190; or ≥160 with family history premature ASCVD; or ≥130 in a child)
- Polygenic hypercholesterolemia
- Hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, cholestasis (secondary)
- For very low LDL-C: hyperthyroidism, advanced liver disease, malabsorption, abetalipoproteinemia, hypobetalipoproteinemia, severe systemic illness or malignancy

### Confounders / acute movers
- Friedewald-calculated LDL-C is unreliable when TG >400 (Martin-Hopkins or direct LDL-C is preferred).
- Recent illness/inflammation transiently lowers LDL-C.
- Pregnancy raises LDL-C significantly.
- Rapid weight loss / very-low-calorie diets transiently raise LDL-C as adipose cholesterol mobilizes.
- Major surgery / trauma can lower LDL-C for weeks.
- Saturated fat intake in the prior days can shift values 5–15%.

## 3. HDL Cholesterol (HDL-C)

### What it is
HDL-C is the cholesterol mass carried within high-density lipoprotein particles. HDL particles are heterogeneous in size, density, and protein cargo (apoA-I is the main structural apolipoprotein). [Source: NLA / *J Clin Lipidol* HDL position papers.]

### Physiological function / mechanism
HDL participates in **reverse cholesterol transport** (RCT): HDL accepts cholesterol from peripheral tissues (notably macrophages in arterial walls, via ABCA1 and ABCG1) and delivers it to the liver for excretion. HDL also has antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antithrombotic effects mediated by its protein cargo (paraoxonase, apoA-I, S1P). The simple mass measurement (HDL-C) is a **crude proxy** for HDL functionality; cholesterol efflux capacity is mechanistically more relevant but not clinically available. [Source: Rohatgi et al., *NEJM* 2014 on cholesterol efflux capacity.]

### What HIGH values indicate
- Historically considered protective ("good cholesterol"). However, multiple large prospective cohorts (Madsen et al., *Eur Heart J* 2017; Liu et al., *Lancet Reg Health West Pac* 2023) document a **U-shaped** relationship between HDL-C and all-cause + cardiovascular mortality.
- **Very high HDL-C** (men ≥80 mg/dL; women ≥100 mg/dL) is associated with:
  - 96% higher all-cause mortality and 71% higher CV mortality in CAD patients (UK + Emory cohort, 2024).
  - Korean KoGES-HEXA cohort (2025): paradoxically increased mortality in non-diabetic males with extremely high HDL-C.
- Common causes of high HDL-C: heavy alcohol use, female sex, exercise, certain genetic variants (CETP loss-of-function — SETP polymorphism — though CETP-inhibitor pharmacotherapy paradoxically failed to reduce events in trials like ILLUMINATE [torcetrapib was halted for harm]).
- Pharmacologic HDL-C raising (CETP inhibitors, niacin, fibrates) has consistently failed to reduce events — a major reason the field has moved away from HDL-C as a therapeutic target.

### What LOW values indicate
- Strong observational association with CVD risk (HDL-C <40 mg/dL men, <50 mg/dL women).
- However, Mendelian randomization (Voight et al., *Lancet* 2012) found that genetic variants raising HDL-C did **NOT** reduce CHD risk — suggesting low HDL-C is a *marker* of cardiometabolic dysfunction (insulin resistance, central adiposity, smoking) rather than a *cause* of CVD.
- Causes of low HDL-C:
  - **Metabolic**: insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, central obesity, metabolic syndrome (most common cause of mildly low HDL-C in young adults).
  - **Lifestyle**: smoking, sedentary behavior, very-low-fat diets, anabolic steroid use (can crash HDL-C to <20).
  - **Genetic** (rare): Tangier disease (homozygous ABCA1 mutations — HDL-C often <5 mg/dL with orange tonsils, hepatosplenomegaly, neuropathy); LCAT deficiency; apoA-I mutations. [Source: GeneReviews — Tangier Disease; MedlinePlus.]
  - **Drugs**: anabolic steroids/testosterone (oral 17α-alkylated worst), progestins, beta-blockers, thiazides at high dose.
- Acute confounders: HDL-C drops transiently during acute infection/inflammation.

### Direction of "better"
**U-shaped on mortality.** Both low (<40 mg/dL men) and very high (≥80–90 mg/dL men) HDL-C are associated with worse outcomes. The "sweet spot" appears to be ~50–80 mg/dL. [Source: Madsen et al., *EHJ* 2017; Liu et al., *Lancet Reg Health WP* 2023; USC Journal review on the U-shaped HDL curve.]

For a 20-year-old male, mid-50s to ~80 mg/dL is most reassuring. Levels >90 mg/dL warrant noting but are more concerning in older adults — in young men, high HDL is more often from genetics or lifestyle rather than dysfunctional HDL.

### Conventional/lab reference range (LabCorp/Quest/AHA)
- **Low (men)**: <40 mg/dL — classified as a CVD risk factor
- **Normal (men)**: 40–59 mg/dL
- **High / "protective" historically**: ≥60 mg/dL
[Source: AHA *What Your Cholesterol Levels Mean*; NCEP ATP III.]

### Optimal range for a healthy 20-year-old male
- **AHA classic**: ≥60 mg/dL = protective (this framing is being revised given the U-shape).
- **Hyman (functional)**: >60 mg/dL ideal. [drhyman.com]
- **Casey Means / Levels**: 50–90 mg/dL is the optimal range; >90 starts to look paradoxical. [Levels guide; caseymeans.com]
- **Attia**: doesn't target HDL-C; doesn't believe raising HDL-C pharmacologically helps. Considers HDL-C primarily a metabolic-health marker.
- **Working synthesis for a 20yo male**: 50–80 mg/dL is the practical sweet spot.

### Specific conditions/diseases it can flag
- Metabolic syndrome / insulin resistance (low HDL-C is one of 5 ATP III criteria)
- Type 2 diabetes risk
- Anabolic steroid abuse (very low HDL-C in a fit-looking young male)
- Tangier disease, LCAT deficiency, apoA-I mutations (HDL-C very low, often <20)
- CETP deficiency (HDL-C very high, often >100)
- Hyperthyroidism (raises HDL-C); hypothyroidism (lowers HDL-C modestly)

### Confounders / acute movers
- Alcohol acutely and chronically raises HDL-C (often the dominant lifestyle effect).
- Aerobic exercise raises HDL-C ~3–10% with sustained training.
- Acute illness lowers HDL-C transiently.
- Smoking lowers HDL-C; quitting raises it ~5%.
- Weight loss raises HDL-C modestly.

## 4. Non-HDL Cholesterol

### What it is
**Non-HDL-C = Total Cholesterol − HDL-C.** It captures the cholesterol carried by *all* atherogenic lipoproteins: LDL, VLDL, IDL, chylomicron remnants, and Lp(a). It requires no fasting and no Friedewald assumption. [Source: NLA Position Paper, *J Clin Lipidol* 2015 — Jacobson et al.]

### Physiological function / mechanism
Same as the underlying particles — non-HDL-C is a **summary measure of atherogenic-particle cholesterol burden**. Each VLDL/IDL/LDL/Lp(a) particle carries one apoB-100 (or apoB-48 for chylomicrons), so non-HDL-C correlates closely with apoB but doesn't equal it (because particles vary in cholesterol content per particle). Non-HDL-C is preferred over LDL-C in patients with high triglycerides because it captures remnant cholesterol — a strongly atherogenic fraction. [Source: Nordestgaard, *Circ Res* 2016 — "Triglyceride-Rich Lipoproteins and ASCVD."]

### What HIGH values indicate
- Same conditions as elevated LDL-C, plus **specifically** elevated VLDL/IDL/remnants (most often from insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, NAFLD, metabolic syndrome).
- Non-HDL-C is a **better predictor** of CVD events than LDL-C in patients with TG >150 mg/dL (Boekholdt et al., *JAMA* 2012 meta-analysis).
- A meta-analysis by Sniderman et al. (*Circ Cardiovasc Qual Outcomes* 2011, 233,455 subjects) found apoB > non-HDL-C > LDL-C for risk prediction, but non-HDL-C was significantly better than LDL-C.

### What LOW values indicate
- Same as low LDL-C — generally favorable; very low values (e.g., <70 mg/dL) without medication may signal hyperthyroidism, malabsorption, or chronic illness.

### Direction of "better"
**Monotonic — lower is better — for CVD risk.** No U-shape has been established for non-HDL-C specifically; like LDL-C and apoB, it captures atherogenic lipoprotein burden, which is causally linked to ASCVD via Mendelian randomization. [Source: Ference et al., *JACC* 2018; 2017 EAS Consensus.]

### Conventional/lab reference range
- **Optimal**: <130 mg/dL (NCEP ATP III; corresponds to LDL-C <100)
- **Above goal**: 130–159 mg/dL
- **Borderline-high**: 160–189 mg/dL
- **High**: 190–219 mg/dL
- **Very high**: ≥220 mg/dL
[Source: NCEP ATP III; reiterated by NLA 2015.]

### Optimal range for a healthy 20-year-old male
- **AHA / NIH general population**: <130 mg/dL [AHA, January 2024 news article on lesser-known lipid numbers]
- **2026 ACC/AHA**: <130 mg/dL low risk; <100 mg/dL high risk (e.g., CAC 100–999); <85 mg/dL very-high-risk ASCVD. [Source: 2026 ACC/AHA Dyslipidemia Guideline.]
- **2019 ESC/EAS**: <100 mg/dL moderate risk; <85 mg/dL high; <85 mg/dL very high (in line with apoB <100 / <80 / <65).
- **Peter Attia**: parallels apoB targets — non-HDL-C ≈ apoB × ~1.2–1.3, so <80 mg/dL parallels apoB <60. [Source: peterattiamd.com #229.]
- **NLA 2015**: non-HDL-C goal of <100 mg/dL for high-risk patients, <130 mg/dL otherwise.

### Specific conditions/diseases it can flag
- Atherogenic dyslipidemia of metabolic syndrome / type 2 diabetes / NAFLD (high TG + low HDL = high non-HDL-C with normal-ish LDL-C)
- Familial combined hyperlipidemia
- Same FH/secondary causes as LDL-C
- Dysbetalipoproteinemia (type III hyperlipidemia, APOE2/2): elevated TC, elevated TG, elevated non-HDL-C, palmar xanthomas

### Confounders / acute movers
- Does not require fasting (unlike Friedewald LDL-C).
- Same illness/pregnancy/medication confounders as TC.

## 5. Triglycerides (TG)

### What it is
Triglycerides are three fatty acids esterified to a glycerol backbone — the body's primary form of stored and transported fat energy. Plasma TG reflects the cholesterol carried in triglyceride-rich lipoproteins: chylomicrons (from dietary fat) and VLDL (from hepatic synthesis), plus their remnants. [Source: Endotext "Triglycerides," NCBI Bookshelf NBK326743.]

### Physiological function / mechanism
- **Chylomicrons** carry dietary TG from the gut to peripheral tissues, where lipoprotein lipase (LPL) hydrolyzes the TG to free fatty acids for muscle/adipose tissue.
- **VLDL** carries hepatic-synthesized TG (often from excess dietary carbohydrate / de novo lipogenesis) to peripheral tissues. VLDL → IDL → LDL via LPL action.
- Insulin promotes adipose TG storage and inhibits hepatic VLDL output. **Insulin resistance** disinhibits VLDL secretion → hypertriglyceridemia.
- Each VLDL/chylomicron remnant particle carries one apoB. Remnant cholesterol (TC − HDL-C − LDL-C) is highly atherogenic. [Source: Nordestgaard, *Circ Res* 2016.]

### What HIGH values indicate
- **Insulin resistance / metabolic syndrome / NAFLD** — by far the most common cause of mild-to-moderate hypertriglyceridemia (150–500 mg/dL) in adults.
- **Diet**: high refined carbohydrate / sugar (especially fructose), excess alcohol, weight gain.
- **Type 2 diabetes** (poorly controlled).
- **Nephrotic syndrome, hypothyroidism**.
- **Drugs**: oral estrogens, tamoxifen, retinoids, atypical antipsychotics, beta-blockers, thiazides, glucocorticoids, protease inhibitors, immunosuppressants.
- **Genetic**:
  - **Familial combined hyperlipidemia** (TG 200–500, mixed pattern).
  - **Familial hypertriglyceridemia** (polygenic, TG 250–1000).
  - **Familial chylomicronemia syndrome (FCS)** — rare monogenic LPL/APOC2/APOA5/GPIHBP1/LMF1 mutations, TG often >2000 mg/dL with childhood-onset pancreatitis.
  - **Type III dysbetalipoproteinemia** (APOE2/2 + a second hit).
- **Pancreatitis risk**: TG >1000 mg/dL → ~10% risk of acute pancreatitis; TG >2000 → ~20% risk. Hypertriglyceridemia is the 3rd leading cause of acute pancreatitis (after gallstones and alcohol). [Source: Endotext NBK279082, *Pancreatitis Secondary to Hypertriglyceridemia*; NLA + ASPC 2024 consensus on persistent chylomicronemia.]

### What LOW values indicate
- Generally benign in healthy people. Very low TG (<40 mg/dL fasting) can indicate:
  - **Hyperthyroidism** (TG often <50 in untreated cases)
  - **Severe malnutrition** or **malabsorption** (celiac, IBD, cystic fibrosis)
  - **Genetic**: abetalipoproteinemia, hypobetalipoproteinemia, PCSK9 loss-of-function, APOC3 loss-of-function (low TG, *protective* against CVD — the basis for volanesorsen and other antisense therapies).
  - **Acute starvation** or **anorexia nervosa** can lower TG.
  [Source: SiPhox, "What causes very low triglyceride levels?" (synthesis citing endocrine literature).]

### Direction of "better"
**Mostly monotonic — lower is better** for CVD risk and pancreatitis risk, with the caveat that very low values (<40 mg/dL) in a non-medicated person should prompt evaluation for hyperthyroidism, malabsorption, or genetic causes. Mendelian randomization (Voight, Do, et al., *Nat Genet* 2013; Dewey et al., *NEJM* 2017 on APOC3) supports a causal CVD effect of triglyceride-rich lipoproteins, particularly the remnant cholesterol they carry.

### Fasting vs. non-fasting (special note)
- **Historic standard**: fasting 9–12 hours.
- **2016 EAS/EFLM consensus** + **2019 ESC/EAS Guidelines** + AHA 2018 statement: **non-fasting lipid measurement is acceptable and even preferred in many contexts**. Postprandial TG actually correlates better with CV events because most of life is non-fasting. [Source: Nordestgaard et al., *Eur Heart J* 2016 EAS/EFLM consensus; 2019 ESC/EAS Guidelines, Mach et al.]
- **Non-fasting cutoffs**: TG ≥175 mg/dL (2 mmol/L) considered elevated. Fasting cutoff ≥150 mg/dL.
- **US guidelines (NLA, 2018 AHA/ACC)** still permit fasting or non-fasting; if non-fasting TG >400, repeat fasting before action.

### Conventional/lab reference range (NCEP/AHA)
**Fasting**:
- **Normal**: <150 mg/dL
- **Borderline-high**: 150–199 mg/dL
- **High**: 200–499 mg/dL
- **Very high**: ≥500 mg/dL (pancreatitis risk concern)

**Non-fasting (ESC 2019)**: <175 mg/dL is "normal" (vs. <150 fasting).

**ESC/EAS 2019 categories** (fasting):
- Optimal: <100 mg/dL (<1.2 mmol/L)
- Borderline: 100–150 mg/dL (1.2–1.7)
- Moderately elevated: 150–500 mg/dL (1.7–5.7)
- Severe: 500–880 mg/dL (5.7–10.0)
- Extreme: >880 mg/dL (>10) — pancreatitis emergency
[Source: Mach et al., *Eur Heart J* 2020.]

### Optimal range for a healthy 20-year-old male
- **AHA**: <150 mg/dL fasting.
- **ESC/EAS optimal**: <100 mg/dL fasting.
- **NLA**: <150 mg/dL.
- **Hyman**: <100 mg/dL. [drhyman.com]
- **Casey Means / Levels**: ideally <80 mg/dL; <70 mg/dL is "very metabolically healthy." [caseymeans.com]
- **Attia**: doesn't fixate on TG in isolation but treats >150 fasting as a flag of insulin resistance and a driver of remnant cholesterol; targets paralleling apoB <60 typically have TG <80. [peterattiamd.com]

### TG/HDL ratio (insulin resistance proxy)
TG/HDL ratio is a **simple, validated proxy for insulin resistance** and small-dense-LDL phenotype:
- **Optimal**: <1.5 (Casey Means' target) [caseymeans.com]
- **<2.0**: good insulin sensitivity [Da Luz et al., *Clinics* 2008]
- **2.0–3.5**: emerging insulin resistance
- **>3.5**: significant insulin resistance, increased CVD risk
- Sex/ethnicity-specific cutoffs: ~2.5 for women, ~2.8 for men in one synthesis (Quispe et al., *Atherosclerosis* 2015). African-Americans: 2.0–2.5; Whites/Hispanics: ~3.0.
- [Source: Salazar et al., *Biomedicines* 2024 (MDPI), "TG/HDL Ratio as a Surrogate Biomarker for Insulin Resistance."]

### Specific conditions/diseases it can flag
- Insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome (NCEP ATP III: TG ≥150 = 1 of 5 criteria)
- NAFLD / MASLD
- Type 2 diabetes (poorly controlled)
- Familial combined hyperlipidemia
- Familial hypertriglyceridemia
- Familial chylomicronemia syndrome (rare; TG >2000)
- Dysbetalipoproteinemia (APOE2/2)
- Hypothyroidism (raises TG modestly)
- Nephrotic syndrome
- For low TG: hyperthyroidism, malabsorption, abetalipoproteinemia, APOC3 loss-of-function (protective)

### Confounders / acute movers
- **Largest confounder**: meal timing — TG can rise 50–100% 4 hours after a fatty meal.
- **Alcohol** — acutely raises TG; even one drink can spike TG by 20–50% in susceptible people. Inhibits LPL acutely. [Van de Wiel, *Int J Vasc Med* 2012.]
- **Acute illness/inflammation** raises TG.
- **Recent intense exercise** can transiently lower TG (next 24–48h).
- **24-hour fast** — TG falls ~50 mg/dL [HCPLive, *Cholesterol Levels Rise After a 24-Hour Fast*].
- **Pregnancy** raises TG 2-3x.
- **High-carb / high-sugar binge** in prior 1–3 days raises TG significantly.

## 6. Total Cholesterol / HDL Ratio (TC:HDL or "Cholesterol Ratio")

### What it is
A simple ratio: TC ÷ HDL-C. It is a derived metric that captures the balance between atherogenic and protective cholesterol fractions in a single number. Used historically in the Framingham Risk Score. [Source: Kannel et al., *Lipids and Risk of Coronary Heart Disease — The Framingham Study*, *Ann Epidemiol* 1992.]

### Physiological function / mechanism
Not a biological entity — a derived risk metric. The biological intuition: TC includes LDL/VLDL/IDL (atherogenic) plus HDL (protective). Dividing by HDL gives a measure of "atherogenic load per unit protection." Captures dyslipidemic phenotypes that might be missed by TC or LDL-C alone (e.g., normal TC with very low HDL).

### What HIGH values indicate
- High atherogenic-particle burden relative to HDL — increased CVD risk.
- Framingham analyses: TC:HDL ratio in the highest tertile carried ~2-3x the CHD risk of the lowest tertile in both men and women. The ratio remained an independent predictor in older adults (men 65–80) when LDL-C alone did not. [Source: Ingelsson et al., *JAMA* 2007; Castelli, *Am J Cardiol* 1988.]
- Driven by either: high TC (high LDL/VLDL) or low HDL (or both — typical in metabolic syndrome).

### What LOW values indicate
- Favorable lipid pattern. No clear concerning lower limit.

### Direction of "better"
**Monotonic — lower is better** within physiologic ranges. Caveat: if the low ratio is being driven by *very high* HDL (>90 mg/dL men), the U-shaped HDL mortality data still applies.

### Conventional/lab reference range (Framingham-derived)
- **Optimal**: <3.5
- **Average risk** (men): ~5
- **High risk** (men): >5
- **Very high risk**: >9 (men), >7 (women)
[Source: Castelli, *Am J Cardiol* 1988; Framingham Heart Study; Cooper Institute summary.]

### Optimal range for a healthy 20-year-old male
- **AHA / Framingham**: <3.5
- **Hyman (functional)**: <3.0 [drhyman.com]
- **Mayo Clinic**: <5; "ideal" <3.5
- **Practical synthesis**: <3.5 is optimal; <3.0 is excellent. A 20-year-old male with TC 170 and HDL 60 has a ratio of 2.83 — excellent.

### Specific conditions/diseases it can flag
- Same conditions as elevated LDL/non-HDL or low HDL — metabolic syndrome, FH, FCH, polygenic dyslipidemia.
- High ratio with normal LDL-C → likely metabolic syndrome / atherogenic-dyslipidemia phenotype (high TG, low HDL, normal-ish LDL).

### Confounders / acute movers
- Inherits all the confounders of TC and HDL.
- Alcohol raises HDL more than TC → can artificially lower the ratio.
- Acute illness lowers HDL transiently → can artificially raise the ratio.

### Caveats from the lipidology field
The TC:HDL ratio has been **largely superseded by apoB and non-HDL-C** in modern guidelines (2018 AHA/ACC, 2019 ESC/EAS, 2026 ACC/AHA). It's still useful as a quick screening glance but should not drive treatment decisions in 2026. [Source: 2018 AHA/ACC Cholesterol Guideline, *Circulation*.]

## 7. Apolipoprotein B (apoB)

### What it is
ApoB is the structural protein of all atherogenic lipoproteins. There are two isoforms:
- **apoB-100**: synthesized by the liver, found on VLDL, IDL, LDL, and Lp(a). One apoB-100 per particle.
- **apoB-48**: synthesized by the intestine, found on chylomicrons. One apoB-48 per particle.

The standard clinical "apoB" assay measures both, giving the **total number of atherogenic lipoprotein particles** in plasma. Reported in mg/dL. Measured by immunoturbidimetric assay; standardized against the WHO reference material. [Source: Sniderman et al., *J Am Heart Assoc* 2024 — "Standardization of Apolipoprotein B, LDL-Cholesterol, and Non-HDL-Cholesterol."]

### Physiological function / mechanism
ApoB is the **ligand for the LDL receptor** and the structural scaffold that holds atherogenic lipoproteins together. Each LDL/VLDL/IDL/Lp(a) particle carries exactly one apoB-100 molecule. The atherogenic process — particle infiltration into the arterial intima, retention by intimal proteoglycans, oxidation, foam-cell formation — is **driven by particle number, not cholesterol content per particle**.

This is the central insight: a person can have "normal" LDL-C but high apoB if their LDL particles are small and cholesterol-poor (typical in insulin resistance / metabolic syndrome). The number of particles bombarding the arterial wall is what matters, not how much cholesterol each carries. [Source: Sniderman et al., "Apolipoprotein B Particles and Cardiovascular Disease: A Narrative Review," *JAMA Cardiology* 2019;4(12):1287-1295.]

### Why apoB is superior to LDL-C
- **Sniderman meta-analysis** (Sniderman, Williams, Contois et al., *Circ Cardiovasc Qual Outcomes* 2011): 12 prospective studies, 233,455 subjects, 22,950 events. Head-to-head:
  - apoB RRR = 1.43 (95% CI 1.35–1.51)
  - non-HDL-C RRR = 1.34 (1.24–1.44)
  - LDL-C RRR = 1.25 (1.18–1.33)
  - apoB was 12% better than LDL-C; over 10 years, an apoB-driven strategy would prevent ~500,000 more events than non-HDL-C.
- **Mendelian randomization** (Ference et al., *JACC* 2018; Richardson et al., *PLoS Med* 2020): when apoB and LDL-C are both included as instrumental variables, **apoB explains essentially all the genetic risk**, and LDL-C adds nothing once apoB is accounted for. Causally, it is the particles that matter.
- **2019 ESC/EAS Guidelines** explicitly state apoB is a more accurate marker of CV risk than LDL-C or non-HDL-C, particularly when TG are elevated, in metabolic syndrome, T2DM, or very low LDL-C. [Source: Mach et al., *Eur Heart J* 2020.]
- **Discordance** between apoB and LDL-C is most pronounced in insulin resistance, where small-dense-LDL particles → more particles per unit LDL-C → apoB > LDL-C-predicted. Wilkins et al. (CARDIA, *J Clin Lipidol* 2016) showed in young adults that apoB-LDL-C discordance predicted coronary calcium independent of LDL-C.
- **Mendelian randomization on apoB** (Richardson et al., *PLoS Med* 2020): each 1-SD genetic increase in apoB → ~1.4x CHD risk; LDL-C added nothing once apoB was conditioned on.

### What HIGH values indicate
- Increased atherogenic-particle burden → CV risk. Causally established.
- Common drivers:
  - **Genetic**: FH (apoB usually >130–150 mg/dL), familial combined hyperlipidemia (apoB high with mild-to-moderate LDL-C and TG elevation).
  - **Metabolic syndrome / insulin resistance / T2DM / NAFLD**: many small dense LDL particles → high apoB even with "normal" LDL-C.
  - **Hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, cholestasis**, dysbetalipoproteinemia.
- **Discordant pattern**: apoB > expected from LDL-C is the classic insulin-resistance signal — it is the metabolic-syndrome lipid phenotype made visible.

### What LOW values indicate
- Generally favorable. Very low apoB (<40 mg/dL) without medication can indicate:
  - **PCSK9 loss-of-function** (genetically protective)
  - **APOB loss-of-function** (familial hypobetalipoproteinemia)
  - **Severe liver disease**, **malnutrition**, **hyperthyroidism**.
- Lifelong low apoB (e.g., from PCSK9 LOF) is associated with very low CV risk and no clear adverse effects. [Source: Cohen et al., *NEJM* 2006.]

### Direction of "better"
**Monotonic — lower is better — for CVD.** Mendelian randomization supports this all the way down to the lowest measured genetic levels. Attia argues for "primordial prevention" — keep apoB low *from young adulthood* to prevent atherosclerosis from initiating. Richardson et al. (*PLoS Med* 2020) found a dose-dependent CV benefit across the entire apoB distribution. [Sources: Ference et al. *JACC* 2018; Richardson et al. *PLoS Med* 2020; Attia AMA #43.]

### Conventional/lab reference range (LabCorp/Quest)
- LabCorp typical reporting: **adult male 66–133 mg/dL, adult female 60–117 mg/dL** as "normal." [Source: LabCorp test 167015.]
- These population-derived ranges represent ~5th–95th percentile of *unselected adults in industrialized countries* — a population with high background ASCVD. They should not be confused with optimal.

### Optimal range for a healthy 20-year-old male
- **2019 ESC/EAS targets** by risk stratum:
  - Low risk: <100 mg/dL
  - Moderate risk: <100 mg/dL
  - High risk: <80 mg/dL
  - Very high risk: <65 mg/dL
- **2026 ACC/AHA Dyslipidemia Guideline**: apoB targets aligning with LDL-C goals — **<55, <70, or <90 mg/dL** depending on risk category. ApoB recommended particularly for residual-risk assessment in T2DM, metabolic syndrome, high TG, or known ASCVD after LDL-C goal achieved. [Source: 2026 ACC/AHA, *Circulation* CIR.0000000000001423.]
- **NLA 2015 (reaffirmed 2024)**: apoB <80 high-risk, <90 mg/dL primary prevention. [Source: Jacobson et al., *J Clin Lipidol* 2015.]
- **Peter Attia (primordial prevention)**: **apoB <60 mg/dL** is the long-running explicit target; "if practical, even lower — 20–30 mg/dL would likely eliminate ASCVD as a public health problem." Argues for treating young adults aggressively if at the population median. [Source: peterattiamd.com #229, #238 AMA #43, "Early and aggressive lowering of apoB."]
- **Mark Hyman**: emphasizes apoB and LDL-P over LDL-C; doesn't publish a single number but aligns with NLA targets. [drhyman.com Ep856.]
- **Levels (Means/Lustig)**: aligned with Attia framing.
- **Ference 10 mg/dL framing**: each 10 mg/dL lower apoB → ~9% lower CV event risk. [Source: Ference et al., *JACC* 2018.]
- **Working synthesis for a healthy 20-year-old male**: <90 is "normal," <80 is "good," <60 is "excellent / Attia-target," <40 is "primordial-prevention range."

### Specific conditions/diseases it can flag
- Familial hypercholesterolemia (apoB usually >130)
- Familial combined hyperlipidemia (apoB elevated out of proportion to LDL-C)
- Metabolic syndrome / insulin resistance phenotype (apoB-LDL-C discordance)
- Type 2 diabetes
- NAFLD / MASLD
- Dysbetalipoproteinemia (APOE2/2)
- Same secondary causes as elevated LDL-C

### Confounders / acute movers
- Less affected by fasting status than TG and Friedewald LDL-C — generally **non-fasting is fine**.
- Recent illness lowers apoB (acute-phase response).
- Pregnancy raises apoB.
- Saturated fat intake raises apoB modestly.
- Alcohol's effect on apoB is variable.
- Compared to LDL-C, apoB is more reproducible across labs once standardized.

### Limitations
- Doesn't distinguish LDL from VLDL from Lp(a) — it lumps all atherogenic particles. For a more granular view, NMR LDL-P or Lp(a) measurement is needed.
- Lp(a) particles each carry one apoB-100 — so people with very high Lp(a) can have elevated apoB driven by Lp(a) rather than LDL. Always measure Lp(a) at least once in life. [Source: NLA 2024 statement on Lp(a).]
- Standardization across assays has improved but historically varied; results from different platforms can differ ~5–10%.

## 8. High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein (hs-CRP)

### What it is
C-reactive protein is an acute-phase pentraxin protein produced by the liver in response to IL-6 (and other cytokines like IL-1β, TNF-α). The **high-sensitivity** assay (hs-CRP) detects very low concentrations (<0.5 mg/L), enabling its use as a chronic low-grade inflammation marker for CV risk stratification — distinct from standard CRP, which is used for acute infection/inflammation. Reported in mg/L (preferred for cardiac risk) or mg/dL. [Source: AHA/CDC Scientific Statement, *Circulation* 2003 — Pearson et al.]

### Physiological function / mechanism
CRP is a soluble pattern-recognition molecule that binds phosphocholine on damaged cell surfaces and bacterial polysaccharides, activating the classical complement pathway and opsonizing for phagocytosis. In the cardiovascular context, hs-CRP is best understood as a **downstream marker of IL-6 signaling** — itself reflecting upstream inflammation in adipose tissue, atherosclerotic plaque, and metabolic dysfunction.

The CANTOS trial (Ridker et al., *NEJM* 2017) directly tested the inflammatory hypothesis of atherosclerosis: canakinumab (anti-IL-1β monoclonal antibody) lowered hs-CRP and IL-6 *without* changing LDL-C, and reduced major adverse CV events by 15% (150 mg dose) in post-MI patients with hs-CRP ≥2 mg/L. This was the first proof that targeting inflammation alone reduces CV events. [Source: Ridker et al., *NEJM* 2017;377:1119-1131.]

### Key trial: JUPITER
JUPITER (Ridker et al., *NEJM* 2008): 17,802 healthy adults with LDL-C <130 mg/dL but **hs-CRP ≥2 mg/L** randomized to rosuvastatin 20 mg vs. placebo. Stopped early; rosuvastatin reduced primary endpoint by 44% (HR 0.56), MI/stroke/CV death by 47%, all-cause mortality by 20%. JUPITER established hs-CRP as a clinically actionable risk marker — even when LDL-C is "normal," elevated hs-CRP identifies a population that benefits from statins. [Source: Ridker et al., *NEJM* 2008;359:2195-2207.]

### What HIGH values indicate
- **Chronic systemic low-grade inflammation** → elevated CV risk independent of lipids.
- **>20 prospective studies** confirm hs-CRP as an independent predictor of MI, stroke, CV death after adjusting for traditional risk factors. [Source: Ridker reviews; Pearson et al., *Circulation* 2003.]
- **Causes of mildly elevated hs-CRP (1–10 mg/L)**:
  - Obesity (adipose tissue is inflammatory; ~strongest single driver in young adults)
  - Insulin resistance / metabolic syndrome / T2DM / NAFLD
  - Smoking
  - Periodontal disease
  - Sleep deprivation, OSA
  - Sedentary behavior
  - Diet high in ultra-processed foods, low in fiber
  - Chronic infections (H. pylori, hepatitis, HIV, TB)
  - Chronic inflammatory conditions (RA, psoriasis, IBD, lupus, ankylosing spondylitis)
  - Estrogen exposure (oral contraceptives, hormone therapy raise hs-CRP 2-4x without raising CV risk — likely a measurement artifact, not true inflammation)
  - Pregnancy
  - Genetic / ethnicity (African ancestry tends to higher hs-CRP at baseline)
- **Very high values (>10 mg/L)**: should be re-tested in 2 weeks; suggests acute infection, trauma, or active flare of chronic inflammatory disease — **discard for CV risk stratification** at these levels.

### What LOW values indicate
- Generally favorable — low chronic inflammation. The JUPITER follow-up data and Ridker analyses (*Circulation* 2004) suggest hs-CRP <0.5 mg/L is associated with very low future CV event rates.
- Concerns: rare. Immunosuppression and advanced liver disease can blunt the CRP response, theoretically masking true infection. Not a clinical worry in healthy individuals.

### Direction of "better"
**Monotonic — lower is better — for CV risk**, with the caveat that a "very low" hs-CRP in someone with active infection might reflect a blunted hepatic acute-phase response (very rare in young healthy people). For practical purposes in a 20-year-old male, lower is unambiguously better. [Source: Ridker, *Circulation* 2004; AHA/CDC 2003 statement.]

### Conventional/lab reference range (AHA/CDC 2003 — still standard)
- **Low CV risk**: <1 mg/L
- **Average / moderate CV risk**: 1–3 mg/L
- **High CV risk**: >3 mg/L
- **>10 mg/L**: re-test; reflects acute inflammation, not chronic — discard for risk stratification.
[Source: Pearson et al., *Circulation* 2003; AHA hs-CRP Toolkit.]

LabCorp / Quest typically report "<1, 1–3, >3 mg/L" tertiles in line with AHA/CDC. Quest hs-CRP reporting range typically 0.1–10 mg/L.

### Optimal range for a healthy 20-year-old male
- **AHA/CDC**: <1 mg/L = low risk.
- **Ridker / Levels / Attia**: aim for **<1 mg/L**; ideally **<0.5 mg/L**. Ridker has noted that very-low hs-CRP correlates with very-low event rates, suggesting "the lower the better" within physiologic ranges. [Sources: Ridker, *Circulation* 2004; Attia podcasts; Levels guide.]
- **Hyman (functional)**: <1 mg/L. [drhyman.com]
- **Median in healthy young adults**: ~0.8 mg/L (AHA hs-CRP toolkit data); 90th percentile ~3.0 mg/L; 99th percentile ~10.
- **Sex difference**: women run ~2.5 mg/L median, men ~1.5 mg/L median in US data (oral contraceptives a major driver in women). For a 20yo male, **<1 mg/L is the practical optimal target**.

### Specific conditions/diseases it can flag
- Subclinical inflammation from obesity, insulin resistance, NAFLD, OSA — most actionable in a young person
- Periodontal disease (commonly unrecognized driver in young adults)
- Active chronic inflammatory disease (RA, IBD, psoriasis, lupus)
- Occult infection
- For very-high values (>10): acute infection, recent surgery/trauma, malignancy

### Confounders / acute movers (CRITICAL — most-confounded marker in this panel)
- **Acute infection** (URI, UTI, COVID, etc.) raises hs-CRP 10–1000-fold; can persist 4–6 weeks. **Do not interpret hs-CRP for CV risk within ~2 weeks of any acute illness or vaccination.**
- **Recent intense exercise** (marathon, heavy resistance) raises hs-CRP for 24–72h.
- **Recent injury / surgery / dental procedure** raises hs-CRP for days to weeks.
- **Acute psychological stress** (modest effect).
- **Oral contraceptives / oral estrogens**: raise hs-CRP 2–4x without raising CV risk (artifact of hepatic estrogen exposure).
- **Chronic inflammatory disease flare**.
- **Periodontal disease** — frequently overlooked.
- **Pregnancy** — hs-CRP rises significantly.
- **Obesity** — adipose tissue continually secretes IL-6.
- AHA recommendation: **average two readings 2 weeks apart**, both at baseline (no acute illness). If >10 mg/L, discard and repeat after 2 weeks. [Source: AHA hs-CRP Toolkit; CAP guidance 2025.]

### Limitations
- Non-specific — elevated hs-CRP doesn't tell you *what* is inflamed.
- Highly variable within individuals; single measurements are noisy.
- Confounded heavily by acute illness/exercise.
- Newer biomarkers may eventually supplant it:
  - **GlycA** (NMR-derived glycoprotein acetyls) — more stable than hs-CRP; reflects multiple inflammatory glycoproteins. [Connelly et al., *Translational Research* 2017.]
  - **IL-6** — may be more upstream and causally relevant per Mendelian randomization. [MESA study, *Atherosclerosis* 2024.]
  - **Lp-PLA2** — vascular-specific inflammation, but trials of darapladib failed.
- For a 20yo male, hs-CRP remains the most clinically useful inflammation marker and is included in Function's basic heart panel. [Source: 2018 AHA/ACC and 2019 ESC/EAS guidelines list hs-CRP as a "risk-enhancer" for borderline-risk patients.]
