# Nutrients, Vitamins, and Minerals Panel — Comprehensive Reference

**Subject context:** 20-year-old male. Reference compiled for interpreting Function Health blood test results. Citations are inline; this is a synthesis of guideline documents (AASLD, EASL, BSG/BSH, IOM/NAM, Endocrine Society), peer-reviewed reviews (Stabler, Lindenbaum, Holick, Manson/VITAL, Neogi/D-Health), and large epidemiologic datasets (NHANES). Where evidence is weak (e.g., serum Zn or serum Mg as whole-body status indicators), I flag the limitation rather than smoothing it over.

**General framing — claim provenance:**
- "Optimal" ranges cited from functional/integrative medicine sources are *not* the same evidence class as IOM RDAs or randomized trials. Where a "performance optimal" target is used, I label it as such.
- Most of these biomarkers are **U-shaped** — too low and too high are both pathologic. Iron stores, calcium, magnesium, zinc, and vitamin D all fit this. Homocysteine is the main exception (monotonic — lower is better, with a floor).
- All ranges in **conventional U.S. units** (ng/mL for vitamin D, mg/dL for serum metals/minerals, µg/dL for iron, etc.). SI conversions noted where relevant.

## 1. Ferritin

### What it is
Ferritin is the intracellular iron-storage protein. Each ferritin shell holds up to ~4,500 Fe³⁺ atoms. A small fraction of cellular ferritin leaks into serum; **serum ferritin under steady-state conditions is the best single estimate of total body iron stores** — except when inflammation is present.

**Assay:** Immunoassay (chemiluminescent, ECLIA, or older ELISA). No standardized reference material across labs — inter-assay CV can run 10–15%. Ferritin is reported in ng/mL (≡ µg/L).

**Critical caveat — ferritin is a positive acute-phase reactant.** Hepatocytes upregulate ferritin synthesis in response to IL-6, IL-1, and TNF-α via NF-κB ([Kell & Pretorius 2014, StatPearls Acute Phase Reactants](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK519570/)). The same cytokine cascade drives **hepcidin** (master iron regulator made in liver), which binds ferroportin on enterocytes and macrophages and triggers its degradation, blocking iron absorption and trapping iron inside macrophages — this is the mechanism of **anemia of chronic disease (ACD)**. Net effect: in inflammation, **ferritin rises while iron availability for erythropoiesis falls**. Ferritin can therefore be normal or high in a patient who is truly iron-deficient ([Daru 2017 — Limitations of serum ferritin in inflammatory conditions](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5878890/), [ASH Hematology 2020](https://ashpublications.org/hematology/article/2020/1/478/474369/)).

### Physiology
- Stores Fe safely (free iron is a Fenton-reaction catalyst → ROS)
- Provides reserve for erythropoiesis (Hb synthesis), myoglobin, cytochromes, ribonucleotide reductase, etc.
- Reflects ~30% body iron in liver/spleen/marrow stores; ~70% functional in Hb

### Reference ranges and interpretation
| Value (ng/mL, men) | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| <15 | Iron deficiency, essentially diagnostic ([WHO 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5878890/)) |
| <30 | Iron deficiency by [BSH/BSG 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8515119/) — most sensitive cutoff |
| 30–45 | Possible iron depletion; some labs use <45 in primary care ([JAMA 2024 ferritin cutoffs](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2821953)) |
| 30–100 with low TSAT (<20%) | Functional iron deficiency in inflammation ([BSG 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8515119/)) |
| 50–150 | Commonly cited "optimal" for young men (functional medicine framing — *not* IOM-validated) |
| 100–300 | Adequate stores; borderline for liver-health-conscious framing |
| >300 (men) | Threshold for hemochromatosis workup if TSAT also >45–50% ([AASLD 2011 / EASL 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35662478/)) |
| >1000 | Significant iron overload, organ damage risk; consider phlebotomy ([AASLD](https://www.aasld.org/practice-guidelines/management-hemochromatosis)) |

**Conventional lab "normal":** Most U.S. labs report 24–336 ng/mL or similar wide range for men. This is too wide for clinical use — values <30 are commonly missed because they fall inside "normal."

**Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male:** **50–150 ng/mL** is the consensus from non-conflicting expert framings. Some practitioners argue **30–80 ng/mL** to avoid passive iron loading from typical diet + occult HFE heterozygosity. AASLD and liver-health-oriented sources prefer **<200** for men to minimize hepatic iron exposure over decades ([AASLD Liver Fellow Network](https://www.aasld.org/liver-fellow-network/core-series/clinical-pearls/deciphering-code-iron-overload)). For a young man without RLS, hair loss, or athletic performance concerns, anywhere in 50–150 is fine; with athletic performance concerns or low-normal hemoglobin, target the upper part of that range.

### Direction of "better" — U-shaped
- **Low (<30):** classic iron deficiency. Causes: GI blood loss (occult — colonoscopy if unexplained low ferritin in a man, since menstrual loss isn't a confound for males), celiac disease, H. pylori, dietary inadequacy (low in vegetarians/vegans without supplementation), endurance training (foot-strike hemolysis, sweat losses, hepcidin elevation post-exercise), frequent blood donation.
- **Low symptoms:** fatigue, exercise intolerance/reduced VO₂max ([studies cited in iron-deficiency reviews](https://scienceinsights.org/what-happens-if-your-ferritin-is-too-low/)) even with normal Hb, restless legs syndrome (CSF iron is the more proximal driver, but serum ferritin <50 is associated with RLS prevalence and severity — [Allen 2018, multiple RLS Foundation reviews](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6183309/)), hair loss (telogen effluvium; dermatology consensus often targets ferritin >70 ng/mL for hair concerns — *evidence quality moderate*), pica, glossitis, koilonychia.
- **High (>300 men):** hereditary hemochromatosis (HFE C282Y homozygotes, ~1:200 in Northern European descent — [NEJM 1999](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199909023411002), [Wikipedia/AAFP review](https://www.aafp.org/pubs/afp/issues/2021/0900/p263.html)), inflammation/infection, alcoholic liver disease, NAFLD/MASLD (dysmetabolic iron overload — common at metabolic-syndrome patients), excess iron supplementation, transfusion overload, hyperthyroidism, malignancy (especially Hodgkin lymphoma, hepatocellular carcinoma), Still's disease and macrophage activation syndromes (ferritin can be >10,000 in MAS/HLH — diagnostic threshold).
- **High symptoms (overload, typically >1000):** fatigue, joint pain (especially 2nd/3rd MCP — "iron fist" arthropathy), erectile dysfunction (pituitary iron deposition → secondary hypogonadism), skin bronzing, "bronze diabetes" from pancreatic iron, cardiomyopathy, hepatic fibrosis/cirrhosis, hepatocellular carcinoma.

### HFE hemochromatosis screening (relevant for any young man with high-normal ferritin)
- HFE genotype: C282Y/C282Y homozygotes account for ~85–95% of typical HH cases ([GeneReviews](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK1440/)). C282Y/H63D compound heterozygotes carry low penetrance ([JCAG 2022](https://academic.oup.com/jcag/article/5/5/240/6651012)).
- **Screen with TSAT + ferritin:** TSAT >45% AND ferritin >300 in men → order HFE genotyping ([AASLD 2011](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3149125/), [EASL 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35662478/)).
- 23andMe data covers C282Y (rs1800562) and H63D (rs1799945). Worth checking if ferritin trends high.
- Population screening is not recommended (low penetrance), but cascade screening of first-degree relatives of confirmed cases is.

### Confounders for ferritin
- **Inflammation** (CRP, IL-6 elevation) — measure CRP alongside; if CRP >5 mg/L, ferritin is unreliable as iron-status marker; use TSAT and consider soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR) instead.
- **Recent illness** can elevate ferritin for weeks
- **Alcohol** intake elevates ferritin without iron overload (ALD signal)
- **NAFLD/metabolic syndrome** — dysmetabolic iron overload elevates ferritin with normal/mild TSAT
- **Liver injury of any cause** releases hepatocellular ferritin
- Hyperthyroidism, hemolysis
- **Acute exercise / endurance training** transiently elevates ferritin (acute phase) but chronic endurance athletes often run *low* ferritin

## 2. Iron (Serum)

### What it is
Serum iron measures the iron currently bound to transferrin in circulation — typically only ~3–4 mg of body's ~3500–4500 mg total iron. **It is highly variable and not a reliable single-snapshot indicator of iron stores.**

**Assay:** Colorimetric (ferrozine or BPT methods); inexpensive but susceptible to hemolysis interference. Reported in µg/dL (×0.179 = µmol/L).

### Physiology
Serum iron equals iron loaded onto transferrin (Tf) — the body's iron transport protein, mostly synthesized by liver. Transferrin shuttles iron from gut/macrophages/liver stores to bone marrow erythroblasts and other consumers via the transferrin receptor (TfR1). Internalization, acidification of the endosome, release of iron, recycling of apo-Tf.

### Reference ranges
- **Conventional adult male:** 65–175 µg/dL (varies by lab — Mayo, LabCorp report similar ranges)
- **SI:** 11.6–31.3 µmol/L
- **Optimal range:** mid-range, ~80–130 µg/dL — there is no validated "optimal" because the value swings dramatically. Persistent low values matter; persistent high values matter; a single low or high in isolation doesn't.

### Diurnal variation — critical
Serum iron has substantial **circadian/diurnal variation**: peaks late morning (~11 AM in adult men, 12 PM in women), declines through afternoon and evening, with troughs overnight ([AJCP 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12090432/), [Kim 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28947322/)). Day-to-day within-person variability can be 30–50%. A morning fasted draw partly standardizes this, but the value can still differ 20%+ from one morning to the next.

A recent 2024 study confirms serum iron and TSAT are circadian-regulated, linked to hepatic Tfrc expression and erythropoiesis cycles ([AJH 2024](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/ajh.27447)).

### What high/low indicates
**Low serum iron:**
- True iron deficiency (paired with high TIBC, low TSAT, low ferritin)
- Anemia of chronic disease/inflammation (paired with **low** TIBC, low TSAT, normal/high ferritin) — distinguishing feature is TIBC direction
- Recent meal effect (postprandial dip), late-day draw
- Acute infection (cytokine-driven hepcidin → iron sequestration in macrophages)

**High serum iron:**
- Iron overload/hemochromatosis (with high TSAT, high ferritin)
- Iron supplementation taken hours before draw (false high — recommend hold supplements 24h before iron studies)
- Hemolysis (in vitro or in vivo)
- Liver disease releasing stored iron
- Lead poisoning
- Excessive transfusion

### Direction of "better" — U-shaped, but driven by TSAT not absolute iron
Serum iron in isolation is most useful only as input to TSAT calculation. Don't over-interpret a single value.

### Confounders
- Time of day, fasting state (fasted morning is convention)
- Iron supplements within 24h (false elevation)
- Hemolysis of sample
- Recent transfusion
- Acute illness/inflammation
- Oral contraceptive pills (raise transferrin → can affect interpretation)

## 3. Iron % Saturation (Transferrin Saturation, TSAT)

### What it is
**TSAT = (Serum Iron / TIBC) × 100**, expressed as %. It estimates what fraction of transferrin's iron-binding sites are currently occupied. **TSAT is the single most useful snapshot indicator of iron-supply status** for both deficiency and overload screening, especially in inflammation where ferritin is unreliable.

### Reference ranges
- **Conventional:** 20–50% in men ([Medscape, LabCorp](https://emedicine.medscape.com/article/2087960-overview))
- **Iron deficiency:** TSAT <20%, often <16%
- **Hemochromatosis screening:** TSAT >45% in men is the AASLD/EASL trigger; >50% specifically suggested by EASL 2022
- **Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male:** **25–40%**. Above 45–50% triggers hemochromatosis workup; below 20% suggests iron-supply issue.

### Iron-study patterns (memorize this table)
| Condition | Serum Fe | TIBC | TSAT | Ferritin |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Iron deficiency | ↓ | ↑ | ↓ | ↓ |
| Anemia of chronic disease | ↓ | ↓ or N | ↓ or N | N or ↑ |
| Hemochromatosis / iron overload | ↑ | ↓ | ↑ (>45%) | ↑ |
| Combined IDA + ACD | ↓ | N | ↓ | N or borderline |
| Hemolysis | ↑ | N | ↑ | ↑ |
| Pregnancy / OCP | N | ↑ | N or ↓ | N |

[Sources: ASH 2020](https://ashpublications.org/hematology/article/2020/1/478/474369/), [Gibson Principles of Nutritional Assessment](https://med.libretexts.org/Bookshelves/Nutrition/Principles_of_Nutritional_Assessment_3e_(Gibson_et_al.)/14:_Iron_(Chapter_17)/14.06:_Serum_iron_TIBC_transferrin_and_transferrin_saturation_(17.6))

### Confounders
- Same diurnal variation as serum iron (TSAT moves with serum iron more than TIBC)
- Recent iron supplementation (false high)
- Hemolyzed sample
- Pregnancy and OCP (increased transferrin → falsely lower TSAT)
- Within-person biological variability is significant — a single high TSAT in isolation should be repeated fasting before triggering hemochromatosis workup ([biological variability study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2151312/))

### Clinical action thresholds for a young man
- TSAT <16% with low ferritin → confirmed iron deficiency, investigate cause (diet, occult GI bleed)
- TSAT 20–45% — normal range
- TSAT >45% on two fasting morning draws + ferritin >300 → HFE genotyping
- TSAT >55% even with normal ferritin warrants attention — ferritin can be normal early in iron loading

## 4. Iron Binding Capacity (TIBC)

### What it is
TIBC is the total amount of iron that serum can bind, dominated by transferrin. **TIBC ≈ transferrin (mg/dL) × 1.41** because each transferrin molecule binds 2 Fe³⁺ ions ([conversion factor 1.4–1.49](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_iron-binding_capacity)). Some labs report transferrin and TIBC; some only one.

### Reference range
- **Conventional adult:** 250–450 µg/dL
- TIBC moves opposite to ferritin under most conditions:
  - **High TIBC** in iron deficiency (body upregulates transferrin to chase scarce iron)
  - **Low TIBC** in anemia of chronic disease, malnutrition, liver disease (decreased transferrin synthesis), nephrotic syndrome (transferrin loss in urine)

### Why TIBC matters — distinguishing IDA vs ACD
Inflammation drops both serum iron AND TIBC. Iron deficiency drops serum iron but RAISES TIBC. So:
- **Low Fe + high TIBC + low ferritin** → IDA, treat with iron
- **Low Fe + low/normal TIBC + normal/high ferritin** → ACD, treat the underlying inflammatory disease, iron supplementation often ineffective without IV iron + addressing inflammation

This is one of the most clinically valuable iron-panel discriminators ([NCBI Pediatric Iron Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9827648/)).

### Confounders
- Pregnancy and oral contraceptives raise transferrin/TIBC by ~25–30% (estrogen effect on hepatic synthesis)
- Liver disease lowers TIBC (decreased synthesis)
- Nephrotic syndrome lowers TIBC (urinary loss)
- Acute inflammation lowers TIBC (decreased transferrin synthesis as part of negative APR)
- Malnutrition lowers TIBC

### Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male
TIBC isn't really optimized in isolation — its value is comparative against serum iron. A TIBC of 300–400 µg/dL in the context of normal serum iron (~100) and ferritin (~80) is unremarkable. Look at the pattern, not the absolute.

## 5. Methylmalonic Acid (MMA) — B12 Functional Proxy

### What it is
MMA is an organic acid that accumulates when B12 (specifically the **adenosylcobalamin** mitochondrial form) is functionally insufficient. The enzyme methylmalonyl-CoA mutase requires AdoCbl to convert methylmalonyl-CoA → succinyl-CoA in the propionate catabolism pathway. Without adequate B12, the substrate (methylmalonyl-CoA) gets hydrolyzed to MMA, which spills into blood and urine.

**Assay:** LC-MS/MS or GC-MS — gold standard ([Stabler review, Biocrates lab](https://biocrates.com/methylmalonic-acid/)). Some commercial labs use ELISA-based methods which are less reliable. Function Health uses LC-MS-based methodology.

**Why MMA matters more than serum B12:**
Serum B12 (cyanocobalamin/total cobalamin assay) misclassifies a substantial fraction of people. The classic Lindenbaum 1988 NEJM study ([Neuropsychiatric disorders caused by cobalamin deficiency in the absence of anemia or macrocytosis](https://www.nejm.org/doi/abs/10.1056/NEJM198806303182604)) documented that among 141 patients with B12-deficiency-caused neurological disease, **2 had serum B12 above 200 pg/mL** (the conventional cutoff) and many had normal MCV with no anemia. Stabler 2013 confirms MMA + homocysteine identify functional B12 deficiency missed by serum cobalamin alone. MMA is more sensitive and specific than serum B12 for detecting tissue-level B12 insufficiency.

### Reference ranges
- **Conventional reference:** <0.4 µmol/L (varies — some labs use 70–270 nmol/L; Mayo uses ≤0.40 µmol/L) ([Biocrates](https://biocrates.com/methylmalonic-acid/), [Lamkin Clinic](https://lamkinclinic.com/methylmalonic-acid/))
- **Optimal:** **<0.20 µmol/L** is widely cited; values 0.20–0.40 are "gray zone"
- Values >0.40 µmol/L → meaningful B12 functional deficiency until renal failure ruled out

### Interpretation matrix
| MMA | Homocysteine | Most likely interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| ↑ | ↑ | B12 deficiency (functional) — confirm and treat |
| ↑ | normal | B12 deficiency OR renal failure (check creatinine/eGFR) OR rare methylmalonic acidemia |
| normal | ↑ | Folate deficiency, B6 deficiency, MTHFR variant, hypothyroidism |
| normal | normal | B12 status adequate; both pathways functional |

### What high MMA indicates
- **B12 deficiency** — most common cause:
  - Pernicious anemia (autoimmune intrinsic-factor antibodies)
  - Atrophic gastritis (common in older adults; hypochlorhydria → less B12 cleavage from food)
  - **Metformin** use (impairs B12 absorption) — note for diabetic users
  - **PPI / H2 blocker** chronic use
  - Vegan/strict vegetarian without B12 supplementation (B12 is essentially absent in plants)
  - Bariatric surgery, ileal resection (terminal ileum is B12 absorption site)
  - SIBO, fish tapeworm, Crohn's, celiac
- **Renal insufficiency** raises MMA via reduced clearance — check creatinine/eGFR before interpreting elevated MMA as B12 deficiency ([Lifelines-MINUTHE study, 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7726887/))
- **Methylmalonic acidemia** (rare inborn error)
- Aging (ages 60+ have rising MMA partly from atrophic gastritis, partly from renal decline)

### What low MMA indicates
Not pathologic. No clinical implication.

### Direction of "better" — monotonic (lower better, with floor)
Lower MMA is better, down to assay limit. No "too low" condition exists.

### Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male
**<0.20 µmol/L**. A 20-year-old without GI disease, metformin, or vegan diet should run very low MMA (often <0.15). Persistent MMA >0.30 in this age group warrants investigation of dietary intake and absorption.

### Confounders
- Renal function (eGFR <60 raises MMA independent of B12)
- Recent B12 injection (suppresses MMA acutely)
- Pregnancy
- Plasma vs urine MMA (urine more sensitive but less commonly run)
- Hemolyzed sample

## 6. Homocysteine

### What it is
Homocysteine (Hcy) is a sulfur-containing amino acid intermediate in the methionine cycle. It's not a dietary amino acid — it's produced when S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) donates methyl groups and is hydrolyzed to homocysteine.

Hcy has two fates ([transsulfuration/remethylation review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10294675/)):
1. **Remethylation** back to methionine — requires either:
   - Methionine synthase (MTR) using **B12** (methylcobalamin) and **5-methyl-THF** (folate via MTHFR)
   - Betaine-homocysteine methyltransferase (BHMT) using betaine (mostly liver/kidney)
2. **Transsulfuration** to cystathionine → cysteine → glutathione — requires **B6** (PLP) via cystathionine β-synthase (CBS) and γ-lyase

If any of B6/B9/B12 is deficient, Hcy accumulates. Hcy is freely measured in plasma after fasting; should be drawn fasting because high-protein meals raise it.

**Assay:** HPLC, immunoassay (FPIA, CLIA), or LC-MS. Reasonably standardized but can vary 10% across methods.

### Reference ranges
- **Conventional U.S. lab "normal":** 5–15 µmol/L — too lax
- **Functional/optimal:** **<8 µmol/L** (some practitioners <7)
- **Hyperhomocysteinemia:** mild 15–30, intermediate 30–100, severe >100 µmol/L
- **Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male:** **<8 µmol/L**, ideally 5–7

### What high homocysteine indicates
- **B-vitamin deficiency** (most common):
  - **Folate** deficiency — biggest single nutritional driver in unfortified populations; in fortified populations (US, since 1998), B12 surpasses folate as cause
  - **B12** deficiency (will also raise MMA)
  - **B6** deficiency (raises Hcy via transsulfuration block; doesn't raise MMA)
- **MTHFR C677T TT homozygote** (~10–15% of populations of European descent) — each T allele ~35% reduction in MTHFR enzyme activity; TT homozygotes have ~70% reduction → reduced 5-methyl-THF production → reduced Hcy remethylation. TT homozygotes have higher Hcy by ~2–3 µmol/L average and more sensitivity to folate insufficiency ([Liew & Gupta 2015 European Journal of Medical Genetics](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/7/8/5303), [AHA Circulation review](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/circulationaha.114.013311)). **If 23andMe / direct-to-consumer SNP data is available, MTHFR C677T = rs1801133, A1298C = rs1801131. Worth checking when Hcy is elevated.**
- **Renal insufficiency** — kidneys clear Hcy and remethylate via BHMT
- **Hypothyroidism**
- **Smoking, heavy coffee** (modest raises)
- **Medications:** methotrexate (folate antagonist), antiepileptics (phenytoin, carbamazepine), nitrous oxide (oxidizes B12 cofactor), metformin (B12 depletion), fenofibrate, niacin (modestly), levodopa (depletes SAM)
- **Aging** (Hcy rises ~1 µmol/L per decade)
- **Alcoholism**
- **CBS deficiency** (homocystinuria — rare inborn error, severe Hcy, marfanoid habitus, lens dislocation, early thrombosis)

### What low homocysteine indicates
Not pathologic. Sometimes seen in pregnancy (estrogen effect, hemodilution), oral contraceptives, and acute illness.

### Cardiovascular risk — read carefully
Observational studies consistently associate elevated Hcy with CVD, stroke, dementia. **However, randomized B-vitamin supplementation trials that lowered Hcy by 20–25% failed to reduce cardiovascular events** ([HOPE-2, NEJM 2006](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa055227); [VITATOPS](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1471-2261-8-24) for stroke recurrence). Mendelian randomization studies are mixed: some find a causal effect on stroke risk per genetically-elevated Hcy SD ([Yuan 2021 BMC Medicine](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-021-01977-8) — OR ~1.11 per SD for any stroke), others find no consistent effect on coronary disease.

**Best interpretation:** Hcy is **a downstream marker of methylation status and B-vitamin sufficiency** rather than a directly causal CVD risk factor. Treating elevated Hcy by addressing B-vitamin status and MTHFR-aware folate (e.g., methylfolate for TT homozygotes) is reasonable as a methylation-status optimization, not as cardiovascular prevention.

### Direction of "better" — essentially monotonic (lower with floor)
Lower is better down to ~5 µmol/L; below this, you're at the assay floor with no health implication.

### Confounders
- Non-fasting state (high-protein meal raises Hcy 10–20%)
- Sample handling — Hcy continues to be released from RBCs after blood draw if not separated promptly; can falsely elevate
- Recent multivitamin / B-complex
- Renal function
- Thyroid status
- Smoking, heavy alcohol, coffee

### Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male
**<8 µmol/L, ideally 5–7**. Above 9 in a 20-year-old → check B12 (with MMA), folate, TSH, creatinine; consider MTHFR genotype from 23andMe data. Above 12 in a 20-year-old is unusual and warrants workup.

## 7. Vitamin D — 25-Hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D]

### What it is
**Vitamin D** is a steroid hormone (technically a prohormone) that exists in two forms:
- D2 (ergocalciferol — plant/fungal, supplements)
- D3 (cholecalciferol — synthesized in skin from 7-dehydrocholesterol on UVB exposure; also from animal foods/supplements)

Both are hydroxylated in liver to **25(OH)D** (calcidiol/calcifediol), the major circulating form with t½ of ~2–3 weeks. **25(OH)D is the marker of total body vitamin D status.** A second hydroxylation in kidney converts 25(OH)D → **1,25(OH)₂D** (calcitriol), the active hormone, with t½ of ~4 hours, tightly regulated by PTH/Ca/phosphate. 

**Critical: do NOT confuse 25(OH)D with 1,25(OH)₂D.** 1,25(OH)₂D can be normal or even elevated in vitamin D deficiency (compensatory PTH-driven activation) and is NOT a deficiency marker — it should only be ordered for specific endocrinology questions (sarcoid, granulomatous disease, certain CKD scenarios, suspected 1α-hydroxylase deficiency). Order **25(OH)D total** for status assessment.

**Assay:** Two methods.
- **LC-MS/MS** (gold standard) — measures D2 and D3 separately, sum = total 25(OH)D. Higher accuracy, less inter-lab variability.
- **Immunoassay** (CLIA, ECLIA — Roche, Abbott, Siemens) — convenient/cheap; can have 10–20% inter-method bias, especially at extremes and with D2 supplementation; underperformance vs LC-MS in DEQAS schemes ([DEQAS analytical variation review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0960076023000833), [PLOS One 2012](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0048774)). Function Health and most commercial labs increasingly use LC-MS, which is preferable.

### Reference ranges and the guideline divergence
| Source | Sufficient | Insufficient | Deficient |
|---|---|---|---|
| **IOM/NAM 2011** | ≥20 ng/mL | 12–20 | <12 |
| **Endocrine Society 2011 (Holick)** | ≥30 ng/mL | 21–29 | <20 |
| **Endocrine Society 2024** | (no longer endorses single threshold for general adults; recommends RDA-only for healthy <75) | — | — |
| **Functional/integrative** | 40–60 ng/mL | 30–39 | <30 |
| **Toxicity threshold** | >100 ng/mL chronic = excess; >150 ng/mL = clinical hypervitaminosis (hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria) | — | — |

[Sources: [IOM 2011 response](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5393439/), [Holick 2011 ES guideline](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21646368/), [Endocrine Society 2024 update](https://www.endocrine.org/news-and-advocacy/news-room/2024/endocrine-society-recommends-healthy-adults-take-the-recommended-daily-allowance-of-vitamin-d), [Vitamin D toxicity StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK557876/)]

**Note on the 2024 Endocrine Society shift:** the 2024 guideline ([Demay et al., JCEM 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38828931/)) recommends *against* routine 25(OH)D testing in healthy adults <75 and recommends following IOM RDA (600 IU/day under age 70). This represents convergence with IOM but is a major retreat from the 2011 Holick framework. Two large RCTs informed this: VITAL and D-Health.

### Trial evidence — VITAL and D-Health
- **VITAL (Manson et al., NEJM 2019)** — 25,871 U.S. adults (men ≥50, women ≥55), 2000 IU/d D3 vs placebo for 5.3 years. **No reduction in cancer incidence or major cardiovascular events** ([NEJM full text](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/nejmoa1809944)). However, baseline median 25(OH)D was 30 ng/mL — most participants were already sufficient. Secondary signals: cancer mortality reduced (HR 0.83), especially excluding first 1–2 years (HR 0.75–0.79). Not adequately powered for many subgroups.
- **D-Health (Neale et al., 2022)** — 21,315 Australians 60–84, monthly 60,000 IU D3 (~2000 IU/d equivalent) vs placebo, 5 years. **No reduction in all-cause mortality, cancer mortality, or CVD mortality** ([Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol 2022, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2213858721003454)). Exploratory analyses excluding early follow-up showed numerically *higher* cancer mortality in vitamin D arm — concerning, though not statistically significant.

**Synthesis:** at population level, vitamin D supplementation in already-sufficient adults does not show outcome benefit. This does not mean 25(OH)D is irrelevant — observational evidence ties low 25(OH)D (<20 ng/mL) to autoimmune disease risk, infection susceptibility, fracture risk in elderly, and possibly all-cause mortality — but the supplementation evidence does not support pushing levels well above the RDA-target.

### Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male
- **40–50 ng/mL** is a reasonable target weighted toward functional medicine framing; **30–60 ng/mL** is the conservative wide-range optimal.
- Below 20: deficient by all definitions; supplement.
- 20–30: borderline; consider supplement especially if dark skin, indoor lifestyle, winter.
- 30–60: target zone.
- 60–80: high but not toxic; check that supplementation isn't excessive.
- 80–100: monitor; reduce dose; check serum and urine calcium.
- >100: stop supplementation, recheck in 3 months, check Ca.

For a 20-year-old male: 1000–2000 IU/d D3 is generally safe and adequate for most people; 4000 IU/d is the IOM upper limit and rarely necessary unless documented deficiency.

### What high indicates
- Excessive supplementation (most common, especially with high-dose D3 5000–10,000 IU daily)
- Granulomatous diseases (sarcoidosis, TB) — these activate 1α-hydroxylase in macrophages and raise 1,25(OH)₂D, but typically don't dramatically raise 25(OH)D unless supplementing
- Williams syndrome (rare, hypersensitivity to vitamin D)
- Toxicity manifests as hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, kidney stones, vascular calcification, nephrocalcinosis at sustained 25(OH)D >150 ng/mL ([StatPearls Vitamin D toxicity](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK557876/), [meta-analysis on supplementation/stones](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522046299))

### What low indicates
- Limited UVB exposure (high latitude, indoor lifestyle, sunscreen)
- Dark skin pigmentation (melanin reduces cutaneous synthesis)
- Obesity (vitamin D sequestered in adipose)
- Malabsorption (celiac, IBD, bariatric surgery, cholestasis)
- Hepatic disease (impaired 25-hydroxylation)
- Renal disease (impaired 1-hydroxylation; affects 1,25 not 25)
- Anticonvulsants, glucocorticoids (induce CYP3A4 → degrade 25(OH)D)
- Nephrotic syndrome (urinary loss of vitamin-D-binding protein)
- Inadequate dietary intake with no supplementation

### NHANES population data
- ~22–25% of U.S. adults are vitamin D deficient (<20 ng/mL); ~40% insufficient (20–30); winter season, female sex, and non-Hispanic Black populations have higher deficiency prevalence ([Cui 2018, 2022 NHANES analyses](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9573946/)).
- 94% of US population doesn't meet vitamin D RDA from food alone ([Linus Pauling Institute](https://lpi.oregonstate.edu/mic/micronutrient-inadequacies/overview)).

### Confounders
- Season (peak summer, trough late winter — 25(OH)D varies 10–15 ng/mL across seasons in temperate latitudes)
- Recent high-dose supplementation
- BMI (obesity confounds — higher BMI sequesters vit D in adipose, lower 25(OH)D)
- Pregnancy
- Ethnicity / skin pigmentation
- Vitamin D binding protein (DBP) genetic variants

## 8. Zinc (Serum)

### What it is
Serum zinc measures circulating zinc, of which ~70% is loosely bound to albumin (transportable) and ~30% tightly bound to α2-macroglobulin. **Critical caveat:** serum zinc represents only ~0.1% of total body zinc (most is intracellular — bone, muscle, liver, prostate). **Serum zinc is therefore a poor marker of whole-body zinc status, but it remains the standard test because better tests (RBC zinc, neutrophil zinc, hair zinc, taste acuity tests) aren't routinely available** ([NIH ODS Zinc fact sheet](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Zinc-HealthProfessional/), [OptimalDx Zinc review](https://www.optimaldx.com/research-blog/mineral-biomarkers-serum-zinc)).

**Assay:** Atomic absorption spectroscopy or ICP-MS. Sensitive to contamination — zinc is everywhere (rubber stoppers, anticoagulants). **Trace-element-grade tube required; many false lows result from contaminated draws.**

### Physiology
Zinc is a cofactor in 300+ enzymes including:
- DNA polymerases, RNA polymerases (cell division)
- Carbonic anhydrase
- Alcohol dehydrogenase
- Carboxypeptidase, aminopeptidase
- Superoxide dismutase (Cu/Zn SOD)
- Matrix metalloproteinases
- 5-α-reductase, aromatase (steroid metabolism)
- Insulin storage in pancreatic β-cell granules

It also serves as a structural element in zinc-finger transcription factors (~10% of human proteome contains a zinc-binding domain).

**Immune function** ([Prasad 2008 — Zinc and immune cells](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2277319/)):
- T-cell maturation requires **thymulin**, a thymic peptide hormone that is enzymatically active only when zinc-bound. Zinc deficiency → inactive thymulin → impaired T-cell development → reduced CD4/CD8 ratio
- NK cell number and activity zinc-dependent
- Th1/Th2 balance — zinc deficiency shifts toward Th2 (decreased IFN-γ, IL-2)
- Phagocyte oxidative burst zinc-dependent
- Mild zinc deficiency in human studies has reduced testosterone, oligospermia, decreased NK activity, reduced IL-2 — restored by supplementation

**Other roles:**
- Wound healing (matrix remodeling, fibroblast proliferation)
- Taste/smell (gustin protein in taste buds is zinc metalloenzyme; hypogeusia is classic Zn deficiency symptom)
- Spermatogenesis (highest tissue Zn concentration in prostate)
- Skin integrity (epidermal proliferation)

### Reference ranges
- **Conventional:** 70–120 µg/dL (10.7–18.4 µmol/L) ([Mayo, NIH ODS](https://pediatric.testcatalog.org/show/ZN_S))
- **Deficiency cutoffs:** <70 µg/dL women, **<74 µg/dL men** (NIH ODS thresholds — IZiNCG, NHANES)
- **Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male:** **80–120 µg/dL**, mid-range. Some sources push 90–120.

### What high indicates
- **Zinc supplementation excess** — most common cause. >40 mg/day chronically is the upper limit; many "men's multis" stack 25–50 mg zinc, and dedicated zinc supplements are often 50 mg.
- Occupational exposure (galvanizing, welding fume — "metal fume fever")
- Hemolysis (RBCs contain zinc)
- Sample contamination (zinc-containing rubber stoppers, talc on gloves)

**Consequence of chronic high zinc:**
- **Copper deficiency (hypocupremia)** — zinc induces metallothionein in enterocytes, which preferentially binds copper (higher affinity than zinc), preventing copper absorption. Chronic zinc >40 mg/d → progressive copper depletion → anemia, leukopenia, neutropenia, **myelopathy/neuropathy** clinically resembling B12 deficiency or subacute combined degeneration ([Wahab 2020 case series](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7495772/), [Zinc-induced hypocupremia case report 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8604455/)). Maintain zinc:copper ratio of 8–15:1 if supplementing chronically.

### What low indicates
- **Vegetarian/vegan diet** — phytates in legumes/grains chelate zinc, reducing absorption ~50% vs omnivorous diets
- **GI loss:** Crohn's, celiac, IBS-D, chronic diarrhea
- **Excess dietary copper or iron** competing for absorption
- **Alcoholism** (chronic alcohol increases urinary zinc loss + dietary inadequacy)
- **Sickle cell disease** (chronic urinary zinc loss)
- **Acrodermatitis enteropathica** — autosomal recessive ZIP4 transporter mutation, severe zinc deficiency from infancy
- **Liver cirrhosis** (low albumin → low total zinc; functional zinc may also be low)
- **Acute phase response — zinc is a NEGATIVE acute phase reactant** (decreases with inflammation as it shifts intracellularly under cytokine signaling). Don't interpret low zinc during acute illness as deficiency without repeat testing.

**Symptoms of low zinc:**
- Hypogeusia (decreased taste), anosmia
- Delayed wound healing
- Hair loss (telogen effluvium)
- Skin lesions (perioral, perianal, acral pustular dermatitis — "acrodermatitis-like")
- Increased infection susceptibility
- Decreased testosterone, hypogonadism, oligospermia
- **Acne** — see below
- Night blindness (interaction with vitamin A retinol-binding protein)
- Diarrhea (in severe deficiency)

### Acne and zinc
Multiple lines of evidence connect zinc status and acne:
- **Yee 2020 systematic review and meta-analysis** ([Dermatologic Therapy 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32860489/)): patients with acne had significantly lower serum zinc levels than controls, and zinc supplementation was superior to placebo for reducing inflammatory papule count.
- **Cervantes 2018 review** (Univ. Miami) and **Brandt 2013 systematic review**: oral zinc as monotherapy or adjunctive treatment showed efficacy for acne, with reasonable safety.
- Mechanisms: anti-inflammatory (reduces neutrophil chemotaxis to *C. acnes*), 5-α-reductase inhibition (reduces DHT-driven sebum), antimicrobial effect on *C. acnes*.
- Typical doses studied: 30–50 mg elemental zinc daily (zinc gluconate or sulfate). Side effects: GI upset, copper deficiency at chronic high doses.

For active acne with low-normal serum zinc (<90 µg/dL), supplementing 25–30 mg elemental zinc daily (with food, not concurrent with iron, with periodic copper monitoring at 2 mg or via diet) is reasonable and supported.

### Direction of "better" — U-shaped
- Below ~70 µg/dL: deficiency, supplement
- 80–120 µg/dL: optimal
- Above 120: investigate supplement excess; risk of copper deficiency over time

### Confounders
- **Time of day** (zinc is highest in morning, drops 20% by afternoon)
- **Postprandial state** (zinc drops 10–20% after meal)
- **Recent supplementation** within 12h (false high)
- **Inflammation/infection** (false low — negative APR; check CRP)
- **Hemolysis** (false high)
- **Sample contamination** (false high) — must use trace-element tubes
- **Albumin** (low albumin = low total serum zinc but doesn't necessarily reflect deficiency)
- **Pregnancy, OCP** (lower zinc)

## 9. Magnesium (Serum)

### What it is
Serum magnesium measures circulating Mg, of which ~33% is protein-bound, ~12% complexed with anions, and ~55% is biologically active free ionized Mg²⁺ ([European J Nutrition 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02916-w)). **Serum Mg captures only ~0.3–1% of total body magnesium** — most is in bone (60%) and muscle/soft tissue (~38%), with serum essentially the smallest pool.

**Assay:** Colorimetric (xylidyl blue or calmagite) or atomic absorption. Reasonable accuracy. Reported in mg/dL (× 0.411 = mmol/L).

**Critical caveat:** Serum Mg can be normal in moderate-to-severe whole-body Mg deficiency because the body draws on bone/muscle Mg to maintain serum levels. Up to 40% of people with normal serum Mg may have deficiency on Mg loading test or RBC Mg ([Costello 2016 — Magnesium consumption review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6316205/), [Workinger 2018 challenges in Mg status](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6163803/)). The most accurate tests in research use:
- **Mg loading test** (parenteral Mg given, urinary excretion measured — retention ≥20% suggests deficiency) — gold standard but impractical
- **RBC magnesium** — better than serum but still imperfect; not validated as reference
- **Ionized Mg** — emerging
- **Serum Mg** — practical default, weak signal

### Physiology
Magnesium is the second most abundant intracellular cation. Cofactor in 600+ enzymes including:
- All ATP-dependent reactions (Mg-ATP is the active form)
- DNA/RNA synthesis
- Glucose metabolism, insulin signaling
- Glutamate/NMDA receptor regulation (Mg blocks NMDA channel at resting potential)
- Cardiac repolarization (especially ventricular IKr potassium channel modulation)
- Vasodilation, calcium channel antagonism in smooth muscle
- GABA-A receptor potentiation (calming/sleep effect)

### Reference ranges
- **Conventional adult:** 1.7–2.4 mg/dL (0.70–1.05 mmol/L) — most labs ([Hypomagnesemia StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK500003/))
- **2022 European J Nutrition recommendation:** evidence-based serum Mg reference 0.85–1.10 mmol/L (2.07–2.68 mg/dL) — values <0.85 mmol/L (<2.07 mg/dL) associated with elevated chronic disease risk ([Costello 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02916-w))
- **Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male:** **2.0–2.4 mg/dL**; ideally >2.0 (mid-to-upper). A serum Mg of 1.7–1.9, while "normal" by lab range, is associated with worse outcomes in observational data and warrants attention to dietary intake.

### What high indicates
- **Renal failure** (most common — kidneys excrete most ingested Mg)
- **Iatrogenic** — IV Mg sulfate (eclampsia, asthma, arrhythmia)
- **Excessive Mg-containing antacids/laxatives** (Mg hydroxide / Milk of Magnesia, Mg citrate)
- **Adrenal insufficiency**
- **Hypothyroidism**
- **Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia** (FHH) often raises Mg
- **Tumor lysis syndrome**, rhabdomyolysis (cell breakdown releases Mg)

Hypermagnesemia symptoms (rare in non-renal-failure): hyporeflexia, lethargy, hypotension, bradycardia, arrhythmia, paralysis at very high levels.

### What low indicates
- **GI loss:** chronic diarrhea, celiac, IBD, short bowel, vomiting
- **PPI use** — well-documented FDA warning (2011); chronic PPI use roughly doubles odds of hypomagnesemia by impairing TRPM6/7-mediated active Mg absorption in colon ([Florentin 2012, Gommers 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3782221/))
- **Diuretics** (loop and thiazide promote Mg wasting; potassium-sparing protective)
- **Chronic alcohol use** — combined GI loss, urinary wasting, dietary inadequacy
- **Refeeding syndrome** (intracellular shift after starvation)
- **Inadequate intake** — about 50% of Americans don't meet RDA ([NHANES](https://www.ars.usda.gov/ARSUserFiles/80400530/pdf/0506/usual_nutrient_intake_vitD_ca_phos_mg_2005-06.pdf))
- **Hypercalcemia** (calcium and Mg compete at TRPV5 channels in distal nephron)
- **Aminoglycoside antibiotics, amphotericin B, cisplatin, calcineurin inhibitors** (renal Mg wasting)
- **Diabetes** (chronic glucosuria → osmotic diuresis → Mg loss)
- **Genetic** (Gitelman syndrome — thiazide-sensitive co-transporter defect; Bartter syndrome variants)

**Symptoms of low Mg:**
- Muscle cramps, twitches, fasciculations
- Paresthesias
- Insomnia, anxiety, irritability
- Migraines
- Cardiac arrhythmias — particularly **torsades de pointes / QT prolongation** (Mg modulates IKr; IV Mg is the antidote regardless of serum level) ([emcrit/torsades](https://emcrit.org/pulmcrit/tdp-magnesium/))
- Atrial fibrillation in cardiac surgery / inpatients
- Hypocalcemia (Mg deficiency causes PTH resistance and impaired PTH release → functional hypoCa that is refractory to Ca alone — must replace Mg first)
- Hypokalemia refractory to K replacement (Mg required for K retention via ROMK channels)
- Tetany, seizures (severe)

### Direction of "better" — U-shaped, but in healthy people with normal kidneys, low-end is the practical concern
- Low: clinically problematic (cramps, arrhythmias, insomnia, refractory hypoK/Ca)
- High: rare without renal disease or excessive supplementation/IV
- Most healthy young adults sit 1.8–2.2; pushing toward 2.0–2.4 is reasonable

### Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male
**2.0–2.4 mg/dL serum**. Serum 1.7–1.9 with symptoms of cramps, insomnia, anxiety → likely benefit from supplementation despite "normal" serum.

### Supplement forms
| Form | Bioavailability | Best use | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Magnesium glycinate / bisglycinate** | High; gentle on GI | General supplementation, sleep, anxiety | Glycine has its own mild calming effect |
| **Magnesium citrate** | High | General supplementation, mild constipation | Laxative at higher doses (>400 mg) |
| **Magnesium L-threonate** | High; preferentially crosses BBB | Cognitive uses, hypothesized for memory | Animal data only; small human trials; expensive |
| **Magnesium malate** | Good | Energy/fatigue contexts | Modest evidence |
| **Magnesium taurate** | Good | Cardiovascular use | Less evidence |
| **Magnesium oxide** | Poor (~4% absorption) | Constipation, antacid | Cheap; not ideal for repletion |
| **Magnesium sulfate** | Poor oral; IV form | IV repletion, Epsom bath | — |
| **Magnesium chloride** | High | Topical/oral | Acidic — supports absorption |

[Source: [Healthline magnesium types review](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/magnesium-types), [PMC bioavailability review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6683096/)]

For most healthy young men: **200–400 mg elemental Mg as glycinate or citrate**, taken evening, is the standard recommendation. RDA is 400 mg/day for 19–30-year-old males.

### Confounders
- Hemolysis (RBC Mg releases on hemolysis → false high)
- Recent Mg-containing supplements / antacids / laxatives
- Acute illness (often shifts Mg)
- Acidosis/alkalosis affects ionized Mg without changing total
- Phosphate-binding agents
- Anticoagulants (some chelate Mg)

## 10. Calcium (Total Serum)

### What it is
Total serum calcium measures all forms in blood: ~50% **ionized (free, biologically active)**, ~40% **albumin-bound (transport reservoir)**, ~10% complexed with anions (citrate, phosphate, bicarbonate). The body **tightly regulates ionized Ca²⁺** via PTH, calcitonin, and 1,25(OH)₂D — serum Ca rarely deviates more than ±5–10% in health.

**Assay:** Colorimetric (o-cresolphthalein complexone, arsenazo III) for total Ca. Ionized Ca requires direct ion-selective electrode on a fresh, anaerobic sample (blood gas analyzer) and is rarely run outpatient.

**Albumin correction (Payne's formula 1973):**
> Corrected Ca (mg/dL) = Measured total Ca + 0.8 × (4.0 − albumin in g/dL)

This adjusts for the ~40% albumin-bound fraction. For each 1 g/dL drop in albumin below 4.0, total Ca falls by ~0.8 mg/dL without affecting ionized Ca. **Critical for hypoalbuminemic patients** (cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, malnutrition, critical illness).

**However:** the Payne formula has known imprecision. Recent literature ([JAMA Network Open 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2829419), [Canadian J General Internal Medicine 2021](https://utppublishing.com/doi/10.22374/cjgim.v11i3.150)) notes the formula overestimates ionized Ca in hypoalbuminemia, causing false negatives for hypocalcemia and false positives for hypercalcemia. **For clinically important calcium decisions (especially suspected hypercalcemia), measure ionized Ca²⁺ directly** when possible. For normoalbuminemic young men, total Ca with albumin correction is fine.

**pH effect:** Acidosis → less calcium bound to albumin → higher ionized Ca with same total. Alkalosis → opposite. This is why hyperventilation (respiratory alkalosis) can produce tetany at normal total Ca (functional hypocalcemia).

### Physiology
Calcium is essential for:
- Bone/teeth structure (>99% of body Ca)
- Neuromuscular excitability (Ca²⁺-dependent action potentials, neurotransmitter release)
- Muscle contraction (sarcomere coupling, calmodulin)
- Coagulation cascade (factor IV)
- Intracellular signaling (ubiquitous second messenger)

Regulation:
- **PTH** (from parathyroid chief cells): rises with low ionized Ca; mobilizes bone Ca, stimulates renal Ca reabsorption + 1,25(OH)₂D synthesis, decreases renal phosphate reabsorption
- **1,25(OH)₂D**: increases gut Ca absorption
- **Calcitonin** (from thyroid C cells): minor role in adults; opposes PTH
- **Calcium-sensing receptor (CASR)**: on parathyroid + kidney; senses ionized Ca

### Reference ranges
- **Conventional total Ca:** 8.5–10.5 mg/dL (2.12–2.62 mmol/L) — most U.S. labs
- **Ionized Ca:** 4.6–5.3 mg/dL (1.15–1.32 mmol/L)
- **Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male:** **9.0–10.0 mg/dL** (mid-range total Ca with normal albumin ~4.5 g/dL). Persistent values ≥10.5 (or corrected ≥10.5) warrant workup even if asymptomatic.

### What high (hypercalcemia) indicates
**Most common causes (90%+):**
- **Primary hyperparathyroidism (PHPT)** — most common outpatient cause; mild persistent hypercalcemia + inappropriately normal/high PTH. Usually a benign adenoma in one parathyroid gland. Diagnostic workup: serum Ca + PTH + 25(OH)D + creatinine + 24-hour urine Ca + creatinine. Ca/Cr clearance ratio <0.01 raises FHH suspicion ([Fifth International Workshop 2022](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article/37/11/2293/7512381), [parathyroid.com/FHH](https://www.parathyroid.com/FHH.htm))
- **Malignancy** — second most common; PTHrP secretion (squamous cell, breast, renal), bone metastases (multiple myeloma, breast cancer), 1,25(OH)₂D production by lymphomas
- These two together account for ~90% of hypercalcemia

**Other causes:**
- **Vitamin D toxicity** (chronic 25(OH)D >100, especially >150 ng/mL) — increases gut Ca absorption
- **Granulomatous diseases** — sarcoidosis, TB, histoplasmosis: macrophage 1α-hydroxylase converts 25(OH)D → 1,25(OH)₂D unregulated
- **Thiazide diuretics** — increase distal tubular Ca reabsorption
- **Lithium** — alters CASR set point
- **Milk-alkali syndrome** (excessive Ca + alkali intake — classic with Ca-carbonate antacid abuse)
- **Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia (FHH)** — autosomal dominant CASR loss-of-function; mild Ca elevation, normal/slightly high PTH, low urine Ca, **calcium/creatinine clearance ratio <0.01** distinguishes from PHPT ([StatPearls FHH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK459190/)) — important because surgery on FHH patients fails
- **Hyperthyroidism** (increased bone turnover)
- **Adrenal insufficiency**
- **Immobilization** (especially with high baseline bone turnover — adolescents, Paget's, spinal cord injury)
- **Vitamin A toxicity**
- **Pheochromocytoma, VIPoma**

**Symptoms (mnemonic — "stones, bones, groans, psychiatric overtones"):**
- Stones: nephrolithiasis, nephrocalcinosis
- Bones: bone pain, osteoporosis (chronic PHPT)
- Groans: abdominal pain, constipation, peptic ulcer, pancreatitis
- Psychiatric: fatigue, depression, confusion, polyuria, polydipsia
- Cardiovascular: short QT, hypertension

### What low (hypocalcemia) indicates
- **Hypoalbuminemia** (most common "low total Ca" in hospitalized patients — apparent hypocalcemia, ionized Ca normal; correct via formula or order ionized Ca)
- **Hypoparathyroidism:**
  - Post-thyroidectomy (most common iatrogenic cause)
  - Autoimmune (isolated or APS-1)
  - DiGeorge syndrome (congenital)
  - Idiopathic
- **Vitamin D deficiency** (severe, with hypocalcemia + secondary hyperparathyroidism)
- **CKD** (impaired 1α-hydroxylation → low 1,25(OH)₂D + phosphate retention)
- **Hyperphosphatemia** (precipitation, decreased 1,25(OH)₂D)
- **Acute pancreatitis** (saponification with free fatty acids)
- **Magnesium deficiency** — causes PTH resistance and impaired PTH secretion → **functional hypocalcemia refractory to Ca alone; must replace Mg first**
- **Pseudohypoparathyroidism** (PTH resistance)
- **Massive transfusion** (citrate chelates Ca)
- **Tumor lysis syndrome** (phosphate release)
- **Sepsis** (multifactorial)
- **Bisphosphonates, denosumab** (anti-resorptive overshoot)

**Symptoms:** perioral numbness, paresthesias, **Chvostek sign** (facial twitch on tapping facial nerve), **Trousseau sign** (carpopedal spasm with BP cuff), tetany, laryngospasm, seizures, prolonged QT, congestive heart failure (severe).

### Direction of "better" — U-shaped, but tightly regulated
Calcium is so tightly regulated that any persistent abnormality outside reference range in a non-acute setting is meaningful. **Mid-range is optimal.**

### Optimal for healthy 20-year-old male
**9.0–10.0 mg/dL** (with normal albumin). Persistent ≥10.5 corrected → workup (PTH, 25(OH)D, Cr, 24-hr urine Ca). Persistent <8.5 corrected → workup (albumin, PTH, 25(OH)D, Mg, phosphate).

### Workup of even mildly elevated Ca in a young man
A 20-year-old with corrected Ca persistently 10.4–10.8 should NOT be ignored. Workup:
1. Repeat Ca with simultaneous albumin (preferably ionized Ca)
2. Intact PTH (if high or inappropriately normal → primary hyperparathyroidism likely)
3. 25(OH)D (rule out vit D toxicity; also affects PHPT interpretation)
4. Creatinine, eGFR
5. Phosphate (low in PHPT, normal in FHH)
6. **24-hour urine Ca and creatinine** (Ca/Cr clearance ratio <0.01 → consider FHH; >0.02 typical for PHPT)
7. If FHH suspected: family history + CASR genetic testing
8. If PHPT confirmed: parathyroid imaging (sestamibi scan, 4D-CT, ultrasound), DXA scan, urinalysis for stone risk

PHPT in a 20-year-old, while uncommon, raises concern for **MEN1 / MEN2A syndromes** — order genetic counseling.

### Confounders
- **Albumin** — most important; always interpret with albumin
- **pH/acid-base status** — affects ionized Ca without changing total
- **Tourniquet time** during draw (prolonged stasis → falsely high)
- **Posture** (standing vs supine — total Ca ~5% higher upright)
- **Hemolysis** (mild effect)
- **Recent meal** (postprandial mild rise)
- **Vitamin D status** at draw time
- **Diuretic use** (thiazide raises, loop lowers)
- **Recent IV fluid** (dilution lowers)

## Cross-Marker Synthesis — Patterns to Recognize

**Iron deficiency syndrome:** ↓ ferritin, ↓ Fe, ↑ TIBC, ↓ TSAT. In a young man, this is GI bleeding until proven otherwise (occult colon cancer, ulcer, celiac, H. pylori) or a specific dietary/training pattern. Don't accept "iron deficiency, treated with iron" without an explanation.

**Iron overload syndrome:** ↑ ferritin (>300 in men), ↑ TSAT (>45–50%), → HFE genotyping (C282Y/H63D). Cross-reference 23andMe data.

**B12 functional deficiency:** ↑ MMA + ↑ Hcy. Order serum B12, intrinsic factor antibodies, anti-parietal cell antibodies. Consider methylcobalamin or hydroxocobalamin supplementation.

**MTHFR + folate-sensitive Hcy:** ↑ Hcy with normal MMA + low or marginal folate → consider methylfolate (5-MTHF / Quatrefolic). Cross-reference rs1801133 (C677T) on 23andMe.

**Vitamin D-PTH-calcium axis:**
- Normal Ca + low 25(OH)D + high PTH → vitamin D deficiency with secondary hyperparathyroidism (treat with D)
- High Ca + high PTH → primary hyperparathyroidism (workup)
- High Ca + low PTH → malignancy/granulomatous/D toxicity (workup)
- Low Ca + low PTH → hypoparathyroidism
- Low Ca + high PTH → vit D deficiency, CKD, pseudohypoparathyroidism

**Mg-Ca-K refractoriness:** Hypocalcemia and hypokalemia refractory to replacement → check and replace Mg first.

**Acne + zinc:** if borderline-low zinc + active acne → trial of 25–30 mg elemental zinc (gluconate or picolinate) for 8–12 weeks, with copper monitoring.

**Inflammation distorts everything:** check CRP/ESR alongside iron studies, ferritin, zinc, MMA. In active inflammation, ferritin and CRP rise; iron, TIBC, zinc, and albumin fall (acute phase response). **TSAT and sTfR are most reliable iron markers in inflammation.**
